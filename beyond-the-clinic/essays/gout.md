Gout sits in an awkward cultural position. It has centuries of baggage: the "disease of kings," the self-inflicted affliction of men who drink too much port. That framing is mostly wrong and entirely unhelpful. Most of my patients with gout aren't drinking excessively. They have a combination of genetic hyperuricaemia, chronic kidney disease, diuretic use, or simply the bad luck of being Maori or Pasifika in a country where gout prevalence runs roughly three to four times higher than in European New Zealanders.[^1] When a patient comes in with a red, exquisitely tender first MTP joint and I find myself mentally noting their build or their lunch, I'm doing them a disservice.

The biology is clean. Monosodium urate crystals precipitate in synovial fluid when serum urate sits chronically above the saturation threshold of approximately 0.36 mmol/L. Below that level, crystals dissolve. This is the entire therapeutic premise: get urate low enough, keep it there, and gout resolves. The acute flare is almost a sideshow: uncomfortable and important to treat, but not the main game.

---

## The acute flare

**Confirm the diagnosis.** The classic presentation -- acute monoarthritis of the first MTP, reaching peak intensity within 24 hours, in a middle-aged man with hyperuricaemia -- is highly specific. If you're uncertain, a serum urate during an acute attack is unreliable (it can be paradoxically normal as urate shifts into crystals).[^2] Joint aspiration and polarising microscopy remains the gold standard but is rarely practical in primary care; reserve it for diagnostic uncertainty or atypical presentations.

**Treat early and treat adequately.** The sooner you start anti-inflammatory treatment, the faster resolution. Options are equivalent in efficacy; choose based on comorbidities.[^3]

- **NSAIDs**: first line if no contraindication. Naproxen 750 mg stat then 500 mg BD, or indomethacin 50 mg TDS (the latter is effective but poorly tolerated in older patients). Cover with a PPI if there's any GI risk. Avoid in eGFR < 30, decompensated heart failure, or anticoagulation without careful thought.
- **Colchicine**: excellent choice, especially where NSAIDs are contraindicated. The low-dose regimen is as effective and far better tolerated than the old high-dose protocol: 1 mg stat, then 0.5 mg one hour later.[^4] Dose-reduce in renal impairment. Avoid with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, cyclosporin).
- **Prednisone**: useful when both NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated (e.g., severe CKD). A short course of 30--40 mg daily for 3--5 days, tapering over 7--10 days, works well.[^5] Rebound flares are real -- don't stop abruptly.

**Do not start allopurinol during an acute flare.** It prolongs the attack by mobilising crystal deposits. If the patient is already on allopurinol, continue it -- stopping and restarting is also a trigger.

**Ice matters.** Topical ice packs have evidence for reducing pain in acute gout and are a genuinely useful adjunct to reach for.[^6]

---

## Flare prophylaxis

This is the piece that often gets skipped. When you initiate urate-lowering therapy (ULT), the drop in serum urate destabilises existing crystal deposits and can precipitate flares for months. Without prophylaxis, up to 75% of patients experience a flare in the first six months of ULT -- which destroys adherence.[^7]

Cover with either:

- **Colchicine 0.5 mg daily** (reduce to alternate days if eGFR 30--60, avoid if eGFR < 30), or
- **Naproxen 250 mg BD** with PPI if colchicine is not tolerated.

Continue prophylaxis for **at least 6 months**, or 3 months after achieving target serum urate -- whichever is longer. BPAC recommends a minimum of 6 months in most patients.[^8]

## Long-term urate-lowering therapy

### Who needs it?

Not everyone with a single gout attack requires ULT. But the threshold is lower than many of us apply in practice. Initiate ULT if any of the following:

- Two or more flares per year
- Tophi (any)
- Chronic gouty arthropathy
- Uric acid nephrolithiasis
- CKD stage 2 or higher (eGFR < 60) with gout
- First attack with serum urate persistently > 0.54 mmol/L

A single attack in an otherwise healthy patient with near-normal urate is a reasonable candidate for watchful waiting plus dietary advice, but set the expectation that another attack changes the calculus.

### The target

**Serum urate < 0.36 mmol/L** in most patients. In those with tophi or severe/recurrent disease, target < 0.30 mmol/L to accelerate crystal dissolution.[^9] Do not treat to a number on a form -- treat until the patient stops having flares.

### Allopurinol: start low, go slow

Allopurinol is first-line ULT in New Zealand and is fully subsidised. The key principle: start low (50--100 mg daily) and titrate upward by 50--100 mg every 4 weeks, monitoring serum urate, until target is reached. Many patients end up needing 300--600 mg daily; there is no arbitrary ceiling.

**HLA-B\*5801 testing** before allopurinol is recommended for patients of Han Chinese, Korean, or Thai descent due to high risk of severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCAR/SJS/TEN).[^10] The prevalence of the allele in these populations approaches 6--8%, compared with < 1% in Europeans. MedSafe has issued guidance on this. The test is available through the NZ laboratory system.

Renal dosing: in CKD, start at 50 mg daily and titrate cautiously -- but note that the old guidance of capping allopurinol at the "CKD-adjusted dose" often leaves patients under-treated. Current evidence supports titrating to urate target regardless of eGFR, with close monitoring.[^11]

### Febuxostat: when allopurinol fails

Febuxostat is subsidised in New Zealand with Special Authority for patients who cannot tolerate or have failed allopurinol. It is more potent (40--120 mg daily) and does not require dose adjustment in mild-moderate CKD. There is a signal for increased cardiovascular events compared with allopurinol in patients with established CVD; use with caution in that group.[^12]

## The consultation framing problem

Patients who have lived with gout for years often have fixed beliefs: that it's caused by diet, that medication is a last resort, that they should just avoid red meat and alcohol and manage flares with ibuprofen. Diet does matter at the margins -- fructose and alcohol (especially beer) increase urate, and dairy and vitamin C have modest urate-lowering effects[^13] -- but dietary change alone rarely gets serum urate to target in established disease.

The conversation I find most useful: *"Think of it like high blood pressure. We don't just tell people to lose weight and exercise; we use medication to get the number down, because the number is what causes the damage. Your urate is the number."*

Check adherence at every visit. Check serum urate 4--6 weeks after each dose change. If a patient on allopurinol is still having flares, the answer is almost always dose optimisation, not a new drug.

---

## A note on ethnicity and equity

Gout prevalence in Maori and Pasifika New Zealanders is among the highest in the world -- estimated at 6--8% in Maori men.[^1] This is substantially genetic, driven by loss-of-function variants in urate transporters (particularly ABCG2) that are more common in these populations.[^14] It is not primarily a diet or lifestyle issue, and framing it as such compounds the clinical harm with a layer of stigma.

These patients are also more likely to present late, to have more severe disease, and to be undertreated with ULT. If your patient list reflects the NZ population, you almost certainly have patients with undertreated gout.

## Quick reference

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Acute flare, no contraindications | NSAID (naproxen 750 mg stat then 500 mg BD) or colchicine 1 mg/0.5 mg |
| Acute flare, CKD/anticoagulation | Prednisone 30 mg daily x 5 days, taper |
| Starting ULT | Allopurinol 50--100 mg, titrate q4wk; cover with colchicine prophylaxis for 6+ months |
| Target serum urate | < 0.36 mmol/L (< 0.30 mmol/L if tophi) |
| Han Chinese/Korean/Thai patient | HLA-B*5801 before allopurinol |
| Allopurinol failure/intolerance | Febuxostat (Special Authority) |
