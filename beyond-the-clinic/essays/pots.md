The patient is a 24-year-old woman. She has been seeing various doctors for two years with fatigue, palpitations, brain fog, and dizziness on standing. She has had a normal ECG, a normal echocardiogram, normal thyroid function, normal full blood count, and a cardiology referral that came back with "no structural cardiac disease, likely anxiety." She has been told to exercise more, to drink more water, and, in one consultation she mentions with some bitterness, to worry less.

She does not have anxiety. She has postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome - POTS - and you could have diagnosed it in the first consultation with a sphygmomanometer, a pulse oximeter, and ten minutes.

## Part I - What POTS actually is

POTS is a form of dysautonomia characterised by an abnormal increase in heart rate upon moving from lying to standing, in the absence of significant orthostatic hypotension. The diagnostic criterion is a sustained heart rate increment of at least 30 beats per minute within ten minutes of standing, or at least 40 beats per minute in those under 19, accompanied by symptoms of orthostatic intolerance. [1]

The distinction from orthostatic hypotension is important. In orthostatic hypotension, blood pressure drops on standing and the patient feels faint. In POTS, blood pressure is relatively preserved but the heart rate compensates excessively for a failure of normal venous return - producing palpitations, chest discomfort, breathlessness, and lightheadedness that are often mistaken for a cardiac or anxiety disorder rather than an autonomic one.

The symptoms are not just cardiovascular. Because the autonomic nervous system regulates an extraordinary range of physiological functions, POTS produces a wide and often baffling symptom cluster: brain fog (typically worse upright, better lying down), fatigue, exercise intolerance, nausea and bloating, headache, sleep disturbance, temperature dysregulation, and in some patients near-syncope or syncope. The cognitive symptoms in particular - the difficulty concentrating, word-finding problems, and mental exhaustion - are among the most disabling and the most frequently attributed to anxiety or depression.

The condition affects women considerably more often than men - roughly 4 to 1 in most series, though this likely reflects both biological factors and the well-documented clinical bias toward dismissing somatic symptoms in young women. [2] It most commonly presents in adolescence or early adulthood, often triggered by an identifiable event: a viral illness, a pregnancy, a significant physical or psychological stressor, or, in the past five years, COVID-19.

## Part II - The subtypes and why they matter

POTS is not a single pathophysiological entity. Understanding the subtype helps direct management.

**Neuropathic POTS** is the most common subtype. Partial dysautonomia affecting the peripheral sympathetic nerves results in impaired vasoconstriction in the dependent limbs on standing, causing venous blood to pool in the legs and pelvis. The heart rate rises compensatorily. Skin biopsy showing reduced sympathetic innervation of small vessels can confirm this mechanism, though it is not routinely available in New Zealand primary care.

**Hyperadrenergic POTS** is characterised by elevated plasma norepinephrine on standing (above 600 pg/mL), and clinically by symptoms that include not just orthostatic tachycardia but hypertension on standing, tremor, anxiety-like symptoms, and marked pallor or flushing. These patients paradoxically may have an elevated blood pressure on standing rather than the expected drop. This subtype responds differently to treatment - beta-blockade is more useful, and fludrocortisone (which can worsen hypertension) should be used cautiously.

**Hypovolemic POTS** involves reduced plasma volume as the primary driver. These patients are often chronically underfilling their intravascular compartment, which exacerbates the orthostatic response. They may appear pale, have low-normal blood pressure at rest, and respond particularly well to volume expansion strategies.

**Secondary POTS** occurs in the context of another condition that damages autonomic function: hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS), where connective tissue laxity affects vascular tone; autoimmune conditions; diabetes mellitus with autonomic neuropathy; or, prominently now, Long COVID. When POTS is identified, a brief screen for these associations is worthwhile - not because the POTS treatment changes substantially but because the underlying condition may have its own management implications.

In practice, subtype analysis is imprecise without access to autonomic laboratory testing (tilt table, sweat testing, plasma catecholamines), which is limited in New Zealand. The clinical distinction that matters most in primary care is the hyperadrenergic subtype, because the treatment differs.

## Part III - How to diagnose it in your rooms

The formal diagnostic procedure - tilt table testing - is available in cardiology and neurology departments and involves monitoring continuous blood pressure and heart rate during passive tilting from supine to 70 degrees. It is the gold standard. It is also not necessary for most diagnoses.

The **active standing test** (sometimes called the NASA lean test) is a validated, equipment-minimal procedure that can be performed in any GP consultation room. [3]

The protocol:

1. Have the patient lie supine for five to ten minutes.
2. Record heart rate and blood pressure.
3. Ask the patient to stand upright, feet together, and lean back lightly against a wall (the lean reduces muscle pumping artefact).
4. Record heart rate and blood pressure at one, three, five, seven, and ten minutes of standing.
5. Note any symptom reproduction during the test - this adds diagnostic weight.

A sustained HR increment of 30 bpm or more from the supine baseline (40 or more in under-19s), maintained across the standing period, without a significant drop in systolic BP (greater than 20 mmHg), meets diagnostic criteria. [1] The test is positive even if the patient sits down before ten minutes because their symptoms have become intolerable - in fact, this is an informative finding.

A few practical notes. The patient should not have taken beta-blockers, midodrine, or fludrocortisone before the test. They should be adequately hydrated. They should not have just eaten a large meal. Morning testing is preferable to afternoon. If the result is borderline, the test can be repeated on a different day.

The common failure mode is checking a lying and standing pulse once and calling it normal. A single standing measurement at 30 seconds is not an orthostatic assessment. POTS is diagnosed over ten minutes - the tachycardia is often not maximal at one minute.

## Part IV - The misdiagnosis pattern

The average diagnostic delay for POTS has been estimated at four to six years. [2] This is not explained by diagnostic complexity. It is explained by a pattern of clinical reasoning that treats POTS presentations as other things.

**Anxiety and panic disorder** is the most common misdiagnosis. The symptom overlap is real: palpitations, breathlessness, chest tightness, lightheadedness, cognitive difficulties, and fatigue are features of both. The distinguishing feature is orthostatic - POTS symptoms are reliably worse upright and better lying down. A panic disorder does not get better when the patient lies on the floor. If a patient describes palpitations that are reproducibly triggered by standing and relieved by sitting or lying, orthostatic tachycardia should be in the differential before anxiety.

**Deconditioning** is the second most common deflection. Exercise intolerance and fatigue are genuine features of POTS, and deconditioning does occur secondarily as patients curtail activity to manage their symptoms. But "deconditioning" offered as a primary explanation, without a positive orthostatic assessment, is an untested hypothesis. It also creates a harmful circular dynamic: the patient is told to exercise more, exercises, feels significantly worse (as they will, because upright exercise is the most potent trigger for POTS symptoms), and is then told their failure to improve with exercise confirms a psychological component.

**Chronic fatigue / ME/CFS** has substantial diagnostic overlap with POTS - many patients meeting ME/CFS criteria have undiagnosed orthostatic intolerance as a primary or contributing mechanism. [4] The clinical implication is that any patient with an ME/CFS-like presentation should be screened with an active standing test before the label is accepted, because the POTS subset have more specific treatment options.

**Vasovagal syncope** is a reasonable differential in patients with syncope, but vasovagal episodes are episodic, typically associated with a clear trigger (pain, fasting, heat, emotional stimulus), and are not accompanied by the persistent daily symptom burden of POTS.

The demographic most affected by diagnostic delay - young women in their twenties and thirties with fatigue and palpitations and normal standard investigations - is the demographic most likely to have their symptoms attributed to emotional causes. This is not a neutral observation about patient characteristics. It is a description of a clinical bias with measurable consequences for time to treatment.

## Part V - Post-COVID POTS

COVID-19 dramatically increased the clinical visibility of POTS by producing a wave of new cases in its wake, making it impossible to continue treating the condition as obscure.

Multiple case series and cohort studies have documented new-onset POTS following acute COVID-19 infection, with estimates suggesting it affects somewhere between 2 and 14 percent of individuals with Long COVID syndrome. [5] The onset is typically weeks to months after the acute infection, following a period of apparent recovery - which distinguishes it from acute viral myocarditis and aligns with a post-infectious autonomic process.

The mechanisms are under active investigation and may be multiple. Proposed contributors include: autoantibodies against adrenergic receptors or angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2); small fibre neuropathy affecting autonomic function; mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS) occurring in parallel; persistent endothelial dysfunction; and direct damage to autonomic ganglia. The autoimmune hypothesis has attracted the most attention - antibodies against alpha-1 adrenergic receptors and beta-2 adrenergic receptors have been found in some POTS patients, and this may be particularly relevant in post-COVID cases. [6]

For the NZ GP, the practical point is that any patient with Long COVID symptoms who describes worsening on standing, palpitations, or cognitive symptoms that are reliably positional should have an active standing test. Post-COVID POTS is managed on the same principles as POTS of other aetiologies, and recovery is possible - though the timeline varies.

## Part VI - The EDS connection

A substantial proportion of patients with POTS - estimates range from 20 to 50 percent in specialist series - also meet criteria for hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) or hypermobility spectrum disorder. [7] The mechanism is plausible: connective tissue laxity affects vessel wall tone, increases venous pooling, and disrupts the mechanical scaffolding that supports normal venous return on standing.

This co-occurrence matters clinically because hEDS is itself underdiagnosed, has its own management implications (joint protection, physiotherapy, pain management), and may direct treatment choices in POTS. It also means that the patient who comes in with POTS and "hypermobile joints" and "stretchy skin" is not presenting with three unrelated curiosities.

A brief screen for hEDS in any patient with confirmed POTS is worthwhile: the Beighton score for hypermobility, questions about joint dislocations and subluxations, skin hyperextensibility, and atrophic scarring. Referral to a clinical geneticist or a specialist with interest in connective tissue disorders is appropriate if the picture is suggestive.

## Part VII - Management: what the evidence supports

Management of POTS is almost entirely non-pharmacological as a first step, and the evidence for non-pharmacological measures is better than for most of the available drugs.

**Fluid and sodium loading** is the bedrock. The physiological rationale is straightforward: more intravascular volume means less relative hypovolaemia on standing, means less compensatory tachycardia. Patients should target two to three litres of fluid per day, with additional sodium intake of approximately 10 grams per day (or 2-3 grams of sodium, roughly equivalent) unless they have the hyperadrenergic subtype with standing hypertension, in which case this approach should be modified. [1] Oral rehydration salts are a practical vehicle for combined fluid and electrolyte loading.

**Compression garments** reduce venous pooling in the legs and abdomen. Waist-high graduated compression stockings are more effective than knee-high because they also compress the pelvic venous reservoir. An abdominal binder provides additional benefit. The practical barrier is that compression garments are uncomfortable, hot, and require significant patient motivation - but the evidence for their effect on orthostatic heart rate is solid.

**Postural and behavioural modifications**: elevating the head of the bed by 10 to 20 degrees (this reduces nocturnal supine expansion of blood volume and reduces morning postural stress); eating smaller, lower-carbohydrate meals (large meals divert blood to the splanchnic circulation); avoiding hot environments and hot showers; using counter-pressure manoeuvres during positional changes (leg crossing, squatting, tensing calf muscles); and pacing activity to avoid post-exertional symptom exacerbation.

**Exercise rehabilitation** is essential and must be approached carefully. The key principle is to begin with recumbent or semi-recumbent exercise - swimming, rowing machine, recumbent cycling - before progressing to upright exercise. The Levine protocol (developed for post-spaceflight deconditioning and validated in POTS) provides a structured progression. [8] Starting a POTS patient on a standard walking or running programme is likely to be counterproductive and to produce post-exertional malaise that reinforces illness beliefs and reduces adherence.

**Pharmacological options** when non-pharmacological measures are insufficient:

*Fludrocortisone* (0.05 to 0.2 mg daily) expands plasma volume through mineralocorticoid action. It is widely used and modestly effective. The evidence from clinical trials is underwhelming - a randomised trial found it less effective than propranolol as monotherapy - but it remains a reasonable adjunct, particularly in hypovolemic POTS. [9] Monitor for hypokalaemia and, in the longer term, for fluid retention.

*Low-dose propranolol* (10 to 20 mg before activities involving standing) reduces heart rate and symptoms, particularly in hyperadrenergic POTS. Higher doses worsen exercise tolerance and fatigue in many patients. A randomised crossover trial found that low-dose propranolol was superior to fludrocortisone for symptom control in POTS. [9]

*Midodrine* (an alpha-1 agonist, 2.5 to 10 mg three times daily) increases venous and arterial tone, reduces venous pooling, and lowers standing heart rate by improving preload. It should not be taken in the evening (supine hypertension risk). It is not currently funded in New Zealand on the community pharmaceutical schedule for POTS, which is a practical barrier.

*Ivabradine* (an If channel inhibitor that selectively reduces heart rate without affecting blood pressure or contractility) has accumulated positive evidence in POTS, particularly in those where beta-blockade is poorly tolerated. [10] It is also not funded in New Zealand for this indication. Advocacy to PHARMAC for funding of ivabradine in POTS is a worthwhile exercise for any GP who sees this condition regularly.

*Pyridostigmine* (an acetylcholinesterase inhibitor) has some evidence in POTS, thought to act by enhancing ganglionic neurotransmission. It is better tolerated than many alternatives but evidence remains limited.

## Part VIII - Having the conversation

The conversation that POTS patients most commonly describe wanting but not receiving is one in which their symptoms are named, explained mechanistically, and taken seriously as a physical condition rather than attributed to psychological causes.

The explanation that tends to land is physiological: "Your autonomic nervous system - the part that regulates heart rate, blood pressure, and blood flow - is not adjusting quickly enough when you stand up. Your heart rate overshoots to compensate. That is what is causing the palpitations, the lightheadedness, and a lot of the fatigue and brain fog. It is real, it is measurable, and we can treat it."

What does not tend to land, and actively damages the therapeutic relationship: "Have you thought about seeing a psychologist?" as the first response to a positive standing test. Or: "A lot of people with this kind of symptom find exercise helps" without acknowledging that exercise is genuinely difficult and must be approached with specific modifications.

The concept of post-exertional malaise - symptom exacerbation following physical or cognitive exertion beyond the patient's current threshold - needs to be explicitly acknowledged. Patients who are told to push through will push through and feel worse, will then feel blamed for not getting better, and will disengage from care. Understanding that pacing is a genuine clinical strategy - and not a synonym for avoidance - changes the conversation.

## Part IX - The New Zealand specifics

New Zealand has limited access to formal autonomic testing (tilt table, quantitative sudomotor axon reflex testing, CASS). Most patients who are diagnosed are diagnosed on clinical and active standing test criteria, which is appropriate. Specialist autonomic neurology or cardiology involvement is appropriate for complex or refractory cases, but for the majority of POTS presentations, GP-led management with allied health input (physiotherapy, dietitian) is both feasible and adequate.

The community pharmaceutical funding gap for midodrine and ivabradine is a genuine clinical problem. Both have evidence, both are used in Australia and the UK as PHARMAC-equivalent listed medications for relevant indications, and the barrier to their use for NZ POTS patients is regulatory rather than clinical. PHARMAC submissions for widened funding of ivabradine in particular have been made by patient advocacy groups including POTS New Zealand.

The overlap of POTS with Long COVID means that a number of NZ patients are presenting through Long COVID clinics rather than through traditional cardiology or neurology pathways. GP awareness of POTS as a component of Long COVID - and of the active standing test as a simple screen that should be performed in any Long COVID patient with relevant symptoms - is the most efficient way to improve diagnostic rates at a population level.
