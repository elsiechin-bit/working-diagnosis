The number of New Zealanders participating in organised endurance events has grown substantially over the past two decades [1]. The Auckland Marathon, Queenstown International Marathon, Rotorua Marathon, and the Wellington and Christchurch events collectively attract tens of thousands of participants annually. The trail running and ultramarathon scene  -  anchored by events like the Tarawera Ultramarathon (50 km to 102 miles), the Kepler Challenge, and dozens of smaller trail events  -  has expanded rapidly since the early 2010s. Ironman New Zealand at Taupo has been running since 1985.

The result is that GPs now regularly see patients who are training for or competing in events that stress the human body at an intensity and duration that falls well outside the scope of anything the epidemiology of exercise benefit was designed to describe. The patient asking for cardiovascular clearance before their first marathon is a different clinical situation from the patient with type 1 diabetes who is training for a 100-mile ultramarathon in the Coromandel. Both deserve a GP who knows the territory rather than one who improvises.

This note covers pre-participation assessment, cardiovascular considerations (including when to investigate and when to refer), the most common clinical presentations in endurance athletes, condition-specific management for the most relevant diagnoses, the specific hazards of ultramarathon events, and the equity and access dimensions of endurance sport medicine. It is a consultation-support document for the GP; it is not a patient handout.

## Part I  -  Pre-participation assessment

The question "do you want me to get cleared before I start marathon training?" is one GPs handle inconsistently, partly because there is no single agreed framework for recreational athletes and partly because the answer depends substantially on the individual.

A useful starting structure is risk stratification by age, cardiovascular history, symptoms, and event type [2].

**For asymptomatic individuals under 35 with no personal or family cardiac history:** there is no evidence base supporting routine ECG or exercise testing before beginning recreational running or marathon training. The 2015 American Heart Association/American College of Cardiology guidelines for competitive athletes recommend a 12-element history and physical examination; routine ECG is endorsed by the European Society of Cardiology but not the AHA, reflecting a genuine methodological disagreement rather than a clinical consensus [3]. In practice, for a healthy 28-year-old planning their first marathon, a focused cardiovascular history and examination is appropriate; ordering an ECG as standard is not clearly supported and risks generating incidental findings that require further investigation. The question to answer is whether there is anything in the history or examination that warrants further evaluation  -  not to provide a document-based clearance for an activity that does not require GP permission.

**For individuals over 35, particularly men and postmenopausal women:** the risk profile shifts. Sudden cardiac death during endurance events in this age group is predominantly from coronary artery disease rather than the structural abnormalities (hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy) that dominate in younger athletes [4]. Assessment should focus on cardiovascular risk factors, exertional symptoms, family history of premature coronary disease, and any history of chest pain, dyspnoea out of proportion to effort, or presyncope. Exercise testing is worth considering for symptomatic patients or those with high traditional cardiovascular risk who are embarking on a new high-intensity programme; it is not routinely indicated for asymptomatic low-risk individuals.

**For patients with known cardiac conditions:** this is the group where pre-participation guidance has the most clinical consequence and is covered in Part II.

**What to ask at the assessment:**
- Any exertional chest pain, pressure, or tightness?
- Any exertional dyspnoea disproportionate to effort?
- Any syncope or near-syncope during or immediately after exercise?
- Any palpitations during exercise, particularly if sustained?
- Family history of sudden cardiac death before age 50?
- Family history of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, Marfan syndrome, or inherited arrhythmia syndromes?
- Current medications  -  particularly any that affect heart rate, rhythm, or exercise physiology?
- For women: menstrual history (relevant to RED-S, below)?
- Any history of stress fractures?
- Dietary adequacy for the proposed training volume?

Any positive answer in the cardiovascular stream warrants more careful assessment before giving the green light. The bar for referring to cardiology should be lower than for other specialties: sudden cardiac death during exercise, while rare, is irreversible.

## Part II  -  Cardiovascular considerations

Sudden cardiac death (SCD) during marathons is rare but well-studied. The estimated incidence is approximately 0.5 to 2 per 100,000 participants for marathon events, with higher estimates for longer and more demanding events [4,5]. This is not zero, and understanding the risk profile matters for both pre-participation assessment and explaining risk to patients.

**In athletes under 35,** the most common structural causes of SCD are hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC), anomalous coronary arteries, and inherited arrhythmia syndromes including long QT syndrome and catecholaminergic polymorphic ventricular tachycardia [6]. Importantly, ARVC has a particular association with endurance sport  -  right ventricular dilation and fibrofatty change may be provoked or accelerated by high-volume endurance training, and ARVC is considered an important cause of exercise-related SCD in endurance athletes specifically [7].

**In athletes over 35,** atherosclerotic coronary artery disease dominates. The presentation may be the first clinical manifestation of previously silent disease. Exercise-triggered plaque rupture, particularly in the context of high sympathetic tone during maximal effort, is the most common mechanism.

**Athlete's heart versus pathological remodelling.** Regular endurance training produces physiological cardiac remodelling  -  increased left ventricular cavity size, modestly increased wall thickness, and resting bradycardia. This is athlete's heart; it is benign and should not be confused with pathological hypertrophy. The distinction from HCM can be genuinely difficult and typically requires echocardiography, with detraining studies or genetic testing reserved for equivocal cases [6]. GPs who discover LVH on ECG in a high-volume endurance athlete should not assume this is pathological  -  but referral to cardiology for echocardiographic assessment is appropriate when there is clinical uncertainty.

**Red flag symptoms requiring urgent evaluation before return to sport:**
- Exertional syncope or presyncope (the most important: syncope during, not after, exercise has a higher pre-test probability of structural or arrhythmic cause than post-exercise syncope, which may be vasovagal)
- Sustained palpitations or irregular rhythm during exercise
- Exertional chest pain
- Any family history of SCD in a first-degree relative under 50

**Managing patients with established cardiac conditions** requires case-by-case judgement, ideally in conjunction with the relevant specialist. The following are approximate working principles:
- Post-MI with preserved LV function: after appropriate rehabilitation, recreational endurance sport is generally safe; competitive endurance sport requires cardiology input.
- Paroxysmal AF: management depends on symptom burden and anticoagulation status; many patients with rate-controlled or ablated AF participate in endurance events.
- WPW: untreated WPW with a short accessory pathway refractory period is a contraindication to competitive endurance sport; refer.
- ICD in situ: international guidelines vary; in general, patients with ICDs have participated in competitive sport with acceptable outcomes, but this is a specialist decision and the NZTA driving standard interactions (if the patient is also a commercial driver) add complexity.

## Part III  -  Common presentations in endurance athletes

**Iron deficiency** is the most common haematological problem in endurance athletes, and its frequency is substantially underappreciated in the GP setting [8]. The mechanisms are multiple: increased iron loss through foot-strike haemolysis (red blood cell destruction from repetitive impact), GI microbleeding from ischaemia during prolonged running, increased sweat losses, and simple dietary inadequacy in athletes eating insufficient red meat or absorbable iron. Female athletes at high training volumes are at highest risk.

The clinical presentation may be fatigue and impaired performance rather than classic iron-deficiency symptoms. Ferritin is the most useful screening test; many sports medicine practitioners use a higher threshold for intervention in endurance athletes (ferritin <30-50 µg/L rather than the standard laboratory reference range lower limit) on the basis that athletes require adequate iron stores for optimal oxygen-carrying capacity and mitochondrial function [8]. Treatment is oral iron, with attention to absorption (take with vitamin C, away from tea and coffee), or IV iron if oral supplementation is insufficient or not tolerated. Investigating the cause of iron deficiency in male athletes and postmenopausal women follows the usual approach  -  haematological investigation, consideration of GI source.

**Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB)** affects a significant minority of endurance athletes  -  higher prevalence estimates in swimming and cold-weather sports, but common across all endurance disciplines [9]. The presentation is cough, wheeze, chest tightness, or dyspnoea during or immediately after exercise; onset after the warm-up phase and resolution within 30-60 minutes of stopping are characteristic. EIB may occur in athletes with no background asthma diagnosis.

Management: a short-acting beta-agonist (salbutamol) taken 15-30 minutes before exercise is effective for most patients with isolated EIB. For patients with more frequent or severe symptoms, an inhaled corticosteroid is indicated. There are two things worth knowing for patients considering competitive events: first, WADA's Prohibited List does not prohibit inhaled beta-agonists at therapeutic doses for events where drug testing applies (though specific agents and doses have specific regulations and patients planning international competition should check current WADA rules); second, vocal cord dysfunction (paradoxical vocal fold motion) is an important mimic of EIB that responds poorly to bronchodilators and is worth considering in patients who don't respond as expected [9].

**Overuse injuries** are the most common medical problem in endurance runners, and their management is bread-and-butter sports medicine. The important GP-level distinctions:

*Stress reactions and stress fractures* exist on a spectrum from periosteal oedema (stress reaction) to frank cortical disruption (stress fracture). Common sites in runners: metatarsals, tibia, navicular, fibula, and  -  the ones that matter most  -  femoral neck and pubic ramus. Femoral neck stress fractures on the compression side are typically low-risk and can be managed conservatively; femoral neck stress fractures on the tension side (superior cortex) carry a significant risk of displacement and require urgent orthopaedic referral. Navicular stress fractures are high-risk for non-union and also warrant specialist referral rather than conservative management and hope. Plain X-ray is insensitive for early stress fracture; MRI is the investigation of choice if the clinical suspicion is high.

Any athlete with a second or third stress fracture, or a stress fracture in an unusual site, should prompt evaluation for RED-S (see Part VI) and bone density assessment, particularly in women.

*Plantar fasciitis, Achilles tendinopathy, and ITB syndrome* are the most common soft tissue overuse problems. They have evidence-based conservative management protocols  -  load management, progressive tendon loading for Achilles, hip strengthening for ITB  -  and most resolve with time and appropriate rehabilitation. GPs can initiate this, though referral to physiotherapy with sports medicine experience improves outcomes.

**Overtraining syndrome** is a diagnosis of exclusion characterised by persistent underperformance despite adequate recovery, often with accompanying mood disturbance, recurrent illness, sleep disruption, and hormonal suppression. It is distinct from the normal short-term fatigue of high training load. Investigations typically show no specific abnormality; the marker is the pattern. Management is reduction in training load and, if necessary, a significant rest period. The distinction from depression, RED-S, and thyroid disease requires clinical assessment. Athletes are often reluctant to reduce training, and the GP's role includes helping them understand that continuing to train through overtraining syndrome prolongs rather than shortens recovery [10].

## Part IV  -  Condition-specific management

**Type 1 diabetes and endurance sport.** Managing T1DM during endurance events requires preparation that most standard diabetes management guidelines are not calibrated to provide. The key principles:

Exercise has variable glycaemic effects depending on intensity, duration, and type. Aerobic exercise at moderate intensity typically reduces blood glucose; high-intensity anaerobic efforts may transiently raise glucose; ultramarathon distances often see significant glucose variability throughout an event.

For insulin pump users: basal rate reduction of 20-50% during the event, with timing of the reduction depending on pump model and individual response; many athletes reduce basal 60-90 minutes before starting. For injection-based regimens: a reduction in rapid-acting insulin with the pre-event meal (by 25-50%, individually calibrated) and consideration of basal insulin reduction. These adjustments are highly individual  -  a patient preparing for their first marathon should be working through this with a diabetes specialist or specialist diabetes nurse, not calibrating it on race morning.

Hypoglycaemia during events is the primary acute risk. Target glucose before starting: 7-10 mmol/L. Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) is extremely useful but has known limitations during rapid exercise: interstitial glucose lags capillary glucose, and acceleration in exercise intensity may produce artifactual glucose changes. Carry rapid-acting carbohydrate. Brief intense efforts (a steep hill) can cause transient hyperglycaemia; the sustained aerobic effort of a long course will typically bring it down.

The risk of delayed hypoglycaemia  -  occurring 12-24 hours after an event  -  is underappreciated and significant. Muscles are rapidly replenishing glycogen stores post-event, and insulin sensitivity may be substantially elevated. Patients should be counselled about reduced insulin doses and increased glucose monitoring for at least the evening and night following a long event.

T2DM managed with diet alone or metformin poses much lower hypoglycaemia risk and most patients can participate in endurance events without significant medication adjustment. Patients on sulfonylureas or insulin should follow the same principles as T1DM, scaled to their regimen.

**Hypertension.** Well-controlled hypertension is not a contraindication to endurance sport, and regular aerobic exercise is a validated non-pharmacological intervention for blood pressure reduction [11]. The clinical questions are: is BP adequately controlled before embarking on high-intensity training? And do the antihypertensive medications interact adversely with endurance exercise?

Beta-blockers attenuate the exercise heart rate response, limiting maximal exercise capacity and making heart rate-based training guidance inaccurate. They are generally not preferred for hypertensive athletes if alternatives are available. ACE inhibitors and ARBs are well tolerated during exercise. Diuretics carry risks of dehydration and electrolyte depletion during prolonged events; thiazide diuretics may also appear on WADA's prohibited list for certain competitive sports as masking agents  -  check current WADA guidance before prescribing to patients who compete.

**Asthma.** Well-controlled asthma is compatible with marathon and ultramarathon participation. The specific EIB management is covered in Part III. The additional consideration for long events is that SABA use may need to be available during the event, and patients with any history of severe exacerbations should have an agreed management plan for exercise-triggered deterioration.

**Thyroid disease.** Hypothyroidism reduces exercise capacity through reduced cardiac output, impaired thermoregulation, and fatigue; adequate thyroxine replacement should be confirmed before attributing poor exercise performance to training factors. Hyperthyroidism  -  including thyrotoxicosis and poorly controlled Graves disease  -  increases the risk of exercise-triggered arrhythmia (particularly AF) and is a reason to defer high-intensity endurance events until euthyroid.

## Part V  -  Ultramarathon-specific hazards

Ultramarathons  -  broadly defined as events longer than 42.2 km, in practice ranging from 50 km trail races to multi-day mountain events  -  present a different medical risk profile from road marathons. The extended duration, wilderness setting, variable climate, and extremes of physical demand produce presentations that are uncommon in shorter events and are worth knowing specifically.

**Exercise-associated hyponatraemia (EAH)** is the most important preventable cause of death in ultramarathon events [12,13]. It is caused primarily by excessive hypotonic fluid intake  -  overdrinking relative to losses  -  compounded in some cases by inappropriate vasopressin (ADH) secretion. The characteristic victim is the slower participant - often a woman of smaller body mass - who has been told to "stay hydrated" and has been drinking to a schedule (e.g. 500-750 mL per hour) rather than to thirst throughout an extended event, rather than the fast elite athlete.

Sodium falls below 135 mmol/L in EAH; below 125 mmol/L, cerebral oedema, encephalopathy, seizures, and death are possible (exercise-associated hyponatraemic encephalopathy, EAHE). The presentation can be difficult to distinguish from heat exhaustion, hypoglycaemia, or exhaustion; the specific features of EAHE include nausea, headache, bloating, confusion, and  -  importantly  -  the absence of the response to oral rehydration that marks heat exhaustion.

**The most important clinical point:** treating EAH with hypotonic or isotonic IV fluids is dangerous. It will dilute serum sodium further. Isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl) does not treat hyponatraemia; hypertonic saline (3% NaCl, 100 mL IV bolus) is the correct treatment for EAHE [13]. Most GP-level involvement is in counselling patients before events  -  the evidence-based advice is drink to thirst, not to a prescribed volume, and to choose electrolyte-containing drinks during extended events rather than plain water.

**The GP pre-event conversation** for ultramarathon athletes should include explicit discussion of EAH and the dangers of overdrinking. This is counter-cultural advice in a community that has been intensively marketed to about hydration; it needs to be stated clearly.

**Rhabdomyolysis.** Exercise-associated rhabdomyolysis  -  skeletal muscle breakdown with release of myoglobin, creatine kinase, and intracellular contents into the circulation  -  is common after ultramarathons, particularly at first attempts at new distances [14]. Mild CK elevation (five to ten times normal) is nearly universal after a 100 km event and is not clinically significant. Severe rhabdomyolysis with acute kidney injury (AKI) is less common but recognised.

Presentation: cola-coloured urine, severe muscle pain beyond the expected post-event soreness, flank pain, reduced urine output. Any athlete presenting post-event with these features needs urgent assessment: U&E, CK, urinalysis. Treatment is aggressive IV hydration targeting urine output of at least 200-300 mL/hour until myoglobin clears.

Risk factors for severe rhabdomyolysis include sickle cell trait (important to note in athletes of African descent), statin use (statins increase rhabdomyolysis risk during extreme exertion, and patients on statins who are planning their first ultramarathon are worth a specific conversation), heat and dehydration, and concurrent NSAID use.

**NSAIDs during ultramarathons.** The use of NSAIDs  -  particularly ibuprofen  -  during ultramarathon events is extremely common. Studies report that between 50 and 70 percent of ultramarathon participants use NSAIDs during or around events, most prophylactically [15]. The rationale is pain prevention and anti-inflammatory effect; the evidence does not support this rationale, and the risks are substantial.

NSAIDs during prolonged exercise and dehydration substantially increase the risk of AKI. They amplify rhabdomyolysis risk. They increase GI permeability and worsen GI symptoms, which are already a major problem in ultramarathons. The combination of NSAID use, dehydration, heat, and high-intensity exercise is a reliable recipe for renal injury. The advice to give patients before endurance events is explicit: do not take NSAIDs during the event. Paracetamol for pain during events is acceptable.

**GI symptoms.** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, and bloating affect more than half of ultramarathon participants and are among the most common reasons for event non-completion [16]. The mechanism involves reduced gut perfusion during high-intensity effort, mechanical jostling, changes in gut motility, and the impact of gels and sports drinks  -  particularly those with high concentrations of fructose  -  on GI mucosa. NSAID use compounds this substantially.

Pre-event preparation: gut training (practising consuming the planned race nutrition during long training runs), identifying individual trigger foods and drinks, and race-pace calibration (higher intensity → lower gut perfusion → more symptoms) reduces but does not eliminate the problem. GPs can help patients understand that GI issues are physiological rather than pathological, that lower race pace (relative to VO2 max) genuinely reduces symptoms, and that NSAID avoidance is essential.

**Heat illness and hypothermia.** New Zealand's trail events traverse terrain where both are possible on the same day. Heat exhaustion (heat, exertion, dehydration, poor perfusion → fatigue, nausea, headache, elevated core temperature) is managed with rest, cooling, and oral or IV rehydration. Heat stroke (core temperature >40°C with neurological dysfunction) is a medical emergency requiring immediate cooling and evacuation. The management distinction between heat stroke and hyponatraemic encephalopathy is important and is not always straightforward clinically; when in doubt at an event medical station, assess sodium if possible.

Hypothermia in mountain events  -  particularly events with mandatory gear lists and long cut-off times that mean slower participants are on course in deteriorating conditions  -  follows standard management principles: insulate, prevent further heat loss, evacuate.

**Sleep deprivation in multi-day events.** Events exceeding 24 hours produce cumulative sleep deprivation with predictable impairments in judgement, proprioception, and emotional regulation. Falls and navigational errors increase. There is no standard GP management for this; the relevant advice is pre-event awareness that cognitive and psychomotor impairment begins after approximately 17-20 hours of wakefulness and is substantially worse after 24 hours [17]. Athletes with conditions affected by sleep disruption (seizure threshold, blood glucose management, mental health) need specific plans.

## Part VI  -  Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S)

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport, described in the IOC consensus statement first published in 2014 and updated in 2023, is the current framework for understanding the downstream health consequences of low energy availability  -  caloric intake insufficient to meet both training energy expenditure and resting physiological needs [18].

Formerly called the Female Athlete Triad (disordered eating, amenorrhoea, low bone density), RED-S is now understood to affect male as well as female athletes, to involve multiple organ systems beyond bone and reproduction, and to be driven by low energy availability rather than requiring a diagnosable eating disorder.

**The clinical picture:**
- *Menstrual disturbance* in female athletes: irregular cycles, amenorrhoea. This is not normal at high training volumes; it is a sign of hypothalamic suppression from energy deficiency. Athletes who have been told that losing their periods when training hard is "just what happens" have been misinformed. Amenorrhoea in a training female athlete is a clinical finding requiring evaluation.
- *Low bone mineral density and stress fractures*: energy deficiency suppresses IGF-1 and oestrogen, reducing bone formation. The practical consequence is stress fracture risk that substantially exceeds what training load alone would predict. A third stress fracture in a committed female runner is not bad luck; it is a signal.
- *Impaired immunity and recurrent illness*: athletes with RED-S are more susceptible to upper respiratory tract infections and have prolonged recovery from illness.
- *Psychological features*: irritability, mood disturbance, poor concentration, and in some cases features of disordered eating or exercise addiction.
- *In male athletes*: testosterone suppression, similar bone and immune effects, and impaired performance.

**The GP's role** is to recognise the pattern  -  particularly in lean, high-volume athletes with menstrual disruption, recurrent stress fractures, or persistently poor performance despite training  -  and not to normalise it. Evaluation includes bone density (DXA), hormonal assessment, and nutritional assessment, ideally with a sports dietitian experienced in endurance athletes. Referring to a sport and exercise physician is appropriate for complex or established cases. The cornerstone of treatment is increasing energy availability  -  which requires addressing both intake and, sometimes, training volume.

## Part VII  -  NZ context and practical navigation

New Zealand's trail running community is active and growing. Several practical points for the GP.

**Major events** that patients commonly train for include: the Tarawera Ultramarathon (February; 50 km, 100 km, and 102-mile distances with the longer events presenting genuinely high-risk scenarios, particularly for first-timers), the Kepler Challenge (December; 60 km alpine terrain), the Tongariro Crossing and associated events, the Routeburn Classic, and city road marathons in Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, Dunedin, and Queenstown. Ironman New Zealand at Taupo (March) adds the triathlon/swim and cycle dimensions.

Many of New Zealand's trail ultramarathon events traverse remote terrain. Medical evacuation may be significantly delayed. Event medical infrastructure varies. GPs counselling patients before remote-location events should include explicit discussion of self-care capacity  -  knowing when to withdraw from an event, recognising the signs of heat illness, hyponatraemia, and rhabdomyolysis, and understanding that continuing despite warning signs in a remote location is a different risk calculation from continuing in an urban event.

**WADA and therapeutic use exemptions (TUEs).** Most recreational runners are not subject to WADA regulations, but athletes competing in events run under Athletics New Zealand or national federation rules may be. Athletes on therapeutic medications  -  inhaled SABA, systemic corticosteroids, stimulant ADHD medications  -  should check current WADA regulations and may need to apply for a TUE before competition. GPs can support TUE applications but should not assume familiarity with the process; it involves specific documentation and may require specialist supporting letters.

**The patient who won't stop.** Endurance athletes are, by definition, people who have trained themselves not to listen to the signals that would make most people stop. This characteristic, highly adaptive during training and competition, is highly maladaptive when the signal is a stress fracture, cardiac symptoms, or overtraining. The consultation challenge is not usually explaining the medical facts; it is helping a patient with strong psychological investment in their sport make the rational decision in the short term to protect the long-term capacity they care about. Framing injury management and recovery as training  -  not as time off  -  is often more effective than framing it as rest.

## A working summary

Endurance athletes are healthy people who are doing physiologically demanding things. Most of them are not asking for medical clearance to do something dangerous; most of them are fine and will remain fine. The GP's role is proportionate to the actual risk  -  which means not over-medicalising the otherwise healthy 32-year-old entering their first marathon, and being appropriately alert with the 55-year-old with hypertension and a family history who is planning their first ironman.

The things most likely to cause serious harm: exertional cardiac events (especially in the over-35 group and in anyone with a cardiac history or red flag symptoms), exercise-associated hyponatraemia (especially in ultramarathon events, especially in slower participants who have been told to drink to a schedule), and RED-S in lean female athletes training at high volumes.

The common things that fill the consultation room: iron deficiency, EIB, overuse injuries, and the question of whether the knee is a stress fracture or tendinopathy. These are manageable and most resolve with appropriate treatment and load adjustment.

NSAIDs during events: advise against. Drink to thirst: say it explicitly. Menstrual irregularity in a training athlete: investigate, don't normalise. Syncope during exercise: refer before they run another step.
