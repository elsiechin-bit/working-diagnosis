New Zealand has an unusual relationship to cultural safety as a concept: we made it up. The term, the framework, and the underlying argument that healthcare encounters are not clinically neutral events but are shaped by power relations that can protect or harm patients - all of this originated in New Zealand nursing in the late 1980s, through the work of a Maori nurse educator named Irihapeti Ramsden.

That origin is worth holding onto as you read the international comparisons below, because it means that when Australia, Canada, and the UK adopt cultural safety frameworks, they are importing a New Zealand idea and adapting it to their own Indigenous and minority health contexts. The debates that have followed in those jurisdictions - about definition, enforceability, ideological loading, and the relationship between cultural safety and clinical autonomy - are, in some sense, responses to a question that New Zealand posed first.

This is an attempt at a fair account of what the MCNZ actually requires, what the evidence base looks like, where the genuine objections sit, and what has happened when other countries have tried to operationalise the same concept.

## Part I - Where it came from: Irihapeti Ramsden and the nursing schools

In the late 1980s, Irihapeti Ramsden was working as a nursing educator and activist, concerned that the New Zealand nursing curriculum was producing practitioners who were, in her framing, culturally unsafe for Maori patients - because of an unreflective Eurocentric clinical paradigm that treated its own assumptions as neutral and universal - harm that operated through professional culture rather than overt racism. [1]

Her argument had two parts. The first was empirical: Maori health outcomes were substantially worse than Pakeha outcomes across almost every metric, and some of this divergence was attributable to the quality of the clinical encounter, independent of genetics or behaviour. Maori patients were less likely to feel heard, more likely to disengage from care, and more likely to receive care that did not account for their values, beliefs, or social context. [2]

The second part was conceptual: Ramsden argued that the existing framework of "cultural competence" - in which practitioners learned facts about particular cultural groups in order to serve them better - was inadequate and potentially counterproductive. It positioned culture as a property of the patient (the "other") and the practitioner as the culturally neutral observer acquiring specialist knowledge. It ignored the power dynamics of the clinical encounter. It encouraged stereotyping by treating cultural groups as internally homogeneous. And it placed the burden of "being correctly known" on patients rather than the burden of "genuinely seeing" on practitioners.

Cultural safety, as Ramsden defined it, shifted the frame. It was not about what the practitioner learned about other cultures. It was about what the practitioner understood about their own cultural position, the power they held in the clinical encounter, and the effect of that power on the patient. And crucially, it was an outcome defined by the patient, not the practitioner: a clinician could not declare their practice culturally safe any more than a surgeon could declare their own operation successful without looking at what happened to the patient.

This framework was embedded in the New Zealand Nursing Council's competency requirements from the early 1990s onwards. [3] It was controversial in nursing from the beginning, and remained so - particularly the requirement for practitioners to engage in explicit self-examination of their own cultural positioning, which some interpreted as an ideological imposition.

## Part II - What the MCNZ actually requires

The Medical Council of New Zealand's 2019 statement on cultural safety is worth reading directly rather than through secondary accounts, because the gap between what it says and what critics often claim it says is significant. [4]

The statement defines cultural safety as "the effective nursing and medical practice of a person or family from another culture, and is determined by that person or family. Culture includes, but is not restricted to, age or generation; gender; sexual orientation; occupation and socioeconomic status; ethnic origin or migrant experience; religious or spiritual belief; and disability." The outcome is defined by the patient.

The MCNZ requires registered medical practitioners to: reflect on their own cultural identity and how it might influence their interactions with patients; be aware of and responsive to cultural factors that affect the patient's health and their engagement with the health system; recognise that the power imbalance in the clinical relationship can affect patient safety; and challenge practices that are culturally unsafe.

Importantly, the MCNZ frames cultural safety as integral to good clinical practice - the same framework that requires informed consent, patient-centred communication, and attention to social determinants of health. It is grounded in the evidence that healthcare encounters which ignore cultural context produce worse outcomes, and sits firmly within the clinical rather than the political domain.

What the MCNZ statement does not do is define a specific set of practices, attitudes, or identities as culturally safe or unsafe. It does not require practitioners to hold particular political views. It does not remove clinical judgment from the practitioner. These points matter for the regulatory controversy discussed below.

## Part III - The conceptual debate

The argument between cultural safety and cultural competence is not merely terminological. It reflects a genuine disagreement about what the problem is and how to solve it.

**Cultural competence** holds that practitioners can learn enough about culturally different patients to provide them with better care. You acquire knowledge: the significance of whanau in Maori decision-making, the role of spiritual belief in Pacific health models, the meaning of specific cultural practices around illness and death. You apply that knowledge in the clinical encounter. You are assessed on whether you have acquired and applied it. This is skills-based and measurable.

**Cultural safety** holds that this model is necessary but insufficient, because it does not address the structural power imbalance of the clinical encounter or the way in which the practitioner's own cultural assumptions shape what they see and how they respond. You cannot learn your way to cultural safety; you have to reflect your way there, continuously, recognising that your own cultural positioning is not neutral and that the patient's experience of the encounter is the measure of its safety.

The strongest version of the case for cultural safety is the health outcomes data. Maori life expectancy at birth is approximately seven years less than non-Maori. [5] Maori are more likely to present late with serious illness, more likely to be seen in emergency rather than primary care settings, more likely to disengage from long-term condition management, and more likely to experience adverse events in hospital. [5] A substantial body of research attributes a portion of these disparities to the quality of the clinical encounter: implicit bias in clinical decision-making, communication failures, and health system structures that were not designed with Maori patients in mind. [6]

The strongest version of the case against is the definitional problem. If cultural safety is defined as an outcome - the patient's sense of safety - rather than a set of behaviours, it is in principle unachievable as a professional standard. A practitioner who does everything that evidence and ethics requires may still have a patient who does not feel safe, for reasons that may or may not be attributable to the practitioner's conduct. Making "culturally unsafe" practice a ground for professional discipline requires a workable definition of what that practice consists of - and the existing framework, critics argue, does not provide one. [7]

A related concern is the conflation of cultural safety with particular political commitments. Some critics have argued that MCNZ and health professional education programmes have operationalised cultural safety in ways that treat specific theoretical frameworks (critical race theory, colonial analysis, particular understandings of Treaty obligations) as clinical requirements rather than as one lens among many. Whether this conflation is intrinsic to the cultural safety model or a feature of particular implementations is genuinely contested.

## Part IV - The evidence on implicit bias

The cultural safety framework rests substantially on the evidence for implicit bias in clinical practice, and that evidence is worth examining on its own terms.

A 2017 systematic review of implicit bias in healthcare professionals found consistent evidence of implicit bias related to race, ethnicity, gender, age, socioeconomic status, and weight, with most studies finding differences in treatment that correlated with measured implicit bias. [8] The effect sizes were modest in many studies, and causal attribution from IAT scores to clinical outcomes remains methodologically contested. But the overall picture - that practitioners systematically vary in their clinical decision-making in ways that track patient demographic characteristics and that are not fully explained by clinical need - is fairly robust.

In a NZ context, several studies have found race-based differences in clinical decision-making. Maori patients receive fewer investigations and referrals for equivalent presentations in some analyses, wait longer in emergency departments, and are prescribed pain relief at lower rates for equivalent pain scores. [9] Whether this reflects implicit bias, structural factors, communication failures, or some combination is not always separable in the data - but the pattern is consistent enough to take seriously as a clinical quality problem.

This evidence does not prove that cultural safety training as currently delivered reduces these disparities. That is a different and harder question. Several reviews have found that cultural competence and cultural safety training programmes have limited evidence of impact on patient outcomes, partly because the outcome measurement is difficult and partly because implementation is variable. [10] The honest position is: the problem is real, the solution is incompletely characterised, and the requirement to engage with it as a professional matter is justified even when the specific interventions remain under evaluation.

## Part V - Canada: the Joyce Echaquan case

In September 2020, Joyce Echaquan - a 37-year-old Atikamekw woman from the community of Manawan, Quebec - died at Centre hospitalier de Lanaudiere in Joliette. In the hours before her death, she used her phone to livestream her treatment on Facebook. The video showed hospital staff making racist comments about her, questioning whether she was there for drugs, and stating that she would have been better off dead. [11]

The video was watched by millions. Echaquan died of pulmonary oedema. A coroner's inquest found that racism had contributed to her death.

The case became a landmark in Canadian Indigenous health and medical ethics. Quebec initially refused to recognise systemic racism as a feature of its health system - a position the provincial government subsequently reversed. In response to the Echaquan case and the broader context of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission's calls to action, several Canadian provinces accelerated the integration of cultural safety requirements into health professional training and regulatory standards.

The specific calls to action most relevant to healthcare (numbers 18 through 24) require governments and health professional organisations to recognise the health disparities of Indigenous peoples, to establish accreditation requirements for cultural competency and cultural safety, and to increase Indigenous health practitioners in the system. [12] Progress on implementation has been uneven across provinces.

The Echaquan case is significant not because it represents the average clinical encounter with an Indigenous patient - it does not - but because it makes visible what implicit bias and institutional racism can look like when they are allowed to operate without constraint. The case for cultural safety requirements is, in part, a case against the conditions that produced that room.

## Part VI - Australia: AHPRA and the ongoing argument

The Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency has been moving toward explicit cultural safety requirements across the health professions it regulates. The Medical Board of Australia updated its Good Medical Practice framework, and cultural safety for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander patients is now framed as a professional standard rather than a training recommendation. [13]

The Australian debate has followed a similar contour to the NZ debate, with some additional intensity because of the specific political context of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander rights in Australia. Defenders of the cultural safety framework point to the consistent and severe health disparities between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Australians - life expectancy gaps, higher rates of almost every major chronic disease, and worse cancer outcomes - and argue that these cannot be addressed without acknowledging the role of the clinical encounter.

Critics have included some medical practitioners who argue the AHPRA framework imposes ideological commitments disguised as clinical standards, and that the definition remains too vague to support professional discipline. A 2022 submission to an inquiry by the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare noted the methodological gaps in evidence linking specific cultural safety interventions to patient outcome improvements, while supporting the principle of cultural safety as a professional value. [14]

One specific Australian controversy has been around the requirement for practitioners to complete mandatory cultural safety training, and whether such training is effective or counterproductive when delivered as a compliance exercise. There is evidence from multiple training contexts that mandatory diversity and cultural training of short duration can entrench resistance rather than produce genuine reflection - an argument for more substantive, longitudinal integration rather than a module-based approach. [10]

## Part VII - United Kingdom: a different frame

The UK has not adopted the cultural safety framework in the form developed in New Zealand and Australia. The General Medical Council's Good Medical Practice includes requirements to treat patients as individuals, respect diversity, and be aware of how personal beliefs and values can affect care. [15] This is closer to the older cultural competence model than to cultural safety.

The UK has had its own significant failures of culturally sensitive care - the Morecambe Bay investigation found that the cultural and professional backgrounds of staff contributed to failures in the maternity unit; the Ockenden Report on Shrewsbury and Telford found cultural factors in clinical decision-making. But these have generally been framed as communication failures or professional culture failures rather than specifically as cultural safety failures in the Ramsden sense.

The absence of a cultural safety framework in the UK does not mean the underlying problems are absent. Black women in the UK are approximately four times more likely to die in childbirth than white women. [16] South Asian patients have systematically worse outcomes in cardiac care. These disparities have been documented, named, and to some extent addressed through specific clinical guidelines - but the regulatory framework that would require individual practitioners to reflect on their own cultural positioning as a professional standard has not been adopted.

Whether this represents a more pragmatic approach (focusing on measurable clinical guidelines rather than aspirational reflective requirements) or a missed opportunity (failing to address the implicit bias substrate that clinical guidelines cannot reach) is a genuine question.

## Part VIII - United States: cultural humility and the competence debate

In the United States, the term "cultural humility" has largely displaced "cultural competence" in academic and policy discourse, though both remain in use. [17] Cultural humility - developed by Tervalon and Murray-Garcia in 1998 - is in many ways closer to the Ramsden cultural safety framework than to competence: it emphasises ongoing self-reflection over acquired knowledge, recognises power imbalances in the clinical relationship, and defines the goal as a process rather than an endpoint.

American medical schools have required some form of cultural competence or cultural humility education for accreditation purposes since the early 2000s. The LCME standards include requirements for medical education to address the social determinants of health and the needs of diverse patient populations. Several states have enacted mandatory cultural competence CME requirements for licence renewal.

The US debate has particular dimensions not present in the NZ context: the intersection of cultural competence requirements with debates about diversity, equity, and inclusion programmes in education and employment; legal challenges to mandatory training requirements in some states; and an ongoing argument about whether framing health disparities primarily through a cultural lens is accurate or whether structural and economic factors deserve greater emphasis.

## Part IX - The regulatory question: can "culturally unsafe" practice sustain a disciplinary finding?

This is the hardest question, and it does not have a clean answer in any jurisdiction.

For a professional standard to support disciplinary action, it must be sufficiently defined that a practitioner can know in advance what conduct is prohibited, a disciplinary body can assess whether that conduct occurred, and an appeal process can review the finding. Vague standards fail on all three counts.

The MCNZ cultural safety standard, as currently framed, defines the outcome (the patient's sense of safety) without clearly specifying the conduct that constitutes a breach. This creates theoretical exposure to complaints that are not clearly grounded in specific clinical failures. In practice, cultural safety has not been a standalone basis for significant disciplinary action in NZ - the cases that have involved cultural factors have typically also involved more conventional clinical failings (failure to examine, communication failures, failure to refer) that are independently assessable.

The more defensible regulatory approach - and arguably the direction in which the framework should develop - is to identify specific, evidence-based practices that constitute culturally unsafe care: failing to use an interpreter when one is needed; dismissing a patient's statement of pain without adequate clinical reason; failing to involve family in the clinical discussion when the patient has indicated this is their preference; treating culturally specific explanatory models of illness as obstacles rather than clinical information. These are specific, assessable, and grounded in evidence that they affect outcomes.

This is, in effect, asking cultural safety to cash itself out in the currency of clinical behaviour rather than reflective attitude - which critics might welcome and defenders might resist as a reduction of the framework to a checklist. The tension is genuine.

## Part X - Where this leaves NZ clinicians

The practical answer for a NZ practitioner is more straightforward than the theoretical debate suggests.

The MCNZ requires you to reflect on your own cultural positioning, to be responsive to the cultural context of your patients, and to challenge practices that are culturally unsafe. None of this requires you to adopt particular political views. It does require you to take Maori and Pacific health disparities seriously as clinical problems rather than epidemiological background noise, to ask how your own assumptions might affect your clinical decisions, and to create conditions in which patients of different cultural backgrounds can engage with you as their GP rather than performing compliance.

The evidence that this matters for patient outcomes is solid enough to justify the requirement even where specific interventions are incompletely characterised. The critics who argue that the framework is ideologically loaded are raising real concerns about implementation - in medical schools, in training programmes, in the culture around the requirement - that are worth engaging rather than dismissing. The critics who argue that health disparities would be better addressed by structural changes than by individual practitioner reflection are not wrong; they are emphasising a necessary and complementary argument, not a sufficient alternative.

Cultural safety began as a New Zealand idea, developed by a Maori nurse who was trying to explain why healthcare was not working for her people. The question it posed - whose norms govern the clinical encounter, and what happens to patients when those norms are invisible to the practitioner - is still open. The arguments about how to operationalise it in regulatory frameworks have followed it around the world and have not yet settled.

They probably will not settle cleanly. The discomfort is, in some sense, the point.
