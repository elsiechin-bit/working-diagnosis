<p>I see more post-bariatric patients in general practice than I used to. Some come from the public programme; more are coming back from private surgery, occasionally offshore. They arrive with a discharge summary, a list of supplements, and an assumption that I know what to do next. Usually I do  -  but the honest truth is that most of us weren't taught this in training, the specialist handover is often thin, and the evidence that primary care follow-up actually happens is not encouraging.<sup>1</sup></p>
<p>This note is my attempt to consolidate what I actually need to know at the coalface. It's not a comprehensive management guide  -  it's a working GP's crib sheet, written in the order that problems tend to present.</p>

<h2>First: know what procedure they had</h2>
<p>The most common procedures in New Zealand are sleeve gastrectomy (SG) and Roux-en-Y gastric bypass (RYGB), with SG now predominating in both public and private sectors.<sup>2</sup> This distinction matters clinically. RYGB has a malabsorptive component  -  it bypasses the duodenum and proximal jejunum  -  which means a significantly higher and broader risk of micronutrient deficiency than sleeve alone.<sup>3</sup> Adjustable gastric banding is now rare but not extinct; its complications (port infection, band erosion, slippage) are specific and require surgical referral.</p>
<p>If the discharge summary doesn't specify the procedure clearly, ask the patient. They almost always know.</p>

<h2>The medication review: do it immediately</h2>
<p>This is the most time-sensitive task and the most commonly missed. Blood pressure and blood glucose can fall dramatically in the first days to weeks after surgery, well ahead of significant weight loss  -  particularly after RYGB, where the metabolic effect on glucose appears partly independent of caloric restriction.<sup>4</sup></p>
<p>For patients on insulin or sulfonylureas, the risk of hypoglycaemia post-operatively is real and potentially dangerous. Both should be reduced or stopped on discharge; the bariatric unit should have done this, but always check the discharge summary carefully and monitor glucose more frequently in the early weeks.<sup>5</sup> SGLT2 inhibitors are generally not continued after bariatric surgery and should not be restarted without specific indication (e.g. heart failure or CKD), given altered anatomy and ketoacidosis risk.<sup>5</sup></p>
<p>Antihypertensives should be reviewed as weight falls; around a third to half of patients will achieve hypertension remission in the longer term.<sup>6</sup> Watch for symptomatic hypotension early.</p>
<p>Two more things that often get missed: NSAIDs are contraindicated post-bariatric surgery due to gastric mucosal risk  -  flag this in the notes and make it visible to anyone prescribing. And modified-release or enteric-coated formulations may have unpredictable absorption after RYGB; if a patient is on something critical (anticonvulsants, warfarin, ciclosporin), review formulation with the pharmacist.<sup>7</sup> Most patients are discharged on a PPI for at least six months  -  continue this.<sup>5</sup></p>

<h2>Nutritional monitoring: this is lifelong</h2>
<p>Guidelines recommend annual blood tests indefinitely after bariatric surgery.<sup>8</sup> In practice, most patients don't get them.<sup>1</sup> A UK cohort study found that only a minority of post-bariatric patients received the recommended annual nutritional review in general practice  -  and that's a problem, because deficiencies accumulate slowly and silently before becoming clinically apparent.<sup>1</sup></p>
<p>The minimum annual panel after sleeve gastrectomy should include: FBC, ferritin, transferrin saturation, vitamin B12, folate, 25-OH vitamin D, calcium, PTH, and albumin. After RYGB, add vitamin A, copper/ceruloplasmin, zinc, and vitamin E.<sup>9</sup></p>
<p>Iron deficiency is the most common deficiency overall, particularly in premenopausal women. Vitamin B12 deficiency is common and underappreciated  -  importantly, if a patient is supplementing folate without B12, the haematological signs of B12 deficiency can be masked while neurological damage progresses.<sup>3</sup> Always check both. Vitamin D insufficiency is prevalent pre-operatively in people with obesity and tends to persist; supplementation should be standard and monitored.<sup>10</sup></p>
<p>Long-term data are sobering: one Italian cohort found that eight years post-surgery, over 80% of patients had at least one nutritional deficiency despite good adherence to prescribed supplements.<sup>11</sup> Supplements reduce deficiency rates but don't eliminate them.</p>

<h2>Weight trajectory and weight regain</h2>
<p>Most patients achieve their nadir weight at 12-18 months. After that, some degree of weight regain is normal and should be anticipated, not treated as failure. The LABS study, following patients for seven years post-RYGB, found that mean regain from nadir was around 10% of initial body weight  -  with substantial individual variation.<sup>12</sup></p>
<p>When a patient presents concerned about weight regain, the first job is to distinguish expected biology from modifiable behaviour from surgical complications (e.g. gastric-gastric fistula after RYGB, or pouch dilation). Reinforce that the goal was never a number on a scale  -  it was metabolic health and functional gain, most of which is retained even with partial weight regain.</p>
<p>GLP-1 receptor agonists are increasingly being used in post-bariatric weight regain, and the evidence base is developing  -  but prescribing them post-bariatric surgery is a specialist conversation, not a primary care initiation, at least for now.<sup>13</sup></p>

<h2>Mental health is not a post-op afterthought</h2>
<p>Pre-operative psychological assessment is part of the bariatric pathway for a reason. But psychological difficulties don't resolve with surgery  -  and some emerge or worsen post-operatively. Depression, anxiety, disordered eating patterns, and alcohol use disorder all deserve monitoring. Alcohol in particular warrants a word: after RYGB, alcohol absorption is faster and the pharmacokinetic curve is steeper, meaning patients can reach significantly higher peak blood alcohol concentrations from the same intake as before surgery. This is not widely known by patients and is worth raising proactively at a routine review.<sup>14</sup></p>
<p>Ask about social support. Consider whether the patient has access to a bariatric support group  -  the evidence that peer support improves adherence and wellbeing post-operatively is reasonable, and the BOMSS framework specifically recommends it.<sup>5</sup></p>

<h2>Pregnancy planning</h2>
<p>Pregnancy after bariatric surgery requires specific consideration and should be planned deliberately. Current guidance recommends avoiding pregnancy for at least 12-18 months post-surgery, during the period of rapid weight loss and nutritional flux. Women who do become pregnant need urgent referral to consultant-led obstetric care, and their multivitamin should be reviewed to exclude vitamin A in retinol form (teratogenic in excess).<sup>5</sup> Folic acid 5 mg daily pre-conceptually is usually recommended, consistent with BPAC guidance for high-risk pregnancies.</p>

<h2>The equity problem we shouldn't ignore</h2>
<p>Access to publicly funded bariatric surgery in Aotearoa is inequitable. Māori and Pacific patients  -  who carry a disproportionate burden of obesity-related disease  -  are significantly less likely to access public bariatric surgery and more likely to drop out of programmes before reaching the operating table.<sup>15,16</sup> Referral rates from general practice vary enormously, and a Counties Manukau study found a 350-fold variation in referral rates per 1000 people across practices in the same district.<sup>17</sup></p>
<p>For GPs, this means two things. First: don't assume your patients who would benefit are already in the system. A thoughtful, non-stigmatising conversation about bariatric surgery with a patient who meets clinical criteria  -  BMI ≥ 40, or ≥ 35 with significant comorbidity, with documented failure of conservative management  -  is a legitimate and important part of our job. Second: be an active advocate for patients who hit the access barriers that data show fall disproportionately on Māori and Pacific wāhine.</p>

<h2>A note on what I keep at hand</h2>
<p>The BOMSS (British Obesity and Metabolic Surgery Society) guidance for GPs is the most practically useful clinical reference I've found; it's free online and updated regularly. It covers medications, nutritional monitoring, complications, and pregnancy in a format that works as a consultation reference. Worth bookmarking.</p>
