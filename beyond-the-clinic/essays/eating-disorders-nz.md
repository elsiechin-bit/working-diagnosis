There is a version of the eating disorder patient that lives in our cultural imagination. She is a teenage girl. She is white. She is thin - visibly, worryingly thin. She comes from a family with enough disposable income to have food anxiety as a presenting problem. She has probably been overexposed to fashion magazines or, more recently, to Instagram. She is, in some tellings, simply taking dieting too far.

This image is not entirely wrong. It describes a real person who really does develop eating disorders. But it is partial enough to be dangerous, because when we mistake the archetype for the population, we systematically fail to recognise eating disorders in anyone who does not fit the template. The male university student restricting to 800 calories. The Pacific woman with bulimia nervosa who presents for something else entirely. The middle-aged man with binge eating disorder who has been told, repeatedly, that his relationship with food is a willpower problem. The non-binary teenager with ARFID who has been written off as a fussy eater.

The gap between who we imagine has eating disorders and who actually has them is one of the more consequential blind spots in New Zealand primary care.

## Part I - The scale of the problem

Eating disorders are not rare. Globally, estimates suggest that around 9% of people will experience an eating disorder at some point in their lives - a figure that, if applied to New Zealand's population, implies several hundred thousand affected individuals at any given time. [1]

The mortality data is stark. Anorexia nervosa has one of the highest standardised mortality ratios of any psychiatric condition - a 2011 meta-analysis of 36 studies found an SMR of approximately 5.86, meaning someone with anorexia nervosa is nearly six times more likely to die than an age-matched member of the general population. [2] The causes of death include medical complications of starvation and suicide, in roughly equal measure. This is not a lifestyle problem. It is a serious psychiatric and medical illness with a death rate that would be considered alarming in any other disease category.

And yet, in New Zealand as elsewhere, eating disorders have historically attracted a fraction of the funding, research attention, and specialist resource that conditions with similar or lower mortality receive. The reasons are multiple and not entirely neutral: the conditions affect predominantly (though not exclusively) women; they were for a long time understood through a moralistic lens as matters of vanity or control; and the archetype that dominates public imagination - the thin white girl - is not a group that tends to be centred in health policy discussions.

## Part II — What "eating disorder" actually means in 2025

The *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (fifth edition), published in 2013, made several changes to the eating disorders category that have not yet fully filtered through to clinical practice or public understanding. [3]

**Binge eating disorder** (BED) was elevated from appendix to full diagnosis. It is characterised by recurrent episodes of binge eating without compensatory behaviours - no purging, no excessive exercise, no restriction between binges. BED is actually the most prevalent eating disorder in most populations, significantly more common than anorexia or bulimia. [1] It affects men and women at roughly equal rates. It is strongly associated with obesity - but this does not make it a synonym for poor self-control; it is a disorder with distinct neurobiological and psychological characteristics that responds to specific evidence-based treatment and does not respond to generic weight management advice. Conflating BED with "overeating" is a clinical error.

**ARFID** (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder) replaced "feeding disorder of infancy and early childhood" and significantly broadened the category. ARFID describes restriction or avoidance of food intake based on sensory characteristics, fear of aversive consequences (choking, vomiting), or lack of interest in food - without the weight and shape preoccupation that characterises anorexia nervosa. It is not picky eating as a personality trait. It is a distinct disorder with significant functional impairment, and it occurs across the lifespan, not only in children.

**OSFED** (Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder) replaced "eating disorder not otherwise specified" and is clinically important because it describes the most common presentation - disordered eating that causes significant distress and functional impairment but does not meet full criteria for one of the other diagnoses. Atypical anorexia nervosa - where someone meets all the criteria for anorexia but is at a normal or above-normal body weight - sits here, and is critically important for reasons discussed below.

## Part III — The weight problem: why you cannot diagnose by looking

Anorexia nervosa, in its classic presentation, includes low body weight as a diagnostic criterion. This has created a deeply damaging clinical heuristic: the idea that an eating disorder is something you can see.

You cannot. The majority of people with clinically significant eating disorders are not underweight. People with bulimia nervosa are typically at a normal weight. People with BED are often overweight or obese. People with atypical anorexia nervosa - in which the psychological and behavioural features of anorexia are fully present but the person has lost weight from a higher starting point - may be at any weight, and may have lost weight at a rate and through means that are medically dangerous, while appearing, to the untrained eye, healthy.

The research on this is consistent and uncomfortable: clinicians are significantly less likely to perceive eating disorder behaviour as pathological, less likely to ask about it, and less likely to refer for treatment in patients who are not visibly thin. [4] A thin patient who is losing weight is alarming. A patient at a higher body weight who is restricting to 600 calories and exercising two hours a day may be receiving compliments from their GP.

This is not a minor issue. It means that the patients most likely to be recognised and referred are those who already fit the cultural image of an eating disorder. It means that patients who do not fit the image - including, disproportionately, patients from non-Pakeha backgrounds - receive delayed diagnosis and delayed treatment, with consequences that are sometimes fatal.

## Part IV — Maori, Pacific, and the recognition gap

Eating disorders have long been characterised, in clinical and cultural discourse, as diseases of white affluence. The evidence does not support this characterisation.

Internationally, large population studies have consistently found eating disorder prevalence across ethnic and socioeconomic groups, with differences in the types of disorders represented and the ways in which distress is expressed rather than in the overall prevalence. [5] The idea that eating disorders are "white diseases" is not epidemiology. It is a product of who gets studied, who gets referred, and whose presentation is taken seriously.

In the New Zealand context, Maori and Pacific peoples are underrepresented in eating disorder treatment populations relative to their overall population share. This is almost certainly not because they have lower rates of eating disorders; it is because the recognition and referral pathway that leads to treatment is filtered through a clinical gaze that was calibrated on a different patient. [6]

Pacific body image norms are frequently cited as a protective factor - the idea being that Pacific cultures valorise larger bodies and therefore buffer their members against the thinness imperative that drives anorexia and bulimia. This observation has a grain of truth and a large halo of oversimplification. Pacific communities are not monolithic. Second-generation New Zealand-born Pacific individuals navigate both Pacific cultural norms and dominant Pakeha thin-ideal messaging simultaneously, and research suggests this dual exposure can increase risk rather than reduce it. [6] Assuming protection where there may be conflict is a form of clinical negligence dressed up as cultural competence.

The practical implication for any New Zealand GP: if you would ask about food restriction, weight preoccupation, and binge-purge behaviours in a thin white teenage patient, you should ask the same questions in Maori and Pacific patients, in male patients, in patients at higher body weights, and in older adults. The default to "this patient doesn't look like they have an eating disorder" is not clinical judgment. It is pattern-matching against a template.

## Part V — Social media: what the evidence actually says

The role of social media in eating disorders is a topic that attracts confident claims in both directions, and deserves more care than it usually gets.

The strongest evidence is from the company itself. Internal research conducted by Facebook (and subsequently leaked by whistleblower Frances Haugen in 2021) found that Instagram knew its platform made body image concerns worse for a significant proportion of teenage girls, and that those concerns were causally related to the platform's recommendation algorithms pushing appearance-related content. [7] The company characterised this as a known risk and continued to operate the algorithms regardless. This falls short of epidemiology, but it is testimony from the entity with the most complete data, and it should be taken seriously.

The broader epidemiological evidence is more complicated. Ecological data showing that eating disorder presentations have increased over the same period that social media use has increased is consistent with a causal relationship but does not establish one. Longitudinal studies examining social media use and eating disorder development have found associations, but associations of modest size, with evidence of bidirectionality - people with existing eating disorder cognitions may selectively engage with appearance-related content, rather than the content causing the cognition. [8]

What social media does appear to do with more consistency is maintain eating disorders once established, by providing communities, comparisons, and content that reinforce disordered cognitions. The "pro-ana" and "pro-mia" community is not new - it predates social media by a decade, existing first in web forums and then on Tumblr - but it has found a more efficient distribution mechanism in algorithmic recommendation systems that learn quickly what a user engages with.

The flip side is that social media is also a source of recovery community, destigmatisation, and access to clinical information for people who would not otherwise encounter it. Eating disorder peer support communities online are not uniformly harmful. The same platform that can serve appearance-comparison content can serve recovery narratives, anti-diet perspectives, and information about treatment pathways.

The honest position is: social media is neither the cause of eating disorders nor irrelevant to their development and maintenance. For an individual patient, the specific content they are engaging with matters more than the platform they are using. A nuanced conversation about their social media environment is more useful than a generic screen time reduction recommendation.

## Part VI — Diet culture, the wellness industry, and orthorexia

New Zealand has a substantial wellness industry that markets food restriction, cleansing, and "clean eating" as health practices. This does not cause eating disorders in people who have no predisposition to them. But it does create an environment in which the early cognitive features of restrictive eating disorders - rigid categorisation of foods as "clean" or "toxic," moral valence attached to food choices, distress at eating "badly" - are normalised, celebrated, and commercially amplified.

Orthorexia nervosa - a preoccupation with the quality and purity of food that causes significant distress and functional impairment - is not yet a formal DSM diagnosis, but it is a clinically recognisable pattern that several research groups are actively working to characterise. [9] It is more common in populations where health consciousness is already high, including some professional groups. A patient who is spending significant time planning meals, refuses to eat food they have not prepared themselves, is distressed when their routines are disrupted, and has restricted their social life because of food-related anxiety may have something that warrants clinical attention, even if their diet by macro-nutrient analysis is excellent.

The wellness framing presents a particular challenge because it provides moral scaffolding for restriction. Patients who frame their restriction as "eating healthy" or "clean eating" are harder to engage with the eating disorder frame than patients who recognise that their relationship with food is problematic. The GP who does not push on this may accept the framing at face value and miss the underlying disorder.

## Part VII — Men and eating disorders

Approximately one in three to one in four people with an eating disorder is male, depending on the disorder. For BED, the sex ratio approaches 1:1. For muscle dysmorphia - a disorder characterised by obsessive preoccupation with muscularity and fear of being too small, often accompanied by extreme dietary restriction and supplementation, and formally classified as a body dysmorphic disorder - male predominance is overwhelming. [10]

Men with eating disorders are less likely to be identified by clinicians, less likely to be referred to specialist services, and less likely to seek help themselves. They are also more likely to have their disordered behaviours framed as positive: a young man who is highly controlled about his food intake and exercises intensively may receive admiration rather than concern. Muscle dysmorphia in particular sits in a cultural space where extreme physique modification is normalised, celebrated in gyms and on social media, and commercially profitable.

When men do present, they are likely to have been unwell for longer, and they may present to GPs with concerns about their training, their supplements, or related physical issues - the eating disorder presenting through a side door. Asking about dietary intake, restriction, and food-related anxiety in male patients presenting with body composition concerns, overtraining symptoms, or fatigue is not overreach. It is appropriate screening.

## Part VIII — LGBTQ+ populations

People who identify as gay, lesbian, bisexual, transgender, or non-binary have elevated rates of disordered eating and clinical eating disorders, across most studies. [11]

The mechanisms are multiple. Gay and bisexual men are exposed to appearance-related pressures within their own communities, and body image concerns are prevalent in this group at rates substantially higher than in heterosexual men - though still underrecognised in primary care. Trans and non-binary individuals may use food restriction or other weight-altering behaviours as a form of body modification, attempting to change a body they experience as wrong in the absence of or as an adjunct to gender-affirming care. The distress driving the behaviour in this group is not about thinness or weight as such; it is about gender dysphoria. Treatment that does not engage with this will not work.

For New Zealand GPs providing care to LGBTQ+ patients, eating and weight are worth asking about with the same directness applied to other health behaviours. The question "how is your relationship with food and eating?" is not intrusive. It opens a door.

## Part IX — The New Zealand system

Specialist eating disorder services in New Zealand are geographically concentrated and under-resourced relative to the burden of disease. Major centres have dedicated services; rural New Zealand largely does not. [12] The consequences for patients outside the main cities are a familiar pattern in New Zealand health: delayed access, greater reliance on the GP to hold clinical complexity that would benefit from specialist input, and outcomes that diverge from those achievable with adequate service.

The Eating Disorders Association of New Zealand (EDANZ) provides peer support, family support, and resource navigation. It is a non-clinical service, and for patients and families trying to find their way in a confusing and under-signposted system, it is often the first useful point of contact. Knowing that it exists and being able to direct patients to it is a practical skill.

For GPs, the evidence-based interventions that are feasible within a primary care context include: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy-Enhanced (CBT-E) delivered by a sufficiently trained psychologist in the community; family-based treatment (Maudsley approach) for adolescents; and pharmacological support where indicated - specifically fluoxetine for bulimia nervosa, where there is reasonable evidence, and (with considerably more caution) pharmacotherapy for BED. For anorexia nervosa with medical compromise, the role of primary care is primarily to monitor physical parameters, coordinate referral, and maintain the therapeutic relationship that keeps the patient engaged with the health system at all.

The medical monitoring of severe anorexia nervosa - electrolyte checks, ECG monitoring, refeeding risk assessment - is something NZ GPs do carry, sometimes without adequate specialist backup. The MARSIPAN guidelines (Management of Really Sick Patients with Anorexia Nervosa) provide a useful framework for this. [13] They are not the most cheerful reading. They are necessary.

## Part X — What better looks like

A handful of things would meaningfully improve eating disorder outcomes in New Zealand.

**Screening that is not filtered by body size or demographic.** Brief validated screening tools like the SCOFF questionnaire (five yes/no questions) take under a minute and can be applied in any population. [14] They should be used in patients who present with concerns about body weight, fatigue, gastrointestinal symptoms, menstrual irregularity, or elevated anxiety about food - regardless of what those patients look like.

**Clinical training that disrupts the archetype.** Medical and nursing training that presents eating disorders only through the lens of the thin white female patient produces clinicians who will miss most of the patients they actually encounter. This is a curriculum problem with a straightforward remedy.

**Honest conversations about the wellness industry.** The normalisation of dietary restriction as virtue is a health literacy problem that GPs are well-placed to name. Patients with food anxiety do not need to hear that their restriction is admirable. They need someone to ask what eating is like for them, and to take the answer seriously.

**Resources matched to the burden of disease.** This is a system problem - and primary care advocacy matters precisely because of that. Eating disorders have the mortality profile of a serious physical illness. The resourcing should reflect that.

There is a version of a New Zealand GP who sees a Maori woman in her thirties, at a normal body weight, who mentions in passing that she tends to eat and eat when she is stressed and then hates herself for it - and who hears that as a lifestyle note rather than a screening cue for binge eating disorder. That GP is not negligent. They have been trained in an image, and the image is incomplete.

The image is worth examining.
