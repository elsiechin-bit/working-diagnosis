Before the mid-twentieth century, general practice had no formal consultation theory. The structure of the encounter was largely determined by professional convention: the doctor took a history, conducted an examination where indicated, and delivered a diagnosis or prescription. The patient's role was to answer questions accurately. In the United Kingdom, consultation times in the 1950s and early 1960s averaged around five to six minutes in general practice settings, and the content was predominantly biomedical.¹

The first systematic effort to study the consultation as a phenomenon came from psychoanalytic rather than medical research. Michael Balint, a Hungarian-British psychoanalyst, conducted seminars with London GPs throughout the 1950s in which actual cases were discussed in depth. His 1957 monograph *The Doctor, His Patient and the Illness* argued that the GP-patient relationship itself had therapeutic properties that were being consistently underused, and that the drug most frequently prescribed in general practice was the doctor.² Balint introduced concepts of the "overall diagnosis" (encompassing psychological and social dimensions) and the "apostolic function" of doctors (the tendency to impose their own values on patients), and established the Balint group as a method of reflective practice that remains in use internationally.

## Structural models: 1970s to 1990s

The Stott-Davis framework, published in the *Journal of the Royal College of General Practitioners* in 1979, moved from the relationship to the structure of what a consultation could contain.³ Stott and Davis identified four tasks: management of the presenting problem, modification of help-seeking behaviour, management of continuing problems, and opportunistic health promotion. The model was significant because it formalised the idea that a single consultation routinely operates on multiple simultaneous agendas, not simply the presenting complaint.

In 1984, Pendleton and colleagues published *The Consultation: An Approach to Learning and Teaching*, which identified seven tasks oriented towards understanding and shared management, and proposed a structured feedback method for teaching consultation skills that began with the learner's own observations before moving to those of the observer.⁴ This feedback model became influential in vocational training.

Roger Neighbour's *The Inner Consultation*, published in 1987, introduced five checkpoints framed as cognitive and interpersonal tests: Connecting (has the patient been heard?), Summarising (can the doctor demonstrate understanding?), Handover (has a mutually agreed plan been reached?), Safety-netting (has the plan accounted for uncertainty?), and Housekeeping (is the doctor in adequate psychological condition to proceed to the next patient?).⁵ The model was notable for foregrounding the doctor's internal state as a clinical variable.

The Calgary-Cambridge Guide, developed by Silverman, Kurtz, and Draper and first published in 1996, became the most widely adopted framework in Anglophone medical education.⁶ It maps the consultation across five sequential phases (initiating the session, gathering information, physical examination, explanation and planning, closing the session) with a continuous parallel process of relationship-building and structuring running throughout. Calgary-Cambridge was developed with explicit reference to communication research, including studies showing that doctors interrupted patients' opening statements after a mean of eighteen seconds, with patients rarely returning to their initial concern once interrupted.⁷

## Diagnostic frameworks

Alongside communication models, a separate tradition focused on clinical reasoning rather than the interpersonal structure of the encounter. John Murtagh, an Australian GP and academic, proposed a five-question diagnostic model directing the clinician to consider: the most likely diagnosis; conditions that must not be missed; conditions that are commonly missed or masquerade as other presentations; whether the patient might have a psychosocial explanation for their presentation; and whether the presentation might signal a specific pattern of illness.⁸ This model was designed for the undifferentiated presentation typical of primary care rather than the specialist referral context.

## Consultation length: historical and international data

A large cross-national study published in *BMJ Open* in 2017 examined mean consultation lengths across sixty-seven countries.⁹ The range was considerable: Sweden averaged 22.5 minutes, the United States 21.1 minutes, and the United Kingdom 9.2 minutes. Bangladesh averaged 0.6 minutes. New Zealand averaged approximately 15 minutes in general practice, though this figure varies with funding model and practice type. The study found that higher consultation length was associated with higher GDP, lower patient-to-physician ratios, and higher-income health systems, but that the relationship between consultation length and quality of care was not straightforward.

In Japan, the outpatient consultation model in hospital-based general medicine has historically been high-volume and brief, with consultations of two to four minutes common in urban settings.¹⁰ This reflects both a structural feature of the Japanese health system, in which patients may self-refer to specialist outpatients without a GP gatekeeper, and a cultural orientation toward technical expertise rather than narrative disclosure as the primary mode of the medical encounter. Japan nonetheless maintains health outcomes comparable to or exceeding those of longer-consultation systems across multiple indicators.¹⁰

In France, general practitioners have historically operated with a more directive consultation style than their Dutch or UK counterparts, with a stronger emphasis on prescription as the expected output of the encounter. A comparative study of European consultation styles found that French GPs were significantly more likely to issue prescriptions and less likely to engage in shared decision-making compared with GPs in the Netherlands and the UK.¹¹ The Netherlands has a distinct tradition of watchful waiting (*afwachten*) and explicit uncertainty-sharing with patients, embedded in both training and professional culture.

In many sub-Saharan African contexts, the biomedical consultation model exists alongside and in tension with traditional healing frameworks in which illness is understood in relational and communal terms. Explanatory models may attribute causation to social disruption, spiritual factors, or interpersonal conflict, and the distinction between somatic and psychological illness is differently drawn.¹² The WHO has noted the coexistence of formal and informal health systems across the African region and the clinical importance of understanding both.¹³

In Indigenous Australian and Maori health contexts, research has documented systematic barriers to the standard Western consultation model, including differing norms around direct questioning, eye contact, disclosure of personal health information to strangers, and the role of family in clinical decision-making.¹⁴ Consultation adaptations developed within these communities include the use of community health workers, extended family presence in the consultation, and narrative rather than question-and-answer formats for history-taking.

## Shared decision-making

The movement toward shared decision-making formalised in the 1990s and 2000s represents a significant departure from both the paternalistic model and the informational model (in which the clinician presents options and the patient chooses). Shared decision-making, as defined by Charles and colleagues in 1997, requires that both parties share information, both participate in the decision-making process, and both agree on the treatment decision.¹⁵ Its implementation has been operationalised through patient decision aids, of which there are now over five hundred evaluated examples catalogued by the Ottawa Patient Decision Aids Research Group.¹⁶ Uptake in routine general practice remains variable.
