Geriatrics

Falls & fracture prevention.

The leading cause of injury hospitalisation in New Zealanders over 65 — and one of the most preventable.

Falls are not random bad luck. They are the predictable outcome of accumulated risk factors acting on a physiologically vulnerable person in an imperfectly adapted environment. In New Zealand, falls are the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in adults over 65, with direct health system costs exceeding $500 million annually.1 Hip fracture carries a one-year mortality of approximately 20–30% in older adults — comparable to many common cancers — yet the urgency this warrants is rarely reflected in how it is managed in primary care.2

Risk factor identification is the starting point. A falls history — specifically, any fall in the past year — is the single best predictor of future falls and should be a routine question in annual reviews for patients over 65.3 Additional risk factors include: gait and balance impairment, lower-limb weakness, cognitive impairment, visual impairment, orthostatic hypotension, high-risk medications (benzodiazepines, sedating antihistamines, anticholinergics, antihypertensives), environmental hazards, and footwear.

The Otago Exercise Programme is the best-evidenced falls prevention intervention available in NZ primary care and is directly ACC-funded. It consists of a personalised home exercise programme of progressive strength and balance exercises, delivered by a physiotherapist over four visits and then maintained independently. Cochrane evidence supports a 35% reduction in fall rate and a 32% reduction in falls-related injuries in community-dwelling older adults.4 It is appropriate for patients who have fallen or are at high risk — including those using walking aids, provided they can follow instructions. The referral is straightforward; ACC funds the initial assessment and follow-up visits.

Medication review is the other high-yield intervention. Psychotropic medications — benzodiazepines, sedating antidepressants, antipsychotics, and Z-drugs — approximately double the fall risk and should trigger an explicit deprescribing conversation.5 Antihypertensives in the context of orthostatic hypotension are a common and underappreciated contributor. Measure lying and standing blood pressure. A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic on standing is clinically significant; it is present in up to 30% of community-dwelling older adults and is often correctable with medication adjustment.

Vision assessment should not be overlooked. Cataract — highly prevalent and highly treatable — meaningfully increases falls risk. Home hazard assessment by an occupational therapist is warranted for patients who have had multiple falls or who are frail; ACC also funds this in certain circumstances. Footwear advice (thin, hard-soled, low-heeled shoes with a closed toe) is evidence-based and costs nothing to deliver.

Fracture risk should be quantified for all patients who have had a falls-related fracture and for any patient with a CFS ≥5. FRAX (with BMD input where available) and the GARVAN fracture risk calculator are both validated for use in New Zealand populations; GARVAN incorporates falls history, which is a meaningful advantage. Osteoporosis remains significantly undertreated in New Zealand following minimal trauma fracture — see the osteoporosis page for further detail.
