Sunlight & vitamin D.

New Zealand's latitude cuts both ways — enough UV for synthesis in summer, not enough in winter south of Auckland. Most people don't know which applies to them.

Vitamin D deficiency (25-hydroxyvitamin D below 50 nmol/L) is common in New Zealand despite the country's reputation for sunshine. New Zealand's latitude ranges from approximately 34°S (Auckland) to 46°S (Invercargill), and UV levels at southern latitudes — particularly between May and August — are insufficient for meaningful cutaneous vitamin D synthesis even with outdoor exposure.1 Year-round deficiency is especially prevalent in people who are housebound or institutionalised, those with darker skin pigmentation (who require longer UV exposure for equivalent synthesis), those who cover their skin for cultural or religious reasons, and those working predominantly indoors. Māori and Pacific peoples have higher rates of deficiency than is commonly assumed, partly because of the melanin-synthesis relationship and partly due to dietary factors.

Testing is appropriate — but not indiscriminate. Routine 25-OH vitamin D testing in asymptomatic low-risk individuals is not recommended and is not funded under the community schedule in New Zealand without clinical justification. Funded indications include: documented risk factors for deficiency (institutionalisation, malabsorption, limited sun exposure, pigmented skin in at-risk climates), symptoms consistent with deficiency (fatigue, bone pain, proximal myopathy), prior fragility fracture or osteoporosis management, and monitoring of established supplementation. Requesting 25-OH vitamin D on every elderly or fatigued patient generates cost without proportionate clinical benefit.

The target serum 25-OH vitamin D level for bone health is ≥50 nmol/L; levels below 25 nmol/L represent severe deficiency. For musculoskeletal and immune function, some guidelines suggest a target of ≥75 nmol/L in high-risk groups, though the evidence for benefits beyond bone health at these higher levels is less robust.2 Supplementation with cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) is the standard approach; ergocalciferol (vitamin D2) is less well absorbed. A dose of 1,000–2,000 IU daily is appropriate for maintenance in deficient adults; loading doses (e.g., 50,000 IU weekly for six to eight weeks) may be used for severe deficiency before switching to maintenance dosing.

The competing risk is melanoma. New Zealand has one of the highest rates of melanoma in the world — a consequence of latitude, ozone depletion history, outdoor culture, and fair-skinned European ancestry in a high-UV environment.3 The public health message is not "maximise sun exposure" but rather "balance: enough UV for synthesis, adequate photoprotection to reduce cancer risk." In practice, this means approximately ten to fifteen minutes of midday sun exposure to the arms and legs on most summer days for fair-skinned adults, with sun protection after that — not prolonged unprotected exposure. In winter in the South Island, meaningful synthesis is unlikely regardless of exposure duration.

Dietary sources of vitamin D are limited: oily fish (salmon, tuna, sardines), egg yolks, and fortified foods (some milks and cereals) contribute, but dietary intake alone is rarely sufficient to maintain adequate levels in deficient individuals. For patients who are housebound, have limited sun access, or live at southern latitudes, supplementation is more practical than attempting to meet requirements through diet and sun alone. Vitamin D supplementation is inexpensive, safe at recommended doses, and appropriate as a long-term recommendation in these groups.

Vitamin D toxicity (hypercalcaemia) from supplementation is rare but documented with very high doses (typically >10,000 IU/day for prolonged periods). Routine monitoring of 25-OH vitamin D levels during supplementation is not necessary at standard doses unless there is a clinical reason to suspect toxicity or malabsorption. The exception is patients with granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis) or lymphoma, who have dysregulated vitamin D metabolism and are at risk of hypercalcaemia even at normal supplementation doses.
