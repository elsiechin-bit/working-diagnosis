Melanoma & skin cancer

New Zealand and Australia share the world's highest melanoma incidence. Early detection in primary care saves lives — and the ABCDE rule is only the starting point.

Melanoma accounts for approximately 4,000 new diagnoses per year in New Zealand, with incidence highest in fair-skinned New Zealanders of European descent and in those over 50.1 Non-melanoma skin cancer (basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma) is far more common but rarely reported in cancer registries, making true incidence estimates difficult.

The ABCDE rule

A useful screen for concerning pigmented lesions — but not a substitute for dermoscopy or clinical gestalt in high-risk patients.

  • A — Asymmetry. One half doesn't mirror the other.
  • B — Border. Irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges.
  • C — Colour. Variation — shades of brown, black, red, white, or blue within the same lesion.
  • D — Diameter. Larger than 6 mm (roughly the diameter of a pencil eraser), though early melanomas can be smaller.
  • E — Evolving. Any change in size, shape, colour, or new symptom (bleeding, itching) over weeks to months.

The "ugly duckling" sign — a lesion that simply looks different from a patient's other naevi — has comparable sensitivity to ABCDE and is worth keeping in mind for patients with many moles.2

When to refer

Urgent (within 2 weeks) referral to dermatology or plastic surgery is appropriate for any lesion you cannot confidently exclude as melanoma. If in doubt, excise with a 2 mm margin for histology rather than watch and wait.

What to tell the patient

Annual full-skin checks for anyone with a personal or first-degree family history of melanoma. Broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen, UV-protective clothing, and avoiding tanning — not just burn avoidance.
