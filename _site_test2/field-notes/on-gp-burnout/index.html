On GP burnout

The NZ numbers, the moral-injury reframing, and why the wellness-industry response is mostly the wrong response.

2 May 2026

The 2024 RNZCGP Workforce Survey, released in July 2025, found that 38% of NZ GPs rated themselves as highly burnt out (level 7–10 on an eleven-point scale), and 70% rated themselves as moderately-to-highly burnt out (level 4–10) [1]. The 38% figure is down from 48% in 2022 — itself a high-water mark — but is substantially higher than the 22% measured in 2016 and the 31% measured in 2020 [1,2]. Whatever has happened in NZ general practice over the last decade has been measurable, and most of it has not been good.

This piece is about that. What "burnout" is, what it isn't, what the NZ data actually show, why the wellness-industry response is mostly the wrong response, and what the evidence suggests does help. Some of this is uncomfortable to write under one's own name in one's own profession. Some of the discomfort is part of the point.

Part I — The numbers

The standard reference for NZ data is the RNZCGP Workforce Survey, conducted biennially since the early 2010s and reweighted to a biennial cycle from 2020. The 2024 survey, released July 2025, was completed by 1,374 College members (24% response rate) [1]. The headline numbers are these.

Self-rated burnout among GPs has tracked: 22% reporting high burnout in 2016, 31% in 2020, 48% in 2022, 38% in 2024 [1,2]. The 2024 dip is real but should be read in context: it follows a pandemic-era peak and remains nearly double the 2016 figure. Including moderate burnout, 70% of GPs in 2024 rated themselves at 4 or above on the eleven-point scale [1]. Female GPs have consistently reported higher rates than male GPs across the survey years [2].

The other workforce indicators are no more reassuring. The median age of NZ GPs is 51, almost unchanged across surveys; 17% are at or past retirement age (65 or older), and 6% are over 70 [1]. The pipeline does not match the outflow: NZ has approximately 74 GPs per 100,000 population in 2021, projected to fall to 70 per 100,000 by 2031, and the College projects a shortfall of approximately 300 GPs against population need over the next decade [1]. Among GPs aged 40–44 — the cohort most likely to determine the shape of the workforce in 2035 — 17% are considering leaving NZ, and 6% intend to do so within five years [1].

A career-recommendation question gives a more direct read on professional morale. Asked whether they would recommend a career in general practice, 54% of respondents said yes in 2020, 39% in 2022, and 43% in 2024 [1]. The 2024 number is up modestly from the 2022 trough but remains well below pre-pandemic levels.

These numbers describe a workforce that has been under sustained pressure, that has aged faster than it has replaced itself, and that — by its own self-assessment — is in distress at a rate that no other profession in NZ would consider remotely acceptable.

Part II — What "burnout" is, and what it isn't

The Maslach framework, which has dominated burnout research since the 1980s, defines occupational burnout as a constellation of three features: emotional exhaustion, depersonalisation (or cynicism), and a reduced sense of personal accomplishment [3]. The Maslach Burnout Inventory remains the most-used measurement instrument internationally. The framework is empirically useful — the three features cluster reliably, they correlate with adverse outcomes for clinicians and patients, and interventions can be tested against them.

It is also, on its own, the wrong frame for most of what NZ GPs are actually experiencing.

The most influential reframing in the recent physician-distress literature is Simon Talbot and Wendy Dean's 2018 STAT article "Physicians aren't 'burning out.' They're suffering from moral injury" [4], elaborated in their 2019 Federal Practitioner piece "Reframing Clinician Distress: Moral Injury Not Burnout" [5]. The argument is conceptual but consequential.

"Burnout" is a description of a constellation of symptoms in the individual. It implies a problem of individual capacity — that the clinician has run out of resources for managing the demands of the work. It directs interventions at the individual: build resilience, manage stress, practise mindfulness, take breaks, see a counsellor. None of these is wrong; they are also not, on the evidence, sufficient.

"Moral injury", by contrast, names a different phenomenon: the experience of repeatedly being unable to deliver the care one knows the patient needs, because of constraints beyond one's control [4,5]. The 14-minute slot for a consultation that needs forty minutes. The medication that would help, that isn't funded. The community-based mental health support that doesn't exist. The hospital admission that prevents a death you would have prevented yourself if you'd had time. The patient who has lost trust in the system before they walked into your room. The slow accumulation of compromises with what the work should be.

Moral injury is borrowed terminology from combat psychology, where it describes the lasting psychological damage of being asked to do, or fail to do, things that violate one's moral framework. The translation to medicine is not perfect — most of what GPs experience is not the same as what a soldier experiences — but the diagnostic shift is useful. It moves the locus of the problem from the individual to the system. It reframes the appropriate response from individual fortification to structural change.

The RNZCGP's own 2020/21 burnout survey, conducted after the 2020 Workforce Survey results came out, identified three themes through the analysis of 1,495 responses and 809 verbatim comments: increased patient need and complexity, structure of the funding formula, and administrative burden [6]. None of these is an individual-resilience problem.

This is not an argument that the Maslach framework is wrong. It is an argument that it captures the symptoms but misnames the cause for most of what NZ GPs are currently experiencing.

Part III — The drivers, in NZ specifically

Several drivers compound, and each of them has structural rather than individual roots.

Patient complexity has risen faster than capacity. Multimorbidity, mental health complexity, social complexity, and presenting demand have all increased over the last decade, while consultation length has not. The capitation funding formula (until the 2025 reweighting, which included multimorbidity, rurality, and deprivation as factors for the first time since 2002) has not adequately captured the time required for higher-complexity patients [7]. The practices serving the highest-needs populations — the practices where the work is hardest — have been systematically underfunded relative to the time their patients require. The gap between "the consultation we can fund" and "the consultation the patient actually needs" lives somewhere in every working day, and it is not closed by individual resilience.

Administrative burden has risen substantially. The combination of electronic health records, prescribing review systems, audit and accreditation requirements, ACC documentation, secondary-care discharge management, repeat prescription processing, and the slow accretion of compliance tasks over a decade has produced a documentation load that would not have been recognisable to a GP twenty years ago. International data suggest that for every hour of patient contact, primary care physicians now spend roughly an additional one to two hours on documentation and administration [8]. Most of this work is unpaid relative to capitation funding; much of it happens outside scheduled hours.

The secondary-care interface has degraded. Long waits for elective referrals, tightening criteria for specialist services, pressure on emergency departments, and the chronic difficulty of accessing community-based mental health services all push clinical work that would have been managed in secondary care back into general practice. The GP becomes the holder of the patient who cannot get the care they need elsewhere — which is, in moral-injury terms, exactly the position that produces the most damage.

Workforce shortage compounds everything. Each GP who retires, leaves the country, or moves to part-time without replacement increases the load on those remaining. The 17% of mid-career GPs considering leaving NZ is not just a statistic; it's a feedback loop. Workforce loss raises pressure on those left, which raises burnout, which raises further workforce loss.

Isolation is structural. General practice is, by its nature, the work of one clinician with one patient in one room. Unlike hospital medicine, there is little institutional structure for processing difficult clinical encounters together — no team handovers, no debrief structures, no peer review built into the day. The administrative isolation of small practices compounds the clinical isolation of the consultation. Both contribute.

None of this is an individual problem. All of it is the system that GPs are working within.

Part IV — What the evidence shows about interventions

The evidence base on burnout interventions is now reasonably mature.

The most-cited synthesis is West, Dyrbye, and colleagues' 2016 Lancet meta-analysis of 52 controlled studies of physician burnout interventions [9]. The headline finding: both individual-focused and organisational-focused interventions can reduce burnout, but organisational-focused interventions produce roughly twice the effect size of individual-focused ones [9]. Subsequent reviews have generally reproduced the direction of this finding.

Individual interventions that have some evidence include mindfulness-based stress reduction, cognitive behavioural approaches, structured peer support groups, and reflective practice [9]. Effect sizes are modest. None of these is harmful, and they may help individual clinicians manage their experience of the work. None is structurally sufficient for the level of distress the workforce data describe.

Organisational interventions with evidence include reductions in workload (the most direct lever), changes to schedule structure, reductions in EHR/documentation burden, leadership development for clinical managers, and team-based reorganisation of clinical work [9,10]. The Stanford and Mayo Clinic literature on physician well-being is the most-cited source; both organisations have invested heavily in organisational-level interventions and report measurable reductions in burnout indicators.

The wellness industry — the meditation apps, the resilience workshops, the "Code Lavender" rapid-response wellness teams that emerged in some US health systems — has been criticised for both empirical and ethical reasons. Empirically, the effect sizes are small and don't address structural drivers. Ethically, the framing implies that the appropriate response to a structurally damaged work environment is to better fortify the workers, which is the same logic that would suggest the appropriate response to lead in the water is to teach individual children to drink more carefully.

This isn't an argument against personal practices that help individual clinicians get through the day. It is an argument against the implicit message that those practices, alone, are the answer.

Part V — What helps, in practice

A few things I think hold up under scrutiny.

Naming the problem accurately. If most of what you're experiencing is moral injury rather than burnout, calling it that — to yourself, to your colleagues, to the people who manage health systems — changes what conversations are possible. It prevents the mistake of assuming the problem is in you when the problem is largely in the conditions of the work.

Colleague relationships. The single intervention with the most consistent evidence across the literature, alongside workload reduction, is structured peer connection — protected time to discuss difficult cases, reflect on hard consultations, and process accumulated clinical experience with people who understand it [9,10]. This is harder to do in solo or small-practice settings than it is in larger group practices, and it is one of the structural advantages of practice consolidation that the literature supports.

Boundaries that hold. The evidence on individual interventions is mixed, but the things that consistently help include: not checking work email outside work hours, having genuinely protected lunch breaks, leaving on time when possible, not taking patient files home. None of these is heroic. All of them are harder to do than they sound.

Reducing the unpaid load. Time spent on admin outside work hours is the single most modifiable individual contributor to burnout in the recent literature [10]. Where the practice can fund clerical or nurse-led support to reduce documentation load, that change has more measurable impact than any wellness intervention. Where it cannot, identifying the documentation tasks that absolutely must be done by the GP versus those that could be delegated is a meaningful exercise.

Knowing when to seek help. GP rates of mental ill-health, suicidal ideation, and completed suicide are higher than population baseline, and significantly higher in some studies for female physicians than for matched-age peers in other professions. The reasons are well-rehearsed: high workload, easy access to information about means, professional reluctance to seek help, concerns about MCNZ implications of disclosure. The Medical Council of New Zealand and the various physician health programmes — including the Doctors' Health Advisory Service — exist for this reason. They are confidential, they are colleague-staffed, and they are the right call long before the situation feels desperate enough to warrant it. The single most evidence-supported predictor of seeking help in time is having decided in advance that you would.

Structural advocacy. The work of arguing for capitation reweighting, for funded clerical support, for reduced compliance burden, for sustainable workforce policy, is not separate from the work of managing burnout. It is the work. Every individual GP cannot fix the system, but the College, the GP Leaders' Forum, and the various sector advocacy bodies do meaningful work that depends on members staying engaged enough to support it.

A closing note

There is a version of this piece that could be written as advocacy — angry, clear, demanding change. There is another version that could be written as comfort — supportive, validating, focused on individual coping. The version I've tried to write is neither of those, because I think both leave most of the readership somewhere they haven't already been.

The version I've tried to write is descriptive. The numbers are what they are. The drivers are what they are. The evidence on interventions is what it is. The reframing from burnout to moral injury isn't a magic word; it is a more accurate description of what most of us are dealing with most of the time. That accuracy is the thing that makes structural change possible to argue for, and individual coping more sustainable when the structural change is slow.

If you are reading this and recognising yourself in the high-burnout 38%, or in the 17% considering leaving — the recognition is itself the start of the work. Talk to a colleague. Talk to the Doctors' Health Advisory Service. Talk to a GP you trust who isn't in your practice. Most of us have been somewhere along this distribution at some point. None of us are alone in it, even when the consultation room makes it feel that way.

That, in the end, is the best that this kind of writing can do.
