## Where the policy is

The current backdrop matters, because most patients have at least a vague sense of it.

In December 2022, NZ passed what was widely described as world-leading tobacco control legislation. The Smokefree Environments and Regulated Products Amendment Act (SERPA) implemented three measures recommended by the 2021 Smokefree Aotearoa 2025 Action Plan: denicotinisation of tobacco, a drastic reduction in tobacco retailers (from approximately 6,000 to 600), and a "smokefree generation" provision making it illegal to ever sell tobacco to anyone born after 1 January 2009 [1,2]. The package was the most ambitious tobacco control framework anywhere in the world at the time.

In early 2024 the coalition government repealed all three measures under urgency [3]. The reasons given were principally fiscal  -  Finance Minister Willis cited an approximately $1 billion impact on the government's books from tobacco excise  -  alongside concerns about illicit trade and consumer autonomy [3]. The repeal was widely condemned. Over 100 NZ organisations signed an open letter opposing it, including ASPIRE Aotearoa, Cancer Society NZ, Hāpai Te Hauora, and the Royal NZ College of General Practitioners; 139 organisations from 53 countries signed a separate international letter [4].

The government's stated position is that it remains committed to the Smokefree 2025 goal  -  defined as fewer than 5% of New Zealanders smoking daily  -  but pursued through a different package: tighter youth vaping rules, a ban on disposable vapes, increased penalties for sale to minors, and a "Getting to Smokefree 2025" plan released by Health NZ in November 2024 [5]. Public health academics have been openly sceptical that this approach can hit the 5% target. Professor Chris Bullen, Director of the National Institute for Health Innovation, said publicly in May 2025 that the goal was unlikely to be met, with approximately 80,000 people needing to quit before year-end [6]. The PHCC commentary on the new plan concluded that the Smokefree 2025 goal will "very likely not be realised" under the current approach [7].

This is the environment in which the smoking consult now happens. Every GP needs to have a working position on it, because patients increasingly do.

## The numbers

The longer-term trend is good. The NZ Health Survey shows daily smoking has fallen from 16.4% in 2011/12  -  when the survey began tracking it  -  to 8.6% in 2023/24 and 6.8% in 2024/25 [5,8]. That is one of the steepest declines of any high-income country.

In absolute numbers, this leaves approximately 300,000 NZ adults still smoking daily as of mid-2025 [6]. The 5% goal would mean roughly 220,000 daily smokers  -  a gap of about 80,000.

The age and ethnic distribution matters more than the headline figure for clinical work. Smoking is now disproportionately a habit of older NZers, of Māori and Pacific peoples, and of people in more deprived neighbourhoods [5,8]. Daily smoking rates for Māori adults remain roughly 2.5–3 times higher than for non-Māori, and Pacific rates are similarly elevated; the inequities the Smokefree 2025 framework was specifically designed to address are still very much present [5].

Age-wise, the picture has changed quickly. Smoking among adolescents and young adults has fallen sharply over the past decade, partly displaced by vaping. The ASH Year 10 Snapshot survey of approximately 30,000 14–15 year olds shows daily smoking now at very low single digits in this age group, while regular vaping has become substantially more common  -  although the most recent survey rounds suggest youth vaping itself may have peaked [9]. The clinical consequence is that the demographic of "the active smoker" has shifted older. Increasingly, the patient in front of you who smokes is in their fifties, sixties, or seventies, and has been smoking for thirty or forty years.

## What active smokers tend to believe

This is the part of the consultation that the published guidelines say least about, and that matters most.

The international evidence on older smokers' beliefs is reasonably consistent. The 2005 ITC analysis across the UK, US, Canada, and Australia identified a cluster of attitudes characteristic of long-term smokers aged 60 and over: lower perceived vulnerability to harm ("having smoked this long and survived, I must be one of the resistant ones"); lower perceived benefit of quitting ("the damage is done"); lower self-efficacy for quitting ("I've tried, I know I can't"); and a general sense that whatever harm was coming has already arrived [10]. Subsequent qualitative work in older smoker populations has reinforced these themes [11,12]. In the NZ context, similar belief patterns appear in clinical consultation, though I'm not aware of NZ-specific quantitative data of comparable scale.

Several of these beliefs are factually wrong in clinically important ways, and the misconceptions are worth naming explicitly.

**"The damage is done."** It isn't. Quitting at 60 still adds years of life expectancy compared with continued smoking, and quitting at 70 still adds quality-adjusted years. Cardiovascular risk begins to fall within weeks of stopping. The decline in lung function slows, even when baseline function is already substantially reduced. The benefits are smaller in absolute terms than for a 30-year-old quitter, but they are real, measurable, and worth talking about explicitly.

**"I've tried, I can't."** This is probably the single most common reason long-term smokers give for not making another attempt. It is also empirically wrong as a predictor  -  older smokers who do attempt to quit are, if anything, more likely to succeed than younger ones, partly because they tend to be more committed when they do attempt [11]. The history of failed attempts is not predictive in the way patients (and sometimes clinicians) treat it.

**"I'm not the kind of person who gets cancer."** Some version of this exemption belief is held by a substantial proportion of long-term smokers [10]. Often it is articulated through family history ("my dad smoked till 90"), through type of smoking ("I only smoke roll-your-owns"), through perceived health ("I've always been fit"). The belief reduces cognitive dissonance but doesn't reduce risk.

**"I'd rather not know."** Pessimistic and avoidant beliefs about cancer are more common among current smokers than non-smokers, including beliefs that cancer is not preventable and not survivable [13]. This affects help-seeking behaviour  -  including delayed presentation of symptoms  -  and is its own clinical issue independent of smoking itself.

**The dementia link is particularly under-used.** Smoking is a substantial modifiable risk factor for dementia, and qualitative work suggests this connection is highly motivational for older smokers when they hear about it  -  but most older smokers are unaware of it [14]. Most smoking conversations in primary care still default to lung cancer and cardiovascular disease, both of which long-term smokers have heard about for decades and have already cognitively processed in ways that minimise personal applicability. The dementia link is news to many of them, and lands differently.

## The relapse data

This is the part of the conversation where evidence-based pessimism actually helps.

Most quit attempts fail. The classic estimate, derived from cross-sectional surveys of successful quitters, was that smokers required on average 5–7 attempts before stopping for good [15]. That estimate substantially understated the difficulty, because it sampled only people who had already succeeded.

The more rigorous 2016 Chaiton estimate, using longitudinal data from 1,277 smokers in the Ontario Tobacco Survey, suggested the actual average number of quit attempts before successful cessation is closer to 30, with successful cessation defined as one year or more of continuous abstinence [15]. The Chaiton paper's central methodological move was to include in the denominator people who had not yet succeeded  -  a population the older estimates excluded by design. The 30-attempt figure is not a magic threshold, and individuals vary enormously, but it is the most defensible single-number summary currently available.

The clinical implication is twofold.

For the patient: relapse is the rule, not the exception. A patient who has tried and failed eight times has not failed at quitting; they are, statistically, somewhere in the middle of the normal trajectory toward eventual success. Reframing each failed attempt as a step in a longer process  -  rather than evidence of personal weakness  -  is, I think, one of the more important things the consult can do.

For the clinician: the appropriate response to a recent relapse is not "let's give it more time before trying again". It is the opposite. The evidence supports prompt re-engagement, with as much pharmacological and behavioural support as the patient is willing to accept. Each attempt slightly raises the probability of eventual success [15].

## What to actually do in clinic

The NZ ABC model  -  Ask, Brief advice, Cessation support  -  remains the standard framework, updated in the 2021 NZ Smoking Cessation Guidelines [16]. The evidence base for each step is solid. Three things worth saying about how it actually plays out.

**Ask.** Smoking status should be documented at every relevant consultation. The 2021 guidelines are explicit that brief advice should be offered to everyone who smokes, regardless of expressed readiness to quit [16]. The "are you ready?" question that used to gate cessation conversations is not supported by the evidence  -  readiness shifts, and the offer of support itself shifts it.

**Brief advice.** Thirty seconds, evidence-based, non-judgemental. "The single most important thing you can do for your health is to stop smoking, and we have effective help available." Variations on this script have been studied repeatedly and produce measurable population-level quit rates [16]. The script does not require that the patient have indicated interest first.

**Cessation support.** Combination NRT (patch plus a faster-acting form  -  gum, lozenge, or inhaler) is the first-line pharmacological intervention and is fully funded in NZ [17]. Bupropion and nortriptyline are also fully funded and have similar efficacy to single-formulation NRT [17]. Varenicline is the most effective single agent, with efficacy comparable to combination NRT, and is funded with Special Authority approval  -  though supply has been a problem; varenicline was unavailable in NZ for several years and was reportedly returning to stock from late March 2025 [17]. Verify current availability before prescribing.

Behavioural support meaningfully increases the success rate of pharmacotherapy alone. Quitline (0800 778 778) is free, evidence-based, and provides ongoing telephone and text support. The 2021 NZ guidelines compare long-term abstinence rates: combination behavioural plus pharmacological support roughly doubles unassisted quit rates [16].

**Vaping for cessation** is the most contested piece. The current government framing is that vaping is the principal route to Smokefree 2025; the 2021 NZ guidelines and the Cochrane evidence base support nicotine vaping as a cessation tool with efficacy comparable to or modestly better than NRT in adult smokers [16,18]. Public health bodies including ASPIRE Aotearoa and the Asthma and Respiratory Foundation NZ are concurrently concerned that the vaping-as-cessation framing is shifting nicotine dependence from one delivery vehicle to another, and that youth vaping uptake represents a new generation of nicotine addiction [4,19]. Both things can be true. The defensible clinical position is to use nicotine vaping as a cessation tool when other approaches have failed in patients clearly motivated to quit, with an explicit goal of subsequently stopping vaping rather than treating it as an indefinite substitute. This is what the 2021 guidelines recommend [16].

## A closing thought

The Smokefree 2025 goal almost certainly will not be hit. The legislative framework that would have hit it was repealed. The replacement framework relies heavily on individual cessation effort supported by primary care.

Which means the work moves into the consult. Each conversation with each long-term smoker  -  particularly the older long-term smokers who now make up a growing proportion of the smoking population  -  is doing the work that policy was supposed to be doing in parallel. The conversation has to handle their understandable fatalism, their accumulated failed attempts, their well-formed self-exempting beliefs, and their (correct) sense that the system has not been making it easy for them. It also has to convey, gently and accurately, that quitting at 65 still substantially extends life and improves its quality, that the next attempt is more likely to succeed than not on a long enough horizon, and that there is genuinely effective help available.

That is the work, in primary care, in 2026.
