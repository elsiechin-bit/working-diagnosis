Science, in the popular imagination, is a dignified enterprise conducted by focused people in white coats who are working on cancer, or climate change, or the genome. What this picture misses is the significant portion of the scientific literature occupied by the question of whether penguins defecate at dangerous pressure, whether retroactive prayer can cure bloodstream infections in patients who were treated ten years ago, and whether roller coasters have any utility in the management of kidney stones.

This literature exists. It is peer-reviewed. Some of it has changed clinical practice. Most of it has changed nothing except the researcher's reputation at dinner parties, which is arguably a contribution to human wellbeing in its own right.

There is a serious argument, which I will make briefly before getting to the penguins, for why this kind of research matters. Science advances partly by testing its own methods — by asking whether the tools and the frameworks produce sensible results when applied to questions where the answer is already known or obviously absurd. The funniest medical studies are often also the sharpest critiques of medical science. And occasionally, underneath the absurd question, there is a genuine finding that turns out to be useful.

But mostly this piece is about the penguins.

## The BMJ Christmas issue, a tradition of distinction

Every December, the *British Medical Journal* publishes its Christmas issue. The Christmas issue is the BMJ's annual exercise in rigorously conducted, properly peer-reviewed research on questions that the rest of the year's literature does not see fit to investigate. The studies are real. The methods are often impeccable. The questions are extraordinary.

The canonical example, still referenced in evidence-based medicine teaching worldwide, is Smith and Pell's 2003 systematic review of randomised controlled trials of parachute use [1]. The authors, noting that "the effectiveness of parachutes has not been subjected to rigorous evaluation by using randomised controlled trials," searched MEDLINE, Web of Science, EMBASE, and the Cochrane Library for trials, found none, and concluded that the evidence base for parachute use in preventing death and major trauma from gravitational challenge was therefore, technically, absent.

The paper's final paragraph is one of the finest in the modern medical literature: "We therefore advocate that, as with many interventions intended to prevent ill health, the most rigorous and objective analysis of the benefits of parachutes is awaited, and that those who advocate evidence based medicine and believe that it should always be applied should prepare themselves to lead by example."

The study has been cited, genuinely and without irony, in methodological papers about the limitations of randomised controlled trial design for interventions where randomisation is impractical. It has been cited in philosophy of science papers. It has been cited by people who, one suspects, have not read past the title. It is a flawless piece of work.

Witcombe and Meyer, also in the BMJ, reported in 2006 on the first systematic investigation of the occupational health of sword swallowers [2]. The study was conducted by surveying forty-six members of the Sword Swallowers' Association International (which exists, and has a newsletter). Key findings: sword swallowers have a higher rate of sore throats than the general population, particularly "when distracted or when new props are being introduced." Perforations and lacerations are documented hazards. The most common complications occur during performances involving multiple simultaneous swords, or when the performer is distracted by members of the audience. Witcombe and Meyer received the Ig Nobel Prize in Medicine for this work in 2007, which they accepted in person at the Harvard ceremony.

The Ig Nobel Prizes — awarded annually at Harvard to research that "first makes you laugh, then makes you think" — have become, over their three-decade history, one of the better guides to genuine scientific weirdness. The winners are real researchers, the ceremonies are real ceremonies, and the ten-second acceptance speech limit is enforced by a small child who begins chanting "please stop, I'm bored" at the eleven-second mark. The prize itself is a certificate and no money, which has not deterred recipients including several Nobel laureates.

## Studies that changed something

**The retroactive prayer trial.** Leonard Leibovici is a clinical epidemiologist and professor of medicine. In 2001, he published in the *BMJ* the results of a randomised controlled trial of remote, retroactive intercessory prayer for patients with bloodstream infection [3]. The trial had 3,393 patients. The intervention was prayer offered for the patients' recovery by a group of people, after the patients had already been treated. The patients had been treated between 1990 and 1996; the prayer was offered in 2000.

The "treatment" group had significantly shorter length of stay in hospital (mean 11 days vs 13 days, p=0.01) and lower mortality (28.1% vs 30.2%). The confidence intervals were narrow. The p-values were valid. The randomisation was adequate.

Leibovici later explained that the study was "a satirical exposition of the absurdity of trials that invoke supernatural elements or that apply statistical tests in inappropriate circumstances." Others read it as a genuine trial that produced a genuine result in need of explanation. The debate about what, exactly, the study demonstrates — about prayer, about statistics, about the time-independence of randomisation, about the sociology of peer review — has continued for twenty-five years. It remains the most efficiently thought-provoking three pages in the history of evidence-based medicine.

**Roller coasters and kidney stones.** David Wartinger is a urologist at Michigan State University. In 2016, he published, with Marc Mitchell, the results of a study prompted by multiple patients who reported passing kidney stones following rides on Big Thunder Mountain Railroad at Walt Disney World [4]. Wartinger and Mitchell constructed a model of a ureter containing kidney stones of various sizes, obtained access to the ride, and rode it 60 times with the model positioned in various orientations. Stones in the lower third of the ureter passed at a rate of 100%. Stones in the upper third passed at a rate of 64% when riders sat at the front of the train, and 17% at the rear — a significant difference.

The clinical implication, which Wartinger stated directly, was that urologists should consider prescribing specific roller coaster rides for patients with small kidney stones that are close to spontaneous passage. The study won the Ig Nobel Prize in Medicine in 2018. One imagines the conversation at the urology outpatient clinic: "I'm going to prescribe three to four circuits of Big Thunder Mountain, front car only, and we'll reassess."

**Nasal packing with cured pork.** In 2012, in the *Annals of Otology, Rhinology and Laryngology*, Pringle and colleagues reported using strips of salt-cured pork as nasal packing for a patient with Glanzmann thrombasthenia and life-threatening epistaxis unresponsive to standard treatments [5]. The rationale was that the pork tissue contained local clotting factors and that the salt created a hostile environment for further bleeding. It worked. The authors noted, with the understatement of people who know exactly what they have done, that "further study is warranted."

The clinical context matters: Glanzmann thrombasthenia is a rare platelet disorder in which standard haemostatic approaches frequently fail. The pork packing was not the first-line intervention; it was the one that worked when nothing else did. Whether this constitutes a finding, a case report, or an exceedingly niche contribution to the haematology literature is a question for the subspecialists. That it constitutes remarkable sentence structure in any clinical notes is beyond dispute.

**Duct tape versus cryotherapy for warts.** Focht and colleagues published a randomised controlled trial in the *Archives of Pediatrics & Adolescent Medicine* in 2002 comparing duct tape occlusion therapy with liquid nitrogen cryotherapy for the treatment of common warts [6]. Duct tape won: 85% resolution versus 60% for cryotherapy over two months. The mechanism was presumed to involve local immune stimulation through tape-induced irritation, though this was not definitively established. The study has been both replicated and contested in subsequent literature, and the meta-analytic picture remains unsettled. This has not prevented an unknowable number of GPs from trying duct tape on warts since 2002, which is arguably a positive outcome regardless of the final effect estimate.

## Studies that changed nothing but definitely happened

**The penguin defecation pressures.** Meyer-Rochow and Gal published in *Polar Biology* in 2003 their calculations of the pressures produced by Adélie and emperor penguins during defecation [7]. The biological purpose of the study was to understand how penguins avoid soiling their plumage (and those of their neighbours) while incubating eggs and unable to stand. Using models of the penguin's anatomy and the observed range of defecation distances, the authors calculated that penguins generate rectal pressures of approximately 10–60 kilopascals — four times the human equivalent. The paper includes physics equations, cloaca anatomy, and the sentence "we deduced that the resultant projectile will travel a distance of approximately 40 cm." The authors won the Ig Nobel Prize in Fluid Dynamics in 2005, and accepted.

**Country music and suicide.** Stack and Gundlach published a study in the sociology journal *Social Forces* in 1992 examining the relationship between country music airplay in US metropolitan areas and suicide rates [8]. The hypothesis was that country music themes — romantic failure, economic hardship, alcohol, trucks — might reinforce suicidal ideation in vulnerable listeners. Controlling for other factors, they found a significant positive association. The study generated a minor literature, including at least one challenge study that failed to replicate, and one letter arguing that the whole thing was confounded by regional cultural factors. It occupies an enviable position in the canon of findings that are methodologically interesting and substantively unfalsifiable.

**Shoe size and penile length.** Siminoski and Bain, in 1993, published a study attempting to determine whether shoe size correlates with penile length [9]. It does not. The study was conducted with appropriate methodology, appropriate measurements, and appropriate statistical analysis, and produced the finding that "there was no significant correlation" — which is itself a finding, in that it puts to rest a piece of folk wisdom that had presumably been circulating for as long as shoe sizes had been standardised. The authors noted that the question "is frequently raised by curious patients and their partners" and that having a definitive answer has practical clinical utility. This is almost certainly true.

**How long to dunk a biscuit.** Len Fisher, a physicist at the University of Bristol, solved the problem of optimal biscuit dunking in 1998 by applying the Washburn equation — a piece of physics used to describe capillary action in porous materials — to model how quickly liquid penetrates a biscuit's molecular structure, and how long before the biscuit loses structural integrity and falls into the tea [10]. The optimal dunking time varies by biscuit type (digestives differ from ginger nuts differ from Rich Teas), depends on the temperature of the liquid, and can be calculated precisely. Fisher won the Ig Nobel Prize in Physics in 1999 and has continued to apply physics to domestic problems including the perfect chip and the ideal breakfast cereal sogginess. The Washburn equation has genuinely useful applications in pharmaceutical tablet dissolution, oil recovery, and textile dyeing. It is also now the equation people use to calculate biscuit dunking.

## What the weird literature tells us

These studies are funny. They are also, read carefully, illuminating.

**They test the method.** The parachute review is funny because it is obviously right that parachutes work, but it is also genuinely right that the RCT is not the appropriate method for evaluating every intervention. The study is a proof by reductio of a real methodological point. Similarly, Leibovici's retroactive prayer trial is genuinely difficult to dismiss on purely methodological grounds — the randomisation was valid, the statistics were correct, the result was real. The only available objection is "the hypothesis is obviously false," which is an epistemic position rather than a statistical one. The study makes you confront what evidence-based medicine is actually doing.

**They surface real questions.** Wartinger's roller coaster study began with patients spontaneously reporting an unexpected clinical observation. The study that followed was methodologically modest (a 3D-printed kidney model, sixty rides) but the finding was real: physical movement affects stone passage, and the geometry of the movement matters. Whether this translates to clinical recommendations that will survive the next randomised trial is uncertain, but the observation was genuine.

**They demonstrate the breadth of what counts as evidence.** A society that decides to study penguin defecation pressures, optimal biscuit dunking, and the occupational health of sword swallowers is a society that has decided there are no questions too small, too undignified, or too absurd to ask carefully. This is, on balance, the correct decision. The history of medicine is full of questions that were dismissed as obvious before someone measured them and found they were wrong. Duct tape for warts seemed ridiculous before the RCT. Hand washing seemed obvious before Semmelweis, and then seemed ridiculous to his colleagues. The sword swallowers, it turned out, had a previously undocumented occupational health profile.

There is something clarifying about reading the weird literature. The seriousness with which it is conducted — the careful methods, the genuine uncertainty, the appropriate statistics — is a reminder that the apparatus of science is a tool, and the interesting question is always what you point it at. Pointing it at penguins is funny. It also produces findings. The two are not mutually exclusive, and the *BMJ* Christmas issue has been making this point, reliably, since 1993.

## A working position

The clinical literature is long. Most of it is about things that matter greatly and are extremely difficult to read. The BMJ Christmas issue, the Ig Nobel Prize proceedings, and the general tradition of scientists investigating questions that make their colleagues squint with confusion are part of what makes the literature sustainable. They are the literature's sense of humour, and a discipline without a sense of humour about itself is a discipline that has lost perspective on itself.

Also, if you have a patient with a small lower ureteric calculus and they happen to be visiting Disney World, it is probably reasonable to mention Big Thunder Mountain. Front car only. The data support it.
