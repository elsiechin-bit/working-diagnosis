You are driving on State Highway 1 when you see a car that has left the road. You pull over. There is a person, unconscious, with a bleeding scalp laceration. You are a GP. You have a basic first-aid kit in your car.

Two questions arise almost simultaneously. The first is clinical: what do I do? The second is legal: if something goes wrong, am I protected?

Most New Zealand clinicians assume the answer to the second question is yes. The assumption is reasonable, widely shared, and not clearly correct.

## Part I - Two completely different legal models

Before comparing countries, it helps to understand that "Good Samaritan law" is used loosely to describe two quite different legal structures that reflect fundamentally different philosophical starting points.

The first model - dominant in common law countries - is **liability protection**. You have no legal duty to help a stranger in distress. You may walk past. But if you choose to help, the law provides some protection against being sued for negligent assistance. The emphasis is on removing a disincentive to help, not on creating an obligation.

The second model - dominant in civil law countries - is **duty to rescue**. You have a positive legal obligation to assist a person in danger, subject to reasonable limitations. Failure to act is a criminal offence. Protection from liability for good-faith assistance is also typically provided, but it is almost an afterthought compared to the primary obligation.

These models reflect different understandings of the relationship between citizens and the state, between individuals and each other, and of what the law is for. Neither is obviously superior. They produce different practical outcomes. Understanding which model you are working under matters.

## Part II - New Zealand: the gap in the floor

New Zealand does not have a comprehensive Good Samaritan Act. This surprises many clinicians, who assume that such protection exists and that it specifically covers them.

The New Zealand Law Commission examined this question and has historically recommended legislative reform. [1] The recommendation has not been enacted in any comprehensive form. What exists instead is a patchwork.

The **Civil Defence and Emergency Management Act 2002** provides liability protection for people carrying out duties or exercising powers under that Act during declared emergencies. [2] This covers Civil Defence volunteers in formal emergencies. It does not cover a doctor who stops at a road accident on an ordinary Tuesday.

The **Volunteers Employment Protection Act 1973** protects volunteer employment rights - nothing to do with liability for negligent assistance.

The common law position in New Zealand, derived from English authority, is that there is no general duty to rescue a stranger. If you do choose to intervene, you assume a duty of care to the person you are assisting - but that duty is assessed against what is reasonable in the circumstances, and courts apply a more forgiving standard to emergency conditions than they do to planned clinical care. This is not a statutory protection; it is a judicial approach that provides some comfort without certainty.

The Medical Council of New Zealand's guidance on professional obligations notes that doctors have an ethical obligation to offer assistance in medical emergencies where it is reasonably practicable to do so. [3] This creates an interesting bind: a professional ethical obligation to help, with no matching statutory protection against liability for that help. The GMC in the UK is in a similar position, which we will come to.

The practical reality is that negligence claims against Good Samaritans are rare in New Zealand, and the courts have generally been sympathetic. But "rare" and "legally protected" are not the same thing, and the absence of statutory clarity creates genuine uncertainty that likely deters some clinicians from intervening when they could.

## Part III - Australia: the statutory model, state by state

Australia has addressed this more directly than New Zealand, though with the complication that Australia is a federation and the relevant laws are state and territory legislation rather than federal.

Most Australian states and territories have enacted specific Good Samaritan provisions. New South Wales provides a useful template. Under the *Civil Liability Act 2002* (NSW), sections 56 to 58, a person is not liable in negligence for an act or omission done in good faith when assisting, or attempting to assist, a person who is apparently injured or at risk of injury. [4] The protection is lost if the person was under the influence of alcohol or drugs, if their actions constituted gross negligence, or if the person was acting in the course of their trade or profession providing services for reward - meaning a nurse employed at the event would not be protected by the Good Samaritan provision, because she is there professionally.

This last carve-out is notable and somewhat counterintuitive: the Good Samaritan provisions in several Australian states apply more clearly to unqualified bystanders than to healthcare professionals acting outside their employment. For healthcare professionals in Australia, the position depends on whether they can be said to be acting outside any professional or employment context - which is often unclear.

The Queensland provision under the *Civil Liability Act 2003* (Qld) is more straightforward for healthcare professionals, specifically including persons who provide assistance consistent with their professional training or qualifications. Victoria's equivalent under the *Wrongs Act 1958* (Vic) has similar coverage. Western Australia, South Australia, Tasmania, the ACT, and the Northern Territory all have their own variants. [5] The patchwork is untidy but the direction of travel is consistent: Australia has moved further than New Zealand toward clear statutory protection.

## Part IV - United Kingdom: the SARAH Act and the professional duty question

The United Kingdom also lacks a comprehensive Good Samaritan statute, relying primarily on two pieces of legislation that provide indirect protection.

The **Compensation Act 2006**, section 1, requires courts, when determining whether a defendant has breached a duty of care, to consider whether requiring a particular standard of care would deter desirable activity for the benefit of society. This is broad and applies to emergency assistance among other contexts, but it is guidance to courts rather than direct protection. [6]

The **Social Action, Responsibility and Heroism Act 2015** (the SARAH Act) goes somewhat further, requiring courts to have regard to whether the defendant was acting heroically, in an emergency, for the benefit of others. [7] It was criticised on introduction as largely declaratory - saying what courts were already doing - without creating meaningful new protection. Academic assessment of the Act's practical effect has been underwhelming. It remains, at best, a signal of legislative intent rather than a reliable liability shield.

The **General Medical Council** guidance for UK doctors states that you must offer help if emergencies arise in clinical settings or in the community, where it would be reasonable to expect you to provide help. This is a professional obligation backed by regulatory consequences if breached, with minimal statutory liability protection attached to it. [8] The same tension as New Zealand: ethical duty to act, limited legal protection for acting.

For UK doctors, the relevant professional indemnity organisations (MDU, MPS) generally provide coverage for Good Samaritan acts, which is the practical answer many clinicians rely on rather than any statute.

## Part V - United States: fifty different answers, and an important evolution

The United States has Good Samaritan legislation in all fifty states, and no two are identical. At the federal level, the **Aviation Medical Assistance Act 1998** specifically protects medical professionals who assist during in-flight medical emergencies, which is notable because aircraft emergencies are a relatively common context in which clinicians are identified and asked to help. [9]

State laws vary on several dimensions: whether they cover all bystanders or only healthcare professionals; whether they require the person to be present by chance (not on duty); whether they extend to the use of AEDs; and what standard of conduct they protect against (ordinary negligence vs gross negligence vs wilful misconduct only).

The most interesting recent development in American Good Samaritan law is the emergence of **drug overdose Good Samaritan laws**, now enacted in most states. [10] These laws were designed to address a specific and documented problem: people witnessing opioid overdoses were not calling emergency services because they feared prosecution for drug possession. The laws provide immunity from prosecution for drug-related offences for people who call for help when someone is overdosing. Several states have extended this to the person overdosing themselves, not just the bystander. Some states have paired this with naloxone access laws, allowing naloxone to be dispensed without prescription in pharmacies.

The evidence on these laws is practically significant. Studies from multiple US states found that enactment of overdose Good Samaritan laws was associated with increases in emergency calls and in naloxone deployment at overdose scenes. [10] The laws appear to have directly saved lives by removing the disincentive to call for help. New Zealand does not yet have equivalent legislation, though community naloxone programmes have expanded. The question of whether statutory protection for bystanders at overdose scenes would increase emergency call rates is worth asking.

## Part VI - France and Germany: when inaction is the crime

The French and German approaches are qualitatively different from anything in the common law world.

In France, **Article 223-6 of the Penal Code** makes it a criminal offence for a person to fail, without risk to themselves or to a third party, to assist a person in danger when they could do so either personally or by calling for help. [11] The offence carries up to five years' imprisonment and a fine of €75,000. France also provides civil liability protection for good-faith emergency assistance. But the primary legislative concern is the obligation, not the protection.

Germany operates similarly. **Paragraph 323c of the Criminal Code** (*Strafgesetzbuch*) requires any person to render first aid at the scene of an accident, emergency, or common danger to the extent that assistance is required and can be expected of them. Failure is a criminal offence. [12] The standard is calibrated to what can reasonably be expected - a cardiologist walking past a cardiac arrest is held to a different standard than a passer-by with no medical training - but the obligation applies to all.

Several other civil law countries, including Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, and most of Scandinavia, have analogous duty-to-rescue provisions in their criminal codes.

The philosophical underpinning is the idea that community membership entails positive obligations to each other's welfare, not merely the negative obligation not to harm. The common law tradition, more influenced by liberal individualism, has historically treated the decision to help as a matter of personal conscience rather than legal duty - and protected good-faith assistance as a way of encouraging what the law does not require.

Neither approach is morally incoherent. The French and German position has the significant advantage that it removes the free-rider problem: in the common law world, you can rationally decline to help and suffer no legal consequence, while the person who does help assumes liability risk. The duty-to-rescue model does not permit this.

## Part VII - Specific situations for NZ clinicians

Given New Zealand's legislative gap, what does this mean practically?

**Road accidents and public emergencies.** You have no statutory Good Samaritan protection, but courts assess your conduct against a reasonable emergency standard, not a planned clinical standard. Document what you did and why if possible. Your professional indemnity insurer (MIPS or equivalent) should be notified of any incident. Check your policy wording - most New Zealand medical indemnity policies do extend to Good Samaritan acts, but confirm this explicitly.

**In-flight medical emergencies.** Airlines typically have policies requesting assistance from medical professionals on board. The legal position on liability varies by jurisdiction depending on where the aircraft is registered and where the incident occurs. The practical position is that you are expected to help, courts will assess you generously, and the airline's indemnity usually provides additional coverage. Help.

**AED use.** Automated external defibrillators are designed for use by untrained bystanders. Several New Zealand districts have registered AED networks. There is no specific New Zealand statute protecting AED users from liability, but the common law reasonable emergency standard applies, and the bar for negligent AED use in an out-of-hospital cardiac arrest is extremely high.

**Opioid overdose.** New Zealand has expanded community naloxone access, and naloxone is available without prescription in some settings. There is no specific Good Samaritan overdose law equivalent to those in many US states. Whether this matters in practice is uncertain - NZ drug possession laws are enforced with more discretion than their American equivalents - but the absence of formal protection is a known gap.

**The professional obligation dimension.** MCNZ guidance creates an ethical obligation to help in emergencies. This means that unlike a random bystander, a NZ doctor faces both an ethical duty to help and no clear statutory protection for doing so. The professional ethics and the legal framework pull in different directions. The practical answer - help, document, notify your indemnifier - is the same in any individual case, but the structural gap is worth naming.

## Part VIII - What better looks like

Australia's state-level legislative approach provides a model. A straightforward New Zealand Good Samaritan Act along the lines of the NSW Civil Liability Act provisions would:

- Provide clear protection from civil liability for good-faith emergency assistance
- Apply specifically to healthcare professionals assisting outside their employment context
- Include an overdose Good Samaritan provision removing the prosecution disincentive at opioid overdose scenes
- Align New Zealand with the standard of peer jurisdictions

The Law Commission has examined this territory. The recommendation for reform has been noted and not acted on for several decades. [1] Meanwhile, New Zealand clinicians who stop at accidents, respond to in-flight emergencies, or assist at public events operate in a legal grey zone that their Australian colleagues do not.

The argument for legislation is not primarily that clinicians are being sued - they largely are not. It is that uncertainty has a chilling effect on assistance that clear protection would remove. The person who drives past the accident because they are not sure they are covered is the policy failure the law has not yet fixed.

Until then: help, be reasonable, document, and know your indemnity policy.
