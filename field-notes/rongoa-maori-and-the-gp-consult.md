This piece is written by a non-Māori GP for an audience that is largely non-Māori. That is the first thing to be honest about. Mātauranga Māori is Māori knowledge. It is held by tohunga, by rongoā practitioners, by kaumātua, by whānau and hapū and iwi. It is not held by me, and it is not held by most readers of this post. What I can usefully do is engage with the substance — historical, regulatory, framework-level — that a working GP needs to know to engage well, and to flag, repeatedly, the points at which the responsible move is to defer to Māori voices rather than to perform competence we don't have.

The cultural competence framework that has dominated NZ medical education for two decades has produced something genuinely useful and something genuinely inadequate. The useful part: most non-Māori clinicians now know the names Te Whare Tapa Whā and Te Tiriti o Waitangi, can recite them in an exam, and have absorbed the surface-level message that Māori health is shaped by more than biomedical variables. The inadequate part: the framework has tended to treat all this as content to be learned rather than as a structural challenge to how Pākehā medicine works, and has often produced a tick-box version of engagement that Māori health professionals have been clear is not what they were asking for.

This piece is an attempt at the deeper version. It is necessarily partial, and it should be read alongside — and ultimately corrected by — Māori sources. I have flagged, throughout, the places where I am summarising rather than speaking with authority.

## Part I — The historical record

The standard story of Māori health under colonisation is a story of catastrophic decline followed by partial recovery, but the timing and shape of that decline matters for how we understand contemporary inequities and the contemporary status of rongoā Māori.

Pre-1840 Māori had a sophisticated, place-based health system, organised around concepts of hauora (well-being), mauri (life force), tapu and noa, and the interconnection of physical, spiritual, ancestral, and environmental dimensions of health. Practitioners included tohunga of various specialisations — including tohunga rongoā, who held expertise in plant medicines, bodywork (mirimiri, romiromi), karakia, and the diagnostic and therapeutic use of those modalities together [1,2]. Knowledge was transmitted through whakapapa-based teaching, often within whānau, and was held to be tapu in ways that cannot be cleanly translated into Pākehā concepts of professional credentialling.

Colonisation introduced new diseases against which Māori had no immunity (measles, influenza, tuberculosis), disrupted the ecological and social systems on which hauora depended (loss of whenua, urbanisation, language suppression), and produced a public health emergency in the late 19th century in which the Māori population fell from an estimated 100,000+ at the time of the Treaty to approximately 42,000 by 1896 [3]. This is the demographic context for the early 20th-century health debates.

**The Tohunga Suppression Act 1907** is the single piece of historical legislation most relevant to the contemporary status of rongoā, and it is more complicated than the standard summary suggests. Introduced by James Carroll (Native Minister, of Māori descent through his mother) and supported by all four Māori MPs at the time — Apirana Ngata, Hōne Heke Ngāpua, Tame Parata, and Henare Kaihau — the Act criminalised certain practices of self-proclaimed tohunga who were judged to be exploiting Māori communities through false claims of supernatural healing, particularly during epidemic disease [4,5]. The supporters of the Act, including the prominent Māori physicians Māui Pōmare and Te Rangi Hīroa (Sir Peter Buck), framed it as protection from "bastard tohunga" — practitioners they considered unqualified pretenders — rather than an attack on legitimate mātauranga [4]. Only nine convictions were ever obtained under the Act, and it was repealed by the Maori Welfare Act 1962 [4,5].

The complicated part is the lasting effect. Whatever the Act's narrow legal application, its broader cultural effect — combined with the parallel suppression of te reo Māori in schools, the urbanisation of Māori populations, and the dominance of Pākehā medicine in the 20th-century health system — was to disrupt the transmission of rongoā knowledge across generations and to push the practice of rongoā out of public life and into private whānau settings [1,5]. Tohunga did not disappear, but the public legitimacy of their practice was substantially diminished, and the institutional space for the integration of rongoā with mainstream health services was effectively closed for most of the 20th century.

**The Treaty / Te Tiriti** signed in 1840 contains, in the Māori text that the Waitangi Tribunal has consistently held to be the operative version, guarantees that include the protection of taonga (treasured things) and tino rangatiratanga (self-determination, chieftainship) over Māori affairs [6]. Both the Crown's article 1 obligation (kāwanatanga, governance) and article 2 (tino rangatiratanga) and 3 (equal rights) commitments have been interpreted in subsequent jurisprudence as creating positive obligations on the Crown to protect Māori health and to support Māori-led health services. The historical record is one of systematic failure to meet those obligations.

**WAI 2575**, the Waitangi Tribunal's Health Services and Outcomes Kaupapa Inquiry, was initiated in 2016 to hear claims of national significance regarding Māori health [7]. Stage One, focused on primary care and the legislative/policy framework, produced the *Hauora Report* in 2019, which found that the Crown had systematically breached Te Tiriti by failing to address persistent Māori health inequities through the primary health care system [8]. The Tribunal's findings and recommendations have shaped much of the subsequent NZ health policy environment, including the 2022 Pae Ora Act and the (subsequently disestablished) Te Aka Whai Ora.

The historical context matters because it answers a question many non-Māori clinicians have without quite asking it: why is rongoā Māori something we are only now learning about? The short answer is that the system within which we trained has, until very recently, treated Māori knowledge systems as either irrelevant or actively suspect. The current cultural moment in NZ health — including ACC's formal funding of rongoā Māori, which I'll come to — is a partial correction to a long historical exclusion.

## Part II — The frameworks

Several Māori health frameworks have entered mainstream NZ health discourse over the last forty years. Each is associated with a specific kaupapa Māori scholar; each is widely cited; each is often deployed shallowly. I'll briefly summarise the three most important and recommend that any GP wanting to use them in clinical practice read the primary sources rather than the summaries.

**Te Whare Tapa Whā** (the Four-Sided House), developed by Sir Mason Durie and articulated in *Whaiora: Māori Health Development* and subsequent works, frames health as the balance of four dimensions — taha tinana (physical), taha hinengaro (mental and emotional), taha whānau (family and social), and taha wairua (spiritual) — supported by the foundation of whenua (land, place, belonging) [9]. The image is of a wharenui in which all four walls are needed to keep the structure standing. The framework is widely used across NZ health services and is the standard reference for thinking holistically about Māori health. It is also the framework most often cited tokenistically — named as if naming were the same as engaging.

**Te Wheke** (the Octopus), developed by Rangimārie Rose Pere, is an older and arguably richer framework that depicts health as an octopus whose head is the child or whānau and whose eight tentacles represent eight interconnected dimensions including wairuatanga (spirituality), hinengaro (mind), tinana (body), whānaungatanga (extended family), mana ake (uniqueness), mauri (life principle), hā ā koro mā ā kui mā (the breath of ancestors), and whatumanawa (emotions) [10]. The interconnection metaphor — eight parts that move together rather than four walls — captures something different from Te Whare Tapa Whā and is often the better framework for thinking about complex chronic health situations.

**Te Pae Mahutonga** (the Southern Cross), also from Mason Durie, is a population-health-oriented framework that uses the four stars and two pointers of the Southern Cross constellation as metaphor for the elements of Māori well-being: mauriora (cultural identity), waiora (physical environment), toiora (healthy lifestyles), te oranga (participation in society), and the two pointers ngā manukura (leadership) and te mana whakahaere (autonomy) [11]. This framework is particularly useful for population-level work and policy thinking rather than the individual consult.

The shared feature of these frameworks, and the feature most commonly missed in their tokenistic deployment, is that they are not lists. They are relational: they describe how dimensions of well-being interact, what happens when one is degraded, what restoration of balance involves. The biomedical model, by comparison, is pathology-and-organ-system-based and tends to assume that components can be addressed in isolation. The frameworks are useful in clinical practice when they are allowed to actually challenge the biomedical reflex, not when they are added as a wairua box at the end of the assessment.

A practical implication: a patient who tells you that their fatigue is connected to whenua loss, to the death of a kuia three months ago, to their disconnection from te reo, and to the recent diagnosis of their tamariki with diabetes is making a clinical statement in a Te Whare Tapa Whā frame. The clinician who treats only the iron studies is missing the diagnosis the patient has already made. This is not a "cultural" overlay on the medical work; it is, in this frame, the medical work.

## Part III — Rongoā Māori in 2026

Rongoā Māori is not "Māori alternative medicine" and the analogy to naturopathy or CAM, while linguistically tempting for non-Māori clinicians, is misleading. Rongoā is a place-based, whakapapa-based knowledge system with its own epistemology, its own teaching tradition, and its own internal standards of practice. The plant medicines that get foregrounded in popular accounts — koromiko, kawakawa, harakeke, kūmarahou, mānuka — are part of rongoā but are not the whole of it.

Donna Kerridge, Ruatau Perez, Jolie Davis, and other contemporary rongoā practitioners typically describe rongoā as comprising at least four interrelated components [12,13]:

- **Rongoā rākau**: the use of plant medicines, often gathered from specific places, with specific tikanga (protocols) about gathering, preparation, and administration.
- **Mirimiri** and **romiromi**: bodywork practices, sometimes glossed as "Māori massage" but functionally distinct in their integration with diagnostic, energetic, and spiritual work.
- **Karakia**: prayer, incantation, and spiritual work — not as decoration but as integral to the therapeutic process.
- **Whitiwhiti kōrero**: structured conversation, advice, and the relational work of healing — a meaningful clinical contact requires time spent in talk, often involving whānau, often connecting current symptoms to whakapapa, whenua, and life circumstances.

The contemporary regulatory situation is unusual and worth understanding clearly. **Rongoā practitioners are not regulated under the HPCA Act 2003** — there is no statutory registering authority. However, **ACC has, since 2020, formally funded rongoā Māori as a social rehabilitation option for clients with covered injuries** [14,15]. ACC maintains a register of approved rongoā practitioners, currently around 100 across NZ from Te Tai Tokerau to Murihiku [15]. As of mid-2024, ACC had funded over 77,000 rongoā Māori sessions in the preceding four years, with claim numbers having approximately doubled in that period [13]. The inaugural Rongoā Māori Conference, held in Rotorua in 2024 and supported by ACC, brought together over 450 practitioners [13].

This is a significant development. The state-funded health system, through ACC, is now formally recognising and resourcing a Māori knowledge system as a clinical option for injury rehabilitation. The implications are still being worked through — the boundaries of what ACC funds, the standards practitioners must meet, the relationship between rongoā credentialling and ACC registration, and the broader question of whether rongoā should be funded outside the injury context — are all live policy questions.

For non-Māori GPs, the practical implication is that rongoā is now part of the available clinical landscape for ACC-eligible injury patients. A patient who asks about rongoā Māori as a rehabilitation option is asking about something that is funded, that has registered practitioners, and that the patient may have specific cultural, whakapapa-based, or geographic reasons for preferring. The right response is interest and facilitation, not the framing of suspicion that's often (and inappropriately) imported from naturopathic or CAM contexts.

Outside the ACC injury context, rongoā operates in a less formalised space — through hauora Māori providers, through marae-based health initiatives, through individual practitioners. Some hauora Māori providers integrate rongoā with primary care; others operate as referral resources alongside conventional general practice. The geographic distribution is uneven, with denser provider networks in regions with higher Māori population proportions.

## Part IV — The clinical engagement

What does serious engagement actually look like in primary care? Several things, and several non-things.

**It does not look like**: asking every Māori patient if they want a karakia. Reading their pepeha out of an electronic record. Adding a wairua box to the assessment template. Placing a Māori artwork in the waiting room and considering the work done. Citing Te Whare Tapa Whā in an HQSC presentation. None of these is wrong; none of them is sufficient.

**It does look like**: structural changes in how the practice operates, what is asked, who is invited into the consultation, and how the clinical work is configured. A few specifics.

**Asking, not assuming.** The question "is there anything important to you culturally or spiritually that I should know about how you'd like your care to go?" is a usable opener. It puts the patient in the driver's seat about whether and how to share. It makes no assumption about what the answer will be. It is, importantly, a question that should be asked of every patient — Māori, Pākehā, Pacific, Asian, refugee, secular — not only of patients whose surnames code as Māori. Cultural framing matters to many patients, in many configurations.

**Inviting whānau.** Many Māori patients will want to bring whānau into significant clinical conversations. Building this expectation into the consultation structure — booking longer appointments when the patient has indicated they'd like family present, having seating for more than one support person, being explicit that whānau presence is welcomed — is structural rather than performative.

**Knowing the local rongoā landscape.** Whether your area has a hauora Māori provider, where ACC-registered rongoā practitioners are located, what the relationships are between local kaumātua and the practice — these are practical bits of knowledge that take time to build but that pay off in the work. The Healthpoint listing for ACC-approved rongoā practitioners is a useful starting point; building the relationships locally is the longer work.

**Appropriate facilitation rather than gatekeeping.** A patient who is using rongoā alongside their conventional care does not need the GP's permission. The GP's role is to ensure that the integration is safe (any plant medicine should be flagged on the medication list; herb-drug interaction checking should be done where relevant) and that the patient has full information from both directions. The naturopath consult framing — where the GP's role is partly to evaluate and gently correct what the patient is being given by another practitioner — does not transfer cleanly to rongoā, and the assumption that it does is one of the more common Pākehā clinical errors.

**Knowing what you don't know.** A Pākehā GP cannot, and should not try to, perform rongoā or provide cultural advice on tikanga. Recognising the boundary of one's own competence is part of the clinical skill. Where the patient needs cultural support that the GP cannot provide, the appropriate move is referral or facilitation — to a hauora Māori provider, to a kaumātua, to a rongoā practitioner — not improvisation.

**Engaging with the equity data.** The persistent gaps in Māori health outcomes, documented across virtually every measurable indicator from infant mortality to life expectancy to specific disease outcomes, are not somebody else's problem. They are the practice's problem. Closing those gaps requires structural attention to the practice's own service: who is enrolled, who is reaching the practice, who is being followed up, who is being lost. Equity audits — which some PHOs and Health NZ initiatives now resource — are one of the more concrete ways the work gets done.

## Part V — What this post can't tell you, and what to read

This is the part that should anchor a post like this and that is most often skipped.

This post is a foundation, written by a non-Māori GP, for a primarily non-Māori readership. It is not, and cannot be, a substitute for engagement with Māori voices, mātauranga Māori in its proper context, or local relationships with the iwi, hapū, and hauora providers in the area where you practise. The substantial work is relational and place-based; it cannot be done from a blog post.

A few primary sources I'd recommend reading rather than my summary of them:

- **Mason Durie**, *Whaiora: Māori Health Development* (2nd ed., 1998). The foundational text for Te Whare Tapa Whā and the contemporary articulation of Māori health from a kaupapa Māori clinical and academic perspective.
- **Rangimārie Rose Pere**, *Te Wheke: A Celebration of Infinite Wisdom* (1991). The primary source for Te Wheke; widely cited but less widely read.
- **Linda Tuhiwai Smith**, *Decolonizing Methodologies: Research and Indigenous Peoples* (1999, 2nd ed. 2012). The foundational text on kaupapa Māori research methodology and the broader question of how indigenous knowledge can and should be engaged with by non-indigenous scholars and clinicians.
- **Hinemoa Elder**, *Aroha: Māori Wisdom for a Contented Life Lived in Harmony with Our Planet* (2020). A more recent, accessible, and clinically-relevant work by a Ngāpuhi child psychiatrist.
- **Waitangi Tribunal**, *Hauora: Report on Stage One of the Health Services and Outcomes Kaupapa Inquiry (Wai 2575)* (2019). The primary document for understanding the Crown's Treaty obligations in health and the basis for current health system reform.
- **He Korowai Oranga: Māori Health Strategy** (Ministry of Health). The current government strategy document and the operational framework most non-Māori clinicians will encounter.

Local engagement matters more than any reading list. The hauora Māori provider closest to your practice; the iwi-Māori partnership board for your region; the kaumātua and rongoā practitioners in your area; the Māori health coordinator (where one exists) within your PHO. These are the relationships that make the work real, and they are relationships that take years rather than evenings to develop.

What this post cannot do, and what no amount of reading can do, is give a non-Māori clinician the standing to speak about mātauranga Māori as if it is theirs. It is not, and it will not be. The honest position for a non-Māori clinician is to engage seriously, to defer appropriately, to keep learning, and to spend the relational time that the actual work requires.

That, I think, is the honest place to land. The cultural competence framework's failure has been to imply that there is a finished version of this engagement that can be reached and ticked off. There isn't. The work is permanent, and being permanent is not a defect but a feature.
