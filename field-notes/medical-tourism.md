It started with a knee. Mrs T had been on the orthopaedic waiting list for fourteen months. Her BMI was just high enough that the threshold kept sliding out of reach. Her daughter had found a clinic in Bangkok  -  accredited, English-speaking, good reviews on TripAdvisor. The total package, flights and five-star recovery hotel included, came in under what Southern Cross would have charged as the uninsured gap. She wanted my blessing.

She didn't get a blessing. She got a conversation.

Medical tourism  -  elective surgery performed in a lower-cost country, often combined with holiday recovery  -  is not new. What *is* new is how normalised it has become, how aggressively it is marketed on social media, and how often it ends up at the GP's door either before or after the trip. We are, increasingly, the pre-clearance stamp and the emergency landing strip. It's worth having a considered view.

---

## Why patients go

The short answer is: cost and queue.

New Zealand's public system is the envy of many countries in principle; in practice, the elective surgery backlog has become chronic. As of January 2025, over 37,000 patients were waiting more than four months for surgery, including more than 8,600 in orthopaedics alone. Over 77,000 were waiting more than four months just for a first specialist assessment.<sup>1</sup> A University of Otago study found that rejected specialist referrals rose from roughly 63,000 to 85,000 per year between 2018 and 2022.<sup>1</sup>

For patients who can't access public funding and can't afford domestic private rates, the arithmetic of medical tourism can look compelling. A hip replacement that costs $22,000-$40,000 privately in New Zealand<sup>2</sup> can be done in India for around NZD $12,000-$17,000,<sup>3</sup> or in Thailand for $13,000-$18,000  -  and that includes the hotel, the concierge service, and the flights.<sup>4</sup> On paper, the patient saves money *and* skips the queue.

The procedures most commonly sought overseas align with our longest waiting lists: joint replacements, bariatric surgery, cosmetic procedures, dental work, and IVF.<sup>5</sup>

## The cost comparison (what patients are actually seeing)

The figures below are NZD estimates based on New Zealand private insurer data and published medical tourism costs converted at current rates. They represent **private costs**  -  the benchmark a self-funding patient faces here. Public care remains free if accessible.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total hip replacement | $22,000-$40,000 | $13,000-$18,000 | $12,000-$17,000 | $10,000-$15,000 |
| Total knee replacement | $25,000-$33,000 | $11,000-$16,000 | $12,000-$17,000 | $9,000-$14,000 |
| Bariatric surgery (sleeve/bypass) | $19,000-$35,000 | $10,000-$14,000 | $8,000-$12,000 | $7,000-$11,000 |
| Breast augmentation | $19,000-$22,000 | $5,000-$8,000 | $4,000-$7,000 | $3,500-$6,000 |
| Facelift | $25,000-$40,000 | $10,000-$20,000 | $10,000-$16,000 | $8,000-$14,000 |
| Dental implant (per tooth) | $4,000-$6,500 | $2,500-$4,000 | $2,000-$3,500 | $1,500-$3,000 |
| IVF cycle | $10,000-$15,000 | $5,000-$8,000 | $4,000-$6,000 | $4,000-$6,000 |
| Coronary artery bypass | $50,000-$75,000 | $18,000-$28,000 | $12,000-$18,000 | $14,000-$20,000 |

*Sources: NZ figures from AIA/nib/Southern Cross insurer data 2023-24.<sup>2</sup> Overseas figures converted from USD estimates at current rates  -  treat as indicative ranges, not fixed prices.<sup>4,6,7</sup> Costs exclude travel, accommodation, and post-operative physiotherapy.*

The headline savings are real. But the table above doesn't include what can go wrong.

## What actually goes wrong

### The flight problem

The most immediately life-threatening issue is the combination of major surgery and long-haul flight. The RACS (Royal Australasian College of Surgeons) recommends at minimum one to two weeks before flying after surgery, and the NZ Association of Plastic Surgeons advises against long-haul travel for six weeks post-procedure.<sup>8,9</sup> Most patients don't want to pay for six weeks in a Bangkok hotel on top of their surgery.

Deep vein thrombosis risk is compounded by surgery *and* immobility in flight  -  these are not independent risks that add; they interact. The Aerospace Medical Association notes that commercial flight altitude and pressure changes also create additional hazards after thoracic or abdominal surgery.<sup>5</sup>

### Infection and AMR

One analysis of international ICU data found a threefold greater postoperative infection rate in centres outside Europe and the US.<sup>5</sup> More concerning for us as GPs: the antimicrobial resistance profile of organisms acquired overseas differs substantially from what we see here. A wound infection with NDM-1-producing *Klebsiella* or carbapenem-resistant *Acinetobacter* presenting to our clinic is a different beast from a garden-variety *Staph aureus* SSI.<sup>10</sup> The CDC has documented outbreaks of highly drug-resistant bacteria among medical tourists returning to multiple countries.<sup>10</sup>

### Continuity of care

Operative notes may not travel with the patient, may be in another language, or may not exist in legible form. Implant details  -  critical for future revision surgery  -  are often incomplete. Many NZ surgeons are appropriately reluctant to accept clinical-legal responsibility for another surgeon's patient, particularly when that surgeon is unreachable in another country.<sup>5</sup>

A 2025 Welsh NHS rapid review found wound infection and wound dehiscence were the most common complications presenting to primary care after medical tourism, with some cases progressing to multi-organ failure from sepsis.<sup>11</sup> Six British nationals died in Turkey following medical procedures in 2023 alone.<sup>11</sup>

### The revision problem

Cosmetic surgery and dental work done overseas often fail at rates that exceed local benchmarks  -  not invariably, but enough to matter. NZ dentists report routinely managing overseas dental work that requires revision, often at the patient's considerable additional cost.<sup>3</sup>

## The GP's role: before the trip

This is where we can add the most value and often where we feel least prepared.

**Don't refuse to engage.** If you decline to discuss medical tourism, the patient proceeds anyway, without your input, and returns to you with complications. A non-judgmental, structured pre-travel conversation is better for the patient and better for you.

**Pre-operative assessment is appropriate.** An ECG, baseline bloods, and medication review before major elective surgery overseas are the same things you'd do before domestic private surgery. Frame it that way.

**Ask about accreditation.** Joint Commission International (JCI) accreditation is the most widely recognised international standard. Its presence is meaningful; its absence should prompt further scrutiny. It is not a guarantee in either direction.<sup>12</sup>

**DVT prophylaxis conversation.** Patients need to understand the flight risk in unambiguous terms. If they are proceeding, establish a plan  -  compression stockings, timing of the return flight, whether prophylactic LMWH is warranted given their individual risk factors. Document the conversation.

**Insurance.** Standard travel insurance excludes elective procedures and their complications. Specialist medical tourism insurance exists but is inconsistently purchased. This matters: complications managed privately in NZ can cost more than the original overseas procedure.<sup>3</sup>

**Request operative documentation.** Ask the patient to obtain and bring back: operative notes, implant details (lot and batch numbers for joint replacements), discharge summary, post-operative medication list, and follow-up instructions.

---

## The GP's role: after the trip

**Low threshold for early review.** Book a review within one to two weeks of return for major surgery, regardless of how the patient reports feeling. Wound inspection, vital signs, and a targeted history may catch early complications before they escalate.

**Consider AMR in wound management.** If a wound infection presents after overseas surgery, a wound swab before empirical antibiotics is important. Your usual first-line agent may be ineffective. Involve ID/microbiology early if the infection is not responding.

**Manage the referral landscape carefully.** If the patient needs revision surgery or specialist follow-up, be upfront that some specialists may be reluctant to take on overseas cases. Frame the referral clearly and completely.

**Psychological sequelae.** This is under-discussed. Patients who have had poor outcomes overseas  -  particularly after cosmetic surgery  -  may present with significant distress, body image concerns, and financial stress on top of their physical complications. Screen for it.

---

## A note on equity

Medical tourism is sometimes framed as a choice for the affluent. In reality, its demographics are broader. Patients who can't afford NZ private rates *and* can't access public funding  -  often working people in the BMI grey zone, or those who've been turned away from the waiting list  -  are disproportionately drawn to it. This is a system failure dressed as a personal choice. The appropriate response from primary care is not judgment; it is harm reduction.

The RACS position is worth reading: it explicitly calls on governments to reduce elective surgery waiting lists as the most effective strategy against medical tourism risk.<sup>9</sup>

---

## Bottom line

Medical tourism is a rational response to a system under pressure. The savings are real. The risks are also real, and they are unevenly distributed  -  falling hardest on patients who can least afford complications. As GPs we are the best-placed clinicians to reduce harm on both sides of the trip - our role is to engage, not to gatekeep or to abstain.

When Mrs T came back  -  knee replaced, walking well, no complications  -  she was grateful for the preparation. She'd taken her operative notes. She'd waited eight days before flying. She'd come in for a two-week review. The DVT prophylaxis conversation had made her nervous enough to be careful.

Not every patient is that lucky. But preparation helps.
