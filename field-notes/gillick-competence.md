A fifteen-year-old comes in alone. She would like to start the pill. She does not want her parents to know. She is not in danger. She is not being coerced. She is, by any reasonable assessment, a thoughtful young person making a considered request about her own body.

Can you prescribe?

The answer, in New Zealand, is yes - and the legal and ethical architecture that makes it yes is one of the more intellectually interesting things in general practice. It involves a determined woman in Norfolk, a House of Lords judgment that nearly did not go the way it did, a subsequent series of cases that complicated the picture considerably, and a framework that New Zealand adopted not by legislation but by the slow, patient work of common law reception. Understanding it properly changes how you handle not just contraception but a wide range of adolescent consultations - mental health, substance use, sexual health, refusal of treatment.

This is an attempt at a reasonably complete account.

## Part I - What Gillick competence actually is

The term "Gillick competence" describes the capacity of a person under the age of 16 to consent to medical treatment without parental knowledge or consent, where that person has "sufficient understanding and intelligence to enable them to understand fully what is proposed." [1]

It is important to be clear about what this is and is not.

It is a common law doctrine, not a statutory one. In New Zealand, the *Care of Children Act 2004* sets out that a person aged 16 or over may consent to medical, surgical, or dental procedures as if they were of full age [2]. For those under 16, the statutory framework is silent on capacity - and into that silence the common law steps, in the form of Gillick.

It is a test of capacity, not of age. A ten-year-old is not Gillick competent for the same reason a ten-year-old cannot understand a mortgage: developmental reality, not an arbitrary rule. A mature fifteen-year-old discussing contraception in coherent, considered terms may well be. The assessment is individual and contextual every time.

It is primarily a doctrine about consent, not refusal. This distinction matters enormously and is where many clinicians get things wrong. We will come to it.

## Part II - The case: Victoria Gillick versus the DHSS

In 1982, Victoria Gillick - a Catholic mother of ten children in Norfolk - wrote to her local Area Health Authority seeking assurance that no doctor would provide contraceptive advice or treatment to any of her daughters under the age of 16 without her knowledge and consent. The DHSS had issued guidance suggesting that in exceptional circumstances, a doctor could prescribe contraception to an under-16 without parental consent. Gillick wanted that guidance declared unlawful.

She lost at first instance. She won at the Court of Appeal. She lost, ultimately and decisively, in the House of Lords, in a judgment handed down in October 1985. [1]

The Lords held, 3 to 2, that the parental right to make decisions for a child was a function of the child's own need for care and protection, derivative rather than independent - and that it yielded, progressively, as the child developed the maturity and understanding to make their own decisions. Lord Scarman wrote the majority's most-cited passage:

*"The parental right to determine whether or not their minor child below the age of 16 will have medical treatment terminates if and when the child achieves a sufficient understanding and intelligence to enable him or her to understand fully what is proposed."* [1]

Lord Fraser took a slightly different route, focusing specifically on contraception and articulating a practical set of criteria that a doctor should satisfy before prescribing to an under-16. These became known as the Fraser guidelines.

The decision was 3-2. Lord Brandon and Lord Templeman dissented, and their dissents are worth reading - both worried, in different ways, about what happens when the law endorses secrecy between parents and children on matters of sexual activity. The majority won, but not by acclamation.

## Part III - The Fraser guidelines: a framework for contraception

Lord Fraser's criteria are specific to contraception and sexual health. They require the doctor to be satisfied that:

1. The young person would understand the advice;
2. The young person cannot be persuaded to inform their parents, or to allow the doctor to do so;
3. The young person is likely to begin, or continue to have, sex whether or not they receive contraceptive advice or treatment;
4. Without contraceptive advice or treatment, the young person's physical or mental health (or both) would be likely to suffer; and
5. The young person's best interests require contraceptive advice or treatment with or without parental consent. [1]

These criteria are often conflated with Gillick competence as a whole, but they are distinct. Gillick competence is the general doctrine about adolescent capacity. The Fraser guidelines are a specific application of that doctrine to contraception, with an additional "best interests" overlay and an explicit acknowledgment that providing contraception without parental knowledge can sometimes be the correct clinical and ethical choice.

In practice, most consultations with a reasonably mature teenager seeking contraception will satisfy all five criteria without requiring a formal checklist. The third criterion - that the young person will have sex anyway - does not mean the doctor is facilitating sexual activity; it means the doctor is making a realistic assessment of the young person's situation rather than a wishful one.

## Part IV - Where Gillick has limits: Re R and Re W

The House of Lords gave with one hand. The Court of Appeal took some of it back.

*Re R* in 1991 concerned a 15-year-old girl who fluctuated between competence and incompetence due to a psychiatric disorder and who was refusing antipsychotic medication during lucid intervals. Lord Donaldson MR held that even where a minor was Gillick competent, this created a "key to unlock the door" - but the door could be unlocked by the parents or the court as well as by the young person themselves. Gillick competence, on this analysis, gave the minor the capacity to consent but not a veto over treatment that others with parental responsibility wished to authorise. [3]

*Re W* in 1992 made this explicit. W was a 16-year-old with anorexia nervosa who refused treatment in a specialist unit. The Court of Appeal held that even the *Care of Children Act* provision about 16-year-olds could be overridden by the court in exercise of its inherent jurisdiction. Lord Donaldson introduced the Gillick competent minor's consent as a "flak jacket" for the doctor - it provided protection against action in battery if treatment proceeded - but it did not prevent the court from authorising treatment over refusal. [4]

What emerges from these cases is an asymmetry that persists to this day: a Gillick competent minor has the full legal capacity to consent to treatment, but the right to refuse treatment that others wish to provide can be overridden by parental authority or court order. Consent and refusal are not mirror images.

The ethical discomfort this creates is real. If we genuinely respect adolescent autonomy, the asymmetry is hard to justify on purely principled grounds. The courts have largely justified it on the basis of the irreversibility of some treatment refusals - you cannot un-die from a refused blood transfusion - and the evolving nature of capacity itself, especially in mental illness. Whether this fully satisfies is a matter of ongoing debate. [5]

The practical implication for GPs: a Gillick competent teenager's consent to treatment you are proposing is legally valid and generally sufficient. A Gillick competent teenager's refusal of treatment you think they need is ethically significant and should be taken seriously, but it does not necessarily end the discussion.

## Part V - How New Zealand received Gillick

New Zealand courts did not need to reinvent the wheel. The Gillick principles were adopted into New Zealand common law, consistent with the general common law reception that applies across the jurisdiction.

The *Care of Children Act 2004* reflects the Gillick framework without explicitly codifying it. Section 36 addresses those aged 16 and over [2], and the explanatory notes and parliamentary debates make clear that the legislators understood Gillick to govern those under 16. The *Health and Disability Commissioner (Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights) Regulations 1996* provides, through Right 7, that every consumer has the right to make an informed choice and give informed consent - and the Code defines "consumer" without age restriction, subject to the capacity analysis that Gillick provides for those under 16. [6]

The Medical Council of New Zealand's statement on consent acknowledges that young people under 16 can consent to treatment if they have sufficient maturity and understanding, and notes that this assessment must be made on an individual basis. [7]

New Zealand courts have had occasion to exercise the jurisdiction that *Re W* identified - the power to authorise treatment over the refusal of a minor, including those aged 16 and over who would otherwise have statutory consent capacity. Cases involving Jehovah's Witness minors refusing blood transfusions have been the most common forum for this, and New Zealand courts have consistently, albeit with evident discomfort, authorised transfusions when the treating team considered them necessary to preserve life. [8]

The discomfort is recorded in the judgments. These are not cases where the courts have been triumphant. They are cases where judges have been asked to override the sincerely held religious beliefs of young people who are, in every other respect, clearly capable of forming and expressing a view - and have done so on the basis that the irreversibility of death outweighs the current refusal of an adolescent whose values may continue to develop. *Re E* in the UK makes the poignancy of this explicit: E was 15, refused a blood transfusion on religious grounds, was overruled by the court, survived, and then - as an adult, with the full legal right to decide for himself - refused again and died. [9]

## Part VI - The contraception caveat in New Zealand

This is where New Zealand has a somewhat cleaner framework than the UK for a specific reason.

The *Contraception, Sterilisation, and Abortion Act 1977* (as amended) treats contraception as a distinct category of healthcare with its own access provisions. The statute does not impose an age floor on access to contraceptive advice and services. This means that for contraception specifically, the legal question for under-16s in New Zealand is not primarily "does this young person satisfy the Gillick test?" but rather "is there any legal barrier to providing this service?" The answer, ordinarily, is no. [10]

This matters because it removes one layer of uncertainty from the analysis. The Gillick framework still applies in the sense that you should still be satisfied the young person understands what is proposed and can make a reasoned decision. The Fraser criteria are still a useful scaffold. But the underlying statutory architecture in New Zealand is arguably more permissive than the UK position, which depends entirely on the common law.

Family Planning New Zealand's position, which is consistent with MCNZ guidance, is that contraception can and should be provided to young people under 16 who request it, provided the young person understands what is being offered and there is no reason to believe they are at risk of harm or exploitation. Inability to obtain parental consent is explicitly not, in itself, a reason to decline. [7]

For sexually active young people under 16, failing to provide contraception on the grounds of parental non-involvement is not a conservative choice. It is a choice that has consequences - unintended pregnancy, continued unprotected sex - and those consequences fall on the young person.

## Part VII - Real-life scenarios

**Scenario 1: A 15-year-old requesting the pill**

She has been with her boyfriend for six months. She does not want her parents to know because her family is conservative and she is not ready to have that conversation. She is articulate, understands the risks of unprotected sex, understands how the pill works, understands what you are proposing.

This is a straightforward Gillick competent consultation. She satisfies the Fraser criteria. Prescribe. Document your assessment of her capacity and your reasoning. A brief note - "assessed capacity to consent, Gillick/Fraser criteria met, COCP commenced" - is sufficient.

**Scenario 2: A 14-year-old with the same request**

The younger the person, the more carefully you should document your capacity assessment. At 14, you should be more thorough in your conversation: does she understand the mechanism of the pill? Does she understand what to do if she misses a dose? Does she understand the failure rate? Does she understand that the pill does not protect against STIs? Does she understand what pregnancy would mean for her?

You should also, sensitively, explore whether the relationship she is in is one of her choosing - a routine part of good clinical practice, approached with care rather than accusation. Statutory rape provisions in New Zealand (under the *Crimes Act 1961*) mean that sexual activity with a person under 16 by someone significantly older is criminal, and while your obligation is primarily to the young person in front of you, a 14-year-old whose "boyfriend" is 30 is a different clinical situation from a 14-year-old in a relationship with a peer. If you have concerns, document them. If you have serious concerns, consider whether a referral to Child, Youth and Family services is warranted - but this is a separate question from whether to prescribe.

**Scenario 3: A 16-year-old refusing psychiatric medication**

He has been diagnosed with schizophrenia. He is acutely unwell, believes the medication is poisoning him, and is refusing treatment. His parents want him treated.

This is the *Re R/Re W* scenario. He has statutory capacity under section 36 of the *Care of Children Act*, but his capacity is arguably impaired by the illness itself - a recognised complication of psychotic disorders that makes the capacity assessment genuinely difficult. Document carefully. Involve a consultant psychiatrist. If inpatient detention and treatment are indicated, the *Mental Health (Compulsory Assessment and Treatment) Act 1992* provides a framework that operates independently of the Gillick/age analysis entirely.

**Scenario 4: A 15-year-old wanting to discuss cannabis use in confidence**

She uses cannabis occasionally and is worried about its effects. She wants to talk to you about it but does not want her parents to know. She is not acutely at risk. She is asking for health information.

She is Gillick competent to engage in this health discussion, and the discussion itself - providing accurate information about cannabis risks in the developing adolescent brain - is a clinical interaction that does not require parental knowledge. The fact that the topic involves an illegal activity does not change the confidentiality analysis: she has disclosed health-related information to you in a clinical context and you have standard confidentiality obligations. The exception, as always, is if she disclosed information suggesting she is in serious danger - in which case your obligations shift. Recreational cannabis use by a 15-year-old, while not without risk, is not that threshold.

**Scenario 5: A 14-year-old Jehovah's Witness refusing a blood transfusion**

This is the hardest scenario, and the one where Gillick's limits are most starkly visible. She is fully competent by any clinical measure. Her religious belief is sincere and considered. She is refusing transfusion for a condition that would likely be fatal without it.

You cannot resolve this unilaterally. You need senior clinical support. You need to involve the ethics committee if time permits. You need to consider an urgent application to the Family Court for authorisation. The court will almost certainly authorise the transfusion. She will likely be angry, and the anger will be reasonable. None of this makes it straightforward.

## Part VIII - Confidentiality and the parental rights question

Gillick gave the minor the right to consent without parental knowledge. It did not fully answer the confidentiality question - but the *HDC Code* does.

Right 7 of the Code gives patients the right to make informed decisions. Right 3 gives patients the right to dignity and privacy. There is no parental exception to the confidentiality obligations that flow from these rights for a Gillick competent minor. If a young person has engaged in a confidential consultation and consented to treatment, you cannot subsequently tell their parents what was discussed without the young person's consent - unless disclosure is required to prevent serious harm, in which case your standard clinical judgment applies.

The practical implication: if a young person comes to you alone, you should tell them at the start what you can and cannot keep confidential. A brief explanation - that you keep what is discussed private, but that if you were worried they were in serious danger you might need to involve others - is good practice and sets expectations before the conversation begins. Most young people, when they understand this, will engage more openly than they would if they were uncertain about where the information goes.

Parental curiosity about what happened at the consultation is not grounds for disclosure. A phone call from a parent the following day saying "my daughter saw you yesterday, what did you prescribe?" is a moment to explain that you cannot discuss consultations without the patient's consent, that the patient is welcome to share whatever they choose, and to offer to make an appointment for the family to discuss any concerns.

## Part IX - A practical synthesis

A few principles worth keeping close.

**Document the capacity assessment, not just the outcome.** "Patient consented" is not sufficient. "Patient demonstrated understanding of the proposed treatment, its risks and benefits, and alternatives; assessed as Gillick competent; Fraser criteria met; COCP commenced" is.

**Gillick competence is dynamic.** A young person who was not competent to make a particular decision at age 13 may be fully competent to make it at 15. The assessment is fresh each time, for each decision.

**The bar for more serious interventions is higher.** Contraception and STI testing are relatively low-stakes decisions with clear benefits to the young person's health. Authorising a decision to refuse life-saving treatment is not. The degree of understanding required is proportionate to the seriousness and irreversibility of the intervention.

**You are not required to act against your clinical judgment.** If you are genuinely uncertain whether a young person has the capacity to make a particular decision, it is appropriate to seek input - from a colleague, a paediatrician, an ethics service. Uncertainty is not a reason to default to either blanket provision or blanket refusal.

**Young people are not a category to be processed.** The Gillick judgment matters because it reflected something true: that adolescent development is real, that a 15-year-old is not a 6-year-old, and that the clinical relationship works better when it treats the person in front of you as a moral agent whose view of their own life counts. The contribution is not a box to tick.

Victoria Gillick lost her case. But the minority arguments she forced into the legal record - about the limits of parental exclusion, about the weight of irreversibility, about the difficulty of predicting whether a 15-year-old's values will look the same at 25 - are still alive in the case law and still worth sitting with.

Gillick competence is not a mechanism for getting around difficult conversations with parents. It is a recognition that those conversations belong, ultimately, to the young person.
