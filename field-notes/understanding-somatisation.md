There is a kind of clinical frustration that most GPs recognise. The patient who comes back repeatedly with symptoms that investigations do not explain. The third normal MRI. The second gastroscopy. The referral that comes back with a letter saying "no pathology identified, please reassure." The patient who is not reassured. The consultation that ends badly, for everyone, again.

The word we tend to reach for in this situation is "somatisation." It is used to mean, more or less, that the patient is experiencing physical symptoms that are caused or maintained by psychological processes rather than by structural or biochemical pathology. The symptoms are real - the patient is not lying, not malingering, not imagining things - but they do not have an "organic" explanation, and therefore, by implication, they have a psychological one.

This framing is clinically useful in some ways and conceptually wrong in others. The distinction it relies on - between physical and psychological, between real and functional, between body and mind - does not reflect how the nervous system actually works. Unpicking that distinction does not just correct a philosophical error; it changes what you think is happening in these patients, what you think is worth treating, and how you talk about it.

## Part I - The Cartesian inheritance

Rene Descartes argued in 1641 that the mind and body were distinct substances - the mind immaterial and thinking, the body material and extended. [1] This was not primarily a medical claim; it was a metaphysical one, with theological motivations. But it mapped onto medicine with remarkable convenience. If mind and body were separate, then illness was either physical (in the body, accessible to investigation and treatment) or mental (in the mind, the province of the alienist or priest). A symptom without a physical cause became, almost definitionally, a symptom of the mind.

Medicine has been trying to escape this framework for most of the last century, with incomplete success. The biopsychosocial model, described by George Engel in a landmark 1977 paper in Science, argued that biological, psychological, and social factors are simultaneously operative in every illness and that the purely biomedical model was inadequate as a framework for clinical practice. [2] This was not a radical claim at the time of writing; it was already well-supported by the evidence. It has still not fully penetrated clinical culture.

The persistence of mind-body dualism in medicine has practical consequences. When a patient's symptom does not have an identifiable physical cause, the implicit conclusion is that it is therefore psychological. When a patient's symptom has a psychological explanation, the implicit conclusion is that it is somehow less real, less deserving of clinical attention, more the patient's fault. Neither inference is justified. Both are common.

## Part II - The nomenclature and what it reveals

The language around somatisation has shifted substantially over the past thirty years, and the shifts are not merely cosmetic - they track genuine conceptual progress as well as the usual sociological pressures on psychiatric nosology.

The older terminology - somatoform disorder, hypochondriasis, psychosomatic illness, hysteria - carried heavy freight. "Hysteria" was explicitly gendered. "Hypochondriasis" framed the patient as a worrier who did not have a real illness. "Psychosomatic" was widely understood by patients (and many doctors) to mean "not really physical," which most patients experienced as an accusation.

The *DSM-5* in 2013 reorganised this territory under the umbrella of Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders. [3] The key conceptual move was to abandon the "medically unexplained" criterion as the primary basis for diagnosis. The older approach diagnosed somatoform disorder essentially by exclusion: if symptoms could not be explained by organic pathology, they were presumed to be psychological. The new approach diagnoses on the basis of positive criteria - specifically, the patient's disproportionate distress, excessive thoughts, and significant impairment in relation to somatic symptoms - and explicitly notes that these criteria can be met even when a medical explanation for the symptoms exists.

This is not a small change. It means that somatic symptom disorder can coexist with a genuine medical illness. A patient with irritable bowel syndrome who is catastrophising about their symptoms, spending hours researching them, and avoiding activities because of them may have both IBS and somatic symptom disorder simultaneously. The clinical task is to address both the organic and the psychological - a choice between explanations is a false one.

The ICD-11, published in 2019, moved to the term "bodily distress disorder," which similarly emphasises the patient's distress and functional impairment rather than the absence of organic explanation. [4]

The term "functional" - as in functional neurological disorder, functional bowel disorder, functional somatic syndrome - is increasingly preferred in clinical communication because it says something positive about the mechanism (disordered function) without implying the symptoms are imaginary. It also has a long clinical history: "functional" in the sense of disturbed physiology without structural pathology is not a new concept and does not carry the same psychological baggage as "somatic" or "psychosomatic."

None of this terminology is perfect. All of it is better than the alternatives we have discarded.

## Part III - What is actually happening in the brain and body

The most useful conceptual frame for understanding somatisation, in the current evidence base, is probably the predictive processing model of brain function. [5]

The brain does not passively receive signals from the body and interpret them. It generates predictions - a continuous model of what is happening in the body - and compares incoming sensory signals to those predictions. What we consciously experience as pain, nausea, fatigue, breathlessness is not a direct readout of bodily state; it is the brain's best inference about what is happening, shaped by prior experience, context, attention, and expectation.

In normal circumstances, this system is remarkably accurate. In pathological circumstances, it can produce persistent, disabling symptoms in the absence of ongoing tissue damage - because the brain's predictive model has become miscalibrated and does not update correctly in response to incoming evidence that the body is not actually damaged.

This is not the same as saying the symptoms are imaginary. A brain generating a false positive alarm for tissue damage is producing a real experience of pain. The pain is genuine even if the damage is not. The distinction matters enormously for how you talk to patients about what is happening.

**Central sensitisation** is a well-characterised neurophysiological process in which the central nervous system becomes sensitised to pain signals, amplifying normal or sub-threshold inputs into perceived pain. [6] It is not a metaphor for psychological distress expressed through the body. It is a measurable change in the way the spinal cord and brain process nociceptive signals. Conditions including fibromyalgia, chronic widespread pain, irritable bowel syndrome, and some presentations of chronic fatigue syndrome involve central sensitisation as a primary mechanism. Treating these as conditions that need a psychological explanation rather than a neurophysiological one is a category error.

**The autonomic nervous system** is the physiological mediator of much that is labelled somatic. Chronic stress - whether psychological, physical, or social - produces sustained activation of the HPA axis and the sympathetic nervous system. This has measurable effects: elevated inflammatory markers, altered gut motility, cardiovascular changes, immune dysregulation. Adverse childhood experiences, chronic interpersonal adversity, and post-traumatic stress are associated with a substantially elevated risk of chronic somatic conditions precisely because chronic stress is not simply a state of mind - it is a state of the body. [7]

This is the neuroscience that the biopsychosocial model was trying to describe, before the neuroscience caught up with the theory.

## Part IV - How common, and in whom

Medically unexplained symptoms are extraordinarily common in primary care. Estimates suggest that between 15 and 30 percent of GP consultations involve symptoms that cannot be fully explained by identified organic pathology. [8] Approximately one in six patients presenting to general practice has a presentation that would meet criteria for somatic symptom disorder if formally assessed.

Most of these patients are not presenting with a psychiatric diagnosis and do not see themselves as having psychological problems. They are presenting with genuine, disabling symptoms. The clinical challenge is not persuading them to see a psychiatrist; it is providing adequate care in primary care for a condition that is poorly served by the available interventions.

Women are more commonly diagnosed with somatic symptom disorders than men, though whether this reflects genuine sex differences in prevalence or systematic bias in clinical recognition is unclear and probably both. Men may present with somatic symptoms in different ways - as chronic pain, as fatigue, as substance use - that are recognised less readily as functional.

Socioeconomic deprivation, adverse childhood experiences, and trauma exposure are consistently associated with higher rates of somatic symptom disorders and chronic functional conditions. [7] This is not a coincidence. The adversity is in the body.

In a New Zealand context, the interaction between social determinants, Maori and Pacific health disparities, and somatic presentations is clinically significant. The higher rates of chronic pain, fatigue syndromes, and functional illness in deprived populations are not explained by individual psychology. They are, in substantial part, the bodily expression of social conditions.

## Part V - The diagnostic challenge: missing disease and over-investigating

The two clinical errors in this territory pull in opposite directions, and both are common.

**Premature attribution to somatisation** means concluding too early that a patient's symptoms are functional and closing down investigation of organic causes. The history of medicine is full of conditions that were called hysterical or psychosomatic before their pathophysiology was characterised: multiple sclerosis, lupus, Addison's disease, coeliac disease, and many others were labelled as functional disorders in individual patients before they were understood as organic ones. A patient with fibromyalgia deserves the same standard of clinical assessment as a patient with rheumatoid arthritis. The absence of a previous organic explanation is not the same as the absence of an organic explanation.

**Diagnostic escalation** is the failure in the other direction: continuing to investigate, to refer, and to look for an organic diagnosis long after the clinical picture suggests a functional one, because neither the patient nor the clinician can accept the functional explanation. This is not without harm. Every additional investigation carries its own risk: incidental findings, false positives, radiation, procedure complications, reinforced illness beliefs. Repeated negative investigations do not reassure patients with somatic symptom disorder; they tend to increase anxiety. [9] The investigation is giving the patient a message that something organic might be there - why else would the doctor keep looking?

The practical calibration is not algorithmic. It depends on clinical acumen about how well a functional explanation fits the overall picture, how the patient's symptoms have evolved, what the prior probability of organic disease is, and what the patient's level of distress and functional impairment looks like. A patient whose symptoms have multiple features of central sensitisation, whose presentation follows a significant adverse life event, and who is not losing weight or reporting alarm symptoms is a different clinical situation from a patient with an atypical presentation of weight loss and night sweats who is anxious about it.

## Part VI - The therapeutic relationship is the treatment

For many patients with somatic symptom disorders, the most effective therapeutic tool available in primary care is a sustained, high-quality therapeutic relationship with a GP who does not give up on them. [10]

This is not a consolation prize. It is evidence-based. A randomised trial by Smith and colleagues found that GP consultation letters providing a framework for managing patients with somatisation significantly reduced their healthcare utilisation and improved their functioning. A long series of studies by the Southampton group found that what patients with medically unexplained symptoms consistently want is to be listened to, taken seriously, and given an explanation that makes sense - not to be told there is nothing wrong. [11]

The explanation that works best is not "your symptoms are real but they're in your mind." It is "your symptoms are real, and here is what we understand about how the nervous system can produce them." Patients who receive a positive, physiologically grounded explanation of what is happening - central sensitisation, autonomic dysregulation, the brain's alarm system becoming miscalibrated - engage better with treatment and improve faster than patients who receive an explanation framed primarily as psychological. [12]

Several things to avoid:

**Dismissal.** "The tests are normal so there's nothing wrong." There manifestly is something wrong - the patient is suffering. The tests being normal means something specific: they have not identified structural pathology. That is different from normal.

**Premature psychological attribution.** "I think this is stress." Even if this is correct, the timing and framing matter enormously. A patient who feels that their doctor has not taken their physical symptoms seriously will not engage with a psychological explanation, and they will be right that they have not been taken seriously.

**Collusion.** Ordering the next investigation to end the consultation. The investigation will not end the consultation; it will generate a new set of results to discuss and reinforces the idea that the answer is organic and has not been found yet.

**Abandonment.** "There's nothing more I can do." There is. Regular scheduled appointments, regardless of whether symptoms have changed, communicate that the patient's care is not contingent on being in crisis. Patients with chronic somatic presentations who have a consistent GP relationship use emergency services less and have better outcomes. [10]

## Part VII - What actually helps

The evidence base for treating somatic symptom disorders is better than many clinicians assume, though not as strong as we would like.

**Cognitive Behavioural Therapy** has the most consistent evidence across multiple functional somatic syndromes - irritable bowel syndrome, chronic fatigue syndrome, chronic pain, and somatisation broadly. [13] The mechanism is not simply "teaching the patient their symptoms are psychological." It targets the maladaptive cognitions (catastrophising, hypervigilance to bodily sensations) and behaviours (avoidance, reassurance-seeking) that maintain the symptom cycle.

**Acceptance and Commitment Therapy** (ACT) is increasingly used and has evidence across chronic pain and functional somatic presentations, particularly for patients who resist the CBT model because they experience it as being told their symptoms are in their head. ACT focuses on values-based functioning despite symptoms rather than reduction of symptoms, which is both more honest about what is achievable and more effective for some patients.

**Physiotherapy**, particularly for functional neurological disorder and chronic pain, has good evidence when it is delivered within a framework that includes education about the mechanism of symptoms. Graded exercise therapy for chronic fatigue syndrome has a more contested evidence base following the PACE trial controversy and should be offered carefully and with significant patient input about pacing. [14]

**Antidepressants** have a role that is separate from treating comorbid depression. Low-dose tricyclics (amitriptyline, nortriptyline) have evidence for chronic pain and IBS through mechanisms that are independent of their antidepressant effect - probably through their effect on central sensitisation and gut motility. SNRIs have evidence in fibromyalgia and chronic pain. The framing of these medications matters: "this helps with the way the nervous system processes pain signals" is true and more acceptable to most patients than "this is an antidepressant."

**Specialist liaison psychiatry and multidisciplinary pain services** are under-resourced in New Zealand but provide the most effective framework for complex presentations. A GP managing severe somatic symptom disorder without specialist input is doing so in difficult conditions.

## Part VIII - Cultural dimensions

The conceptual architecture of somatisation is not culturally neutral. It assumes that emotional distress is most naturally expressed as psychological symptoms - anxiety, depression, grief - and that the expression of distress through the body is a secondary process requiring explanation. This assumption reflects a particular cultural tradition.

In many cultural contexts, the communication of distress through the body is the primary mode, not a secondary one. In Maori tikanga, the concept of hauora encompasses physical, mental, spiritual, and social wellbeing as an integrated whole - not as separate domains with primary and secondary forms. Kaumātua presenting with chest pain that is related to grief, or tamariki with abdominal pain related to family disruption, are not "somatising" in a way that requires explanation; they are expressing distress in a way that the conventional clinical framework is not well designed to receive.

The risk for a NZ GP is the mirror of the diagnostic errors described above: applying a somatic label to a cultural communication style, or missing the emotional content of a physical presentation, because the clinical frame does not make space for it. Asking a Pacific patient "what has been happening in your life lately?" and sitting with the answer may be more diagnostically and therapeutically productive than ordering the next test.

This does not mean abandoning a thorough clinical assessment of physical symptoms in Maori and Pacific patients - that would be a form of cultural incompetence. It means holding both possibilities with equal seriousness.

## Part IX - The ACC layer in New Zealand

New Zealand's no-fault accident compensation system creates a specific dynamic in somatic presentations that does not exist in most other jurisdictions, and it is worth naming.

ACC provides compensation and treatment funding for physical injuries and, under certain conditions, mental injuries arising from certain events. For a patient with chronic pain following a workplace injury, an accident, or a specific covered trauma, ACC entitlements are linked to ongoing diagnosis and functional impairment.

This creates a set of incentives that can interact, in complex ways, with the psychological maintenance of somatic symptoms. A patient with chronic pain who is receiving ACC compensation, who has structured their identity and daily life around their injury, and who has legal proceedings pending faces a different set of pressures around recovery than a patient with no such context. This is not the same as malingering - patients with compensation claims have real suffering and real functional impairment. But the system can inadvertently provide reinforcers for illness behaviour that are not present in other contexts.

The GP's role in this is not to act as an investigator of claim validity. It is to provide clinical care that is accurate, to document findings honestly, and to be aware of the dynamic without letting it corrupt either overcautious under-treatment or cynical dismissal. A patient can have both a genuine functional somatic disorder and a legitimate ACC claim.

The interaction between ACC, chronic pain, and medically unexplained symptoms is one of the areas where the NZ primary care system is most poorly equipped, and where the funding and coordination of care is most fragmented.

## Part X - A way of thinking about it

The patient who returns for the fifth consultation with unexplained symptoms is not a failure of the health system or a manipulative person or a test of your patience. They are a person with real suffering, a clinical presentation that is difficult to explain using the framework they have been given, and a strong desire to be taken seriously by someone with clinical authority.

The things that help: taking the symptoms seriously without ordering the next investigation reflexively; providing a coherent, neurophysiologically grounded explanation for what might be happening; scheduling regular appointments rather than making care contingent on crisis; being honest about what the investigations have and have not shown; and connecting the patient to psychological or allied health support in a way that does not feel like a dismissal.

The things that do not help: telling patients there is nothing wrong; ordering investigations to avoid a difficult conversation; framing the presentation as primarily psychiatric without adequate clinical justification; or disengaging from care because the patient is not improving on the expected trajectory.

Somatisation is not the word we reach for when we run out of other words. It is the description of a real process - the expression of distress through the body, or the body's dysregulation in response to adversity, or the brain's miscalibration of its own predictive model - that deserves the same clinical seriousness as any other diagnosis. The mind-body distinction that makes it seem like a lesser category of illness is a philosophical relic, not a clinical reality.
