<p>He's six. He's in Year 1. His teacher has flagged him three times this term. His mother brings a printed email from school and sits across from me with the particular exhaustion of someone who has been managing this child since he could walk, and who has been told, in various softened ways, that he is <em>a lot</em>.</p>

<p>He is, by any account, a lot. He interrupted the consultation four times in the first two minutes. He picked up my otoscope, put it down, picked it up again. He cannot tell me what he had for breakfast but he can tell me, in extraordinary detail, about a Minecraft biome update. His mother says he's sweet. She also says she's exhausted. Both things are completely true.</p>

<p>The question on the table is whether this child has ADHD, and whether we do anything about it. What follows is me thinking out loud  -  the neuroscience, the diagnostic problem, and the pharmacology  -  because I find I give better consultations when I've actually worked through the evidence rather than rehearsing a script.</p>

<h2>The diagnostic problem at six</h2>

<p>DSM-5 requires that symptoms be present before age twelve, present in two or more settings, and cause functional impairment.<sup>1</sup> That last criterion is doing a lot of work. At six, in a New Zealand classroom with twenty-seven other children and a stretched teacher, a normally-developing boy with a high-energy temperament can look clinically significant. The reverse is also true: a genuinely impaired child in a small, structured, patient environment may not yet have been noticed.</p>

<p>The tools we reach for  -  the Conners scales, the SNAP-IV, the Vanderbilt  -  are rating instruments, not diagnostic instruments. They quantify what observers see. They are normed on populations that skew toward older children and American school settings, and their sensitivity/specificity at age five to six is meaningfully lower than at age eight to ten.<sup>2</sup> The AAP recommends evaluation from age four; the Royal Australasian College of Physicians has no formal lower age limit but advises caution before age six precisely because developmental range is so wide.<sup>3</sup></p>

<p>What I want to know, and what no rating scale tells me cleanly: is this a child whose neurodevelopmental trajectory is diverging from peers in a durable way, or is this a child at the energetic tail of the normal distribution who will regulate better by age eight with a bit of support? I can't answer that with certainty in one consultation. I can take a thorough history, I can apply validated tools, and I can refer  -  and then hold the uncertainty with the family, which is itself a clinical act.</p>

<h2>What's actually happening in the brain</h2>

<p>This is the part I find myself wanting to understand properly rather than summarise glibly. The popular version  -  "ADHD is a dopamine deficiency"  -  is a useful handle that is also not quite right.</p>

<h3>Structure</h3>

<p>Neuroimaging studies, including the large ABCD cohort and meta-analyses of structural MRI data, consistently show that children with ADHD have, on average, smaller total brain volume, with the most reliable differences in the caudate nucleus, putamen, and cerebellar vermis.<sup>4</sup> The prefrontal cortex (PFC)  -  the region most associated with executive function, working memory, and response inhibition  -  shows both volumetric reduction and, crucially, delayed cortical maturation.<sup>5</sup></p>

<p>That last point matters more than the volume difference. Shaw and colleagues at the NIMH showed that peak cortical thickness in children with ADHD lags behind typically-developing peers by an average of three years  -  the median age of attaining peak thickness in the prefrontal cortex was 10.5 years in ADHD versus 7.5 years in controls.<sup>5</sup> This reframes ADHD not as a fixed deficit but as a maturational delay. Some of these children do, in fact, catch up. The subset who don't are the ones more likely to carry an adult diagnosis.</p>

<h3>Circuits</h3>

<p>The structural findings map onto two partially separable functional circuits, both of which are implicated in ADHD:<sup>6</sup></p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>The fronto-striatal circuit</strong>  -  prefrontal cortex → striatum (caudate, putamen) → thalamus → back to PFC. This is the "executive control" loop. It governs working memory, planning, inhibitory control, and the ability to delay gratification. Dopamine is the primary neuromodulator. When this circuit underperforms, you get the inattentive, disorganised, easily derailed child.</li>
  <li><strong>The fronto-cerebellar circuit</strong>  -  PFC → cerebellum → thalamus → PFC. This loop is involved in timing, prediction, and the regulation of motor responses. Cerebellar volume reduction in ADHD is one of the more replicable structural findings, and connects to the motoric impulsivity  -  the blurting, the interrupting, the inability to wait one's turn.</li>
</ol>

<p>A third circuit deserves mention: the default mode network (DMN), the set of regions active at rest and during self-referential thought. In typical cognition, the DMN suppresses when a task demands external attention. In ADHD, this suppression is impaired  -  the DMN "intrudes" during attention tasks, which is observable on fMRI as mind-wandering, task-unrelated thought, and the phenomenon parents describe as "he just drifts off".<sup>7</sup></p>

<h3>Neurotransmitters</h3>

<p>Dopamine is the central character. But the story isn't simple depletion  -  it's about signalling dynamics in specific circuits.</p>

<p>Dopamine modulates PFC function through a narrow inverted-U dose-response curve first described in primate work by Arnsten and colleagues.<sup>8</sup> Too little dopamine at PFC D1 receptors → poor signal-to-noise, working memory fails. Too much → the same result. The PFC is finicky. ADHD is associated with a leftward shift on this curve  -  baseline dopamine tone in the PFC is insufficient to maintain the signal-to-noise ratio needed for sustained attention and response inhibition.</p>

<p>In the striatum, the relevant mechanism is different. The dopamine reward prediction error signal  -  the "this is better than expected" burst from the ventral tegmental area  -  is blunted or mistimed in ADHD.<sup>9</sup> This is why children with ADHD can sustain extraordinary attention for immediately rewarding activities (Minecraft, Lego, anything with instant feedback) but cannot sustain attention for tasks with deferred reward (worksheets, listening, waiting). It's not a global attention deficit. It's an attention system that responds normally  -  or even hyperactively  -  to immediate reward, and poorly to delayed reward. This is the "delay aversion" model of Sonuga-Barke, and it explains more of the phenomenology than a simple arousal deficit model does.<sup>10</sup></p>

<p>Norepinephrine (noradrenaline) acts on the PFC through α2A receptors and has a complementary role to dopamine in maintaining working memory networks. It modulates the "signal" side of the equation while dopamine modulates the "noise". Drugs that act on norepinephrine systems  -  atomoxetine, guanfacine  -  improve ADHD symptoms partly through this mechanism, independently of dopamine.<sup>11</sup></p>

<h3>Genetics</h3>

<p>ADHD is one of the most heritable psychiatric conditions we know of, with twin studies consistently returning heritability estimates of 70–80%.<sup>12</sup> It is not a single-gene disorder; GWAS studies have implicated dozens of common variants, each with small effect sizes, in genes involved in dopamine signalling, synaptic scaffolding, and neuronal migration.<sup>13</sup> When a parent sits across from me and says "his father was just like this at school," they are usually right in a biologically meaningful sense.</p>

<h2>The pharmacology</h2>

<h3>Why stimulants work</h3>

<p>Methylphenidate and dexamfetamine are both stimulants, but their primary mechanisms differ.</p>

<p><strong>Methylphenidate</strong> (Ritalin, Rubifen, Concerta) is a reuptake inhibitor. It blocks the dopamine transporter (DAT) and, to a lesser degree, the norepinephrine transporter (NET), increasing synaptic availability of both monoamines.<sup>14</sup> In the PFC, this rightward shift on the inverted-U curve brings dopamine tone into the effective range for signal-to-noise modulation. In the striatum, it augments the reward prediction error signal, making deferred rewards relatively more salient.</p>

<p>The paradox  -  that a stimulant calms a hyperactive child  -  resolves when you understand the mechanism. The stimulant is not globally suppressing activity; it is improving the signal-to-noise ratio in circuits that were underperforming, allowing the PFC to do its inhibitory work on the motor and limbic systems. The child is not sedated. They are, for the first time, able to apply the brakes they already had.</p>

<p>In NZ, methylphenidate is the funded first-line agent for children with ADHD, initiated by a paediatrician or psychiatrist (GPs cannot initiate, but can prescribe under a shared care arrangement).<sup>15</sup> It requires a Class B controlled drug prescription. Short-acting forms (Ritalin, Rubifen) give three to five hours of effect; long-acting forms (Concerta OROS, Rubifen LA) give eight to twelve hours, covering the school day without a lunchtime dose.</p>

<p>Common side effects  -  reduced appetite, delayed sleep onset, mild growth attenuation with long-term use  -  are real and require monitoring. The growth effect is modest (roughly 1–2 cm over two to three years in some studies) and appears to attenuate with time; drug holidays over summer are sometimes used, though evidence for their superiority over continuous treatment on growth outcomes is mixed.<sup>16</sup></p>

<p><strong>Dexamfetamine</strong> (dexamphetamine) works differently. It is a substrate for DAT and NET  -  it enters the presynaptic terminal and causes active reverse transport of dopamine and norepinephrine into the synapse, independent of vesicular release.<sup>14</sup> It also inhibits monoamine oxidase. The net effect is a larger and more sustained increase in synaptic monoamines than methylphenidate produces. It is more potent mg for mg, has a longer half-life, and the effect feels qualitatively different to many patients  -  smoother onset, longer tail.</p>

<p>In NZ, dexamfetamine is funded as a second-line agent, typically when methylphenidate has failed or caused intolerable side effects. It is also a Class B controlled drug. Because of its higher abuse potential and longer duration, prescribing is more tightly monitored. For some children  -  particularly those with comorbid anxiety where methylphenidate's noradrenergic effects are too activating  -  dexamfetamine is paradoxically better tolerated.</p>

<h3>The non-stimulant options</h3>

<p><strong>Atomoxetine</strong> (Strattera) is a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor  -  the same mechanism as some antidepressants, but its therapeutic action in ADHD is primarily via NET inhibition in the PFC, increasing norepinephrine tone and, through downstream effects, prefrontal dopamine as well.<sup>11</sup> It is not a controlled drug. It takes four to six weeks to reach full effect (unlike stimulants, which work the same day). It does not cause rebound effects at the end of the day and does not impair sleep onset. It is particularly useful when: there is comorbid anxiety, there are tic disorders, there are substance misuse concerns (in older adolescents), or the family is strongly averse to controlled drugs.</p>

<p>In NZ, atomoxetine is funded but remains second-line. A small number of children respond better to it than to either stimulant; predicting who those children are in advance is not yet possible. Hepatotoxicity is a rare but real risk requiring monitoring; suicidality is listed as a warning (class effect from antidepressant trials), though the absolute risk in ADHD populations appears low.<sup>17</sup></p>

<p><strong>Guanfacine extended-release</strong> (Intuniv) is an α2A adrenoceptor agonist. It strengthens the norepinephrine signal in PFC networks directly  -  the same receptor that Arnsten's primate work identified as critical for working memory maintenance.<sup>8</sup> It is not funded in NZ as of this writing and requires Special Authority or private prescription. It is sedating (particularly on initiation), reduces blood pressure, and requires ECG monitoring. Its niche is as an adjunct to stimulants in children who have a partial response, or as monotherapy when stimulants are contraindicated. For children with comorbid tic disorders or oppositional symptoms, it has a reasonable evidence base.<sup>18</sup></p>

<h2>Back to the boy in the room</h2>

<p>He is six. He cannot yet have been assessed by a paediatrician, because the wait in our DHB catchment is four to six months. His mother wants to know if she should push for a diagnosis, or wait and see.</p>

<p>I tell her what I actually think: that the behaviour she is describing is consistent with ADHD, that six is young but not too young to evaluate, that a diagnosis  -  if it comes  -  is not a life sentence but a map, and that medication is neither the first step nor necessarily the destination. I refer to Community Paediatrics. I give her the SNAP-IV to take to the teacher. I talk about what the school can put in place under their Universal Design for Learning obligations without waiting for a diagnosis.</p>

<p>And I note, for myself, that the science here is actually quite beautiful  -  a prefrontal cortex that is three years behind the clock, a dopamine signal that fires perfectly for Minecraft and poorly for waiting in line, a child who is not broken but whose reward architecture is tuned to a frequency the classroom doesn't broadcast on.</p>

<p>He'll probably be fine. I want to be honest about how much of that is evidence and how much is clinical intuition. It's some of both.</p>
