The conversation that should have happened didn't, and now the patient is in ICU intubated, the family is divided, the partner is sure she wouldn't have wanted this, the daughter is sure she would. Nobody has the document. The patient cannot be asked.

Some version of this scene plays out in NZ hospitals every week. The work to prevent it is done  -  when it is done at all  -  months or years earlier, in primary care, in conversations that take time we don't have, with patients who often don't want to be having them, in a system that doesn't reward them.

Advance care planning is one of the things general practice is uniquely placed to do, and one of the things general practice consistently fails to do at scale. The reasons are structural as much as clinical, but the conversation itself, when it happens well, is durable and consequential. This piece is about what advance care planning looks like in NZ in 2026  -  the empirical case, the legal framework, the cultural considerations, and what the consult actually involves.

## Part I  -  Why it matters

The case for advance care planning is empirical, relational, and ethical, and the strongest version threads all three.

The empirical case rests substantially on the Detering 2010 BMJ randomised controlled trial  -  309 medical inpatients aged 80 or over at Austin Health in Melbourne, randomised to facilitated advance care planning or usual care, followed for six months or until death [1]. The intervention group's wishes were much more likely to be known and followed at end of life. Bereaved families in the intervention group experienced significantly less anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress in the months following the patient's death [1]. The effect sizes were not subtle. Subsequent systematic reviews and meta-analyses across multiple healthcare contexts have largely confirmed the direction of these findings: ACP improves concordance between patient wishes and care received, reduces unwanted intensive treatment at end of life, and reduces psychological morbidity in surviving family [2].

The relational case is the part that the empirical literature can't fully capture. People who have explicit conversations with their families about what they would and wouldn't want, recorded in some form, leave their families with something. People who don't, leave their families with the work of guessing  -  and the lifelong uncertainty about whether they got the guess right. The 82-year-old whose adult children are still arguing seven years after her death about whether they should have agreed to the second cycle of chemotherapy is a recognisable figure. So is the partner who, eighteen months after deciding to withdraw the ventilator, is still wondering.

The ethical case is the simplest. Patients are entitled to have their preferences known and respected. The system cannot respect preferences it doesn't know about. The conversation is the mechanism.

None of this means ACP solves end-of-life care. It doesn't. It changes a small but consequential portion of clinical and family experience around death, in ways that the evidence shows are predominantly beneficial. That is sufficient justification for the work.

## Part II  -  The legal architecture

NZ ACP sits within a layered legal framework. The key pieces, briefly.

**The Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights** (the Code), enforced by the Health and Disability Commissioner, applies to all health and disability services and gives patients explicit rights to information, informed consent, and refusal of treatment [3]. The Code is the foundational rights framework for any health interaction in NZ, including ACP discussions and documentation.

**The Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988** (PPPR Act) is the central statute for substitute decision-making when a person loses capacity. It establishes the framework for Enduring Powers of Attorney (EPA), which come in two forms: EPA for property, and EPA for personal care and welfare (PCW) [4]. The personal care and welfare EPA is the relevant one for medical decision-making. It can be activated only when the person has been certified as lacking capacity for the relevant decision, and it requires specific formal documentation (signed and witnessed in prescribed form).

A point that is widely misunderstood: an EPA-PCW attorney **cannot refuse standard medical treatment intended to save life or prevent serious damage to health** [4,5]. They cannot, for example, refuse CPR on behalf of the person. The attorney's role is to make decisions consistent with the person's wishes and best interests within the bounds the Act sets, not to override the clinical judgement that life-saving treatment is appropriate.

**Advance directives** are different from an EPA. An advance directive is a written or oral instruction made by a competent person about the future treatment they want or don't want. They are recognised under right 7(5) of the Code [3] and are binding on health practitioners, provided the directive applies to the situation that has actually arisen, the person had capacity when they made it, the directive was made of free will, and the person was adequately informed at the time. The threshold for treating an advance directive as binding is therefore not trivial  -  it has to fit the situation closely enough that the clinician can be confident the patient meant *this* situation.

**The End of Life Choice Act 2019**, which came into force on 7 November 2021, established assisted dying as a legal option in NZ for eligible patients [6]. The eligibility criteria are tight: NZ citizenship or permanent residence, age 18 or over, terminal illness with prognosis of less than six months, advanced irreversible physical decline, unbearable suffering not relievable in a tolerable manner, and capacity to make an informed decision [6]. Crucially for primary care, **section 8 of the Act prohibits health practitioners from initiating discussion of assisted dying** with a patient  -  the patient must raise it first [6]. Conscientious objection is protected, and a practitioner who objects must inform the patient of the SCENZ Group as the route to access the service.

This last point matters for ACP conversations. A patient with terminal cancer who is engaging in advance care planning may want to discuss assisted dying as one option among others. Under section 8, the GP can respond fully if the patient raises it, but cannot raise it themselves. The legal restriction sits awkwardly alongside the clinical reality that ACP conversations naturally cover the full range of end-of-life options. The 2024 review of the End of Life Choice Act considered this issue without recommending change to section 8 [7].

## Part III  -  The cultural dimension

ACP frameworks designed for individual-autonomy-based decision-making translate imperfectly to cultural contexts where end-of-life decisions are properly held by whānau, by collective, or by spiritual frameworks the framework doesn't acknowledge.

The Te Whare Tapa Whā model, developed by Sir Mason Durie, frames health as a balance of four dimensions  -  taha tinana (physical), taha hinengaro (mental and emotional), taha whānau (family and social), and taha wairua (spiritual) [8]. The framework is widely used across NZ health services and is the standard reference for thinking about Māori health holistically. For ACP, the implication is that a conversation focused narrowly on physical-treatment preferences misses three of the four dimensions that the patient and whānau may understand to be central.

Several practical points follow.

**Whānau is often the decision-making unit, not the individual.** ACP conversations that assume the patient will be making decisions in isolation may misread how the family expects the conversation to go. Inviting whānau to be part of the discussion  -  at the patient's invitation, with the patient's permission  -  is often not just culturally appropriate but clinically more accurate, because the people who will actually be present at the bedside are the people who need to have heard the conversation.

**Tikanga around death matters.** Specific cultural protocols around death, dying, the body, karakia, and tangihanga vary by iwi and by family. Asking what tikanga is important to the patient and their whānau is part of the ACP conversation, not separate from it. This isn't about the GP being expert in tikanga  -  it's about leaving space for the patient to identify what matters and ensuring that what matters gets recorded.

**Wairua is part of the medical conversation.** A patient who tells you that being able to die at home, near their marae, with their whānau and karakia present, matters more than another six weeks of life on chemotherapy is making a clinical decision in a wairua frame. The framework that thinks of this as "non-medical" is the wrong framework.

**Pacific cultural perspectives** also vary substantially across Tongan, Samoan, Niuean, Cook Islands Māori, Tokelauan, Fijian, and other Pacific communities, and similarly tend to centre family and faith in end-of-life decision-making. Generic ACP templates designed around an individualist framework sit awkwardly with these contexts as well.

The clinical implication is not that ACP conversations need to be reinvented for each cultural context  -  the underlying ethical work (knowing what matters to the patient, ensuring this is recorded, preventing the bad death) is universal. It is that the framework around the conversation needs to be flexible enough to hold the things that matter to the patient and their whānau, in their language, in their frame.

## Part IV  -  The clinical practice

The Te Whatu Ora ACP programme (which moved from HQSC to Te Whatu Ora in July 2024) provides national resources, training, and the standardised ACP document at myacp.org.nz [9]. The programme materials are usable, but the clinical practice is the work.

**When to start.** Earlier than feels comfortable. The standard prompt  -  when you would not be surprised if the patient died within the next twelve months  -  captures the patients who most need an ACP, but in practice, advance care planning works better when initiated earlier in a chronic illness trajectory, before the conversation is freighted with the proximity of death. Patients with new diagnoses of advanced heart failure, COPD, cirrhosis, dementia, or metastatic cancer should be offered the opportunity to think about an ACP, not at the diagnostic appointment but in a planned follow-up. Patients in their seventies and eighties without specific terminal diagnoses also benefit from earlier rather than later conversations.

**Who initiates.** General practice is the natural setting because of the longitudinal relationship, but the conversation often gets initiated by the people the patient has had the most recent significant clinical encounter with  -  the cardiologist after a heart failure admission, the oncologist after a treatment plan discussion, the geriatrician after a fall. The relationship-based work in primary care is most useful when it follows up and integrates these earlier conversations rather than starting from scratch each time.

**How to start.** A useful framing is "I'd like to talk about what would matter to you if you got more unwell  -  both so I know how to look after you, and so your family knows your wishes if there's ever a time when you can't tell them." This is less confronting than "I want to discuss your end-of-life care" and tends to open the conversation more easily. The structure of the actual document then provides a useful scaffold: what the patient understands about their condition, what matters most to them, what they would and wouldn't want, who they want to speak for them.

**The substrate.** The ACP document recorded on paper or in a practice management system is necessary but not sufficient. The conversation itself, witnessed by family and clinical team, is what produces durable understanding. The document records the outcome; the conversation produces the alignment. Both matter.

**The documentation problem.** This is the structural weak point. ACP documents recorded in primary care don't reliably reach hospital systems, ED systems, or after-hours services. ED clinicians at 2am with a deteriorating patient often don't have access to the ACP document the patient has at home. Health NZ has been working toward a national shared digital record, but the integration is incomplete. Until it is, GPs need to ensure that the document goes home with the patient (and their whānau), is sent to the relevant specialty teams, is uploaded into hospital systems where possible, and is reviewed and reconfirmed periodically  -  not just filed and forgotten.

**Reviewing and updating.** ACPs should be reviewed when circumstances change: new diagnosis, change in functional status, transition to residential care, after each significant hospital admission. A 75-year-old's preferences in 2020, before her dementia and her partner's death, may not be the preferences her 80-year-old self would now express. The static document is useful; the regular review keeps it accurate.

## Part V  -  The CPR conversation

This is the conversation most people associate with ACP, and it is also the one that is most often handled badly.

Two errors are common. The first is collapsing all of advance care planning into the CPR question. Whether to attempt CPR is one decision in a much wider field of decisions about treatment goals, place of care, and what matters most. Asking "do you want CPR?" out of the blue, without the broader conversation, produces answers that don't mean what they appear to mean. Patients agreeing to CPR are often agreeing to "anything that might help"; patients declining CPR are sometimes declining "being kept alive on machines"  -  neither is the actual question.

The second error is framing CPR as an option of equivalent likelihood of benefit to other interventions. Survival to hospital discharge after in-hospital CPR is in the order of 15–25% in unselected adult populations and substantially lower in older patients with multiple comorbidities, frailty, or terminal illness. For specific patient groups  -  advanced metastatic cancer, end-stage heart failure, severe frailty  -  meaningful survival with restoration of function is rare. The conversation that frames CPR as a routine option, rather than a specific intervention with specific likely outcomes for *this* patient, gives the patient an inaccurate picture of what they are choosing.

A more honest version is: "If your heart stops, we can try to restart it with chest compressions and shocks. For a healthy person, that works perhaps a quarter of the time. For someone in your situation, the chance of it working in a way that means you wake up and go home doing the things you currently do is much smaller  -  maybe 5% or less. And the process itself involves rib fractures, a breathing tube, and ICU. Knowing that, what would you want?" This is harder to say than "do you want CPR if your heart stops?", but it is more accurate to what is actually being decided.

The "Allow Natural Death" framing  -  increasingly used in some NZ services as an alternative to "Do Not Attempt CPR"  -  is part of the same shift. The framing matters because the language matters. "Do not attempt CPR" sounds like withholding; "Allow natural death" sounds like permitting. The clinical decision is the same; the conversation about it lands differently.

## A working position

A few things I think are defensible.

**Start ACP conversations earlier than you think you should.** The patient with advanced heart failure who is still cognitively intact and clinically stable is the patient with whom the conversation will work best. The same patient three months later, mid-decompensation, in hospital, is the patient with whom it will not.

**Document specifically, but don't fetishise the document.** The ACP form is a record of a conversation, not a substitute for it. The conversation is the durable goods.

**Bring whānau in if the patient wants it.** The clinical work that gets done is more accurate, the family work that follows the death is easier, and the cultural work is less likely to be done badly.

**Don't let the CPR question swallow the rest of the conversation.** It's one decision in a much broader field. Treat it accordingly.

**Don't initiate discussion of assisted dying.** Section 8 of the End of Life Choice Act is clear, and the prohibition is binding on conscientious-objector and non-objector practitioners alike. If the patient raises it, you can engage fully. If they don't, you can't be the one to bring it up, even if you suspect it might be relevant to their situation.

**Review periodically.** The ACP from three years ago may not be the ACP that fits the patient's current situation. Make a habit of asking, every twelve to eighteen months, whether the document still reflects what the person wants.

The conversation that should have happened is the conversation that prevents the scene at the start of this piece  -  the patient in ICU, the family divided, the document not present, the wishes unknown. Each conversation that does happen is, in some small way, a future scene that doesn't.

That, I think, is what the work amounts to.
