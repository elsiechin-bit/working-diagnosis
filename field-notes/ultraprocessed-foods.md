The term *ultraprocessed* entered the nutrition lexicon in 2009 when Brazilian epidemiologist Carlos Monteiro's group proposed the NOVA classification - a system that groups foods by the degree and purpose of industrial processing applied to them, rather than by nutrient content.[1] It was a deliberate provocation against reductionist nutrient-by-nutrient thinking, and it has been quietly reshaping how researchers frame dietary risk ever since.

## What NOVA actually says

NOVA divides foods into four groups.

**Group 1 - Unprocessed or minimally processed foods.** Whole fruit, vegetables, plain meat, eggs, milk, legumes, plain nuts. The benchmark.

**Group 2 - Processed culinary ingredients.** Oils, butter, flour, sugar, salt - things extracted from Group 1 foods and used in cooking. Not meant to be eaten alone.

**Group 3 - Processed foods.** Products made by combining Group 1 and Group 2 items: cheese, cured meats, tinned fish, freshly baked bread, beer. Processing here is primarily for preservation or flavour.

**Group 4 - Ultraprocessed foods (UPFs).** Industrial formulations that contain ingredients rarely or never found in domestic kitchens - emulsifiers, stabilisers, flavour enhancers, artificial sweeteners, colourings, modified starches, hydrogenated oils. The defining feature is that the product is assembled from fractionated food components - ingredients rarely or never used in domestic cooking - usually to make it hyper-palatable, cheap, shelf-stable, and convenient.[1]

Familiar UPFs: packaged bread and buns, breakfast cereals, reconstituted meat products, flavoured yoghurts, instant noodles, soft drinks, mass-market confectionery, most fast food, many plant-based meat alternatives.

The classification has real-world friction. Some wholesome-seeming products (plain wholegrain bread from a supermarket) land in Group 4 because of emulsifiers; some intuitively "junk" items (artisan salami) land in Group 3. This is a feature, not a bug - NOVA is trying to capture industrial processing as an exposure in its own right, separate from nutrient density.

## The epidemiological signal

The association between high UPF intake and health outcomes is now one of the most replicated findings in nutritional epidemiology. A 2024 umbrella review in the *British Medical Journal* synthesised 45 pooled meta-analyses across 9,888,373 participants and found higher UPF consumption associated with greater risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, anxiety, depression, obesity, and several cancers - including colorectal cancer and breast cancer.[2]

The signal is dose-dependent in most analyses. The NutriNet-Santé cohort found a 10% increase in the proportion of UPFs in the diet was associated with a 12% increase in cancer risk overall and an 11% increase in breast cancer risk.[3] A large UK Biobank analysis found each 10% increment in UPF intake associated with a 6% higher risk of all-cause mortality and a 9% higher risk of mental health multimorbidity.[4,5]

In New Zealand, UPFs are estimated to contribute around 45-50% of daily energy intake in adults - comparable to Australia and the United Kingdom, and substantially higher than in Spain, France, or Brazil.[6]

## What we don't yet know

Observational epidemiology can establish association; it cannot resolve mechanism. Three main candidates are debated.

**Nutrient displacement.** People who eat more UPFs eat less fibre, less whole fruit and vegetables, less protein from unprocessed sources. Much of the observed risk may simply reflect what UPFs are crowding out.[7] Adjust for nutrient intake and the associations attenuate - but do not disappear - in most analyses.

**Additives.** Emulsifiers (particularly carboxymethylcellulose and polysorbate-80) disrupt the intestinal mucus layer and alter gut microbiota composition in animal models.[8] Human data are emerging but limited. Artificial sweeteners have been linked to cardiovascular events in some cohort data, though the field is contested.[9]

**Hyper-palatability and eating rate.** The only randomised controlled trial to date - Hall et al. at the NIH, 2019 - randomised 20 adults to ad libitum UPF or unprocessed diet for two weeks, crossed over. Participants on the UPF diet consumed approximately 500 kcal/day more and gained roughly 0.9 kg; those on the unprocessed diet lost similar weight. The diets were matched for offered macronutrients and fibre. The authors suggested eating rate and texture as mediators - UPFs are engineered to be eaten fast.[10] The trial is small and short, but it is the best causal evidence we have.

The honest answer is that mechanism is probably multifactorial, and the NOVA classification may be capturing several different exposures simultaneously. That is both its strength (it predicts harm) and its limitation (it cannot tell us which component to target).

## Classification challenges in practice

NOVA is researcher-facing, not patient-facing. Asking patients to classify their foods into four groups based on industrial processing is not a practical consultation strategy. Some pragmatic translations:

- If the ingredient list includes things you wouldn't find in a home kitchen, it's probably a UPF.
- If it has more than five or six ingredients, check for emulsifiers, stabilisers, and flavour enhancers.
- Packaging designed to signal healthfulness (high protein, low fat, added fibre) is often applied to UPFs - the label does not redeem the product.

## What to say in the consultation

The evidence licences a clear general message: diets with less UPF content are associated with better outcomes across multiple domains, and the only RCT suggests the relationship is at least partly causal. You do not need to invoke mechanism to say that.

Practical framing that tends to land well: *"The more of your diet that comes from food you or someone you know actually cooked, the better - that's probably the single most robust dietary finding of the last decade."*

Avoid demonising specific products; focus on pattern. A supermarket bun is not poison; a diet where most meals come from packets and drive-throughs has a real signal attached to it. Acknowledge the structural drivers: UPFs are cheap, convenient, heavily marketed, and engineered to be satisfying. This is an environment problem as well as a choice problem.

For patients with high UPF intake, a useful low-friction swap is cooking in batches once or twice weekly - reducing reliance on convenience foods without requiring daily effort.

For patients in lower socioeconomic circumstances, be careful not to frame this as a simple choice. Tinned tomatoes, dried legumes, frozen vegetables, oats, and eggs are cheap, minimally processed, and nutritionally excellent. A practical intervention is helping people identify the Group 1 and Group 3 foods they can already afford, rather than implying they need to shop at a farmers market.

## The broader picture

UPF research sits within a longer tension in nutritional science between reductionism (nutrients) and wholism (food patterns). The NOVA framework is the most coherent articulation of the wholism position to date, and the epidemiological evidence behind it is now substantial enough that the World Health Organization, the Pan American Health Organization, and several national dietary guidelines have begun incorporating UPF language.[11] New Zealand's own dietary guidelines have not yet adopted NOVA explicitly, but the 2020 Eating and Activity Guidelines align with its principles in their emphasis on whole and minimally processed foods.[12]

It is also worth noting what UPF research does not say. It does not say that food additives are acutely toxic. It does not say that occasional convenience food causes harm. It does not licence orthorexic anxiety about ingredient lists. The signal is about pattern and proportion - and that is how it should be communicated.
