There is a version of this conversation that goes badly in a specific way. A patient  -  or, more often, a family  -  hears "Do Not Attempt Resuscitation" and understands it to mean "we are stopping treating them." They hear it as withdrawal. They understand it as abandonment. They push back, sometimes forcefully, because they love the person and they don't understand why the doctor is writing an order to let them die.

The confusion is neither irrational nor unusual, and it points to the central communicative problem with resuscitation orders: the terminology frames the decision as something being withheld from the patient  -  resuscitation, an apparently straightforward good  -  rather than as what it actually is, which is a clinical judgement about whether a particular intervention is likely to help a particular person, and a record that the patient's preferences have been heard. Getting the clinical reality of CPR right, before any conversation about orders occurs, is the prerequisite for having the conversation well.

This note is about resuscitation orders specifically  -  their legal basis in NZ, the practical architecture in community and hospital settings, the ceiling-of-care framework, the documentation gaps, and where the common clinical and ethical errors are. It complements, rather than repeats, the ACP note on this site; the broader advance care planning context is dealt with there.

## Part I  -  What CPR actually involves

The case for being clinically precise about CPR before discussing resuscitation orders is that the conversation is only meaningful if both parties have an accurate model of what they are deciding about.

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation as delivered in practice involves chest compressions at 100-120 per minute to depth of approximately 5-6 centimetres  -  enough to fracture ribs in a significant proportion of adult patients, particularly older patients with reduced bone density [1]. Airway management typically involves either bag-mask ventilation or definitive airway (endotracheal intubation). Cardiac rhythm is monitored and, where shockable rhythms are present (ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia), defibrillation is delivered. Advanced life support medications  -  adrenaline, amiodarone  -  are administered. The process is physically violent relative to the expected experience of dying.

The outcome data depend substantially on context. For in-hospital cardiac arrest in unselected adult populations, survival to hospital discharge is approximately 15-20% in well-resourced systems, though with substantial variation by rhythm (shockable rhythms have substantially better outcomes than non-shockable), by setting (monitored ICU patients versus general ward), and by the patient's underlying health [2]. For out-of-hospital cardiac arrest, survival to hospital discharge in NZ and Australian data is in the range of 8-12%, again with substantial variation by rhythm, bystander CPR, and response time [3].

For the patient groups in whom resuscitation orders are most commonly relevant in general practice  -  patients with advanced cancer, end-stage organ failure, significant frailty, or advanced dementia  -  the outcomes are materially worse. Studies of CPR in patients with metastatic cancer consistently report survival to discharge in single figures, often below 5%, and studies of frail elderly patients show comparable results [4,5]. The probability that a person with, say, end-stage heart failure and severe frailty will survive CPR to hospital discharge in a neurologically intact state is very low  -  not zero, but low enough that a decision to forego CPR in such a patient is a clinical judgement with a strong evidence base, not an arbitrary withdrawal of care.

The reason to be precise about this is not to make CPR sound hopeless in all circumstances  -  it is not, and its use in previously healthy patients who arrest from reversible causes is genuinely life-saving. The reason is that the population of patients in whom GPs are writing DNACPR orders in community practice is predominantly the population in whom survival to meaningful recovery is unlikely, and the conversation about resuscitation needs to be grounded in what is actually probable for *this* patient, not in population-level averages abstracted from their clinical situation.

## Part II  -  The terminology problem

The vocabulary of resuscitation decisions has not kept pace with the ethical thinking, and the terminology is doing active harm in some clinical settings.

**"Do Not Resuscitate" (DNR)**  -  the older term  -  implies that resuscitation is being withheld from a patient who would otherwise receive it. The framing is withdrawal.

**"Do Not Attempt CPR" (DNACPR)**  -  the current preferred NZ and UK term  -  is more accurate: it captures the "attempt" language (CPR is an attempt, not a guarantee) and specifies that the decision is about CPR specifically, not about all treatment [6]. The addition of "attempt" matters because it shifts the framing from withholding to accurate representation of what CPR is  -  an intervention with a probability of success that, in this patient, is low.

**"Allow Natural Death" (AND)**  -  increasingly used in some NZ services and in the US  -  frames the decision differently again, as permitting a natural process rather than withholding an intervention. Families tend to find this framing less confronting. Whether "Allow Natural Death" accurately represents what is happening when DNACPR is written alongside active disease management  -  chemotherapy, insulin, dialysis  -  is contested; some clinicians consider the framing misleading where the patient is receiving active treatment.

**"Ceiling of care"** is the most clinically useful frame, though it is not a form or a specific order. It describes the upper limit of escalation that is in keeping with a patient's goals and the clinical realities: not for CPR, not for mechanical ventilation, not for ICU admission, and so on. The ceiling frame makes explicit that DNACPR is one point on a spectrum of decisions about escalation, not a binary that separates treated patients from untreated ones.

The practical implication for clinical communication: **"DNACPR" should never be introduced without explicit clarification of what it does not mean**. It does not mean no treatment, no antibiotics, no pain relief, no hospital admission, no engagement. The failure to say this clearly and proactively is one of the most consistent communication errors in this area.

## Part III  -  The legal architecture in New Zealand

The legal basis for resuscitation decisions in NZ is layered across statute, common law, and regulatory instruments.

The foundational principle is patient autonomy under the **Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights** [7]. A competent patient's decision to refuse CPR  -  or any other treatment  -  is legally binding on the clinical team and must be respected, irrespective of the clinical team's view of the decision's wisdom. Right 7(2) and 7(3) of the Code are clear: consumers may refuse services, including services that may save or prolong life.

Where a patient has lost decision-making capacity, the decision rests with the clinical team, informed by the patient's previously expressed wishes and the views of family and whānau. An advance directive specifying refusal of CPR is binding on the clinical team, provided it applies to the circumstances that have actually arisen and the patient had capacity at the time it was made [7]. An advance directive cannot be overridden by family members, EPA-PCW attorneys, or clinical disagreement  -  it represents the patient's own exercise of their right to refuse, operative from the moment they lost capacity to express it directly.

**Enduring Power of Attorney for personal care and welfare (EPA-PCW)** creates a substitute decision-maker for personal welfare decisions when the patient lacks capacity. However, the EPA-PCW attorney cannot require the clinical team to provide treatment the team considers futile or contrary to the patient's best interests. The Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988 [8] specifies that the attorney's function is to make decisions *consistent with the person's wishes and best interests*, not to override clinical judgement about what treatment is appropriate. A family member who is also the EPA attorney cannot compel the clinical team to attempt CPR if the team has documented that CPR would not be in the patient's best interests.

Where there is no advance directive and the patient lacks capacity, the clinical team makes the determination in the patient's best interests, with appropriate consultation with family or whānau. The team is not required to provide treatment it judges to be futile, but the decision and the reasoning for it should be clearly documented.

A legal point that generates more uncertainty than it should in practice: **there is no requirement in NZ law for family consent to a DNACPR order**. Families must be engaged  -  ethically, practically, and in terms of good clinical communication  -  but they do not hold a legal veto over a DNACPR order written for a patient who lacks capacity. The conflation of "family consultation" with "family consent" is a significant source of clinical confusion, and one that can result in DNACPR orders not being written in circumstances where they clearly should be, because a family member has objected and the clinical team is uncertain of their legal position.

## Part IV  -  Community DNACPR in NZ: the practical gap

Hospital DNACPR orders sit within a documented system: the order is in the chart, nursing staff know it exists, the on-call team is aware, the MET team won't be called. The system is imperfect but functional.

Community DNACPR orders sit within a substantially more fragmented system, and the fragmentation creates real clinical problems.

When a patient arrests at home and someone calls 111, **St John Ambulance will attend and will attempt resuscitation by default** unless there is a valid DNACPR order that the attending crew can verify [9]. A DNACPR order filed in the GP practice management system is not visible to an ambulance crew responding at 2am. A DNACPR conversation that has been documented in a clinic note but not communicated in a form the ambulance service can act on is, from the perspective of a crew standing in the patient's lounge, not an order at all.

The practical implications for GPs writing community DNACPR orders:

**The order needs to exist in a form that goes with the patient.** This means a physical document  -  the practice or DHB/HNZ DNACPR form  -  kept somewhere accessible in the home (often attached to the fridge, by widespread convention, because this is where paramedics are trained to look). The patient, family, and anyone else who might be present when the patient deteriorates needs to know the document exists and where it is.

**Communication to St John.** The Palliative Care Coordination System (PCCS), in regions where it is operational, allows notification to St John that a patient has a DNACPR order and a care plan, reducing the likelihood of an unwanted resuscitation attempt [10]. Not all regions have this. The GP should confirm what coordination mechanism exists locally and use it.

**Communication to after-hours services.** The after-hours GP or nurse who takes a call at 11pm about a deteriorating patient needs to know that a DNACPR order exists and what it says. The record in the PMS is not always accessible to after-hours providers. A care plan summary  -  physical, with the patient, or transferred to whatever system is in use  -  is the clinical standard for this kind of communication.

**Review and dating.** A DNACPR order written three years ago may not reflect the patient's current wishes or clinical situation. Community DNACPR orders should be reviewed and re-dated when clinical circumstances change significantly.

The absence of a national POLST-equivalent framework in NZ  -  a portable medical order that travels with the patient across settings and that first responders, hospital ED, after-hours providers, and primary care teams all recognise  -  is a meaningful structural gap in the system for community end-of-life care. Several districts have developed local solutions, but there is no national standard.

## Part V  -  Ceiling of care: beyond the binary

The binary framing of resuscitation decisions  -  for CPR or not for CPR  -  is clinically inadequate for many patients, particularly those in the middle of a complex illness trajectory where the ceiling-of-care question applies to a range of interventions, not only CPR.

A more complete escalation planning framework addresses, at minimum:

**CPR.** For or not for CPR in the event of cardiac arrest. This is the DNACPR order.

**Mechanical ventilation.** For or not for intubation and mechanical ventilation. A patient may be not for CPR but for non-invasive ventilation (NIV, BiPAP) in the event of respiratory failure; alternatively, a patient may be not for ventilation of any kind while remaining for active treatment short of that.

**ICU admission.** For or not for ICU admission. The ward patient for whom ICU admission would not change the clinical trajectory  -  because their underlying disease is not reversible  -  but who is not yet at the point of purely palliative management may benefit from an explicit "ward maximum" designation.

**Hospital admission.** For some patients at the end of life, the explicit preference is not to be admitted to hospital at all  -  to remain at home, managed by community teams and family, with hospital admission only if pain or symptoms cannot be managed at home. This preference needs to be documented and communicated as explicitly as the DNACPR.

**Active disease management.** DNACPR does not mean no antibiotics, no transfusions, no intravenous fluids, no analgesia. This is the confusion most damaging to families, and the one most worth naming explicitly.

In the UK, the **ReSPECT process** (Recommended Summary Plan for Emergency Care and Treatment) attempts to address the ceiling-of-care problem with a single, portable form that captures the patient's priorities (on a spectrum from "prioritise life-prolonging treatment" to "prioritise comfort"), their CPR status, and the clinical recommendations across all emergency interventions [11]. The form is signed by clinician and patient (or, where the patient lacks capacity, clinician with family documentation), is intended to be valid across settings, and has been adopted across a significant proportion of NHS Trusts since its introduction in 2016.

In the US, **POLST** (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment, now sometimes MOLST  -  Medical Orders) is a standardised portable medical order, distinct from an advance directive, that translates patient goals into specific orders that travel with the patient [12]. Developed in Oregon in the 1990s and now used in most US states in some form, POLST covers CPR, medical interventions (full/selective/comfort only), and artificial nutrition. The evidence from POLST jurisdictions suggests it substantially improves concordance between patient wishes and care received across settings [12].

Australian states have moved toward **Goals of Care** frameworks  -  notably Victoria and Queensland  -  that use a structured designation (in Victoria: Full Active Treatment, Limited Treatment, Comfort-Focused Treatment) to capture the escalation ceiling in a single designator that travels across the hospital record and is visible to the after-hours team responding to a clinical deterioration [13].

NZ lacks a national equivalent to any of these, which is worth naming as a system-level gap rather than a failure of individual clinical practice. The HQSC has been working on national ACP infrastructure, but the portable medical order form for emergency care has not yet been standardised nationally.

## Part VI  -  Common errors

**Writing DNACPR without explaining what it doesn't mean.** The most common error, and the one most reliably corrected by explicit statement: "This form means that if your heart stops, we won't attempt to restart it. It does not change anything else about how we look after you  -  your medications, your specialist reviews, your access to hospital if you need it. Everything else stays."

**Conflating DNACPR with "comfort measures only."** These are different clinical decisions on different timescales. A patient can be not for CPR and simultaneously receiving aggressive chemotherapy, dialysis, or antiretrovirals. The failure to distinguish these decisions creates family confusion and, in some cases, the inappropriate withdrawal of active treatment following a DNACPR order.

**Treating the DNACPR conversation as a one-off.** Clinical circumstances change. A DNACPR order written in the context of a specific acute admission may not reflect the patient's wishes or clinical picture six months later. A DNACPR order written at diagnosis of a progressive disease should be reviewed at significant clinical transitions.

**Not writing the order because the family objects.** As discussed under the legal framework: family consultation is mandatory, family consent is not. A family that objects to a DNACPR order deserves a full explanation, adequate time to process, and access to support including, where appropriate, a clinical ethics consultation or a second clinical opinion. They do not hold a legal veto, and a clinical team that declines to document a clinically indicated DNACPR because a family is distressed is not serving the patient.

**Assuming that a hospital DNACPR order is visible in community.** On discharge from hospital, the hospital DNACPR order does not automatically transfer to a form that St John can act on. The GP who receives a patient home from a hospital admission with a DNACPR in place needs to confirm that a community-valid, patient-accessible document exists.

**Writing DNACPR orders paternalistically, without adequate conversation.** The converse error: a DNACPR order written by a clinician without adequate engagement with a patient who has decision-making capacity, in the absence of a conversation, is an infringement of the patient's right to be informed and to refuse or consent. Even where the clinical judgement is that CPR would be futile, the conversation needs to happen if the patient has capacity. The *clinical judgement* that CPR is futile does not override the *communicative obligation* to discuss it with the patient [7].

**The "ceiling" being applied to all treatment, not just escalation.** A ward patient whose medical team has written "ceiling of care: ward maximum, DNACPR" should not be interpreted by the nursing staff as a patient for whom all active intervention is suspended. Ceiling of care applies to escalation; it is not synonymous with palliative management unless that is explicitly stated.

## Part VII  -  The conversation

Given the ACP note on this site covers the CPR conversation in detail, I will be brief and address the specific features of the DNACPR conversation in community practice that are distinctive.

**The frame matters.** "I want to talk to you about what we'd do if your heart stopped" is more clinically grounded than "I need to fill out a form." The form follows the conversation; the conversation is not a mechanism for completing the form.

**Lead with clinical realism, not probability arithmetic.** A patient with advanced heart failure and significant frailty does not benefit from being told "your survival chance with CPR is under 5%." They benefit from being told, in plain language, what CPR actually involves for a person in their situation, and what "surviving CPR" would look like  -  likely another hospital stay, likely ICU, likely no improvement in the underlying condition. The probability data support the conversation; they should not be the conversation.

**Explicitly state what doesn't change.** Every DNACPR conversation should include the words "this doesn't change anything else about your care" or their equivalent, unless the situation is genuinely one of comfort-focused management only, in which case that separate and important conversation is a different one from the DNACPR conversation.

**Involve whānau where the patient wants them involved.** Many patients, particularly Māori and Pacific patients, will want whānau present for significant clinical conversations including resuscitation discussions. Building this into the structure of the appointment is easier than managing the consequences of having had the conversation without the people who will be at the bedside.

**Document specifically.** A clinical note that says "DNACPR discussed, patient agreed" is inadequate. A clinical note should document: that the patient had decision-making capacity; what was explained; what the patient's response and expressed preferences were; who else was present; and that the DNACPR form was completed and its location communicated. This documentation level matters not only for clinical continuity but because the HDC has examined DNACPR-related complaints where the adequacy of documentation was a central issue.

## A working position

A few things I think are defensible.

**DNACPR is a clinical finding as much as a clinical conversation.** For a patient with, say, grade 4 glioblastoma, severe frailty, and end-stage COPD, the clinical judgement that CPR would not achieve the goals of care is an evidence-based determination. The conversation with the patient is necessary and important, but it is not a conversation about whether the evidence exists  -  it is a conversation about what the evidence means for *this* patient, and what *they* want documented.

**The community-hospital DNACPR gap is a systemic problem that individual clinicians cannot fully solve.** A GP who writes a careful community DNACPR order, communicates it to the patient and family, and puts the form on the fridge, has done their part. Whether the ambulance crew sees it, whether after-hours knows it exists, and whether it is picked up on hospital admission depends on system infrastructure that does not currently work reliably in NZ. Advocacy for a national portable medical order framework is part of the professional work.

**"Not for resuscitation" is not the same as "giving up."** The implicit message that DNACPR carries  -  that the medical team is withdrawing effort  -  is the most clinically harmful misconception in this area, and correcting it explicitly and repeatedly is part of the clinical work, not a bureaucratic add-on. Every DNACPR conversation is also a commitment to the patient about what continues.

**Review.** A DNACPR order that has not been reviewed in twelve months in a patient whose clinical picture has changed is a DNACPR order that may no longer reflect either the patient's wishes or the clinical situation. Make reviewing it part of scheduled chronic disease review.

**Know your local systems.** The gap between what should happen when a patient with a community DNACPR deteriorates and what actually happens depends almost entirely on local coordination mechanisms  -  the PCCS, the after-hours handover system, the palliative care team's communication infrastructure. Knowing what those mechanisms are in your region, and using them, is part of safe practice.

The conversation that nobody wants to have is also, routinely, the conversation that patients and families look back on as the most important one the GP had with them. That is not an argument for having it badly, quickly, or bureaucratically. It is an argument for having it well, with adequate time, with clinical precision, and with a clear commitment to what continues after the form is signed.
