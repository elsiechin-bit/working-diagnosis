The fifteen-minute consult is the unit in which most NZ general practice happens. It is also a unit we rarely interrogate. Patients book it. Practice management systems are scheduled around it. PHO contracts assume it. Capitation funding is calibrated to it. Most clinical guidelines implicitly presuppose it. Almost nobody, including most GPs, has explicit views on whether it is the right unit  -  partly because nobody can remember anyone offering a coherent alternative.

It is worth interrogating. What we work in is short, by international standards. Performance frameworks built around it  -  in the UK, Australia, and now incrementally in NZ  -  measure things that are easy to measure rather than things that are clinically important. The question of what general practice work should be funded for, and what we should be expected to deliver in the time funded, is one of the most consequential questions in NZ primary care, and one of the least talked-about.

## Part I  -  How long is a primary care consultation, internationally?

The most-cited comparative work is Irving and colleagues' 2017 systematic review in BMJ Open, covering primary care consultation length data from 67 countries, 179 studies, and approximately 28.5 million consultations [1]. The range was startling: from 48 seconds in Bangladesh to 22.5 minutes in Sweden [1]. Eighteen countries, accounting for roughly half the world's population, had average primary care consultation lengths of five minutes or less [1].

The countries at the long end were predominantly Northern European: Sweden at 22.5 minutes, Bulgaria around 20, the United States and several Western European nations between 15 and 20, the Netherlands around 10. The UK at the time of the review averaged around 9-10 minutes (since extended in places to 15). NZ wasn't in the higher-quality dataset of the Irving review, but the broadly accepted figure for typical scheduled NZ general practice consultation length is around 15 minutes  -  comparable to the UK now and to standard Australian Medicare item 23 consultations, shorter than Australia's longer-item consultations, and substantially shorter than the Northern European systems.

The Irving paper found significant associations between consultation length and several variables: healthcare spending per capita (longer in higher-spending systems); rates of admission for ambulatory-sensitive conditions (shorter consultations associated with higher rates); primary care physician density (more GPs per population, longer consultations); and physician satisfaction (longer consultations, less burnout) [1]. Cross-sectional data cannot disentangle the direction of causation. But the pattern is suggestive: countries that fund primary care more generously, train and retain more GPs, and give those GPs more time per patient tend to have lower hospital admission rates for conditions general practice is supposed to manage.

Two independent lines of evidence support the same direction. The Cochrane review of interventions that change consultation length found longer consultations are associated with more discussion of preventive care, more discussion of psychosocial issues, lower antibiotic prescribing, and higher patient satisfaction [2]. Mercer and colleagues' work in deprived Scottish practices showed that consultations with adequate time for complexity were associated with substantially higher patient enablement scores  -  particularly for patients with multimorbidity, where the benefits of additional time scale roughly with the number of clinical problems [3]. The Mercer paper is worth reading in full; it is one of the cleaner demonstrations available that consultation length is a genuine clinical input, with the same standing as any other resource allocation decision.

## Part II  -  What shapes consultation length

Consultation length isn't set by the doctor. It is set by the funding model, the demand pressure, and the patient demographic.

**Funding models** are the largest single determinant. There are three main types internationally. *Fee-for-service* systems (parts of the US, much of Australia through Medicare item numbers, much of continental Europe) pay for each consultation as a discrete unit. The incentive structure favours volume  -  shorter consultations within reason, more patients seen per session  -  though Australia's tiered items (23/36/44, distinguishing standard from long from prolonged) create an explicit financial reward for spending more time. *Capitation* systems (the UK NHS, NZ since 2002, parts of Canada) pay per enrolled patient per year, regardless of contact frequency. The incentive is more subtle: more enrolled patients, fewer per-patient contacts, no per-consultation volume incentive within a given session. *Salary* systems (Sweden, Finland, parts of NHS hospital-based primary care) pay a flat rate. The incentive structure is essentially neither volume nor enrolment: time per patient is determined by professional norm, demand, and management.

The hybrid character of most real systems makes simple comparisons hard. The UK NHS is capitation-based but heavily overlaid with the Quality and Outcomes Framework, which pays per indicator achieved. NZ is capitation-based with patient co-payments, plus a layer of contracts and incentives through PHOs and Health NZ. The structural result for NZ is that capitation incentivises seeing the patient at all (because the income is the same whether they come in or not), but does not incentivise spending more time per consultation than is needed to clear the queue.

**Demand pressure** is the other large determinant. The denominator question  -  patients per GP per day  -  sets how much time per patient is available, before any other consideration. NZ general practice currently runs in many places at the limit of what its workforce can absorb. The RNZCGP has consistently documented workforce pressure, ageing demographics among GPs, and access difficulties, particularly outside the main centres. When demand exceeds supply, consultation length is the main valve that gets adjusted.

**Patient demographic** compounds the picture. Patients with multimorbidity, mental health complexity, low health literacy, or non-English first language predictably need longer consultations to be done well. NZ patients in lower-decile areas have substantially higher complexity at a given age than those in higher-decile areas. The capitation funding formula introduced in 2002 has been roundly criticised for failing to adequately reflect this  -  it adjusts for age and gender, not for deprivation, ethnicity, multimorbidity, or rurality [4]. In practice, this means practices serving the highest-need populations have been systematically underfunded relative to the time their patients require, and have had to either run shorter consultations, charge higher co-payments (which reduces access), or absorb the financial mismatch.

The capitation reweighting announced in July 2025 is the first major review of the formula since its introduction in 2002 [4]. The Health NZ proposal includes weighting for multimorbidity, rurality, and deprivation, with subsequent reviews scheduled every five years and the first follow-up in 2028 [4]. Whether the magnitude of the reweighting is sufficient to materially change the consultation-length pressure remains to be seen.

## Part III  -  What gets measured: performance indicators across systems

Different systems measure different things, and what gets measured shapes what gets done.

**The UK QOF** is the most-studied performance framework in primary care globally. Introduced in 2004 as part of the General Medical Services contract, the QOF rewards practices for achievement against a defined set of clinical indicators: blood pressure control in hypertensive patients, HbA1c targets in diabetes, statin prescribing in cardiovascular disease, vaccination coverage, AF anticoagulation per CHA2DS2-VASc score, and so on. The 2024/25 QOF measured 76 indicators against a maximum of 635 points, with each point worth approximately £220 [5]. Participation is voluntary but at 97.6% effectively universal [5]. The 2025/26 QOF underwent the largest restructuring in years: 32 indicators retired, total available points dropped to 564, and £198 million of funding concentrated into nine cardiovascular disease prevention indicators with much higher achievement thresholds (85-90%, up from 73-80%) [6].

**The Australian Practice Incentives Programme (PIP)** pays practices for participation in quality improvement activities, eHealth integration, and specific clinical programmes  -  less indicator-driven than QOF but operating on a similar principle of payment tied to defined process or outcome measures.

**NZ's emerging contingent capitation** is the most interesting recent local development. From 1 July 2025, a stream of $60 million in capitation funding became "contingent" on practice participation in data-sharing with Health NZ, with the explicit goal of building a national administrative primary care data repository to enable performance monitoring and equity improvements [7]. This is the structural beginning of NZ moving toward a QOF-like performance framework, though to date the contingency is on data participation rather than indicator achievement. The trajectory is clear; the destination is not yet defined.

**The US Medicare MIPS** and the HEDIS framework collectively measure dozens of process and outcome indicators across primary and specialty care, with payment adjustments based on performance.

A pattern emerges across these systems. Performance indicators measure things that can be defined as binary or numerical outcomes against a denominator: did this hypertensive patient have BP controlled below target this year? Was this diabetic patient's HbA1c measured? Was the AF patient on anticoagulation if their CHA2DS2-VASc score warranted it? These are measurable things, and they are clinically important things  -  well-controlled hypertension prevents stroke and heart disease, well-managed diabetes prevents complications, anticoagulation in AF prevents stroke.

What gets harder to measure: the quality of the diagnostic conversation. The accurate identification of the patient's actual concern within the seven minutes of presented symptoms. The therapeutic effect of the relationship itself. The decision not to investigate when investigation would harm. The timely recognition of mental distress disguised as physical symptoms. The prevention of an admission through a phone call to a worried family member of a frail elderly patient at 4pm. The slow work of trust-building with a patient who has had bad experiences with healthcare and is now suspicious of all of it.

These things matter. They are most of what good general practice does. They are also extremely difficult to measure, and so they generally don't appear in performance frameworks.

## Part IV  -  What the evidence actually shows

The QOF is by far the best-studied performance framework in primary care, and the systematic reviews and analyses are sobering.

A 2017 systematic review of QOF effects on long-term condition care concluded that the framework was associated with modest improvements in some specific indicators (a 14% improvement in a composite type 2 diabetes indicator in the first year, declining to 8% by year three; modest reductions in emergency admission rates for some long-term conditions; modest increases in consultation rates in severe mental illness) [8]. There was no clear effect on mortality. Crucially, the review found "no evidence that the QOF influences integration or coordination of care, holistic care, self-care, or patient experience" [8]. The aspects of primary care that matter most for the experience of being a patient  -  the broad, relational, integrative work  -  were apparently unaffected by the framework that paid for the narrow, technical work.

Two further critiques are worth noting.

First, performance frameworks create perverse incentives that distort clinical work. The "tick-box medicine" complaint about QOF is well-documented in the qualitative literature. Time spent in consultation gets reallocated toward whatever the framework rewards  -  checking smoking status, taking blood pressure, recording HbA1c  -  at the expense of whatever the patient came in to discuss. This is not because individual GPs are mercenary, but because practice systems are configured around the framework, with prompts and recall systems that surface indicator-relevant tasks. The framework does not crowd out good clinical work because the doctor is distracted; it crowds it out because the system is structurally arranged around the indicators.

Second, the framework rewards practices that are good at coding, not necessarily practices that are good at care. Practices serving healthier, wealthier populations achieve higher QOF scores not because they deliver better care but because their populations are easier to get to target. Adjustments for deprivation, where they exist, tend to be inadequate. The English literature has consistently shown a gradient in QOF achievement scores that maps fairly closely onto practice-population deprivation, with practices in deprived areas systematically scoring lower despite often delivering more clinically demanding work.

The honest summary is that performance frameworks in primary care produce modest gains in narrow clinical indicators, no demonstrable effect on most aspects of holistic care, and substantial reshaping of how clinical time gets allocated  -  generally away from things that aren't measured.

## Where this leaves NZ, and a working position

NZ sits at an interesting moment. The capitation funding formula is being rewritten for the first time in 23 years. Contingent capitation is the structural beginning of indicator-based payment. The workforce is under sustained pressure, and the demographic complexity of the patient population is rising. The 15-minute consult is increasingly a fiction in practices serving complex populations, and increasingly a constraint that doesn't match the work.

A few things I think are defensible.

**The comparative international data should anchor expectations.** NZ general practice consultations are short by Northern European standards and roughly equivalent to current UK and standard Australian length. The associations between consultation length and quality outcomes are robust across multiple independent lines of evidence. The political question of whether to fund longer consultations is separable from the empirical question of whether longer consultations produce better care; the evidence on the second is reasonably clear.

**Performance frameworks should be treated with appropriate scepticism.** The QOF evidence base is the strongest available, and it shows modest gains in narrow domains, with substantial concerns about distortion and equity. NZ moving toward a QOF-like framework  -  if that is what the contingent capitation arrangement evolves into  -  should be done with full awareness of what those frameworks have and have not delivered elsewhere, and with explicit thought about what the indicators will not measure. Importing a system that hasn't worked elsewhere on the assumption that it will work here is a strategy I would not recommend a patient adopt.

**The equity dimension is the one most worth defending.** Higher-needs populations need more time per consultation, not less. Capitation formulas that don't adjust adequately for complexity systematically transfer money from practices serving harder populations to practices serving easier ones. Performance frameworks that don't adjust adequately for population complexity do the same thing twice over. The 2025 capitation reweighting is the most important development in NZ primary care funding in two decades. Whether it is sufficient remains to be seen.

**The clinical work that performance frameworks miss is most of what general practice does.** The relationship, the diagnostic conversation, the slow work of building trust with patients who have reasons not to trust, the recognition of distress, the prevention of admissions through continuity. These are the durable goods of general practice, and they are hard to fund and easy to lose. Whatever the indicators end up being, the work has to continue regardless.

The fifteen-minute consult, in the end, is not a unit set by clinical evidence. It is a unit set by funding choices made decades ago, and renegotiated incrementally since. The work that fits inside it is shaped by those choices. And the work that doesn't fit inside it  -  increasingly, much of the work that matters most  -  happens anyway, in the time the system doesn't pay for, until either the system or the workforce changes.

That is the present situation, accurately described. It is also, I think, the case for change.
