Driving is not, in the abstract, a medical matter. In practice, it is one of the most consequential clinical questions a GP navigates, and one of the least consistently handled. The patient who cannot safely operate a vehicle because of uncontrolled epilepsy, untreated obstructive sleep apnoea, advanced dementia, or insulin-treated diabetes with hypoglycaemia unawareness is a patient whose continued driving is a risk to themselves and to everyone else on the road. The GP who knows about the condition and takes no action is a GP with both a professional problem and an ethical one.

The medical fitness to drive framework in New Zealand is structured and, once understood, navigable. The primary clinical reference is the NZTA publication *Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive* [1], which provides condition-by-condition standards for both private (Group 1) and commercial (Group 2) licences. This document should be on every GP's desktop. What this note does is orient the GP to the framework, set out the high-yield conditions in clinical detail, explain the notification pathway, and address the clinical conversation  -  particularly the dementia conversation  -  that practitioners find most difficult.

## Part I  -  The legal structure

The foundational obligations are in the Land Transport Act 1998 and the Land Transport (Driver Licensing) Rule 1999 [2,3]. The key points.

**The primary obligation is on the driver.** Under the Land Transport (Driver Licensing) Rule 1999, licence holders are required to inform NZTA if they develop a medical condition that may affect their ability to drive safely [3]. The GP's role begins when the patient does not  -  or cannot  -  meet this obligation.

**The GP's role has two distinct components.** First, when a patient applies for or renews a driver's licence and their medical condition is relevant, NZTA or the patient may request a medical certificate (Form DL9). Completing a DL9 is an assessment of current fitness to drive against the NZTA medical standards; it is not merely a formality, and signing it without considering the clinical picture carries potential liability. Second, when a GP becomes aware that a patient is driving with a condition that makes this unsafe, and the patient either refuses to self-report or lacks the capacity to do so, the GP has both a professional and a legal basis to notify NZTA directly [1,4].

**The licence categories matter.** Group 1 licences cover ordinary private vehicles. Group 2 licences cover heavy motor vehicles (including trucks and buses) and small passenger service vehicles (taxis, rideshare). The medical standards for Group 2 are substantially more stringent across almost every condition  -  a medical event that results in a short temporary restriction for a Group 1 driver often results in a much longer restriction or permanent disqualification for a Group 2 driver. GPs treating patients who hold commercial licences need to apply the Group 2 standards, which are set out separately in the NZTA guide and are the clinically relevant reference for that population.

**The privacy override.** The Privacy Act 2020 permits disclosure of health information without consent where there is a serious, credible, and not-otherwise-manageable risk to public safety [5]. A patient who continues to drive with a condition that meets the NZTA standard for notification falls within this exception. This is the legal basis for notifying NZTA without the patient's consent  -  and for doing so without this constituting a breach of the Code of Rights.

## Part II  -  The NZTA Medical Aspects guide

*Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive* is the authoritative NZ clinical reference for this area. It is available from the NZTA website, is updated periodically, and should be used directly rather than through summaries (including this one) when completing a DL9 or assessing a specific clinical situation [1].

The guide is organised by condition or system. For each, it sets out the standard for Group 1 and Group 2 licences, the required period off driving after a relevant event, what needs to be assessed before return to driving, and whether specialist input is required. There are separate sections for vision standards, hearing, and specific occupational contexts.

The practical habit to develop is: any time a patient presents with a new diagnosis or event in a category that affects driving  -  first seizure, MI, stroke, new dementia diagnosis, ICD implantation, new insulin initiation  -  open the guide and check the relevant section before concluding the consultation. The standard is often more nuanced than the remembered rule, and the Group 2 standard is frequently materially different from Group 1.

## Part III  -  High-yield conditions

### Epilepsy and seizures

Epilepsy generates more NZTA-related clinical decisions in general practice than any other neurological condition, and the standards are specific enough to warrant careful attention.

**Group 1 (private licence):**

A *first unprovoked seizure* requires a minimum of three months off driving [1]. The reasoning is that a person who has had a single unprovoked seizure has a meaningful risk of a second, and the three-month period allows for investigation, initiation of treatment if indicated, and establishment of whether further seizures occur.

*Established epilepsy* (two or more unprovoked seizures) requires twelve months seizure-free before return to driving [1]. This applies whether the person is on anticonvulsant treatment or not. The twelve-month period resets with each seizure  -  a person who has been seizure-free for eleven months and has a breakthrough seizure starts the twelve months again.

*Provoked seizures*  -  those with a clearly identified and reversible precipitant (severe hypoglycaemia, acute metabolic disturbance, significant alcohol withdrawal, acute structural brain lesion)  -  are assessed individually [1]. The principle is that once the provocation is removed and the risk of recurrence is low, return to driving can occur sooner. This requires documentation of the provocation and evidence that it has been adequately managed; it is not a free pass.

*Nocturnal seizures only*  -  some people have seizures exclusively during sleep or within thirty minutes of waking. After three years of exclusively nocturnal seizures, NZTA guidance may allow return to Group 1 driving; this is individual assessment territory [1].

**Group 2 (commercial licence):**

The standards are substantially stricter. For heavy vehicle and passenger service licences, NZTA generally requires a prolonged period (often ten years) free of seizures and off anticonvulsant medication before return to commercial driving is considered  -  because a seizure at the wheel of a heavy vehicle or bus has a categorically different risk profile than a seizure in a private vehicle [1]. For many drivers, a diagnosis of epilepsy is career-ending in the commercial context. This needs to be communicated clearly and early.

**Practical point:** the patient on a long-standing anticonvulsant for a historical seizure disorder who has been seizure-free for fifteen years is not the same clinical situation as the newly diagnosed patient. The NZTA guide distinguishes these; the clinical assessment should too.

### Cardiovascular conditions

**Acute myocardial infarction and coronary intervention:**

After acute MI, a minimum of four weeks off Group 1 driving is required [1]. After percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) for stable angina, four weeks is also the standard. After coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG), four weeks for Group 1  -  though the sternotomy and physical recovery often mean the patient is not functionally ready to drive for longer.

Group 2 commercial standards after MI are stricter: a minimum of three months, with functional assessment (exercise tolerance testing or equivalent), before return to commercial driving.

**Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs):**

The ICD situation is clinically important because it is common and the standards differ significantly between primary and secondary prevention indications.

For *secondary prevention* ICD (implanted following a life-threatening ventricular arrhythmia or resuscitated cardiac arrest): six months off driving for Group 1 [1]. The rationale is that the condition that caused the event may recur and produce loss of consciousness before the ICD fires. After six months without a treated arrhythmia, Group 1 driving can usually resume under specialist review.

For *primary prevention* ICD (implanted as prophylaxis, without a prior life-threatening event): four weeks off driving for Group 1, after which the implantation site has healed and there is no specific restriction attributable to the ICD itself [1].

For Group 2 commercial licences, ICD implantation generally results in permanent disqualification in most categories  -  the risk of a treated arrhythmia while driving a heavy vehicle is considered unacceptable regardless of primary or secondary prevention indication [1].

A point that generates confusion: the GP should not be certifying return to driving after ICD implantation without cardiology input. The cardiologist managing the ICD is the appropriate person to advise on driving, and the GP's role is to ensure this advice has been sought and documented.

**Syncope and unexplained loss of consciousness:**

Single vasovagal syncope with a clear precipitant and no recurrence: four weeks off driving is the standard, after which return to Group 1 driving is reasonable in most cases [1].

Unexplained syncope or loss of consciousness  -  where the mechanism has not been established  -  requires investigation before return to driving. The risk is that an arrhythmic or structural cause will recur without warning. The standard is: driving suspended until the cause is identified and treated, or specialist assessment confirms the risk is acceptable [1].

Syncope associated with a documented arrhythmia follows the arrhythmia-specific standard.

### Diabetes

The driving standards in diabetes are primarily about hypoglycaemia risk. The concern centres on hypoglycaemia in insulin-treated patients, which can produce the abrupt loss of consciousness that makes driving dangerous. Hyperglycaemia alone is unlikely to do this.

**Group 1, insulin-treated diabetes:**

Can drive provided: adequate hypoglycaemia awareness is present; no severe hypoglycaemia episode (requiring third-party assistance) in the preceding twelve months; blood glucose is checked before driving and is ≥5 mmol/L; blood glucose is checked every two hours on long trips; and the patient carries a fast-acting glucose source in the vehicle [1]. Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) have changed this landscape  -  a well-calibrated CGM with alarms set appropriately is a meaningful safety tool, and patients who use one should be informed of its relevance to driving safety.

*Hypoglycaemia unawareness* is an absolute contraindication to driving until awareness is restored [1]. This is the most clinically important point in this section. A patient who cannot feel a hypo coming cannot stop the car before losing consciousness. Restoring awareness generally requires a structured period of strict hypoglycaemia avoidance; endocrinology input is appropriate.

**Group 2, insulin-treated diabetes:**

The Group 2 standard for insulin-treated diabetes is complex and has evolved. NZTA guidance should be consulted directly; specialist (endocrinologist) assessment and regular monitoring are required, and some commercial categories are not accessible to insulin-treated drivers [1].

**Non-insulin treated diabetes:**

Significantly lower risk. The main concern is sulfonylurea use, which does carry hypoglycaemia risk. The driving standards for non-insulin-treated diabetes on low-risk medications (metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists) are less restrictive; sulfonylurea-treated patients should observe similar glucose-checking precautions to insulin-treated patients [1].

### Obstructive sleep apnoea

Untreated obstructive sleep apnoea with excessive daytime sleepiness is a significant road safety risk  -  epidemiological studies consistently show elevated crash risk in untreated OSA, and the dose-response with severity is established [6]. The driving implications are particularly significant for commercial drivers, for whom shift work and long-distance driving compound the baseline risk.

**The standard:** A person with untreated symptomatic OSA (Epworth Sleepiness Scale score ≥8, or documented evidence of excessive daytime sleepiness, or significant apnoea-hypopnoea index) should not be driving until treatment is initiated and effective [1]. For Group 2 commercial licences, this is stricter: documented compliance with CPAP therapy (typically demonstrated by device download data) and resolution of sleepiness is required before return to commercial driving.

**The clinical issue in general practice** is that many patients with OSA present with fatigue and have not connected their symptoms to driving safety. The GP who diagnoses or suspects OSA should routinely ask about driving, advise about the driving restriction pending treatment, document this conversation, and follow up on treatment initiation and adherence. A commercial driver with newly diagnosed OSA is off the road until CPAP compliance is established; this needs to be said clearly.

### Cognitive impairment and dementia

This is the most clinically difficult category because the impairment is progressive, the patient's insight is often reduced, the conversation involves taking away something that represents independence and identity, and the clinical tools available to GPs are imperfect predictors of driving performance.

**The standard:** There is no automatic driving prohibition at the point of a dementia diagnosis. The NZTA standard is functional: the question is whether the cognitive impairment is affecting the abilities necessary for safe driving  -  attention, executive function, processing speed, navigation, and the ability to respond appropriately to unexpected events [1]. Early dementia with preserved function may permit continued driving under regular monitoring; moderate or advanced dementia, or any stage where driving-relevant abilities are materially impaired, requires cessation.

**The clinical tools:**

The GP Cognitive Screen (GPCOG) and the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) are the most commonly used brief cognitive assessments in primary care. Their correlation with actual driving performance is moderate, not strong  -  a person can score within the normal range on both and still have driving-relevant deficits in executive function or processing speed [7]. Conversely, someone with mild word-finding difficulties may score poorly without any impairment in the specific cognitive domains relevant to driving.

The gold standard for assessing driving fitness in cognitive impairment is an *on-road assessment conducted by an occupational therapist* specialising in driving [1,7]. NZTA guidance recommends this when there is doubt. Referral for OT driving assessment is the appropriate action when cognitive screening raises concerns but is not unequivocally disqualifying. It is also the action that most reliably produces defensible documentation.

**The conversation:**

The dementia-and-driving conversation is among the most difficult in general practice. It involves confronting a person with reduced insight about the loss of something that represents autonomy, independence, often identity, and  -  in rural areas  -  frequently the ability to access food, medical care, and social connection. Family members are often present and may be either pushing for the GP to intervene or pushing back against it.

Some practical frames:

*Frame it as a safety issue for others, not a judgment of the patient.* "The concern isn't that you're a bad driver  -  the concern is that the condition can produce moments of confusion or slow reaction that you wouldn't be aware of, and those moments can cause harm to other people on the road." This is more likely to land than an implied accusation of incapacity.

*Use the OT driving assessment as a constructive referral, not a final sentence.* "I'd like to refer you to someone who specifically assesses driving ability  -  they'll do an actual on-road test and can tell us more specifically what, if anything, has changed." This is honest, it delays the definitive conversation pending better information, and it often produces the conclusive assessment that is more persuasive to the patient than the GP's clinical opinion.

*Plan the conversation early.* The best time to discuss the future of driving in a patient with early dementia is at or shortly after diagnosis, when the patient has more insight, when the family can be present and briefed, and when the transition can be planned rather than imposed in a crisis. A plan that says "we'll reassess driving every six months, and if there are concerns we'll arrange a formal assessment" is more acceptable than a sudden prohibition later.

*Document thoroughly.* The conversation, the cognitive test scores, the advice given, the patient's response, and the agreed plan should all be in the record. If the situation subsequently requires notification to NZTA, the documentation trail supports the action and the basis for it.

## Part IV  -  The DL9 certificate

Form DL9 is the medical certificate used when NZTA requires a medical assessment of a driver  -  typically at licence renewal, or when concerns have been raised about a driver's fitness. The GP completing a DL9 is certifying, on the basis of their clinical assessment, that the person does or does not meet the NZTA medical standards for the relevant licence class.

Several points about DL9 completion.

**It is not a formality.** A DL9 signed without reviewing the relevant NZTA standards, without a clinical assessment adequate to form a view on the relevant conditions, and without consideration of the Group 2 standards if a commercial licence is held, is not good practice. The GP signing the DL9 takes on a professional responsibility for that assessment.

**The GP is not the only assessor.** For conditions requiring specialist input  -  cardiology review after ICD, neurology review for epilepsy, endocrinology assessment for insulin-treated diabetes in commercial drivers  -  the GP should not be completing the DL9 without that input. The DL9 process allows for specialist involvement; the GP's role may be to coordinate rather than to assess independently.

**A DL9 can record conditional fitness.** The certificate can reflect that the person is fit to drive subject to conditions  -  annual review, restricted driving hours, restriction to automatic transmission, restriction to specific distances. These conditions are enforced by NZTA, not the GP; the GP's role is to record the clinical assessment accurately.

## Part V  -  The notification pathway

As discussed in the mandatory reporting note on this site, the notification pathway for a patient who continues to drive unsafely and will not self-report is:

1. Document the condition, the clinical basis for concern, and the driving standard it engages
2. Have an explicit conversation with the patient: inform them of the standard, advise them to stop driving, advise them of their obligation to notify NZTA, and set a specific timeline
3. Document that conversation and the patient's response
4. If the patient does not self-notify within the agreed period, notify NZTA Medical directly  -  in writing, identifying the patient, their condition, and the clinical basis for concern
5. Inform the patient that you have notified NZTA

NZTA Medical then contacts the patient and initiates its own assessment process. The GP is protected from liability for notifying in good faith under the Land Transport Act 1998 [2].

The situations where this pathway is most commonly needed: uncontrolled epilepsy in a patient who denies the standard applies to them; dementia with poor insight; significant alcohol dependence with blackouts; severe untreated sleep apnoea in a commercial driver who is reluctant to engage with treatment.

The situations where the process sometimes stalls: the GP who documents the concern but never has the explicit conversation; the GP who has the conversation but doesn't set a specific timeline; the patient who says "I'll stop" and doesn't. Each of these stall points needs to be moved through actively, because the risk to others continues until the notification is made.

## Part VI  -  Rural, equity, and access considerations

NZ is a car-dependent country, and the distribution of that dependency is unequal. The rural patient who cannot drive to the nearest town for groceries and GP appointments is not in the same position as the urban patient who can take a bus or a rideshare. The commercial driver whose licence is the instrument of their income is not in the same position as the person who drives recreationally.

None of this changes the clinical standard. It does change the conversation.

*Tell the truth about the consequences before announcing the decision.* The rural patient, the commercial driver, the elderly person living alone  -  each needs to hear the clinical finding and its basis, but each also needs to know that the implications have been considered and that there is a plan for what comes next. "You can't drive" without "and here's what that means for getting to appointments, and here are the options" is a conversation that serves neither the patient nor the therapeutic relationship.

*Alternative transport planning is part of the clinical work.* Community transport services, NZTA's Total Mobility scheme for people with impairments, local disability support services, whānau involvement, and the GP's knowledge of the local resource environment are all relevant. The clinical obligation does not end with the driving assessment; it includes reasonable effort to ensure the patient's access needs are addressed.

*Equity of access to driving assessment.* The OT driving assessment that is the gold standard for cognitive impairment and complex cases is not universally accessible  -  it is privately funded in most circumstances, costs several hundred dollars, and is not available in all regions. For patients who cannot afford or access it, the GP may be required to make clinical judgements without the formal assessment, with appropriate documentation of why the standard pathway was not used.

## A working position

**Know the guide.** The NZTA *Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive* is the reference document. The condition-specific standards are detailed and nuanced in ways that this note cannot fully represent. Using it directly is the clinical standard; relying on memory or summaries for clinical decisions in this area is not adequate practice.

**The Group 2 commercial standard is different.** For every patient who holds or is applying for a commercial licence, the Group 2 standard applies. It is almost always stricter. Many commercial drivers are not aware that their medical conditions have specific implications for their commercial licence that are more significant than for a private licence.

**Dementia conversations should start early.** The worst time to have the dementia-and-driving conversation is when it is urgent. The best time is at or shortly after diagnosis, when the patient has more insight and the family can be part of a forward plan rather than a crisis.

**A DL9 signed without review is a liability.** Completing a medical certificate without checking the relevant NZTA standard, without an adequate clinical assessment, and without appropriate specialist input where required is both clinically inadequate and professionally risky.

**The notification process requires follow-through.** A documented concern about unsafe driving that is not followed through to either patient self-notification or GP notification of NZTA is a documented concern that has not produced the outcome it needed to. The patient who agrees to stop driving and continues to drive, the patient who will not engage  -  these situations require active follow-through, not continued documentation of the same concern.

**The loss of driving is a significant clinical event.** It warrants the same thoughtfulness, adequate time, and planning for consequences that any significant lifestyle change requires. Done well, the driving cessation conversation preserves trust and produces cooperation. Done badly, it produces conflict, denial, and, sometimes, a patient who stops attending the practice and keeps driving.
