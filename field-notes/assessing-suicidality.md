Most people who die by suicide in New Zealand have seen a GP in the year before their death. [#ref-1] A substantial proportion have seen one in the month before. This is not an indictment of general practice - it reflects that GPs are where distressed people go - but it is a fact that gives the topic of suicidality assessment a particular weight in primary care that it does not always receive in training.

This note covers the clinical, contextual, and ethical territory. It is not a protocol. Protocols exist, are available from the Ministry of Health and your local DHB/Te Whatu Ora service, and should be read. This is the thinking behind the protocol - the evidence for how we ask, what we can predict, what actually reduces risk, and where the received wisdom is weaker than it appears.

A note on language. "Suicidality" is a clinical umbrella term covering ideation (thoughts of suicide), intent (desire to act), plan (a specific method in mind), and behaviour (attempts, preparatory acts). These exist on a spectrum and do not progress linearly. A person can have a detailed plan without intent. A person can have an impulsive attempt without prior ideation. Language precision matters in documentation and in the clinical conversation.

## Part I - The New Zealand context

New Zealand's suicide rate is not, at the population level, catastrophically high by international comparison. Where it becomes distressing is in the youth data. For adolescents and young adults aged 15 to 24, New Zealand has consistently ranked among the highest in the OECD - in the top three or four, depending on the year. [#ref-2] For young Maori, the rate is roughly twice that of non-Maori in the same age group. [#ref-3] This is not a minor statistical quirk. It is a pattern of preventable deaths distributed unequally across the population.

The Chief Coroner publishes annual suicide mortality data. The figures fluctuate year to year, but the structural patterns are stable: male sex, youth and early adulthood, Maori and Pacific ethnicity, rural residence, socioeconomic deprivation, recent contact with the justice system, and prior psychiatric history are consistently associated with elevated rates. [3] None of these are deterministic. They are population-level signals, and their value in the individual clinical encounter is limited in ways discussed below.

What is less often discussed is the evidence on what actually moves the dial at a population level. Means restriction - reducing access to lethal methods - has the most consistent evidence base. [#ref-4] In New Zealand, this has translated into work around firearm access, bridge barriers, and medication packaging. It is unglamorous, difficult politically in some quarters, and substantially more effective than most clinical interventions. For a GP, the practical implication is that asking about access to firearms, medication stockpiles, and other lethal means is not paranoid - it is one of the highest-yield questions in a suicidality assessment.

New Zealand also has specific media reporting guidelines for suicide, developed in line with international "safe messaging" principles. [#ref-5] The "Werther effect" - the well-documented increase in suicide rates following prominent, detailed media coverage of suicide - is real and replicated. [#ref-6] The converse, sometimes called the "Papageno effect," is more tentative but suggests that narratives of crisis survived can reduce ideation in at-risk populations. [#ref-7] For GPs, the takeaway is clinical rather than policy: how you discuss a patient's own history of attempts, and how you frame their crisis, can reinforce or disrupt patterns of suicidal thinking.

## Part II - Does asking about suicide increase risk?

The concern that asking a patient about suicidal thoughts will plant or amplify those thoughts is one of the most persistent myths in clinical medicine. It is why GPs add PHQ-9 item 9 to a form rather than raising it in conversation, and why the question sometimes arrives at the end of a consultation in a tone that discourages an honest answer.

The evidence does not support this concern. A systematic review and meta-analysis by Dazzi and colleagues found no evidence that asking about suicidal ideation in research or clinical settings increases suicidal ideation, distress, or behaviour. In some high-risk groups, asking appeared to be associated with a small decrease in distress. [#ref-8]

This matters enough to state plainly: asking about suicide does not make it more likely. Not asking leaves the clinician without information that may be clinically critical.

The quality of the asking matters more than the fact of it. Questions delivered as checkbox items at the end of a consultation, or embedded in a tone of evident discomfort, communicate to the patient that this is not a topic you want to actually discuss. Questions asked with directness and without flinching - "I want to ask you directly whether you have had any thoughts of suicide or of not wanting to be alive" - communicate that the topic is survivable, that you can hold it, and that honest answers will be received rather than dismissed.

Indirect approaches - "you're not thinking of doing anything silly, are you?" or "you wouldn't hurt yourself, would you?" - are not neutral. They are leading questions that communicate an expected answer, and they produce data that is less clinically useful than honest disclosure.

## Part III - How to ask: a clinical framework

The clinical goal in a suicidality assessment is to establish, with reasonable confidence, where this person is on the spectrum from passive ideation to active intent with a specific plan and the means available - and to understand the context well enough to formulate an approach.

The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) is the most widely validated tool for this structured assessment. [#ref-9] It moves from passive ideation ("have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?") through active ideation with and without intent, to planning and behaviour. You do not need to use the formal instrument in every consultation, but its logic is worth internalising: ideation, intent, plan, means. If a patient endorses one level, you continue to the next. You stop when the conversation ends naturally or when you have the information you need.

In a time-pressured GP consultation, a practical sequence:

**Start with passive ideation.** "Have you had thoughts that life isn't worth living, or that you'd be better off dead?" This is a lower-threshold question that many patients will answer honestly who would hesitate at a more direct question.

**Move to active ideation if indicated.** "Have you had thoughts of actually ending your life?" Some patients with passive ideation have not moved to active ideation; this distinction changes the urgency of the response.

**Assess intent and plan.** "Have you thought about how you might do it?" A patient with ideation and a specific plan is in a different clinical position from a patient with ideation and no plan.

**Ask about means.** "Do you have access to [the method they described]?" - or, as a standing question regardless: "Do you have access to firearms or large quantities of medications at home?" Means access is one of the modifiable risk factors, and asking about it opens the door to means restriction conversations.

**Ask about timing.** "Is there a time you have in mind?" Recent or imminent timing significantly increases urgency.

**Ask about intent to tell.** "Is this something you were planning to talk to anyone about?" Complete secrecy is a risk factor. The decision to tell you is already, in some sense, a protective act.

**Ask about protective factors.** "What has kept you going?" Children, relationships, religious belief, future plans, responsibility to others, fear - these are not dismissible as naive positives. They are clinically real and feature in safety planning.

Alongside this conversational assessment, validated brief screening tools have a role. The PHQ-9 item 9 ("how often have you been bothered by thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself in some way?") is a reasonable screening prompt for ideation, though it is a screen and not a risk stratification tool. [#ref-10] The SAD PERSONS scale has been widely used but its predictive validity is poor enough that several systematic reviews have argued against its continued use. [#ref-11]

## Part IV - Risk stratification: what it can and cannot do

Clinical risk stratification - categorising a patient as low, moderate, or high risk - is deeply embedded in psychiatric and general practice culture around suicidality. It is also, in its actuarial form, of limited value in predicting individual outcomes.

The fundamental problem is that suicide is a rare event at the individual level, even in high-risk groups. A large meta-analysis found that even among people with the highest risk factors, the majority did not die by suicide, and predictive tools performed only modestly better than chance at identifying who would. [#ref-12] Binary high/low risk classification produces high false positive rates (excessive hospitalisation, therapeutic damage) and accepts false negatives (deaths in people categorised as low risk) as an unavoidable cost.

The shift in evidence-based practice has been away from actuarial risk categorisation and toward individualised formulation. The question is not "is this person high, medium, or low risk?" but rather "what specific factors are driving this person's suicidality right now, which of those are modifiable in the short term, and what would need to change for the risk to reduce?" This framing is more clinically useful, less falsely reassuring, and more likely to produce an actionable plan.

The static risk factors - sex, age, prior attempts, family history, psychiatric diagnosis, chronic pain - provide important background. The dynamic risk factors - recent loss, acute intoxication, hopelessness, sleep deprivation, current access to means, recent discharge from inpatient care - are what matter most for immediate risk and are the targets for intervention.

Prior attempts are the single most important predictor of future suicide. [#ref-12] A patient who has made a previous serious attempt should be treated as chronically elevated baseline risk, and any current ideation should be assessed accordingly. "You've talked about this before and nothing happened" is not a valid clinical inference.

## Part V - Safety planning: the evidence for what works

The safety plan, as a clinical intervention, is not the same thing as a list of phone numbers. It is a collaborative document, developed with the patient, that works through what to do when the crisis arrives - not after it has passed.

The Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention (SPI) is the most studied model. [#ref-13] Its six components, in order of least to most intensive escalation, are:

1. Warning signs - the specific thoughts, images, behaviours, or situations that signal that suicidal crisis is building
2. Internal coping strategies - things the person can do alone to reduce distress (not phone calls - solo strategies first)
3. Social contacts for distraction - people and settings the person can engage with to take their mind off the crisis
4. Social contacts to ask for help - specific named people the person can contact to say they are struggling
5. Professionals and agencies to contact - the GP, the crisis line, the emergency department, in order
6. Making the environment safe - means restriction: removing or securing access to lethal means

The sequence matters. The plan is designed to escalate through steps rather than jump immediately to hospitalisation or crisis line. Most suicidal crises are time-limited. If a person can navigate through steps one and two and the crisis diminishes, hospitalisation was not needed. The safety plan builds capacity at the earlier steps precisely to reduce unnecessary escalation.

The evidence for SPI is encouraging. Multiple randomised controlled trials and observational studies have found that patients who receive a safety plan have lower rates of suicidal behaviour, greater treatment engagement, and lower rates of ED presentation. [#ref-14] It is not magic, and it requires genuine collaboration to work - a plan handed to a patient without discussion is not a safety plan, it is a piece of paper.

**No-harm contracts**, by contrast, have no credible evidence base and should not be used. The idea that a patient's signature on a statement that they will not hurt themselves provides clinical protection is not supported by any rigorous evidence, transfers inappropriate responsibility to the patient, and provides false reassurance to the clinician. [#ref-15] If your safety management plan is a signed contract, it is not an evidence-based plan.

## Part VI - Hospitalisation: the difficult evidence

The reflexive response to high suicidality risk is hospital admission. This is understandable - the GP wants the person physically safe tonight, wants the decision and responsibility shared, and wants to be able to document that they did not simply send a high-risk patient home.

The clinical evidence is more complicated. Brief psychiatric admission for suicidality is inconsistently supported by outcome data. Several studies have found no difference in suicide rates between admitted and non-admitted patients. [#ref-16] For patients with borderline personality disorder - who account for a significant proportion of suicidality presentations - repeated brief admissions may reinforce suicidal behaviour as a pathway to care and worsen long-term outcomes. [#ref-17]

This does not mean hospitalisation is never indicated. It clearly is. Acutely psychotic patients with command hallucinations to harm themselves. Patients who have made a recent serious attempt and whose safety at home cannot be established. Patients whose level of intoxication prevents any meaningful safety assessment or planning. Patients who explicitly state they cannot keep themselves safe and have no supports to turn to.

What the evidence argues against is hospitalisation as a default risk management tool that replaces individualised clinical judgment. Sending a patient to the emergency department because the clinical situation is uncomfortable, without a specific clinical reason why the hospital environment will be safer or more therapeutic than home, is not good practice - and, particularly for patients with recurrent suicidality, may be actively unhelpful.

The conversation with the patient about why you are or are not recommending admission is itself therapeutic. "I want to think with you about whether hospital is the right place for you tonight" is a different conversation from "I need to refer you because I'm worried." The former preserves the patient's agency and the therapeutic alliance; the latter positions the clinician as acting on the patient rather than with them.

When admission is indicated, the handover to the emergency department should include a clear summary of the clinical picture, the reason for referral, the safety plan if one has been developed, and what your expectation is for ongoing GP involvement after discharge. The post-discharge period - particularly the first week after inpatient discharge - is the highest-risk window. [#ref-1] A follow-up appointment booked before the patient leaves the hospital, or confirmed in writing by you, reduces the probability of the patient falling through the gap.

## Part VII - Chronic suicidality

General practice routinely holds patients with chronic suicidal ideation - most commonly in the context of recurrent depressive disorder, borderline personality disorder, treatment-resistant depression, and chronic pain. These are different clinical situations from acute suicidal crisis and require a different framework.

For the patient who has had suicidal ideation for years, who has discussed it in previous consultations, and who is not in acute crisis, the clinical challenge is holding ongoing risk without allowing it to saturate every consultation or, conversely, without normalising it to the point of under-responding to genuine deterioration.

Several principles help.

**Establish a clear baseline.** Know what this person's usual level of ideation looks like - its frequency, intensity, and quality. Document it. This makes it possible to identify deterioration when it occurs, rather than treating every presentation as a fresh emergency.

**Distinguish passive from active ideation in the chronic context.** A patient with borderline PD who reports that they think about suicide daily, passively, is in a different clinical position from the same patient who reports that they have been thinking about a specific plan for the past week. The frequency of ideation is less clinically important than the quality and intentionality.

**Calibrate the response proportionately.** Over-responding to stable chronic ideation - treating every mention as an acute crisis, repeatedly referring to emergency services - erodes the therapeutic alliance, teaches the patient that disclosure leads to escalation they do not want, and reduces the reliability of future disclosure. Under-responding misses genuine deterioration. The clinical skill is knowing which situation you are in.

**Maintain the relationship.** Long-term follow-up by a consistent clinician is one of the few interventions with clear evidence of benefit in reducing suicide in high-risk groups. [#ref-18] The therapeutic relationship is not soft background - it is a clinical intervention. Regular appointments, even brief ones, even without crisis content, communicate that the patient has a place in the health system that is not contingent on being in crisis.

## Part VIII - Confidentiality, duty of care, and the limits of the relationship

New Zealand has no statute that mandates reporting of suicidal ideation. The *Health and Disability Commissioner (Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights) Regulations 1996* protects patient confidentiality; it does not require clinicians to report suicidality to any authority. [#ref-19]

The common law position is that a clinician may breach confidentiality where there is a serious and imminent risk of harm to the patient or others, where the risk cannot be adequately managed within the confidential relationship, and where disclosure is limited to what is necessary to prevent the harm. This is a judgment call, not a mandatory trigger.

The practical implication: calling a patient's family member to disclose suicidal content without the patient's consent is a confidentiality breach that requires clinical justification. It may sometimes be the right call - a patient who is acutely at risk and whose family member can provide safety tonight. But it is not a default, and doing it without explanation to the patient damages trust in ways that may reduce future disclosure.

The better approach, when involving family members is clinically indicated, is to discuss this with the patient explicitly. "I'm wondering whether it would help to have someone with you tonight - is there someone you trust that we could call?" is a different move from unilaterally disclosing to a third party. Whenever possible, involve the patient in decisions about who is told and what they are told.

The tension between duty of care and therapeutic alliance is real, and it does not resolve cleanly. A clinician who never breaches confidentiality risks failing to prevent a preventable death. A clinician who routinely breaches confidentiality around suicidality will find that patients stop disclosing suicidal thoughts - producing apparent safety with actual increased risk. There is no rule that navigates this tension perfectly. The goal is clinical judgment that is genuinely patient-centred, documented clearly, and regularly reviewed.

## Part IX - A practical synthesis

Some things worth keeping close.

**The consultation is the intervention.** A patient who discloses suicidal ideation to their GP and is heard, assessed properly, and engaged in genuine safety planning has already received something therapeutic. The quality of that engagement matters independently of whatever is referred or prescribed.

**Document your reasoning, not just your conclusion.** "Patient at low risk, follow up in two weeks" is not a useful clinical note. "Patient describes passive ideation without intent or plan, denies access to firearms or medication stockpiles, has support from partner, has previous safety plan in place, will present to ED if thoughts escalate - review in one week" is.

**The post-discharge gap is the danger zone.** Any patient recently discharged from inpatient care should have a GP follow-up booked, preferably in the first week. The first two weeks post-discharge carry the highest absolute risk of suicide of any period in the natural history of a psychiatric illness. [#ref-1] Do not leave this to chance.

**Ask about means as a standard question.** Access to firearms and large quantities of medications is modifiable. Asking about it, framing it as a practical safety measure rather than a judgment, and discussing how to reduce access (locking medication away with a family member, handing over firearms to police or a shooting club) is one of the highest-yield clinical interventions in this space.

**Seek consultation when you are uncertain.** The mental health crisis team, the local psychiatric service, and your colleagues are resources. Lone clinical management of complex suicidality in primary care, without any specialist input, carries more risk than the referral it avoids.

**Look after yourself.** The death by suicide of a patient is one of the most distressing events in a GP's professional life. It affects clinicians at rates that are rarely acknowledged in training or peer culture. The RNZCGP and NZMA have support resources. Using them is not a sign of inadequacy.

Suicidality assessment is not a box-ticking exercise and it is not a liability management exercise. It is a clinical encounter between a person in distress and a clinician who, if they get it right, can be part of the reason that person is still alive next year. The stakes are about as high as clinical stakes get, and they are worth treating that way.
