The advice "you should exercise more" is one of the most-given and least-useful things general practice produces. It is given because it is true. It is unhelpful because it ignores almost everything that actually shapes whether the patient will, or can, do anything about it.

The honest version of this clinical work is more complicated. The evidence base is strong. The NZ data are sliding in the wrong direction. The sedentary-lifestyle problem is structurally distinct from the activity problem and requires its own framing. The socioeconomic barriers  -  cost, time, built environment, cold housing  -  are real and do not yield to motivational interviewing. The body-image and disordered-eating risks of activity counselling are also real and tend to be ignored. And what the consult can usefully do is genuinely smaller than the public-health discourse implies, but is not nothing.

This is the longer version of the conversation.

## Part I  -  What activity actually does

The evidence on physical activity is among the strongest in clinical medicine, and it is worth being clear about what the magnitude actually is, because the magnitude is what justifies the work.

Regular physical activity reduces all-cause mortality by approximately 30%, and the effect is consistent across age groups, sexes, and most ethnic populations [1]. It reduces cardiovascular disease mortality, incident type 2 diabetes, dementia incidence, several specific cancer incidences (notably colon, breast, and endometrial), depression, anxiety, fall-related fractures in older adults, and frailty progression [1,2]. The effects are dose-dependent up to a point, with steepest gains at the bottom end  -  moving from "no activity" to "a little activity" produces larger marginal benefits than moving from "a lot" to "even more". The mortality benefit is largely independent of weight change, which is the single most clinically misunderstood feature of the literature [1].

The current WHO guidelines, adopted in NZ, recommend at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity (or 75 minutes of vigorous), spread across the week, plus muscle-strengthening activity on at least two days [3]. These are population guidelines, not individual prescriptions. The framing matters: the guideline level is the threshold above which most chronic-disease prevention benefits are achieved, but substantial benefit accrues well below it. A patient moving from 0 to 60 minutes per week is doing something clinically meaningful even if they are still well short of the 150-minute target.

Two further points from the recent literature deserve clinical attention.

First, the **all-or-nothing framing is wrong.** The 2018 update to the US Physical Activity Guidelines and subsequent international guidance removed the requirement that activity bouts be at least 10 minutes long [4]. Cumulative short bursts of activity throughout the day  -  climbing stairs, walking to the car park, gardening, an active commute  -  count toward the same physiological benefits as continuous sessions. This is a meaningful shift for clinical conversations. The patient who can't carve out a half-hour walk can still accumulate twenty minutes across the day, and the benefit is comparable.

Second, **the relationship between vigorous and moderate activity is more interesting than the simple 75/150 framing suggests.** Recent analyses (Stamatakis and colleagues 2022, building on earlier work) suggest that even very small amounts of vigorous activity  -  under five minutes per day  -  are associated with substantial mortality benefit in previously inactive adults [5]. The "weekend warrior" pattern, where adults concentrate activity into one or two days per week, is now reasonably well-supported as approximately equivalent to the same total volume spread across the week [6]. The clinical implication is that the patient whose work and family schedule only allows weekend activity is not doing it wrong.

## Part II  -  Where NZ actually sits

The NZ Health Survey 2024/25, released November 2025, found that 46.2% of adults met the physical activity guidelines (at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week, spread across the week), down from 52.2% in 2019/20 [7]. Approximately one in eight adults (12.3%) did "little or no" physical activity, defined as less than 30 minutes total in the previous week [7]. The trajectory across the last six surveys is unambiguous: 50.9% (2018/19), 52.2% (2019/20), 51.3% (2021/22), 46.5% (2022/23), 46.6% (2023/24), 46.2% (2024/25). NZ adults are, on average, becoming less active.

The gradient by deprivation is steep and durable. In 2023/24, 19.9% of adults living in the most deprived neighbourhoods did little or no physical activity, compared with 11.1% in the least deprived [8]. The pattern persists across surveys and is structurally consistent with international evidence on socioeconomic gradients in activity. Active transport in children mirrors the trend: 38.6% of 5–14 year olds usually walked or cycled to school in 2024/25, down from 42.7% in 2019/20 [7]. The trajectory of walking and cycling to school across NZ has been declining for at least two decades.

These are the numbers behind the clinical picture: roughly half of NZ adults aren't meeting activity guidelines; one in eight is barely active at all; the gradient by deprivation is a strong predictor of who falls into the bottom group; and the trajectory across the population is the wrong direction.

## Part III  -  The sedentary problem

This is the part that has changed most in the last decade, and that the typical "exercise more" framing misses entirely.

Sedentary behaviour  -  defined as time spent sitting or reclining at energy expenditure near resting  -  is now understood to be an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality, separate from the absence of moderate-to-vigorous activity [9,10]. The clinical implication is that two patients with identical exercise habits  -  same gym sessions, same weekly minutes  -  will have different mortality risk profiles if one of them sits eleven hours a day at a desk and the other moves around in a physically active job. Sitting is a thing in itself, not just the absence of activity.

The most-cited synthesis is Ekelund and colleagues' 2016 *Lancet* harmonised meta-analysis of more than one million participants [9]. The headline finding was that approximately 60–75 minutes per day of moderate-intensity activity can eliminate the increased mortality risk associated with high sitting time (≥8 hours/day) [9]. A subsequent accelerometer-based analysis suggested 30–40 minutes per day of moderate-vigorous activity was sufficient to attenuate the prolonged-sitting mortality risk [11]. Either way: meaningful exercise can offset the harms of prolonged sitting, but the volume required is higher than most adults achieve.

The clinical translation is twofold. The patient who sits at a desk for eight hours daily and exercises for forty-five minutes after work is doing better than the patient who doesn't exercise at all  -  but is not capturing the full benefit available from the activity, because the underlying sedentary load is partly cancelling it out. The intervention worth discussing is *interrupting* the sitting itself: standing or walking breaks every thirty to sixty minutes, taking phone calls walking, sit-stand desk options where available, walking meetings. The clinical message "move more often, throughout the day" is structurally different from "do thirty minutes of exercise" and deserves its own space in the consultation.

## Part IV  -  The socioeconomic dimensions

This is where most "encouraging activity" conversations fail. The structural barriers low-income patients face are not a matter of motivation, and addressing them in motivational terms is one of the more common ways primary care squanders the opportunity. A few of the specifics, mostly familiar but worth saying clearly.

**Time poverty is the largest single barrier.** Patients working multiple jobs, with shift work, with caregiving responsibilities (often for both children and ageing parents), with long commutes, do not have the discretionary time that the activity guidelines presuppose. The 150 minutes a week the guidelines recommend is achievable for someone with a 40-hour fixed-schedule job and predictable evenings. It is not necessarily achievable for someone whose work schedule changes weekly, who finishes shifts at 11pm, and who is solely responsible for two school-age children.

**Cost is real even when activity is "free".** The advice to "go for a walk" assumes safe walkable streets, weather-appropriate clothing, decent shoes, and the kind of neighbourhood where walking for exercise doesn't read as suspicious. Gym membership, sports club fees, swimming pool access, equipment, and even the petrol to get to a park are real costs for patients on the lowest incomes. The structural reframing is that physical activity is, in practice, easier to access for those with money than for those without.

**The built environment matters.** NZ urban planning over the last fifty years has been dominated by car-dependent suburban development, with relatively few neighbourhoods built for walking or cycling as primary transport. Lower-decile neighbourhoods often have poorer pedestrian infrastructure, fewer parks, longer distances to public amenities, and higher traffic volumes. The "active transport" gap in children mirrors this  -  kids in walkable neighbourhoods walk to school; kids in car-dependent neighbourhoods are driven.

**Cold houses are an NZ-specific issue.** Homes that are cold, damp, and difficult to heat affect physical activity in two specific ways: they make exercising indoors unpleasant or impossible, and they predispose to respiratory illness, joint pain, and fatigue that further reduce activity capacity. The He Kāinga Oranga housing research programme at Otago has documented these effects extensively over two decades [12]. The structural intervention here is not exercise advice; it is housing.

**Disability and chronic illness compound the picture.** Patients with chronic pain, mobility limitations, fatigue conditions, mental illness, or significant comorbidity have legitimate physiological reasons that activity is harder. The standard advice tends to assume an able-bodied baseline that many patients in primary care don't actually have. The clinical work is finding the achievable form of activity for the actual body in front of you, not generic advice that doesn't apply.

**The cultural framing of "exercise" can itself be exclusionary.** Working-class, manual-labour, and rural patients often correctly observe that "exercise" as a leisure activity is largely a middle-class cultural artefact  -  that their grandparents, who walked everywhere and did physical work, did not do "exercise" and were healthier than current populations. The advice to "exercise" can land as a class-coded prescription rather than a clinical recommendation. Reframing in terms of "movement" or "activity"  -  which honours the activity built into manual work and active daily life  -  can land better.

The structural implication is uncomfortable: most of what determines population-level activity levels is set by housing, urban planning, work conditions, and economic policy, not by what GPs say in clinic. This is true and is not a reason to do nothing in clinic; it is a reason to be honest about scale.

## Part V  -  What helps in clinic

Within those limits, there is meaningful work the consult can do.

**The Green Prescription** remains the most NZ-appropriate primary care intervention. The programme  -  established in 1998, supported by structured RCT evidence beginning with Elley et al's 2003 *BMJ* cluster RCT [13], and reaffirmed by long-term effectiveness data including Hamlin et al 2016 [14]  -  pairs a GP/practice-nurse referral with telephone-based follow-up from a regional sports trust counsellor. The 12-month effectiveness data show modest but real increases in activity, with one of the better cost-effectiveness profiles among preventive interventions [15]. Roughly 40,000 referrals per year are written by NZ clinicians. The programme is publicly funded, free for patients, and structurally addresses one of the largest gaps in standard activity advice  -  the absence of follow-up.

**Brief intervention works, modestly.** A simple verbal recommendation from the GP, taking thirty seconds, produces a small but measurable increase in patient activity in the subsequent months [13]. The advice does not need to be elaborate to have effect. The classic 5As framework (Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange) is reasonable scaffolding, though the brief version  -  ask whether they're active, advise that increasing activity would help with their specific condition, ask whether they'd like a Green Prescription referral  -  does most of the work.

**Walking is the most defensible default recommendation.** It is free, requires no equipment beyond shoes, can be done in almost any environment, scales easily with the patient's current capacity, has the broadest evidence base, and avoids the cultural and class-coded baggage of "exercise". For most sedentary patients, "more walking" is the right starting point regardless of long-term goals.

**The frame that probably shouldn't be used: weight loss.** Activity has substantial all-cause mortality and disease-prevention benefits that are largely independent of weight change [1]. Counselling activity primarily as a weight-loss strategy is empirically misleading  -  the calorie deficit produced by typical activity volumes is small compared with dietary intake, and weight loss is not the mechanism through which activity reduces cardiovascular or cancer mortality. It is also clinically risky in patients vulnerable to disordered eating, where prescribed exercise can become compulsive or punitive.

**Body image and eating disorder considerations.** A meaningful minority of patients  -  particularly women, particularly adolescents and young adults, particularly those with histories of restriction or compulsive exercise  -  are at risk of disordered relationships with activity if it is framed in shame or weight-loss terms. The right clinical posture is to frame activity in terms of function, energy, mood, and disease prevention; to avoid the language of "burning calories" or "earning food"; to ask explicitly about exercise compulsion in patients you suspect may be at risk; and to be willing to *de-prescribe* activity in patients whose relationship with it has become disordered. The standard advice doesn't apply uniformly; the clinical work is knowing which patients need encouragement and which need a different conversation.

**Starting where the patient is.** Specific, concrete, achievable goals work better than generic recommendations. "Could you walk for ten minutes after dinner three nights a week?" is a more useful target than "you should aim for 150 minutes a week." Once a small target is achieved consistently, it can be built on. Most behavioural change happens incrementally; large prescriptions usually fail.

**Sedentary interruption is its own intervention.** Asking the patient how much they sit during the day (often surprisingly large numbers when prompted), and discussing strategies to interrupt it  -  standing for phone calls, walking meetings, a thirty-minute reminder to stand  -  addresses a separate risk factor and is often more achievable than adding scheduled exercise.

**Recognising that follow-up matters more than the initial advice.** Single-session counselling without follow-up has small effects. Counselling combined with structured follow-up (Green Prescription, practice nurse review, return appointment) has larger effects. The system of follow-up is the active ingredient.

## A working position

The honest version of "encouraging physical activity" is not a wellness lecture. It is structural. The patient's activity is mostly determined by their housing, their work, their neighbourhood, their income, their physical and mental health, and their accumulated relationship with the body  -  and only marginally by what is said to them in a fifteen-minute consultation.

What the consult can usefully do, within those limits, is roughly this. Mention activity as a clinical recommendation, briefly and without moralising. Frame it in terms of function and disease prevention rather than weight or appearance. Default to walking unless there's a specific reason to recommend something else. Acknowledge the structural constraints honestly when the patient raises them  -  don't moralise about people who can't do what is being recommended. Refer to Green Prescription where appropriate, because the follow-up is the active ingredient. Watch for disordered exercise patterns and adjust the framing accordingly. Address sedentary time as a separate clinical issue from scheduled exercise. Recognise that the population-level work that actually moves the needle  -  housing, urban planning, active transport infrastructure, workplace policy, paid time off  -  happens outside the consultation room.

The advice "you should exercise more" remains true. The clinical work is what comes after.
