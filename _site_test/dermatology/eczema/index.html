Dermatology

Eczema / atopic dermatitis

The most common inflammatory skin condition in primary care. The diagnosis is usually clinical; the management is mostly about emollients done properly and steroids used correctly.

Atopic dermatitis affects around 15–20% of children and 2–5% of adults in New Zealand.1 It is part of the atopic triad alongside asthma and allergic rhinitis, and family history is strongly predictive. The skin barrier dysfunction model — rather than a purely immunological one — has reshaped how we think about prevention and early treatment.

Diagnosis

Clinical. Chronic or relapsing pruritic dermatitis with typical distribution (flexural in older children and adults; facial and extensor in infants) plus at least three of: personal or family history of atopy, dry skin, onset before age 2, visible flexural involvement.2

Stepwise management

  • Step 1 — Emollients, always. Applied at least twice daily to all affected and unaffected skin. This is the foundation. Quantities matter — a child with moderate eczema may need 250 g per week.
  • Step 2 — Mild topical corticosteroid for flares. 1% hydrocortisone for face and flexures. Moderate potency (e.g. betamethasone valerate 0.02%) for body flares.
  • Step 3 — Moderate/potent TCS short courses. For persistent or severe flares, with clear instructions on duration and tapering.
  • Step 4 — Topical calcineurin inhibitors. Tacrolimus or pimecrolimus for facial or sensitive-site eczema where steroid side-effects are a concern.
  • Step 5 — Refer. Dupilumab (PHARMAC-funded with criteria) for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis not controlled by optimised topical therapy.3

What to tell the patient

Steroid phobia is a real barrier to treatment. Patients often under-use topical steroids from fear of skin thinning — reassure clearly. Emollient-first, steroid-second is the frame that helps.
