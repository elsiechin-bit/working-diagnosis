Dermatology

Acne vulgaris

The most common skin condition in people under 30 — and one that is consistently undertreated, particularly when post-inflammatory pigmentation complicates the picture.

Acne results from the interaction of four processes: follicular hyperkeratinisation, excess sebum production under androgen influence, colonisation by Cutibacterium acnes, and the resulting inflammatory cascade. Most presentations in primary care are comedonal (non-inflammatory) or mild-to-moderate papulopustular disease. Nodulocystic acne — deep, painful nodules with significant scarring potential — warrants earlier referral or consideration of isotretinoin. Grading (mild/moderate/severe) drives treatment choice, but patient distress and risk of scarring are equally important parameters.

Treatment by severity

  • Mild (comedonal or few papules) — topical retinoids are the cornerstone: adapalene 0.1% gel is available over-the-counter in New Zealand and is well tolerated. Benzoyl peroxide 2.5–5% reduces C. acnes colonisation and does not contribute to antibiotic resistance. These two are complementary and can be combined (apply benzoyl peroxide in the morning, adapalene at night). Advise patients to start slowly — nightly use every second day initially — to minimise early irritation, and that improvement takes eight to twelve weeks.
  • Moderate (multiple papulopustules, some nodules) — add topical clindamycin 1%, always combined with benzoyl peroxide to reduce resistance selection pressure. Topical clindamycin monotherapy should be avoided. If topical therapy is insufficient after two to three months, oral antibiotics are appropriate: doxycycline 50–100 mg daily is first-line. Limit oral antibiotic courses to three to six months; combining with topical benzoyl peroxide throughout reduces resistance. Avoid concurrent oral and topical antibiotics of different classes.
  • Severe or scarring (nodulocystic, significant scarring, treatment failure) — refer for consideration of isotretinoin. In New Zealand, oral isotretinoin is a PHARMAC Special Authority medication requiring dermatologist initiation. SA criteria include severe nodulocystic acne, significant scarring, or failure of adequate oral antibiotic therapy. Isotretinoin is teratogenic — two forms of contraception are required in females of reproductive potential, and the prescriber's pregnancy prevention obligations are significant.

Hormonal therapy

In females with acne that worsens premenstrually, or with other features of androgen excess (hirsutism, irregular cycles), hormonal options should be considered. Combined oral contraceptives containing an antiandrogen progestogen (e.g., norgestimate, drospirenone) reduce sebum production. Spironolactone 50–100 mg daily has good evidence for acne in females and is particularly useful in adult women who are not candidates for or prefer to avoid the COCP; it is not subsidised in New Zealand for the acne indication specifically but is generally accessible. Spironolactone should not be used in pregnancy and requires monitoring of potassium, particularly in those on ACE inhibitors or with renal impairment.1

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) — the dark marks left after acne lesions resolve — is particularly pronounced in people with Fitzpatrick skin types IV–VI, including many Māori, Pacific, and Asian patients. For some patients, PIH is their primary complaint, more distressing than the active acne itself. Treating active acne aggressively to prevent new lesions is the most effective strategy. For existing PIH, topical azelaic acid 20% (available on prescription) and adapalene both have evidence for reducing pigmentation over several months. Sun protection is essential — UV exposure deepens PIH markedly. Hydroquinone 4% can be effective for persistent PIH but is not widely available over-the-counter in New Zealand. Refer if pigmentation is treatment-resistant or the diagnosis is uncertain (consider melasma, lichen planus pigmentosus).

Practical points

Acne management requires explicit expectation-setting. The three most common reasons for apparent treatment failure are: stopping treatment too soon (minimum eight weeks before judging response), using products inconsistently due to irritation that could be managed by adjusting frequency, and not addressing maintenance (acne usually requires long-term topical therapy, not just a course). Diet has a modest evidence base — high glycaemic load diets and dairy may worsen acne in some individuals, but dietary manipulation is an adjunct, not a replacement for topical therapy.2 Moisturiser and non-comedogenic sunscreen should be incorporated into the routine from the start, as retinoids and benzoyl peroxide both increase photosensitivity and dryness.
