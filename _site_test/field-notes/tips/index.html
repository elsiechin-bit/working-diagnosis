Tips & Tricks

Clinical pearls from the consult room — small things that work, passed on informally and rarely written down.

❍ Dermatology

The burrow ink test for scabies

Apply ink broadly over the suspicious area — a fountain pen or felt-tip works — then wipe off the excess with an alcohol swab. Ink retained in burrow tracks reveals the characteristic linear or S-shaped burrow of Sarcoptes scabiei clearly against the skin. Useful when the rash is atypical or distribution is uncertain. Costs nothing, takes 30 seconds.

Dermatology · Johnston & Sladden, BMJ 2005

❍ Gastroenterology

Gladwrap across the toilet bowl for stool samples

Tell patients to stretch a piece of cling film across the toilet bowl before opening their bowels. The sample collects on the film rather than dispersing into the water, making collection clean and straightforward. Works for the specimen quality needed for calprotectin, C. difficile PCR, or culture. This is the step most labs forget to mention on the request form.

❍ MSK · Neurology

Froment's sign with a piece of paper

Ask the patient to grip a piece of paper between their thumb and index finger while you try to pull it away. In ulnar nerve palsy, adductor pollicis is weak, so the thumb's flexor pollicis longus (median-innervated) compensates — you see the thumb IP joint flex to maintain grip. A positive Froment's sign is specific for ulnar neuropathy at the elbow or wrist. A page of A4 on the desk is all the equipment you need.

❍ Paediatrics

Give the toddler the otoscope first

Before attempting to examine a reluctant toddler, hand them the otoscope with the speculum removed. Let them hold it, look through it, point it at you. By the time you take it back and fit the speculum, you've established yourself as someone who shares things rather than someone who approaches with instruments. Works for the ophthalmoscope too. Doesn't succeed with every child — but it's a better opening than the chase-and-restrain.

❍ Dermatology · Minor procedures

Dermoscopy gel as shave biopsy medium

Ultrasound gel (or dedicated dermoscopy immersion fluid) used as an interface medium for a shave biopsy gives a slightly more controlled slide plane than dry shaving. It doesn't affect the histological specimen, it's on the bench in any room that does dermoscopy, and it reduces the catch-and-drag sensation patients notice with a dry blade. Small tip, modest benefit — worth trying if you do high-volume shave biopsies.

❍ Consultation skills

"What were you hoping I'd say?"

When a patient has pushed back on your explanation, or the stated concern doesn't feel like the whole story, try: "What were you hoping I'd say today?" It's more direct than a full ICE framework in a tight consult, and often cuts straight to the hidden agenda — the reassurance sought, the referral hoped for, the diagnosis feared but not named. It signals you're interested in their agenda without being procedural about it.
