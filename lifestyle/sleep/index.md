    <p class="pillar-label">Lifestyle Medicine</p>
    <h1>Sleep.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">Undervalued, underassessed, and — in the case of insomnia — often managed with a prescription when a conversation would have been more effective.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Chronic sleep insufficiency is not a personality trait. It is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, depression, impaired immune function, and all-cause mortality — with a consistent signal in the literature at less than six hours of sleep per night.<sup>1</sup> Adults need seven to nine hours; the proportion who reliably achieve this is substantially lower than most people assume. In New Zealand, shift work, caregiving demands, and screen-based light exposure in the evening are among the most common modifiable contributors.
    </p>

    <p>
      When assessing a patient with sleep difficulties, distinguish between the three commonest presentations: difficulty initiating sleep (sleep-onset insomnia, often anxiety-driven), difficulty maintaining sleep (frequent waking, common in depression and pain), and early morning awakening (classic in melancholic depression, but also in obstructive sleep apnoea). Daytime somnolence, witnessed apnoeas, snoring, and morning headaches point toward OSA rather than behavioural insomnia — a distinction with entirely different management.
    </p>

    <p>
      Obstructive sleep apnoea is significantly underdiagnosed in New Zealand primary care. The Epworth Sleepiness Scale is a quick validated screen; the STOP-BANG questionnaire is well suited to identifying high-risk patients before referral for diagnostic sleep study. OSA prevalence is markedly higher in Māori and Pacific peoples, and in people with obesity — active screening in these groups is warranted.<sup>2</sup> Where overnight oximetry is available in primary care, it can identify moderate-to-severe OSA efficiently ahead of formal polysomnography.
    </p>

    <p>
      For chronic insomnia disorder — defined as difficulty sleeping at least three nights per week for at least three months, causing daytime impairment — the first-line treatment is Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I), not pharmacotherapy.<sup>3</sup> CBT-I consistently outperforms sedative hypnotics at six-month follow-up and produces durable improvements without dependence. It includes sleep restriction, stimulus control, sleep hygiene, and cognitive restructuring. It can be delivered face-to-face or via validated online programmes (Sleepio and the US DoD's CBT-I Coach app are both well-evidenced). Referring to a clinical psychologist trained in CBT-I is the gold-standard referral.
    </p>

    <p>
      Sleep hygiene advice alone — fixed wake time, limiting alcohol, reducing screen exposure before bed, cool dark room — has minimal evidence as a standalone treatment for established insomnia, but it is still worth delivering as adjunct guidance and is appropriate first-line advice for mild or subclinical difficulties. The most evidence-based single recommendation is a consistent wake time, seven days a week, regardless of how poorly the person slept. Sleeping in after a bad night perpetuates the cycle.
    </p>

    <p>
      When pharmacotherapy is needed — for short-term insomnia around a specific stressor, or bridging while CBT-I is initiated — low-dose melatonin (0.5–1 mg, thirty minutes before intended sleep time) is the safest option and is appropriate in older adults. Temazepam should be used sparingly, at the lowest effective dose, for the shortest practical duration, and with an explicit plan for cessation. It is not appropriate for long-term management. Avoid prescribing sedating antihistamines — they impair sleep architecture, cause morning-after impairment, and have meaningful anticholinergic burden in older adults.
    </p>
