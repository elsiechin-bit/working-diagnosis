    <p class="pillar-label">Lifestyle Medicine</p>
    <h1>Alcohol.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">The most widely used recreational drug in New Zealand, the most socially normalised, and the one most likely to be underreported in a GP consultation.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      New Zealand has one of the highest rates of hazardous alcohol use in the OECD.<sup>1</sup> Approximately one in five New Zealand adults drink at hazardous levels — defined by the AUDIT-C as a score of 5 or above in women and 6 or above in men. Hazardous use is not the same as dependence; most people drinking at harmful levels are not physically dependent, are not aware their consumption is a health risk, and have never been asked about it in a healthcare setting. This is where brief intervention has its effect.
    </p>

    <p>
      The AUDIT-C (the first three questions of the full Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test) is a validated three-question screen with good sensitivity and specificity for hazardous and harmful use in primary care settings.<sup>2</sup> It takes under a minute and can be administered as part of a health review or annual check. A positive screen should prompt completion of the full ten-item AUDIT to characterise the level of use and assess for dependence. The CAGE questionnaire is less sensitive than AUDIT and is no longer the recommended screening tool.
    </p>

    <p>
      The New Zealand guidelines on low-risk alcohol use, published in 2024, align with the Canadian and Australian move toward substantially lower thresholds than earlier guidance.<sup>3</sup> The key messages: no amount of alcohol is completely risk-free; the risk of cancer (particularly breast, bowel, liver, and oropharyngeal) begins at low levels of use; and the previous thresholds of "two standard drinks per day" should no longer be framed as "safe." These are not fringe positions — they reflect a significant shift in the evidence base on alcohol and cancer that has consolidated over the past decade.
    </p>

    <p>
      Brief intervention (BI) for hazardous alcohol use is one of the most cost-effective interventions in primary care. A five-minute structured conversation using the FRAMES framework (Feedback, Responsibility, Advice, Menu of options, Empathy, Self-efficacy) reliably reduces alcohol consumption at six-month follow-up in non-dependent drinkers.<sup>4</sup> The goal is not necessarily abstinence — a reduction to lower-risk levels is a meaningful clinical outcome. The Alcohol Drug Helpline (0800 787 797) and the ALAC resources at alcohol.org.nz provide patient-facing support.
    </p>

    <p>
      Alcohol dependence — characterised by loss of control over use, withdrawal phenomena, and continued use despite harm — requires more intensive management. Assess for withdrawal risk using the CIWA-Ar scale before attempting detoxification; significant withdrawal (CIWA ≥10) is a medical emergency and should be managed with supervised benzodiazepine therapy, thiamine replacement, and close monitoring. Community-managed detoxification is appropriate for uncomplicated cases; hospital admission is warranted when there is a history of withdrawal seizures or delirium tremens, significant comorbidity, or poor social support. Thiamine 300 mg IM or IV before any glucose-containing fluid is mandatory in any nutritionally depleted patient — Wernicke's encephalopathy is preventable and devastating.
    </p>

    <p>
      Pharmacological relapse prevention after detoxification is underutilised in New Zealand practice. Naltrexone 50 mg daily reduces cravings and the reward response to alcohol and has a good evidence base in motivated patients; it is available in New Zealand but not subsidised, limiting access. Acamprosate reduces glutamate-mediated excitability during abstinence and is subsidised under Special Authority criteria for post-detoxification relapse prevention. Disulfiram remains an option for highly motivated patients with good social support. Referral to addiction services, community alcohol and drug teams (CAD teams), or SMART Recovery should accompany any pharmacological treatment.
    </p>
