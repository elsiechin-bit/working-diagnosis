<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Current NZ/WHO guidance: 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, or 75-150 minutes of vigorous-intensity, plus muscle-strengthening on 2 or more days. These are minimums, not targets - more is better up to a plateau.</li>
    <li>Sedentary behaviour carries independent metabolic risk even in people who meet activity guidelines. Breaking up sitting time is a distinct clinical goal, not just a proxy for overall activity.</li>
    <li>For older adults: balance and falls-prevention training has its own recommendation. The 5-times sit-to-stand test (timed) is a validated, zero-equipment functional proxy worth incorporating into routine review.</li>
    <li>Physical activity is effective for: type 2 diabetes prevention and management, cardiovascular risk reduction, depression, anxiety, cognitive decline, osteoporosis, and cancer recurrence. Prescribe it with the same specificity as a drug.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Physical inactivity is one of the leading modifiable risk factors for non-communicable disease globally. Yet physical activity prescription in general practice remains inconsistent and often vague. "Exercise more" is not a prescription. The evidence base supports specific, dose-dependent recommendations that produce measurable outcomes - and the GP visit is one of the most effective places to deliver them.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Activity recommendations by age group</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Adults (18-64 years):</strong> At least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming at a conversational pace), or 75-150 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity (running, vigorous cycling, competitive sport), or an equivalent combination. Muscle-strengthening activities on 2 or more days per week targeting all major muscle groups.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Older adults (65 and over):</strong> Same aerobic and strengthening recommendations, with the addition of multicomponent activity that emphasises functional balance and fall prevention on 3 or more days per week. Frail older adults should be active to the extent their capacity allows - no minimum threshold should be used as a reason to advise inactivity.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Children and young people (5-17 years):</strong> At least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity daily, with vigorous-intensity aerobic and muscle- and bone-strengthening activities at least 3 days per week.
  </p>
  <p>
    Any activity is better than none. For patients currently inactive, the greatest health gains come from moving from sedentary to lightly active, not from moving from active to very active.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Sedentary behaviour: an independent risk</p>
  <p>
    Prolonged sitting carries independent metabolic and cardiovascular risk, even in people who meet the aerobic activity guidelines. The mechanisms are distinct from those of low overall activity: prolonged sitting impairs lipoprotein lipase activity, reduces postprandial glucose disposal, and reduces lower limb blood flow.
  </p>
  <p>
    The practical recommendation is to interrupt prolonged sitting. Breaking up sitting time every 30-60 minutes with brief standing or walking - even 2-3 minutes - attenuates the postprandial metabolic effects of prolonged sitting. This is a distinct goal from meeting the weekly activity guidelines, not a substitute for them.
  </p>
  <p>
    For desk workers, standing desks and walk-and-talk meetings are the most practical strategies. For patients who are housebound or have limited mobility, brief standing activities count - the evidence applies across the mobility spectrum.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Assessment in older adults</p>
  <p>
    Functional assessment of physical capacity is a core component of geriatric review in primary care. The 5-times sit-to-stand test (time taken to rise from a chair and sit back down 5 consecutive times, without using arms) is a validated proxy for lower limb strength, balance, and fall risk. It requires no equipment and takes under 2 minutes. A time above 12 seconds in adults under 70, or above 15 seconds in adults over 70, is associated with elevated fall risk and warrants intervention.
  </p>
  <p>
    Gait speed is equally informative: usual pace over 4 metres. A gait speed below 0.8 m/s is associated with frailty, hospitalisation risk, and mortality independently of other clinical features.
  </p>
  <p>
    Refer frail older adults to physiotherapy for a supervised progressive exercise programme, or to an ACC-funded community exercise programme (Green Prescription, Otago Programme) where available. The Green Prescription (GRx) is an NZ-specific programme supporting patients to become more active through community-based support.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Disease-specific evidence and prescribing</p>
  <p>
    Physical activity prescription should be as specific as a drug prescription: type of activity, intensity, frequency, duration, and progression. "Try to walk more" is not a prescription.
  </p>
  <p>
    Key disease-specific evidence: type 2 diabetes - structured exercise improves HbA1c by approximately 0.7% independently of weight loss; both aerobic and resistance training are effective. Depression and anxiety - aerobic exercise at moderate intensity, 3-5 times per week, produces effects comparable to antidepressants in mild-to-moderate depression. Cardiovascular disease - cardiac rehabilitation exercise programmes reduce all-cause mortality by approximately 20% post-MI. Osteoporosis - weight-bearing and resistance exercise reduce fracture risk independent of bone density effects. Dementia prevention - physical activity reduces incident dementia risk by approximately 30%.
  </p>
  <p>
    The Green Prescription (GRx) is funded by the Ministry of Health and available through regional sport trusts. GPs can issue a Green Prescription to eligible patients; a health and wellness coach then provides support over 3-6 months. It is particularly effective for patients who benefit from structured follow-up and social connection.
  </p>
</div>
