The Fog Machine.

Most sedating substances work the same way: they slow down your central nervous system and make your brain quieter. The problem is that these effects stack. One thing might be unremarkable. Three things together is a different situation entirely -- and one that is often harder to notice from the inside.

What is actually happening in your brain?

Your brain runs on electrical signals. Neurons talk to each other by releasing chemicals that either excite or inhibit the next neuron in line. Sedating drugs -- and sleep deprivation -- tip the balance toward inhibition. Everything slows: reaction time, decision-making, coordination, emotional regulation.

Most of the drugs in this list work through one of two mechanisms. Benzodiazepines, alcohol, and Z-drugs boost GABA, the brain's main inhibitory signal. Opioids work through a different pathway but with the same net effect: quieter, slower. First-generation antihistamines block histamine in the brain, which is partly what keeps you awake. Sedating antidepressants often combine histamine blockade with other effects.

When you combine these substances, their effects don't just add -- they can amplify each other. A standard drink affects the GABA system. So does a temazepam. Together, they do considerably more than either would alone.

Sleep deprivation is not just tiredness. After 17 hours without sleep, cognitive performance is comparable to a blood alcohol level of around 0.05% -- the legal limit for driving in New Zealand. After 24 hours awake, the comparison is approximately 0.10%.1 Crucially, sleep-deprived people consistently underestimate how impaired they are.
Why the combination is the problem

Alcohol plus a benzodiazepine isn't just twice as sedating -- it's more. Both drugs enhance GABA activity, but through slightly different receptor sites, and the combined effect on breathing suppression is disproportionately large. This is the mechanism behind most sedative overdose deaths.

Tolerance to effect vs impairment

Regular users of sedating medications often feel less drowsy than when they started. But behavioural testing shows their reaction times and coordination remain impaired even after tolerance to the subjective sedation develops. Feeling fine and being fine are not the same.

Cannabis and driving

Cannabis impairs reaction time, tracking ability, and divided attention for 3--4 hours after smoking. Edibles have unpredictable onset and can impair for 8--12 hours. The impairment is real even in experienced users, though it may be less obvious to them.2

Zopiclone the morning after

Zopiclone (Imovane) has a half-life of 5--6 hours. It is detectable in blood for 12 hours or more. Studies show meaningful driving impairment the morning after a standard dose, particularly in adults over 60.3

Opioids and benzodiazepines together are the most dangerous commonly prescribed drug combination. They work through different but complementary mechanisms to suppress breathing: opioids suppress the brainstem's drive to breathe; benzodiazepines reduce the arousal response that would normally wake you if your oxygen levels drop. Together, they remove both the problem and the alarm.

In Aotearoa New Zealand and internationally, this combination is responsible for the majority of prescription drug overdose deaths. If you are prescribed both and are about to drink alcohol, the risk escalates further. Naloxone (Nyxoid nasal spray) is available in NZ without prescription at some pharmacies -- if you or a family member is on opioids, it is worth having.

New Zealand legal position Under section 56 of the Land Transport Act 1998, it is an offence to drive while impaired by any drug, including prescription medications, cannabis, alcohol, or their combination. Impairment is assessed by police using standardised field sobriety tests and, where indicated, compulsory blood or urine testing. A blood alcohol limit of 50 mg/100 mL applies for drivers aged 20 and over (zero for those under 20 or on restricted/learner licences). There is no legal blood limit for other drugs -- impairment itself is the offence.

New Zealand's drug-driving law does not require a specific threshold level of a drug in your blood. If a Police officer has reasonable grounds to believe you are impaired, you can be required to undergo testing. The NZ Transport Agency estimates that drug impairment (including from prescription medications) is a factor in approximately 12% of serious crashes.4

If you are on regular sedating medication, discuss with your GP whether it is safe to drive. Your GP can assess your individual situation, and in some cases driving may be restricted. This is not a punitive process -- it is part of safe prescribing.

If your sedation score is above 2, the safest options are walking, a taxi or rideshare (Uber, Ola), a designated sober driver, or public transport. If you are in a rural area and this is difficult, waiting -- ideally with food and water, and lying down -- is better than driving.

The NZ Police website and the NZTA DriveCheck tool have further guidance. If you are unsure about a specific medication and driving, your GP or pharmacist is the right first call -- not Google, which tends to give answers calibrated to American law.

If someone you know is severely sedated and cannot be roused normally, call 111. This is particularly important if they have taken opioids in any combination with alcohol or benzodiazepines.

The sedation units in this tool are a simplified educational index, not a clinical measurement. They are based on the relative sedative properties of these substances as described in pharmacology literature, and scaled to make the additive and synergistic effects intuitive. The thresholds (mild, moderate, etc.) correspond to the ranges at which driving impairment has been demonstrated in controlled studies.
