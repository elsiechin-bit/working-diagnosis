<p>
      The clinical frame matters. Obesity is a chronic, relapsing condition driven by interactions between genetic predisposition, hormonal regulation, the food environment, sleep, stress, medications, and socioeconomic factors  -  not a failure of personal discipline. Treating it as the latter produces shame without clinical benefit and causes people to avoid care. The language of weight management in general practice has shifted toward person-centred, non-stigmatising framing: "weight" rather than "obese," discussing health impacts rather than appearance, and always asking permission before raising the topic. This is not performative sensitivity  -  stigmatising language is associated with worse outcomes.<sup>1</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
      BMI is a useful population-level epidemiological tool and a poor individual clinical measure. It does not capture fat distribution, muscle mass, or metabolic risk with sufficient precision to guide individual management. Waist circumference is more strongly associated with cardiovascular and metabolic risk than BMI alone and is straightforward to measure: a cut-off of ≥94 cm in men and ≥80 cm in women (≥90 cm and ≥80 cm for Asian, Māori, and Pacific patients using ethnicity-adjusted thresholds) identifies elevated metabolic risk.<sup>2</sup> It is worth knowing which cut-offs apply to which patients. Māori and Pacific peoples are at significantly higher metabolic risk at any given BMI, which means standard thresholds underestimate risk in these populations.
    </p>

    <p>
      Metabolic syndrome  -  the clustering of central adiposity, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and impaired glucose metabolism  -  identifies individuals at substantially elevated cardiovascular and type 2 diabetes risk. Treatment is aimed at each component, but weight loss (even modest, 5–10% of body weight) produces meaningful improvements across all parameters and should be the primary focus where achievable. The response to weight loss is disproportionate: a 5% reduction in body weight produces roughly 30–50% improvement in insulin sensitivity, significant blood pressure reduction, and improved lipid profiles.<sup>3</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
      Lifestyle intervention remains the foundation of management: structured dietary change (Mediterranean-pattern and low-carbohydrate diets both have evidence for metabolic benefit), increased physical activity (150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week as a minimum  -  see the <a href="/lifestyle/physical-activity/">physical activity page</a>), sleep optimisation, and alcohol reduction. The combination of dietary change and exercise consistently outperforms either alone. In practice, the most sustainable approach is the one the patient is willing to maintain  -  a dietary pattern that causes misery and is abandoned after three months is less useful than a modest improvement that continues.
    </p>

    <p>
      Pharmacological treatment has changed substantially in the past five years. GLP-1 receptor agonists  -  originally developed for type 2 diabetes  -  have robust evidence for weight reduction and cardiovascular risk reduction in people with obesity. Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) produces mean weight loss of 12–15% in clinical trials, with significant improvements in cardiovascular endpoints in people with established CVD and overweight or obesity.<sup>4</sup> In New Zealand, semaglutide is currently subsidised only for type 2 diabetes indications; access for weight management alone remains limited by cost. This is a rapidly evolving area  -  expect subsidy landscape changes.
    </p>

    <p>
      Bariatric surgery produces the most durable long-term weight loss and is the most effective treatment for severe obesity with metabolic comorbidities. Referral criteria in New Zealand include BMI ≥40 or BMI ≥35 with significant comorbidity, failure of supervised lifestyle and medical management, and psychological clearance. Waitlists are long in the public system; private options are available but costly. In patients who have had bariatric surgery, nutritional deficiencies  -  iron, B12, thiamine, calcium, and vitamin D  -  require active monitoring and supplementation long-term.
    </p>
