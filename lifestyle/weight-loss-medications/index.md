# Medications for Weight Management

❍ *When lifestyle changes need a hand*

Weight management medications are not a quick fix, and they are not for everyone. But for the right person, they can make a meaningful difference — shifting the metabolic dial enough to make sustained lifestyle change more achievable, and in some cases substantially reducing the health risks that come with significant excess weight. This page explains the options available in New Zealand, what the evidence actually shows, and what a realistic conversation with your GP might look like.

---

## A starting point: what medications can and cannot do

No medication works in isolation. Every weight management drug studied has been trialled alongside dietary and behavioural support — and that is where most of the benefit lives. What medications can do is make the hard work of changing your eating and activity habits somewhat easier: they can reduce appetite, increase the feeling of fullness, or reduce the amount of fat your gut absorbs. What they cannot do is change the underlying environment, habits, or circumstances that drive weight gain in the first place.

They are also not permanent solutions in most cases. Stopping medication typically leads to partial or complete weight regain over time, which is not a personal failure — it reflects the fact that obesity involves persistent changes in the hormones and brain circuits that regulate weight, not simply a lack of discipline.¹

---

## Who might be considered for medication?

There is no single threshold, and decisions are made in conversation with your GP. General criteria that guide most prescribing are:

A body mass index (BMI) of 30 kg/m² or above, or a BMI of 27 kg/m² or above in someone with a weight-related health condition such as type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, obstructive sleep apnoea, or significant joint disease. BMI has real limitations as a measure of health — it does not account for muscle mass, ethnicity, or where body fat is distributed — and your GP will consider the broader picture rather than treating a number as a binary threshold.

Medications are generally only considered after meaningful attempts at dietary change and increased physical activity. They work alongside those efforts, not instead of them.

Certain conditions and medications can make some options unsuitable. Your GP will review your full history before prescribing.

## The options in New Zealand

### Orlistat (Xenical)

**How it works:** Orlistat blocks an enzyme in your gut called pancreatic lipase, which is responsible for breaking down dietary fat. Roughly a third of the fat you eat passes through undigested rather than being absorbed. It does not affect appetite.

**What the evidence shows:** Over one year, orlistat produces an average additional weight loss of around 2.9 kg compared with placebo when used alongside dietary advice.² The XENDOS trial, which followed participants for four years, found orlistat reduced progression from pre-diabetes to type 2 diabetes, though much of this benefit was attributable to the weight loss itself.³ Weight loss with orlistat is modest on average, though some people respond considerably better than others.

**Side effects:** The main ones are gastrointestinal — loose fatty stools, urgency, oily spotting, and flatulence. These are a direct consequence of unabsorbed fat reaching the large bowel, and they are significantly worse if you eat a high-fat meal. Many people find this has a useful behavioural effect (it discourages high-fat eating), while others find it simply unacceptable. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are less well absorbed, so a daily multivitamin taken at a different time to the medication is sensible.

**Availability in New Zealand:** Orlistat 120 mg is available on prescription and is Pharmac-funded with criteria — your GP can advise whether you meet them. It is also available over the counter at a lower dose (60 mg, marketed as Alli), though this requires pharmacist supervision and is less effective than the prescription dose.

### Liraglutide (Saxenda)

**How it works:** Liraglutide is a GLP-1 receptor agonist — a class of drug that mimics the action of a gut hormone called glucagon-like peptide 1, which is released after eating. It slows gastric emptying, increases feelings of fullness, and acts on appetite-regulating centres in the brain. At the 3 mg daily dose used for weight management (compared with the lower doses used in type 2 diabetes), it produces meaningful appetite suppression in most people.

**What the evidence shows:** The SCALE trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine, found that over 56 weeks, liraglutide 3 mg produced an average weight loss of 8.4% of body weight, compared with 2.8% in the placebo group.⁴ Roughly 63% of participants lost at least 5% of their body weight, versus 27% on placebo. People with pre-diabetes who took liraglutide also had a significantly lower rate of progression to type 2 diabetes.

**Side effects:** Nausea is the most common, affecting the majority of people, particularly in the first few weeks. It typically improves as the dose is increased gradually over several weeks. Vomiting, constipation, and diarrhoea also occur. Rare but more serious risks include pancreatitis (inflammation of the pancreas) and, based on animal data, a possible signal for thyroid C-cell tumours — liraglutide is contraindicated in people with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2. Gallstones are more common with any rapid weight loss.

**Availability in New Zealand:** Liraglutide 3 mg (Saxenda) is not currently Pharmac-funded for weight management in New Zealand and must be paid for privately, making it expensive for many people. It requires a prescription. Your GP can assess whether it is appropriate and discuss the cost.

### Semaglutide (Ozempic / Wegovy)

**How it works:** Semaglutide is also a GLP-1 receptor agonist, structurally similar to liraglutide but with a longer half-life, allowing once-weekly rather than daily injection. At high doses, its appetite-suppressing and weight-loss effects are substantially greater than liraglutide.

**What the evidence shows:** The STEP 1 trial, published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2021, found that semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly (the dose used for weight management, marketed as Wegovy) produced an average weight loss of 14.9% of body weight over 68 weeks, compared with 2.4% with placebo.⁵ Nearly a third of participants lost more than 20% of their body weight. This represents the largest effect size of any currently available weight management medication, and approaches the results seen with bariatric surgery in some individuals.

**Side effects:** Similar to liraglutide — nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, and constipation are common, particularly during dose escalation. The same contraindications around thyroid cancer history apply. Pancreatitis risk exists but appears low. There is ongoing interest in potential effects on muscle mass with rapid weight loss, and adequate protein intake during treatment is generally recommended.

**Availability in New Zealand:** This is the area of most rapid change, and your GP will have the most current information. Ozempic (semaglutide 0.5–2 mg weekly) is Pharmac-funded for type 2 diabetes management with specific criteria. Wegovy (semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly, licensed specifically for obesity) had limited availability in New Zealand as of early 2025 due to global supply constraints, and is not Pharmac-funded for weight management. Prescribing of Ozempic off-label for weight loss without a diabetes indication has also been a point of active discussion, given supply pressures on people who need it for diabetes. This is a conversation to have with your GP, not to navigate alone.

---

### Phentermine

**How it works:** Phentermine is a sympathomimetic stimulant — related to amphetamines — that suppresses appetite by increasing the release of noradrenaline in the brain. It is one of the oldest weight management drugs available.

**What the evidence shows:** Phentermine produces modest short-term weight loss, typically 3–5 kg more than placebo over 12 weeks.⁶ There are few long-term trials because regulatory approval historically restricted it to short-term use (typically up to 12 weeks). It tends to lose effectiveness over time as the body adapts.

**Side effects:** Increased heart rate and blood pressure, insomnia, dry mouth, restlessness, and constipation are common. It is not suitable for people with cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, hyperthyroidism, or a history of stimulant misuse. Because it is a Schedule 4 controlled substance in New Zealand, prescribing is more tightly regulated than for other weight management options.

**Availability in New Zealand:** Available on prescription for short-term use. Not commonly used as a first-line option given the limitations of short-term-only evidence and the stimulant side effect profile. Some private weight management clinics prescribe it; its use in public primary care is less common.

## A note on tirzepatide and newer agents

Tirzepatide (Mounjaro) is a dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist that has shown even larger average weight losses than semaglutide in the SURMOUNT trial programme — around 20–22% of body weight with the highest doses.⁷ As of early 2025 it was approved in New Zealand for type 2 diabetes but not yet widely available or funded for weight management. This space is evolving quickly, and the treatment landscape in a year or two will likely look different from today.

## What to expect if you start a weight loss medication

**It is not instant.** Most medications take weeks to reach therapeutic effect, and full weight loss response is typically assessed at 12–16 weeks. Starting a GLP-1 agonist involves a gradual dose titration over several weeks to manage nausea.

**Not everyone responds.** A meaningful proportion of people lose little weight on any given medication. If you have not lost at least 5% of your body weight after three to six months at the full dose, it is generally not worth continuing that particular medication — there are other options to discuss.

**Stopping usually means regaining weight.** Multiple studies following people who stopped semaglutide after 68 weeks found they regained most of the weight within a year.⁸ This is not inevitable for everyone, particularly if sustainable lifestyle changes are embedded during the treatment period, but it is the realistic expectation for most people. Whether long-term or intermittent use makes sense is a conversation to have with your GP.

**Side effects are common in the early weeks.** Particularly with GLP-1 agonists, nausea during dose escalation is nearly universal. For most people it settles; for some it does not and discontinuation is necessary. Eating smaller, lower-fat meals and avoiding lying down after eating can help.

## Practical considerations

**Cost:** Access to the more effective modern medications (particularly semaglutide and liraglutide for weight management) is largely unfunded in New Zealand, making them unaffordable for many people. Orlistat has funded access. The equity implications of this are significant and worth raising with your GP — if cost is a barrier, say so.

**Monitoring:** Most medications warrant periodic follow-up — blood pressure and pulse checks with phentermine, liver function considerations with orlistat in some guidelines, and general review of response and tolerability for all.

**Other medications and conditions:** Some weight management medications interact with other drugs or are contraindicated in certain conditions. A full medication review before starting is essential.

## Useful resources in New Zealand

BPAC NZ publishes prescribing guidance for GPs that is updated periodically and reflects New Zealand's funding environment. Pharmac's website lists funded medicines and their criteria. Your GP can also refer you to a dietitian through primary care, which is the most evidence-based complement to any pharmacological approach.
