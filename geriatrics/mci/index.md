<p>Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) describes a state of objective cognitive decline - measurable on standardised testing - that does not significantly impair daily functioning. It sits between age-related cognitive change and dementia, and it is clinically important because approximately 10-15% of people with MCI will convert to dementia per year, compared with 1-2% of cognitively normal older adults.<sup>1</sup> It also matters because it is often the presentation that prompts families - not patients - to book an appointment.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Diagnosis, workup, and management</p>
  <p>The working definition of MCI requires: subjective cognitive complaint (from the patient or informant), objective cognitive impairment on testing, preserved general functional ability, and the absence of a dementia diagnosis.<sup>2</sup> The MoCA is more sensitive than the MMSE for detecting MCI; a score of 18-25 typically falls in the MCI range, though interpretation requires age and education adjustment. Single-domain MCI (memory-only) carries the highest rate of conversion to Alzheimer's disease; multi-domain MCI has broader implications.</p>
  <p>Reversible contributors must be excluded before MCI is accepted as a diagnosis. The standard workup: thyroid function, B12, folate, FBC, eGFR, HbA1c, calcium, and LFTs. Depression is a particularly important differential: it impairs attention, processing speed, and memory in ways that can closely mimic MCI and should be treated before cognitive test results are interpreted at face value. Sleep disorders, medication effects (especially anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, and opioids), alcohol, and vascular risk factors should all be assessed.</p>
  <p>There is currently no pharmacological treatment proven to slow conversion from MCI to dementia; cholinesterase inhibitors are not indicated at this stage.<sup>3</sup> The most evidence-supported modifiable factors for slowing cognitive decline are: management of cardiovascular risk (hypertension has the strongest evidence), structured aerobic exercise (consistent benefit in RCTs), cognitive engagement, social activity, adequate sleep, and a Mediterranean-pattern diet. Frame these positively - they give the patient something actionable rather than a diagnosis and a waiting room.</p>
  <p>A six-monthly or annual cognitive review is appropriate - using the same tool each time to allow meaningful comparison. Functional change is the key clinical signal: when daily activities become impaired (managing finances, medications, or complex meals), MCI has likely converted to dementia. Use this as the trigger to revisit driving, EPA, and ACP conversations.</p>
</div>
