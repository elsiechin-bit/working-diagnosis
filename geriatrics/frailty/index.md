<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Use the <strong>Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS)</strong> - a 9-point scale scored from a brief functional history in under 3 minutes. CFS ≥5 triggers structured review and flags elevated surgical/procedural risk.</li>
    <li>Frailty is not irreversible. Resistance exercise, adequate protein intake (1.2-1.5 g/kg/day), and medication rationalisation all improve functional trajectory in frail older adults.</li>
    <li>When frailty is identified: quantify it (CFS), address reversible contributors (anaemia, hypothyroidism, malnutrition, pain, depression, polypharmacy), refer to physiotherapy or community exercise.</li>
    <li>Falls risk assessment and driving fitness should be considered from CFS 5 and are mandatory at CFS 6 and above.</li>
    <li>Advance care planning conversations should begin early - ideally at CFS 5 when capacity is intact and decisions can be patient-led.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Frailty is a state of accumulated physiological vulnerability that reduces the body's capacity to withstand stressors. It predicts hospitalisation, prolonged hospital stay, institutionalisation, and mortality independently of age and comorbidity. Recognising it early creates the opportunity to intervene before a preventable deterioration event.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ The Clinical Frailty Scale in practice</p>
  <p>
    The CFS is a nine-point ordinal scale running from 1 (very fit - exercises regularly) to 9 (terminally ill). It is scored from a brief functional history covering mobility, self-care, activity level, and independence in daily tasks. It takes under three minutes, requires no equipment, and correlates well with both the Fried phenotype and the Rockwood cumulative deficit index.<sup>3</sup>
  </p>
  <p>
    Practical thresholds:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>CFS 1-3:</strong> fit to managing well. Monitor, optimise lifestyle, primary prevention.</li>
    <li><strong>CFS 4:</strong> vulnerable - independent in ADLs but symptomatic limitation. Review medications and nutrition.</li>
    <li><strong>CFS 5 (mildly frail):</strong> dependent for instrumental ADLs (shopping, cooking, transport). Trigger structured frailty review. Begin ACP conversations. Assess driving fitness and falls risk.</li>
    <li><strong>CFS 6 (moderately frail):</strong> needs help with all outdoor activities and some indoor ones. Driving assessment mandatory. Residential care planning should be underway.</li>
    <li><strong>CFS 7-8:</strong> severely and very severely frail. Goals of care and advance directive discussions are urgent.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Document the CFS score at each relevant visit. It will follow the patient into secondary care - hospital systems increasingly use it for triage, ICU admission, and peri-operative risk stratification, and your documentation informs those decisions.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Reversible contributors: what to look for</p>
  <p>
    Before accepting frailty as a fixed state, systematically exclude and address reversible contributors:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Anaemia:</strong> FBC with iron studies, B12, folate. Iron deficiency is common and treatable.</li>
    <li><strong>Hypothyroidism:</strong> TFTs at diagnosis and with any unexplained functional decline.</li>
