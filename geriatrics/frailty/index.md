    <h1>Frailty <em>assessment</em>.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">Not a diagnosis — a syndrome. And one that predicts adverse outcomes more reliably than age or disease burden alone.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Frailty is best understood as a state of accumulated physiological vulnerability that reduces the body's capacity to withstand stressors. Two models dominate the literature and both are clinically useful: the Fried phenotype defines frailty as the presence of three or more of five criteria (unintentional weight loss, self-reported exhaustion, weakness by grip strength, slow gait speed, low physical activity); the Rockwood Cumulative Deficit Model operationalises frailty as the proportion of health deficits accumulated across multiple domains.<sup>1,2</sup> In general practice, neither model is used in its pure research form.
    </p>

    <p>
      The Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) is the more practical instrument for a GP consultation. It is a nine-point ordinal scale ranging from 1 (very fit) to 9 (terminally ill), scored from a brief functional history covering mobility, self-care, activity, and independence. It takes less than three minutes and correlates well with both the Fried phenotype and the deficit accumulation index.<sup>3</sup> A CFS of 5 or above (mildly frail — dependent for instrumental activities of daily living) is a reasonable threshold for triggering a structured review and for flagging surgical or procedural risk.
    </p>

    <p>
      The predictive validity of the CFS is well established: higher CFS scores are independently associated with hospitalisation, institutionalisation, prolonged hospital stay, and mortality — including in ICU and peri-operative settings.<sup>3</sup> The scale is increasingly used by hospital systems for triage and treatment decisions, which means your CFS documentation may follow the patient into secondary care.
    </p>

    <p>
      Frailty is not irreversible. This is the part most often omitted in consultations, and it matters. Resistance exercise — even in the very old — consistently improves muscle mass, strength, and functional performance.<sup>4</sup> Protein intake is frequently inadequate in frail older adults, and a target of 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day is supported by evidence, though it requires active discussion given appetite changes and cost.<sup>5</sup> Medication rationalisation can meaningfully reduce falls, sedation, and functional decline. The patient who is frail today is not necessarily the patient who will be frail in six months with active intervention.
    </p>

    <p>
      In practice, the most important things a GP can do when frailty is identified are: quantify it (CFS), address reversible contributors (anaemia, hypothyroidism, malnutrition, pain, depression, polypharmacy), refer to physiotherapy or a community exercise programme, and ensure advance care planning conversations have started. Falls risk assessment and driving fitness should be considered at CFS 5 and are mandatory at CFS 6 and above. Residential care planning conversations should begin early enough to be unhurried.
    </p>

    <p>
      A note on language: many patients find the word "frailty" confronting or stigmatising. It can be worth introducing it as "your body's resilience" or "how well you bounce back from illness" — framing that captures the clinical meaning without the social weight the word sometimes carries.
    </p>
