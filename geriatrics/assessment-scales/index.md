<p>
      The five instruments below cover different dimensions of geriatric health: disease burden (CIRS-G), frailty phenotype (FRAIL scale), basic self-care (Katz ADL), higher-order independence (Lawton IADL), and holistic frailty judgment (Rockwood CFS). Used in sequence, they give a comprehensive snapshot in under 25 minutes. The interactive version is available in the <a href="/workshop/tools/geriatric-assessment/">Geriatric Assessment Toolkit</a>.
    </p>

    <h2>1. CIRS-G: Cumulative Illness Rating Scale - Geriatric</h2>

    <p>
      CIRS-G measures <strong>disease burden and comorbidity complexity</strong> across 14 organ systems. It captures the <em>number</em> and <em>severity</em> of chronic conditions, not function. The 14 domains are: cardiovascular, respiratory, GI (upper and lower), hepatic, renal, GU, MSK, neurologic, psychiatric, endocrine, ENT, and eyes. Each domain is rated 0 (none) to 4 (very severe, life-threatening). The total score sums all severities; the comorbidity index counts domains rated 3 or 4; the adjusted score multiplies the total by (1 + comorbidity index / 14).
    </p>

    <p>Scores interpret as follows:</p>

    | Score | Severity | Clinical implication |
    |-------|----------|----------------------|
    | &le;20 | Mild | Low disease burden; routine preventive care |
    | 21&ndash;40 | Moderate | Multiple conditions; monitor for decline; medication review |
    | &gt;40 | Severe | High complexity; polypharmacy risk; geriatric assessment indicated |

    <p>
      Use CIRS-G as a baseline assessment for any older patient with multiple diagnoses. It predicts hospitalisation, mortality, and nursing home placement.<sup>1,2</sup> A score above 40 should prompt an urgent medication audit and deprescribing review. Plan for 5&ndash;10 minutes to complete.
    </p>

    <h2>2. FRAIL Scale</h2>

    <p>
      The FRAIL scale is a quick frailty phenotype screen that can usually be administered conversationally in under 2 minutes. It covers five yes/no items: <strong>F</strong>atigue (tired most of the time), <strong>R</strong>esistance (cannot climb one flight of stairs unaided), <strong>A</strong>mbulation (walks slowly), <strong>I</strong>llness (more than 5 chronic conditions), and <strong>L</strong>oss of weight (unintentional loss of more than 4.5 kg in the past year). Each positive answer scores 1, giving a total of 0&ndash;5.
    </p>

    | Score | Category | Clinical implication |
    |-------|----------|----------------------|
    | 0&ndash;1 | Robust | No frailty concerns; standard preventive care |
    | 2 | Pre-frail | High risk of progression; counsel on exercise, nutrition, medication review |
    | 3&ndash;5 | Frail | Frailty phenotype present; intensive review indicated |

    <p>
      Derived from the Cardiovascular Health Study cohort and prospectively validated, the FRAIL scale is recommended by the American Geriatrics Society.<sup>3,4</sup> A score of 3 or above should trigger a falls assessment, sarcopenia screen, and nutritional support discussion.
    </p>

    <h2>3. Katz Index of Activities of Daily Living</h2>

    <p>
      The Katz ADL assesses <strong>basic self-care independence</strong> across six domains: bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring (bed to chair), continence, and feeding. Each item is binary (independent or dependent), scored 0&ndash;6 with 6 representing full independence.
    </p>

    | Score | Status | Clinical implication |
    |-------|--------|----------------------|
    | 6 | Fully independent | No ADL support needed |
    | 5 | Independent except bathing | Minor home modifications; consider grab rails |
    | 4 | Independent except bathing and dressing | Assistance needed 1&ndash;2 times per day |
    | 3 | ADL impairment in 3&ndash;4 domains | Needs care co-ordination; home help or day centre |
    | 0&ndash;2 | Severe dependence | Likely requires residential care or 24-hour support |

    <p>
      Developed in 1963, Katz remains the most widely used ADL measure.<sup>5</sup> Repeat it at each major illness or admission: longitudinal decline is a reliable marker of poor prognosis. Any functional loss warrants reassessment of cause (acute illness, medication effect, depression, cognitive decline) before attributing it to ageing. Takes 3&ndash;5 minutes.
    </p>

    <h2>4. Lawton Instrumental Activities of Daily Living</h2>

    <p>
      Lawton IADL measures <strong>higher-order activities</strong> needed for independent living: using a telephone, shopping, cooking, housework, laundry, managing transportation, managing money and bills, and managing medications. Eight binary items, scored 0&ndash;8 with 8 representing full independence.
    </p>

    | Score | Status | Clinical implication |
    |-------|--------|----------------------|
    | 8 | Fully independent | No IADL support needed |
    | 6&ndash;7 | Mostly independent | May need help with complex tasks (finances, medications) |
    | 4&ndash;5 | Significant impairment | Needs care co-ordination, day centre, home support |
    | &le;3 | Severe dependence | Likely requires residential care |

    <p>
      IADL loss typically precedes Katz ADL loss and is often the first sign of cognitive or functional decline.<sup>6</sup> Two items are particularly flagging: loss of <em>medication management</em> is sensitive for early dementia or significant depression; loss of <em>financial management</em> raises concern for cognitive impairment and financial exploitation. Sudden IADL decline should prompt cognitive screening, medication audit, and a social assessment. Takes 3&ndash;5 minutes.
    </p>

    <h2>5. Rockwood Clinical Frailty Scale</h2>

    <p>
      The CFS is a holistic clinical judgment tool: a 9-point ordinal scale assigned by the clinician after a brief functional history covering mobility, self-care, activity level, and independence. It integrates fitness, comorbidity, disability, and cognition into a single score. It correlates well with both the Fried frailty phenotype and the deficit accumulation index and takes under 2 minutes to complete.<sup>7</sup>
    </p>

    | Level | Status | Description |
    |-------|--------|-------------|
    | 1 | Very Fit | Excellent physical and cognitive function; exercises regularly |
    | 2 | Well | Controlled chronic conditions; good activity level |
    | 3 | Managing Well | Multiple conditions well-managed; independent but less fit than 2 |
    | 4 | Vulnerable | Not dependent, but showing slowing - weight loss, exhaustion, low activity, slow gait, or mild cognitive impairment |
    | 5 | Mildly Frail | Dependent for some IADLs (shopping, cooking, finances, medications) but independent in all ADLs |
    | 6 | Moderately Frail | Needs help for some ADLs (dressing, bathing) and most IADLs |
    | 7 | Severely Frail | Completely dependent for ADLs; minimal cognition; requires 24-hour supervision |
    | 8 | Very Severely Frail | Complete dependence; not expected to survive more than 6 months from frailty alone |
    | 9 | Terminally Ill | Life expectancy less than 6 months; palliative care appropriate |

    <p>
      CFS 4 is the clinical inflection point: the patient is still independent but at high risk, and targeted intervention (exercise, polypharmacy review, nutritional support) at this stage can genuinely alter trajectory.<sup>8</sup> CFS 5&ndash;6 warrants MDT involvement including geriatrics, physiotherapy, and occupational therapy. CFS 7&ndash;9 should shift the framing toward advance care planning and goals-of-care conversations. The CFS is increasingly used by hospital services for surgical and procedural risk assessment, which means your documentation may follow the patient into secondary care.
    </p>

    <h2>Using them together</h2>

    <p>
      A practical sequence for a patient presenting with non-specific decline or complex multimorbidity: start with the FRAIL scale (quick screen, 2 minutes) and CFS (holistic judgment, 2 minutes) to establish the frailty picture. Add Katz ADL and Lawton IADL (8 minutes combined) to map functional dependence and identify care needs. If there is polypharmacy or diagnostic complexity, complete CIRS-G last (10 minutes) to quantify disease burden and identify deprescribing opportunities. The interactive <a href="/workshop/tools/geriatric-assessment/">Geriatric Assessment Toolkit</a> runs all five scales and updates a consultation flags panel in real time.
    </p>

    <h2>NZ context</h2>

    <p>
      Canterbury HealthPathways includes a frailty assessment pathway. BPAC's polypharmacy guidance pairs well with a high CIRS-G score. MoH Older People Strategy (2019) emphasises structured assessment before intervention; PHO expectations include frailty screening for patients aged 75 and over. For referral thresholds: CFS 5&ndash;6 typically triggers a geriatrics or aged-care consultation; Katz 0&ndash;3 or CFS 6&ndash;7 is the usual range for rest home assessment; IADL loss with CFS 4&ndash;5 supports a social service referral for community support.
    </p>
