    <h1>Falls &amp; fracture <em>prevention</em>.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">The leading cause of injury hospitalisation in New Zealanders over 65 — and one of the most preventable.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Falls are not random bad luck. They are the predictable outcome of accumulated risk factors acting on a physiologically vulnerable person in an imperfectly adapted environment. In New Zealand, falls are the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in adults over 65, with direct health system costs exceeding $500 million annually.<sup>1</sup> Hip fracture carries a one-year mortality of approximately 20–30% in older adults — comparable to many common cancers — yet the urgency this warrants is rarely reflected in how it is managed in primary care.<sup>2</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
      Risk factor identification is the starting point. A falls history — specifically, any fall in the past year — is the single best predictor of future falls and should be a routine question in annual reviews for patients over 65.<sup>3</sup> Additional risk factors include: gait and balance impairment, lower-limb weakness, cognitive impairment, visual impairment, orthostatic hypotension, high-risk medications (benzodiazepines, sedating antihistamines, anticholinergics, antihypertensives), environmental hazards, and footwear.
    </p>

    <p>
      The Otago Exercise Programme is the best-evidenced falls prevention intervention available in NZ primary care and is directly ACC-funded. It consists of a personalised home exercise programme of progressive strength and balance exercises, delivered by a physiotherapist over four visits and then maintained independently. Cochrane evidence supports a 35% reduction in fall rate and a 32% reduction in falls-related injuries in community-dwelling older adults.<sup>4</sup> It is appropriate for patients who have fallen or are at high risk — including those using walking aids, provided they can follow instructions. The referral is straightforward; ACC funds the initial assessment and follow-up visits.
    </p>

    <p>
      Medication review is the other high-yield intervention. Psychotropic medications — benzodiazepines, sedating antidepressants, antipsychotics, and Z-drugs — approximately double the fall risk and should trigger an explicit deprescribing conversation.<sup>5</sup> Antihypertensives in the context of orthostatic hypotension are a common and underappreciated contributor. Measure lying and standing blood pressure. A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic on standing is clinically significant; it is present in up to 30% of community-dwelling older adults and is often correctable with medication adjustment.
    </p>

    <p>
      Vision assessment should not be overlooked. Cataract — highly prevalent and highly treatable — meaningfully increases falls risk. Home hazard assessment by an occupational therapist is warranted for patients who have had multiple falls or who are frail; ACC also funds this in certain circumstances. Footwear advice (thin, hard-soled, low-heeled shoes with a closed toe) is evidence-based and costs nothing to deliver.
    </p>

    <p>
      Fracture risk should be quantified for all patients who have had a falls-related fracture and for any patient with a CFS ≥5. FRAX (with BMD input where available) and the GARVAN fracture risk calculator are both validated for use in New Zealand populations; GARVAN incorporates falls history, which is a meaningful advantage. Osteoporosis remains significantly undertreated in New Zealand following minimal trauma fracture — see the <a href="/geriatrics/osteoporosis/">osteoporosis page</a> for further detail.
    </p>
