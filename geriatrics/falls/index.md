<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Ask every patient over 65 about falls in the past year at their annual review. A positive history is the single best predictor of future falls.</li>
    <li>Refer high-risk patients to the <strong>Otago Exercise Programme</strong> (ACC-funded, free to patient): 35% reduction in fall rate, 32% reduction in falls-related injuries.<sup>4</sup></li>
    <li>Psychotropic medications approximately double fall risk. Benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, sedating antidepressants, and antipsychotics all warrant an explicit deprescribing conversation.<sup>5</sup></li>
    <li>Measure lying and standing BP at every falls assessment. An orthostatic drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic is present in up to 30% of older adults and is often correctable with medication adjustment.</li>
    <li>Quantify fracture risk with FRAX or GARVAN for any patient with a falls-related fracture or CFS ≥5. GARVAN is preferred as it incorporates falls history.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Falls are not random bad luck. They are the predictable outcome of accumulated risk factors acting on a physiologically vulnerable person in an imperfectly adapted environment. In New Zealand, falls are the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in adults over 65, with direct health system costs exceeding $500 million annually.<sup>1</sup> Hip fracture carries a one-year mortality of approximately 20–30% in older adults, comparable to many common cancers, yet the urgency this warrants is rarely reflected in how it is managed in primary care.<sup>2</sup>
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Risk factor assessment</p>
  <p>A falls history is the starting point. Ask specifically about any fall in the past year, the circumstances, and whether there was a loss of consciousness. Additional risk factors to assess at annual review or following a fall:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Gait and balance:</strong> Timed Up and Go (TUG) &gt;12 seconds is a useful threshold; observe for hesitancy, step width, and arm swing.</li>
    <li><strong>Lower-limb weakness:</strong> Chair stand test or simple clinical observation.</li>
    <li><strong>Cognitive impairment:</strong> A brief cognitive screen if not recently done; dual-task walking is particularly sensitive.</li>
    <li><strong>Visual impairment:</strong> Uncorrected refractive error and untreated cataract are both modifiable and meaningfully increase risk.</li>
    <li><strong>Orthostatic hypotension:</strong> Measure BP lying and standing after 1 and 3 minutes. A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic is clinically significant.</li>
    <li><strong>Medications:</strong> Psychotropics, antihypertensives, diuretics, and polypharmacy (&gt;4 medications) all increase risk.</li>
    <li><strong>Environment and footwear:</strong> Ask about loose rugs, poor lighting, absence of grab rails, and footwear with poor grip or high heels.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ The Otago Exercise Programme</p>
  <p>
    The Otago Exercise Programme is the best-evidenced falls prevention intervention available in NZ primary care. It consists of a personalised home exercise programme of progressive lower-limb strength and balance exercises, delivered by a physiotherapist over four home visits and then maintained independently with monthly phone follow-up.
  </p>
  <p>
    ACC funds the full programme at no cost to the patient. It is appropriate for patients who have already fallen and for those at high risk, including those using walking aids, provided they are able to follow instructions. Refer via a standard ACC physiotherapy referral; the physiotherapist completes the ACC paperwork. Do not wait for a second fall before referring.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Medication review and deprescribing</p>
  <p>
    Psychotropic medications are the highest-yield target. Benzodiazepines, Z-drugs (zopiclone, zolpidem), sedating antidepressants (particularly tricyclics and mirtazapine at higher doses), and antipsychotics approximately double fall risk and should trigger a deprescribing conversation at every review.<sup>5</sup> Framing it as a sleep or function goal rather than a falls risk reduction tends to land better.
  </p>
  <p>
    Antihypertensives are an underappreciated contributor, particularly in patients with symptomatic orthostatic hypotension. Consider dose reduction if a lying-standing BP drop is documented. Diuretics and first-generation antihistamines are also worth reviewing in any patient who has fallen. For complex polypharmacy, a structured medicines reconciliation with a clinical pharmacist is a reasonable referral.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Other targeted interventions</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Vision:</strong> Refer for optometry or ophthalmology if not recently assessed. Cataract extraction meaningfully reduces falls risk and is funded in NZ for patients who meet threshold criteria.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Home hazard assessment:</strong> Occupational therapy home assessment is warranted for patients who have had multiple falls or who are frail. ACC funds this via certain pathways; the physiotherapist completing the Otago referral can flag eligibility.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Footwear:</strong> Advise thin, hard-soled shoes with a low heel and closed toe. Thick-soled running shoes and slippers both impair proprioception. This is evidence-based, costs nothing, and is often overlooked.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Fracture risk quantification</p>
  <p>
    Fracture risk should be formally quantified for all patients who have sustained a falls-related fracture, and for any patient with a Clinical Frailty Scale score of ≥5 regardless of fracture history. Two calculators are validated for use in New Zealand populations:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>FRAX</strong> (WHO): Uses age, sex, BMD (optional), and clinical risk factors to estimate 10-year major osteoporotic fracture probability. Input BMD from DXA scan if available. The NZ-specific model is available at <a href="https://www.sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX/tool.aspx?country=35" target="_blank" rel="noopener">FRAX online</a>.</li>
    <li><strong>GARVAN</strong> (Garvan Institute): Incorporates falls history as a direct input, which is a meaningful clinical advantage in this population. Available at <a href="https://www.garvan.org.au/promotions/bone-fracture-risk/calculator/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Garvan online</a>.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Treatment thresholds in NZ are guided by the BPAC fracture risk management pathway and NZ MoH guidelines. Osteoporosis remains significantly undertreated following minimal trauma fracture in NZ primary care. See the <a href="/geriatrics/osteoporosis/">osteoporosis page</a> for investigation, prescribing thresholds, and funded medication options.
  </p>
</div>
