<p>The End of Life Choice Act 2019 (EOLCA) came into force in New Zealand on 7 November 2021, making New Zealand one of a small number of countries to have legalised assisted dying for eligible adults. The Act creates specific obligations for all registered health practitioners - not only those who choose to participate. Understanding the framework is professional necessity, not optional.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Eligibility criteria and physician roles</p>
  <p>A person may request assisted dying if they: are 18 or over; are a New Zealand citizen or permanent resident; have a terminal illness likely to end their life within six months; are in an advanced state of irreversible decline in physical capability; experience grievous and irremediable suffering that cannot be relieved in a manner acceptable to them; and are competent to make the decision.<sup>1</sup> Mental illness or disability alone do not constitute eligibility under the Act. The criteria are cumulative - all must be met.</p>
  <p>Two doctors must independently assess the person's eligibility. The attending medical practitioner (AMP) is typically the person's own GP or specialist; the independent medical practitioner (IMP) has no prior treating relationship. Both must confirm eligibility; if either has doubt about the person's competence, a psychiatrist assessment is required. GPs may find themselves approached to act as the AMP - this role involves clinical assessment, completing the statutory forms, and (if the person chooses oral medication) potentially prescribing the lethal medication.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Conscientious objection</p>
  <p>A health practitioner who has a conscientious objection to assisted dying is not required to participate. However, they have an explicit legal obligation under the Act to inform the patient of their objection and to tell the patient they can contact the Registrar (Ministry of Health) for information about how to access the service - or to refer the patient to a practitioner who does not object.<sup>1</sup> The obligation to inform and refer exists regardless of personal views. Leaving a patient without a pathway is not a legally permissible response to conscientious objection.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Documentation, safeguards, and practical encounters</p>
  <p>The Act imposes specific documentation requirements at each stage: the initial request, the two independent assessments, the 48-hour reflection period, and the administration of medication. All forms are prescribed by regulation. The Ministry of Health provides the statutory forms and a support line for health practitioners navigating the process. The Act also requires the Registrar to be notified at multiple points, and the information is used for national monitoring and reporting.</p>
