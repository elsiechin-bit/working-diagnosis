<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>A DEXA scan measures bone mineral density at the lumbar spine and hip. T-score at or above -1.0 is normal; -1.0 to -2.5 is osteopenia; -2.5 or below is osteoporosis. The T-score alone rarely determines treatment - combine with FRAX fracture risk assessment.<sup>3</sup></li>
    <li>"Osteopenia" is a statistical label, not a disease category. Most people with osteopenia will never have a fragility fracture. Context matters: a 50-year-old with T-score -1.5 and no risk factors is very different from a 78-year-old with the same score after a wrist fracture.</li>
    <li>FRAX (NZ-calibrated version) translates bone density and clinical risk factors into a 10-year absolute fracture probability. This is the decision-support tool that guides treatment, not the T-score in isolation.<sup>5,6</sup></li>
    <li>After fragility fracture, bisphosphonate is the most commonly omitted treatment in NZ. Alendronate weekly reduces vertebral fracture risk by approximately half and hip fracture risk by around a third in established osteoporosis.<sup>7</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>A bone density scan (DEXA - dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) measures how much mineral is packed into a given area of bone at the lumbar spine and hip. It uses two low-energy X-ray beams and delivers roughly 1-3 microsieverts - comparable to a few hours of ordinary background radiation.<sup>1</sup> The result has real clinical meaning, but interpreting a T-score without fracture risk context is one of the most common errors in bone health management.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Who to test and how to read results</p>
  <p>DEXA is recommended - not as a universal screen - for women aged 65 or older and men aged 70 or older as baseline assessment.<sup>2</sup> Scan earlier if risk factors are present: fragility fracture, prolonged corticosteroid use, early menopause (before 45), rheumatoid arthritis, coeliac disease or malabsorptive conditions, chronic renal or hepatic disease, hypogonadism in men, or first-degree relative with hip fracture. Funding varies by region and indication - check locally.</p>
  <p>The T-score compares bone mineral density to the average peak bone mass of a healthy young adult of the same sex (standard deviations above or below). WHO categories: ≥-1.0 is normal; -1.0 to -2.5 is osteopenia; ≤-2.5 is osteoporosis.<sup>3</sup> The Z-score compares to age-matched peers and is more useful in younger patients - a Z-score below -2.0 in a younger patient should prompt a search for secondary causes (medication effects, endocrine disorders, malabsorption).</p>
  <p>"Osteopenia" deserves more scrutiny than it usually receives. The term was coined in the 1990s as a statistical descriptor and was not originally intended as a disease category. Most people with osteopenia will never have a fragility fracture.<sup>4</sup> Always calculate fracture risk before discussing treatment.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Fracture risk assessment and treatment decisions</p>
  <p>FRAX (WHO Fracture Risk Assessment Tool) takes the T-score and combines it with age, sex, weight, height, smoking status, alcohol intake, previous fracture, parental hip fracture history, corticosteroid use, rheumatoid arthritis, and secondary osteoporosis.<sup>5</sup> Output is 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture. Use the NZ-calibrated version - fracture rates and background mortality differ between countries.<sup>6</sup> The GARVAN Fracture Risk Calculator (Australian calibration) includes falls history, which FRAX does not, and produces 5- and 10-year absolute risk estimates.</p>
  <p>First-line treatment for most patients is a bisphosphonate - alendronate (weekly). Bisphosphonates reduce vertebral fracture risk by approximately half and hip fracture risk by around a third in established osteoporosis.<sup>7</sup> Take with a full glass of water on an empty stomach; remain upright for 30 minutes afterward to avoid oesophageal irritation. Calcium and vitamin D supplement standard dietary intake but are not a substitute for bisphosphonate in high-risk patients. Non-pharmacological measures - weight-bearing exercise, balance training, home hazard reduction - have evidence and are consistently underemphasised.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Monitoring, alternatives, and NZ resources</p>
  <p>If treatment has been started, a repeat DEXA at two years assesses response. If no treatment is required but density is lower than ideal, repeat in one to three years depending on risk profile. Annual scanning is rarely necessary - bone density changes slowly enough that more frequent scans do not add meaningful information and may lead to unnecessary anxiety about normal measurement variation.<sup>4</sup></p>
  <p>For patients who cannot tolerate bisphosphonates, or where risk is very high, alternatives include denosumab and anabolic agents (teriparatide, romosozumab) - usually initiated by a specialist. Denosumab requires ongoing dosing; cessation without transitioning to a bisphosphonate carries rebound fracture risk. Vitamin D deficiency is common in NZ older adults (see the <a href="/lifestyle/sunlight-vitamin-d/">vitamin D page</a>) and should be corrected alongside any bone treatment.<sup>8</sup></p>
  <p>Osteoporosis New Zealand (osteoporosis.org.nz) publishes patient-facing resources and maintains a directory of bone health clinics. BPAC NZ has well-written clinician guidance on fracture risk thresholds and prescribing.</p>
</div>
