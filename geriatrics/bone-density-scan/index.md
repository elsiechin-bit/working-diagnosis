# Bone Density Scans

❍ *Understanding your DEXA scan*

A bone density scan is one of the most useful investigations a GP can order for an older patient, yet the results are often handed over with minimal explanation. A number appears on a page, it may say something like "osteopenia," and the patient is left to fill in the blanks. This page is an attempt to fill them properly.

---

## What is a bone density scan?

The standard test is called a DEXA scan, which stands for dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry. It uses two low-energy X-ray beams to measure how dense your bones are, specifically how much mineral is packed into a given area of bone. Denser bone is stronger bone, and stronger bone is less likely to break.

The scan is usually done at the lower spine (lumbar vertebrae L1 to L4) and the hip, because these are the sites where osteoporotic fractures most commonly occur and where measurements are most clinically meaningful. Some centres also measure the forearm, particularly if the hip or spine cannot be assessed reliably, for example in severe arthritis or after hip replacement surgery.

DEXA involves very little radiation. A single scan delivers roughly 1 to 3 microsieverts, which is comparable to a few hours of ordinary background radiation, or about one-hundredth of the dose from a standard chest X-ray.¹ It is not a scan to be concerned about from a radiation standpoint.

The test itself takes around 10 to 20 minutes. You lie on a table while a scanning arm passes over you. You do not go into a tunnel. It is not noisy. Most people find it straightforward.

---

## Who should have one?

DEXA scanning is recommended in New Zealand for people at elevated risk of fracture, not as a universal population screen. Your GP will consider a scan if you are a woman aged 65 or older, or a man aged 70 or older, as a baseline assessment.² Younger patients are considered if risk factors are present, including a fragility fracture (a fracture from a fall from standing height or less), prolonged corticosteroid use, early menopause before age 45, rheumatoid arthritis, coeliac disease or other malabsorptive conditions, chronic liver or kidney disease, hypogonadism in men, or a family history of hip fracture in a first-degree relative.

Funding for DEXA in New Zealand is not universal. Your GP will be able to tell you whether your scan is publicly funded, partially funded, or requires an out-of-pocket payment, which varies by region and indication.

---

## How to prepare

Preparation is minimal. You should avoid taking calcium supplements for 24 hours before the scan, as a large recent calcium dose can affect the result. Wear comfortable clothing without metal fastenings if possible, as metal objects in the scan area need to be removed. Jewellery, underwire bras, and belt buckles in the hip region are the usual culprits.

Tell the radiology team if you have had a nuclear medicine scan or intravenous contrast in the preceding week, as these can interfere with the measurement. Also let them know if you have had any spinal surgery, hip replacement, or compression fractures, as these affect which sites can be measured.

---

## What your results mean

Your DEXA report will contain two numbers you need to understand: the T-score and the Z-score.

**The T-score** compares your bone mineral density to the average peak bone mass of a healthy young adult of the same sex. It is expressed in standard deviations above or below that average. The World Health Organization established the diagnostic categories in 1994 and they remain in widespread use.³ A T-score of -1.0 or above is considered normal. A T-score between -1.0 and -2.5 is classified as osteopenia, meaning bone density is lower than the young adult average but not yet in the osteoporosis range. A T-score of -2.5 or below meets the definition of osteoporosis.

**The Z-score** compares your bone density to the average for people of your age and sex. This is particularly useful in younger patients and premenopausal women, where a low T-score may simply reflect the fact that peak bone mass was never very high rather than a bone loss problem. A Z-score below -2.0 is considered below the expected range for age, and in a younger patient this should prompt a search for secondary causes of bone loss such as medication effects, endocrine disorders, or malabsorption.

The report may also include a separate measurement for each site (left hip, right hip, lumbar spine) and an overall summary. It is normal for different sites to give slightly different T-scores.

---

## A note on the word "osteopenia"

"Osteopenia" is a label that causes considerable anxiety, and it deserves more scrutiny than it usually receives. The term was coined in the 1990s as a statistical descriptor, the region of the T-score distribution between normal and osteoporosis. It was not originally intended as a disease category, and it does not automatically mean you need treatment.⁴

Most people diagnosed with osteopenia will never have a fragility fracture. The relevance of the finding depends heavily on your overall clinical picture: your age, sex, and whether you have other risk factors for fracture. A 50-year-old woman with a T-score of -1.5 and no other risk factors is in a very different position from a 78-year-old man with the same score who smokes, is underweight, and has had a previous wrist fracture.

This is why the T-score alone is rarely sufficient to guide treatment decisions.

---

## What happens next: fracture risk assessment

The most widely used tool for translating a DEXA result into a clinical decision is FRAX, the Fracture Risk Assessment Tool developed by the World Health Organization.⁵ FRAX takes your T-score and combines it with clinical risk factors including age, sex, weight, height, smoking status, alcohol intake, previous fracture, parental hip fracture history, corticosteroid use, rheumatoid arthritis, and secondary osteoporosis. The output is a 10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture (hip, spine, wrist, or shoulder) and hip fracture specifically.

New Zealand has its own FRAX calibration, which is important because fracture rates and background mortality differ between countries.⁶ Your GP should be using the NZ-specific version when calculating your risk.

The GARVAN Fracture Risk Calculator, developed in Australia, is another tool sometimes used in New Zealand. It includes falls history in its calculation, which FRAX does not, and produces a 5- and 10-year absolute fracture risk estimate.

These tools help your GP have a more grounded conversation with you about whether intervention is warranted, and what kind. Not everyone with a low T-score needs medication. Equally, some people with T-scores in the osteopenia range do have a high enough overall fracture risk that treatment makes sense.

---

## Treatment and what it aims to do

If treatment is recommended, the first-line approach for most people is a bisphosphonate, most commonly alendronate (taken weekly). Bisphosphonates work by slowing the rate at which bone is broken down, allowing gradual improvement in density over time. They reduce the risk of vertebral fractures by approximately half and hip fractures by around a third in people with established osteoporosis.⁷ They are generally well tolerated, though they should be taken with a full glass of water on an empty stomach, and you should remain upright for at least 30 minutes afterward to avoid oesophageal irritation.

Calcium and vitamin D are important, but not a substitute for bisphosphonate therapy in people with established osteoporosis or high fracture risk. Dietary calcium is preferable to supplements where possible. Vitamin D deficiency is common in New Zealand, particularly in older adults and those with limited sun exposure, and is worth checking and correcting.⁸

Non-pharmacological measures matter and are underemphasised. Regular weight-bearing exercise, balance training to reduce falls risk, home hazard reduction, and sensible footwear all have evidence behind them and contribute meaningfully to fracture prevention alongside any medication.

For people who cannot tolerate bisphosphonates, or where the risk profile is very high, other agents are available including denosumab and, for the highest-risk patients, anabolic agents such as teriparatide or romosozumab. These are usually initiated by a specialist.

---

## How often should I be rescanned?

If treatment has been started, a repeat scan is usually done at two years to assess response. If no treatment is required but bone density is lower than ideal, your GP may recommend a repeat scan in one to three years depending on your risk profile. Annual scanning is rarely necessary or useful; bone density changes slowly enough that more frequent scans do not add meaningful information and may lead to unnecessary anxiety about normal variation between measurements.

## Useful resources in New Zealand

Osteoporosis New Zealand (osteoporosis.org.nz) publishes patient-facing resources and maintains a directory of bone health clinics. BPAC NZ has well-written guidance your GP will be familiar with. If your GP has referred you to a falls and fractures clinic or a rheumatologist, that is appropriate for more complex cases.
