    <h1>Polypharmacy &amp; <em>deprescribing</em>.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">The majority of community-dwelling New Zealanders over 75 take five or more regular medicines. No single prescriber intended the cumulative result.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Polypharmacy is conventionally defined as five or more regular medications; hyperpolypharmacy as ten or more. Both are common: New Zealand data show that the proportion of older adults on five or more medicines has increased significantly over the past decade, driven by multimorbidity, guideline-directed therapy across multiple conditions, and the cumulative effect of prescriptions inherited across multiple providers.<sup>1</sup> The harms are real and often insidious: falls, delirium, cognitive blunting, constipation, urinary retention, orthostatic hypotension, and drug–drug interactions that no individual prescriber intended when they added their contribution to the list.
    </p>

    <p>
      Structured medication review is distinct from a routine repeat prescription. It involves listing every medicine the patient actually takes (including over-the-counter, herbal, and those prescribed by other providers), cross-referencing against the current problem list, and explicitly asking: Is this still indicated? Is the dose appropriate? Is there an interaction? Is the patient actually taking it? The last question is often neglected — adherence to complex regimens is typically far lower than assumed, and a medicine the patient has quietly stopped is still worth documenting as stopped.
    </p>

    <p>
      The STOPP/START tool (version 3, 2023) is the most practically applicable structured framework for identifying potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) in older adults — see the <a href="/geriatrics/stopp-start/">STOPP/START reference page</a> for a summary. The NZF explicitly flags a number of STOPP medicines. Beers Criteria v2023 is also widely used, though it is US-derived and some items require local adaptation. In practice, a few drug classes account for the majority of preventable harm in this age group: benzodiazepines and Z-drugs, anticholinergics, NSAIDs, antihypertensives (in the context of frailty and orthostatic hypotension), and opioids.
    </p>

    <p>
      Deprescribing is not the same as stopping things. It is a deliberate, patient-centred clinical process — and it requires the same care as initiating a medicine. Patients often have strong emotional attachments to specific medicines ("my heart tablet"), legitimate concerns about withdrawal effects, and fear of what stopping might mean for their underlying condition. The conversation should acknowledge this explicitly. Validate the reason the medicine was started, explain the current risk-benefit calculation, and propose a trial reduction rather than abrupt cessation where possible.
    </p>

    <p>
      Prioritise by harm potential. The highest-yield targets in older adults are benzodiazepines and Z-drugs (falls risk, cognitive impairment, dependence), anticholinergics (cognitive load, urinary retention, constipation), proton pump inhibitors without ongoing indication, and antihypertensives in frail patients with low blood pressure or recurrent falls. The prescribe.org.nz deprescribing algorithms are practical, evidence-based, and freely available online — they include patient information sheets and tapering guides.
    </p>

    <p>
      A useful rule of thumb from geriatric medicine: if you cannot remember why a medicine was started, and the patient cannot either, that is itself a reason to consider stopping it. Annual medication review is a reasonable minimum for patients over 75 on five or more medicines; more frequent review is warranted after any hospitalisation, fall, or significant functional change.
    </p>
