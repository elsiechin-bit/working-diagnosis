<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Polypharmacy (5+ regular medicines) is common and causes insidious harm in older adults: falls, delirium, cognitive blunting, constipation, urinary retention, and orthostatic hypotension from combinations no individual prescriber intended.</li>
    <li>Structured medication review asks: Is this still indicated? Is the dose appropriate? Is there an interaction? Is the patient actually taking it? It is distinct from repeat prescribing and should happen at least annually in patients over 75 on 5+ medicines.</li>
    <li>Highest-yield targets for deprescribing: benzodiazepines/Z-drugs (falls, cognitive impairment), anticholinergics (cognitive load, retention), NSAIDs, antihypertensives in frail patients with low BP or recurrent falls, and PPIs without ongoing indication.</li>
    <li>Deprescribing requires the same care as initiating a medicine. Propose a trial reduction rather than abrupt cessation. Acknowledge why the medicine was started. The prescribe.org.nz algorithms and tapering guides are practical and free.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Polypharmacy is defined as five or more regular medications; hyperpolypharmacy as ten or more. Both are common in New Zealand: the proportion of older adults on five or more medicines has increased significantly over the past decade, driven by multimorbidity, guideline-directed therapy across multiple conditions, and prescriptions inherited from multiple providers.<sup>1</sup> The harms are real and often insidious - falls, delirium, cognitive blunting, constipation, urinary retention, orthostatic hypotension, and drug-drug interactions that no individual prescriber intended when they added their contribution to the list.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Structured medication review</p>
  <p>
    Structured medication review is distinct from a routine repeat prescription. It involves:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>Listing every medicine the patient actually takes - including over-the-counter, herbal, and those prescribed by other providers</li>
    <li>Cross-referencing against the current problem list</li>
    <li>Explicitly asking for each medicine: Is this still indicated? Is the dose appropriate for the patient's current weight, renal function, and frailty level? Is there a drug-drug or drug-disease interaction?</li>
    <li>Asking whether the patient is actually taking it - adherence to complex regimens is typically far lower than assumed, and a medicine quietly stopped is still worth documenting as stopped</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Annual medication review is a reasonable minimum for patients over 75 on five or more medicines. Review more frequently after any hospitalisation, fall, or significant functional change - these events often alter the risk-benefit calculation for several medicines simultaneously.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Using STOPP/START</p>
  <p>
    The STOPP/START tool (version 3, 2023) is the most practically applicable structured framework for identifying potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) in older adults.<sup>2</sup> STOPP criteria identify medicines to consider stopping; START criteria identify medicines that may be undertreated. The NZF explicitly flags a number of STOPP medicines. See the <a href="/geriatrics/stopp-start/">STOPP/START reference page</a> for a summarised list.
  </p>
  <p>
    In practice, a few drug classes account for the majority of preventable harm in older adults:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs:</strong> falls risk, cognitive impairment, dependence, paradoxical agitation</li>
    <li><strong>Anticholinergics:</strong> cognitive load (cumulative anticholinergic burden across the medication list), urinary retention, constipation, dry mouth, blurred vision; particularly oxybutynin, amitriptyline, promethazine, first-generation antihistamines</li>
    <li><strong>NSAIDs:</strong> GI bleeding, renal impairment, fluid retention, hypertension - especially with concurrent antihypertensives, anticoagulants, or diuretics</li>
    <li><strong>Antihypertensives in frail patients:</strong> orthostatic hypotension, falls, syncope; recalibrate target BP in frailty</li>
    <li><strong>PPIs without ongoing indication:</strong> commonly started and never stopped; associated with hypomagnesaemia, C. difficile, and B12 deficiency with long-term use</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ The deprescribing conversation</p>
  <p>
    Deprescribing is not the same as stopping things. It is a deliberate, patient-centred clinical process that requires the same care as initiating a medicine. Patients often have strong emotional attachments to specific medicines, legitimate concerns about withdrawal effects, and fear of what stopping might mean for their underlying condition.
  </p>
  <p>
    A practical framework for the conversation:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>Validate the reason the medicine was started - this was the right decision at the time</li>
    <li>Explain the current risk-benefit calculation in plain terms</li>
    <li>Propose a trial reduction rather than abrupt cessation where possible</li>
    <li>Offer to reinstate if the patient notices a difference</li>
    <li>One medicine at a time - changing multiple medicines simultaneously makes it impossible to attribute any effect</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    A useful rule of thumb: if you cannot remember why a medicine was started, and the patient cannot either, that is itself a reason to consider stopping it. Absence of a documented indication is not the same as absence of one - but it is a prompt to investigate.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Resources and tapering guidance</p>
  <p>
    The prescribe.org.nz deprescribing algorithms are practical, evidence-based, and freely available online. They include stepwise algorithms for benzodiazepines, PPIs, antihypertensives, antidepressants, and other commonly overprescribed drug classes, plus patient information sheets and tapering guides suitable for handing to patients. These are worth knowing about and linking to in your clinical workflow.
  </p>
  <p>
    For benzodiazepine deprescribing: a slow taper of 10% of the current dose every 2-4 weeks, with slower reductions at lower doses, is the standard approach. Diazepam is preferred for taper over short-acting agents (lorazepam, oxazepam) due to its longer half-life producing a smoother reduction. Psychological support during taper improves completion rates - referral to a clinical psychologist for concurrent CBT is worth arranging.
  </p>
  <p>
    In complex cases with significant polypharmacy, frailty, and multiple comorbidities, geriatric medicine input is appropriate. A comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) by a geriatrician can systematically address medication, function, cognition, mood, and social factors together.
  </p>
</div>
