<p>Advance care planning (ACP) is the process by which a person, ideally with their family and clinical team, articulates their values, goals, and preferences for future healthcare - particularly in situations where they may no longer be able to make or communicate decisions. The evidence base is consistent: people who engage in ACP are more likely to die in their preferred place, less likely to receive unwanted aggressive intervention at end of life, and their families report significantly better bereavement outcomes.<sup>1</sup> Despite this, ACP conversations remain uncommon in routine general practice.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ The NZ ACP framework and what to cover</p>
  <p>The barriers are real. Time, uncertainty about how to open the conversation, concern about distressing the patient, and the mistaken belief that ACP is only relevant in terminal illness all contribute. In reality, ACP is most valuable when it happens long before it is urgently needed - when the person is well enough to reflect, to have multiple conversations if needed, and to involve the people who matter to them.</p>
  <p>The New Zealand ACP framework (hqsc.govt.nz) provides a structured approach and patient-facing documents including the <em>My Advance Care Plan</em> booklet. Key domains to cover:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>The person's values and what makes life meaningful to them</li>
    <li>Acceptable and unacceptable health states - how they would feel about living with significant disability, cognitive impairment, or dependency</li>
    <li>Preferences around cardiopulmonary resuscitation</li>
    <li>Preferences around artificial nutrition, hydration, and ventilation if recovery is unlikely</li>
    <li>Who holds enduring power of attorney (EPA) for personal care and welfare, and whether they have been chosen and formally documented</li>
  </ul>
  <p>ACP conversations happen in layers - an initial framing conversation, a follow-up after the patient has had time to think, and updates as health status changes. Document each conversation explicitly in the notes: what was discussed, who was present, and what the person's stated preferences were. A documented ACP conversation, even without a completed formal document, provides evidence of the person's wishes that a treating team can draw on.</p>
</div>
