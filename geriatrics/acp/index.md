<p>
      Advance care planning (ACP) is the process by which a person, ideally with their family and clinical team, articulates their values, goals, and preferences for future healthcare  -  particularly in situations where they may no longer be able to make or communicate decisions. The evidence base is consistent: people who engage in ACP are more likely to die in their preferred place, less likely to receive unwanted aggressive intervention at end of life, and their families report significantly better bereavement outcomes.<sup>1</sup> Despite this, ACP conversations remain uncommon in routine general practice.
    </p>

    <p>
      The barriers are real. Time, uncertainty about how to open the conversation, concern about distressing the patient, and the mistaken belief that ACP is only relevant in terminal illness all contribute. In reality, ACP is most valuable when it happens long before it is urgently needed  -  when the person is well enough to reflect, to have multiple conversations if needed, and to involve the people who matter to them. A diagnosis of mild cognitive impairment, a significant hospitalisation, a change in function, or a patient's own enquiry about what lies ahead are all natural entry points.
    </p>

    <p>
      The New Zealand ACP framework (available at hqsc.govt.nz) provides a structured approach and patient-facing documents including the <em>My Advance Care Plan</em> booklet, which many patients find helpful to take home between appointments. Key domains to cover include: the person's values and what makes life meaningful to them; acceptable and unacceptable health states; preferences around cardiopulmonary resuscitation; preferences around artificial nutrition, hydration, and ventilation if recovery is unlikely; and who holds enduring power of attorney (EPA) for personal care and welfare.
    </p>

    <p>
      The EPA for personal care and welfare is distinct from the EPA for property. It only activates when the person lacks capacity to make a particular decision  -  the attorney cannot override a capable person's choices. GPs are often asked to certify capacity in the context of EPA  -  see the <a href="/geriatrics/medicolegal/">medicolegal page</a> for detail on the PPPR Act standard. The key practical point: EPA must be established while the person has capacity. This means early, and explicitly. Many patients have assumed their family will simply "sort it out"  -  without understanding that this requires legal documentation.
    </p>

    <p>
      ACP conversations do not require a single definitive appointment. They often happen in layers  -  an initial framing conversation, a follow-up after the patient has had time to think, and updates as health status changes. Document the conversation explicitly in the notes: what was discussed, who was present, and what the person's stated preferences were. A documented ACP conversation, even without a completed formal document, is clinically and legally meaningful  -  it provides evidence of the person's wishes that a treating team can draw on.
    </p>

    <p>
      The End of Life Choice Act 2019 (in force from November 2021) creates specific obligations for NZ GPs that are distinct from the broader ACP conversation  -  see the <a href="/geriatrics/eolca/">EOLCA page</a>. It is worth knowing your own position on conscientious objection before the question arises in a consultation, rather than discovering it mid-appointment.
    </p>
