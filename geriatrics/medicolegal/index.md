<p>The medicolegal obligations of general practice are most concentrated in care of older patients - where capacity, elder abuse, driving fitness, and end-of-life decisions converge in ways that require both clinical skill and legal awareness.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Capacity, EPA, and consent</p>
  <p>The presumption of competence holds until there is specific reason to question it. Cognitive impairment does not equal incapacity - capacity is decision-specific, time-specific, and must be assessed for the particular decision at hand. The New Zealand standard derives from the Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988 (PPPR Act) and case law: the patient must be able to understand the relevant information, retain it long enough to make a decision, weigh it against their own values and situation, and communicate a decision.<sup>1</sup> A person may lack capacity for a complex financial decision while retaining it for a healthcare choice. Assess for the specific decision, at the time it needs to be made, and document your assessment explicitly.</p>
  <p>An EPA for personal care and welfare only activates when the person lacks capacity to make a particular decision - the attorney cannot override the choices of a capable person. GPs are commonly asked to certify that a person lacked capacity at a particular time (often retrospectively, for legal or family reasons). This should be approached carefully: it is a clinical opinion, should reference the specific decision and the PPPR Act criteria, and should be documented with the same rigour as any medical report. If the person has not yet established an EPA, encourage this early - while capacity is clear and unhurried.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Firearms and driving</p>
  <p>GPs are not required to actively screen patients for firearm ownership, but have an obligation to notify the Police Arms Officer if they believe a patient poses a risk to themselves or others and holds or has access to firearms. Dementia and significant depression are the most common triggers in geriatric practice. The notification is a professional duty under the Arms Act 1983 as amended; it overrides usual confidentiality obligations in the same way that NZTA notification does. Document the clinical reasoning and the notification.<sup>3</sup></p>
  <p>GPs have both an ethical and a legal obligation to advise patients who are medically unfit to drive, and to notify NZTA if the patient does not comply and continues to drive when they pose a serious risk to road safety. See the <a href="/geriatrics/driving/">fitness to drive page</a> for the condition-specific standards from the NZTA's <em>Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive</em> guide.</p>
</div>
