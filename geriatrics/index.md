<p>
      New Zealand's population is ageing fast. By 2034, one in five New Zealanders will be 65 or older.<sup>1</sup> For most GPs, that trend is already visible in the appointment book. Older patients bring a particular kind of clinical complexity: multimorbidity, polypharmacy, functional decline, carer strain, grief, and legal questions that rarely come up in younger cohorts  -  all layered on top of whatever brought them in today.
    </p>

    <p>
      The notes below aren't a geriatric medicine textbook. They're a working GP's map of the territory  -  what to look for, what the evidence actually supports, what the NZ medicolegal landscape requires, and where the common traps are.
    </p>

<section class="pillars-grid">
  <div class="wrap">
    <p class="section-label">Seven themes</p>
    <div class="pillars">

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">01</span>
        <h3>Frailty<br><em>assessment</em></h3>
        <p>
          Frailty is not a diagnosis  -  it's a syndrome of accumulated physiological decline that predicts adverse outcomes independently of age or disease burden. The Fried phenotype (weight loss, exhaustion, weakness, slowness, low activity) and the Rockwood deficit accumulation model (Clinical Frailty Scale) both have good evidence behind them and both are usable in general practice without specialist referral.<sup>2,3</sup>
        </p>
        <p>
          The Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) is the more practical option for a GP consultation: a nine-point ordinal scale scored from a brief functional history, with strong predictive validity for hospitalisation, institutionalisation, and mortality.<sup>4</sup> A CFS of 5 or above (mildly frail) is a reasonable threshold for triggering a structured review.
        </p>
        <p>
          Frailty is not irreversible. Resistance exercise, protein adequacy, and medication rationalisation all have evidence for meaningful functional improvement even in older cohorts.<sup>5</sup>
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/frailty/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">02</span>
        <h3>Falls &amp; fracture<br><em>prevention</em></h3>
        <p>
          Falls are the leading cause of injury hospitalisation in New Zealand adults over 65, and the direct costs to the health system exceed $500 million annually.<sup>6</sup> Hip fracture carries a one-year mortality of roughly 20-30% in older adults  -  comparable to many cancers  -  yet it rarely prompts the same urgency in primary care.<sup>7</sup>
        </p>
        <p>
          Multifactorial falls risk assessment and intervention remain the best-evidenced approach: medication review (particularly sedatives, antihypertensives, and anticholinergics), strength and balance exercise programmes (Otago or similar), home hazard assessment, and vision correction.<sup>8</sup> The ACC-funded Otago Exercise Programme is available nationally and is a direct referral.
        </p>
        <p>
          Fracture risk should be quantified  -  FRAX with BMD input or GARVAN  -  and osteoporosis treated actively. Under-treatment of osteoporosis in post-fracture patients remains a significant quality gap in NZ primary care.<sup>9</sup>
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/falls/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">03</span>
        <h3>Cognitive decline<br><em>&amp; dementia</em></h3>
        <p>
          Dementia affects approximately 70,000 New Zealanders, a figure projected to exceed 170,000 by 2050.<sup>10</sup> Māori and Pacific peoples develop dementia at younger ages and at higher rates, and present later  -  factors that a culturally responsive GP consultation should actively address.<sup>11</sup>
        </p>
        <p>
          Cognitive assessment in general practice sits somewhere uncomfortable: patients often don't want it, families often do, and the tools we use (MMSE, MoCA, GPCOG) each have meaningful floor and ceiling effects. The MoCA is currently better validated for detecting mild cognitive impairment than the MMSE.<sup>12</sup> The GPCOG has the advantage of brevity and an informant component.
        </p>
        <p>
          A diagnosis of dementia triggers a series of practical obligations: driving assessment, firearms (if applicable), enduring power of attorney while capacity exists, and  -  eventually  -  residential care planning. None of these should wait until the patient is no longer able to participate.
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/dementia/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">04</span>
        <h3>Polypharmacy &amp;<br><em>deprescribing</em></h3>
        <p>
          Polypharmacy  -  conventionally defined as five or more regular medications  -  affects the majority of community-dwelling New Zealanders over 75.<sup>13</sup> The harms are cumulative and often insidious: falls, delirium, cognitive blunting, constipation, urinary retention, and drug-drug interactions that no individual prescriber intended when they added their contribution to the list.
        </p>
        <p>
          The Beers Criteria and STOPP/START tool both provide structured frameworks for identifying potentially inappropriate medications in older adults.<sup>14,15</sup> STOPP/START has stronger evidence for reducing adverse drug events and is more applicable to New Zealand practice. The NZF explicitly flags a number of STOPP medications.
        </p>
        <p>
          Deprescribing is not just stopping things  -  it requires patient buy-in, a plan for monitoring withdrawal effects, and attention to the psychological weight some patients place on certain medications. Start with the highest-risk, lowest-benefit medicines and go slowly.
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/polypharmacy/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">05</span>
        <h3>Continence</h3>
        <p>
          Urinary incontinence affects roughly one in three older women and one in five older men, yet the majority never raise it with their GP  -  and most GPs don't ask.<sup>16</sup> It is among the most treatable conditions in geriatric medicine and among the most consequential if left unaddressed: falls, skin breakdown, social isolation, residential care admission, and carer burnout all have documented associations with incontinence.
        </p>
        <p>
          The first step is typing: urge (overactive bladder), stress, mixed, or overflow  -  because the interventions diverge sharply. Bladder diaries, post-void residual measurement (or bladder scan), and a medication review (diuretics, anticholinergics, alpha-blockers) should precede any pharmacotherapy. Pelvic floor physiotherapy is underutilised and underreferred in this age group.
        </p>
        <p>
          Faecal incontinence deserves separate attention: often attributed to ageing, often treatable, and virtually never volunteered by patients.
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/continence/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar">
        <span class="pnum">06</span>
        <h3>End-of-life &amp;<br><em>ACP</em></h3>
        <p>
          Advance care planning (ACP) is both a clinical skill and a relational one. The evidence is consistent: people who engage in ACP are more likely to die in their preferred place, less likely to receive unwanted aggressive intervention, and their families report better bereavement outcomes.<sup>17</sup> It is also chronically underdone in general practice.
        </p>
        <p>
          The New Zealand ACP framework provides a structured approach and patient-facing documents (including the <em>My Advance Care Plan</em> booklet) that work well in a GP context. Conversations should happen before crisis, ideally at a dedicated appointment, and should explicitly address: values and goals, acceptable and unacceptable health states, CPR preferences, and who holds enduring power of attorney for personal care and welfare.
        </p>
        <p>
          The End of Life Choice Act 2019 came into force in November 2021. GPs have specific obligations around conscientious objection, referral, and documentation  -  regardless of their personal position on assisted dying.<sup>18</sup>
        </p>
        <a href="/geriatrics/acp/" class="more">Read more →</a>
      </div>

      <div class="pillar" style="grid-column: 1 / -1; border-right: none; border-top: 1px solid var(--rule); padding-top: 28px; margin-top: 0;">
        <span class="pnum">07</span>
        <h3>Medicolegal<br><em>issues</em></h3>
        <p>
          General practice with older patients generates a distinctive set of medicolegal obligations that sit outside the usual clinical decision-making frame. Knowing them in advance is significantly less stressful than discovering them mid-consultation.
        </p>
        <p>
          <strong>Capacity and consent.</strong> The presumption of competence holds until there is specific reason to question it. Cognitive impairment does not equal incapacity  -  capacity is decision-specific, time-specific, and must be assessed for the particular decision at hand. The New Zealand standard derives from the Protection of Personal and Property Rights Act 1988 (PPPR Act) and case law: the patient must understand the information, retain it, weigh it, and communicate a decision.<sup>19</sup>
        </p>
        <p>
          <strong>Enduring powers of attorney.</strong> An EPA for personal care and welfare only activates when the person lacks capacity  -  the attorney cannot override a capable person's decision. GPs are commonly asked to certify that a person lacked capacity at a particular time; this should be approached carefully and documented with reference to the specific decision and the PPPR Act criteria.
        </p>
        <p>
          <strong>Driving.</strong> GPs have both an ethical and a legal obligation to advise patients who are medically unfit to drive, and to notify the NZ Transport Agency if the patient does not comply and poses a serious risk to road safety. The NZTA Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive guide sets out condition-specific standards. Dementia, poorly controlled epilepsy, significant visual field loss, and certain cardiac conditions trigger mandatory assessment. Document all conversations.
        </p>
        <p>
          <strong>Elder abuse.</strong> Estimated to affect 2-10% of older adults, elder abuse is underidentified in primary care.<sup>20</sup> It encompasses physical, psychological, financial, and sexual abuse, as well as neglect  -  often by a family member or carer. GPs have no mandatory reporting obligation in NZ (unlike child abuse), but do have professional obligations under the Health Practitioners Competence Assurance Act to act in the patient's best interest, which may include referral to Age Concern's Elder Abuse Response Service or, in urgent situations, police.
        </p>
        <p>
          <strong>Firearms.</strong> GPs are not required to actively screen for firearm ownership, but have an obligation to notify the Police Arms Officer if they believe a patient poses a risk to themselves or others and holds or has access to firearms. Dementia and significant depression are the most common triggers in geriatric practice.
        </p>
