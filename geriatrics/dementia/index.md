<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>A missed or delayed dementia diagnosis delays EPA setup, driving assessment, and advance care planning - all of which require the person to have capacity. These conversations need to happen early, not late.</li>
    <li>Preferred screening tools: <strong>GPCOG</strong> (GP-friendly, validated in primary care, includes informant component) or <strong>MoCA</strong> (more sensitive for MCI and mild dementia, ~10 minutes). Avoid relying on the MMSE alone - it has significant ceiling effects in educated patients and poor sensitivity for MCI.</li>
    <li>Standard workup: TFTs, FBC, B12, folate, eGFR, LFTs, HbA1c, calcium, MSU (if any delirium component). Brain imaging (CT or MRI) before diagnosis in most cases.</li>
    <li>Post-diagnosis obligations: driving assessment promptly; enduring powers of attorney (property and personal care) while capacity remains; firearms if relevant; begin residential care planning conversations in moderate - not severe - stages.</li>
    <li>Māori and Pacific peoples develop dementia at younger ages and present to specialist services later. Ask explicitly, use validated tools (Kimihia for Māori patients), and take a whānau-centred approach.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Cognitive assessment in general practice sits somewhere uncomfortable. Patients often don't want it. Families often do. The consultation that starts as a "routine check" can end very differently, and there is rarely enough time to do it properly in a standard appointment. All of this is real, and none of it changes the importance of getting it right. A missed dementia diagnosis delays legal and financial planning, driving assessment, and the advance care conversations that can only happen while the person still has capacity to lead them.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Cognitive assessment: choosing a tool</p>
  <p>
    The three principal screening tools in NZ primary care each have different profiles:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>GPCOG</strong> (gpcog.com.au): the most GP-practical option. A brief patient component and a structured informant component, validated specifically in primary care settings. Free to use.</li>
    <li><strong>MoCA</strong> (moca-test.org): more sensitive than the MMSE for mild cognitive impairment and mild dementia. Takes around ten minutes. Free for non-commercial use. Preferred when MCI is suspected or the patient has higher baseline education.</li>
    <li><strong>MMSE:</strong> widely familiar but limited by significant ceiling effects - educated patients can score in the normal range while meaningfully impaired. Reserve for monitoring over time rather than as the primary screening instrument.</li>
    <li><strong>Kimihia:</strong> a cognitive assessment tool adapted and validated for Māori patients in a NZ context. Use this in preference to standard tools for Māori patients where available.<sup>2</sup></li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Always supplement the objective tool with an informant history. A family member or close contact often provides the most clinically useful information, particularly about functional change over time.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Workup before diagnosis</p>
  <p>
    Before attributing cognitive impairment to a primary neurodegenerative process, exclude reversible causes. Standard bloods: TFTs, FBC, B12, folate, eGFR, LFTs, HbA1c, calcium. Add MSU if there is any delirium component or acute-on-chronic presentation.
  </p>
  <p>
    Brain imaging is appropriate in most cases. CT is adequate for most primary care-level assessments. MRI adds value if there is clinical suspicion of normal pressure hydrocephalus (gait, incontinence, and cognitive decline triad), vascular dementia, or early-onset presentation (under 65). Imaging is not about confirming dementia - it is about excluding structural, vascular, or reversible causes and characterising subtype.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Disclosure</p>
  <p>
    Disclosure of a dementia diagnosis is a consultation in its own right - not something to append to the end of a results review. Allocate a dedicated appointment with enough time, no competing agenda items, and ideally a support person present if the patient agrees.
  </p>
  <p>
    Address the patient directly, not their family member. Name the diagnosis clearly rather than burying it in euphemism. Most people, once told, report relief at having an explanation; avoidance rarely serves anyone. Provide written information (Alzheimers New Zealand produces excellent resources in plain language and te reo). Offer a follow-up appointment specifically for questions - there will be many once the initial shock settles. Document the conversation in detail.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Post-diagnosis obligations</p>
  <p>
    A dementia diagnosis triggers a series of time-sensitive practical tasks. Work through these systematically across the first few consultations rather than attempting to cover everything at once:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Driving:</strong> assess fitness to drive promptly. This is a legal and safety obligation - see the <a href="/geriatrics/driving/">fitness to drive page</a> for the NZ-specific framework.</li>
    <li><strong>Enduring powers of attorney (EPA):</strong> both property and personal care and welfare should be established while the person has capacity to appoint them. Refer to a solicitor early. If an EPA already exists, confirm who holds it and whether activation criteria have been met.</li>
    <li><strong>Firearms:</strong> address directly if the patient holds a firearms licence. This is a legal requirement.</li>
    <li><strong>Residential care planning:</strong> begin these conversations in moderate stages, not severe ones. Families absorb enormous planning burden when this is left too late.</li>
    <li><strong>Carer support:</strong> the whānau or family system often carries significant invisible burden. Ask about it. Alzheimers NZ and local support groups provide caregiver resources.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ When to refer</p>
  <p>
    Referral to a memory clinic or geriatric medicine service is warranted when: the diagnosis is clinically uncertain; onset is before age 65 (early-onset dementia has a broader differential and warrants specialist workup); the patient or family is requesting specialist confirmation; there is diagnostic complexity (e.g. suspected Lewy body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, or NPH); or symptoms are atypical.
  </p>
  <p>
    For early-onset presentations, neuropsychology assessment and MRI are almost always warranted before a primary care diagnosis is made. The differential is wider and the stakes for accuracy are higher given the impact on employment, financial capacity, and younger family dependants.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Equity considerations</p>
  <p>
    Māori and Pacific peoples develop dementia at younger ages and at higher rates than the general population, present to specialist services later, and are underrepresented in clinical trials and research that underpins most of the tools and guidelines we use.<sup>3</sup> This combination means standard cognitive screening thresholds and normative data may not translate directly.
  </p>
  <p>
    A culturally responsive approach means not waiting for patients or whānau to raise cognitive concerns - asking explicitly as part of routine older adult care. Use the Kimihia tool where available. Ensure interpreters are present when needed; cognitive assessment through a second language systematically underestimates function. Understand that whānau-centred decision making is the norm in many Māori and Pacific families and plan disclosure and post-diagnosis conversations accordingly.
  </p>
  <p>
    The care burden on whānau in dementia is substantial and often invisible to the health system. Ask about it at every visit. Respite care, Needs Assessment and Service Coordination (NASC), and Alzheimers NZ carer support are the main NZ-funded pathways.
  </p>
</div>
