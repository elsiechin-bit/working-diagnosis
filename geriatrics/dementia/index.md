    <h1>Dementia diagnosis <em>&amp; disclosure</em>.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">70,000 New Zealanders today. 170,000 by 2050. The GP consultation is where most of them will first be assessed — and where the conversation begins.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Cognitive assessment in general practice sits somewhere uncomfortable. Patients often don't want it. Families often do. The consultation that starts as a "routine check" can end very differently, and there is rarely enough time to do it properly in a standard appointment. All of this is real, and none of it changes the importance of getting it right. A missed dementia diagnosis delays legal and financial planning, driving assessment, and — critically — the advance care conversations that can only happen while the person still has capacity to lead them.
    </p>

    <p>
      The principal cognitive screening tools used in New Zealand primary care are the MMSE, the MoCA, and the GPCOG. Each has meaningful limitations. The MMSE is widely familiar but has significant ceiling effects — educated patients can score in the normal range while meaningfully impaired — and poor sensitivity for mild cognitive impairment.<sup>1</sup> The MoCA is more sensitive for MCI and mild dementia, takes about ten minutes, and is freely available for non-commercial use (moca-test.org). The GPCOG (GP Assessment of Cognition) is the most GP-friendly: a brief patient component and an informant component, validated in primary care, and available from gpcog.com.au. For Māori patients, the Kimihia tool has been adapted and validated in a New Zealand context and is worth seeking out.<sup>2</sup>
    </p>

    <p>
      Assessment should include: screening tool with informant history, thyroid function, FBC, B12, folate, eGFR, LFTs, HbA1c, calcium, and MSU if there is any delirium component. Brain imaging is appropriate in most cases prior to diagnosis — CT is adequate in most instances; MRI adds value if there is clinical suspicion of normal pressure hydrocephalus, vascular dementia, or early-onset presentation. Referral to a memory clinic or geriatric medicine is warranted when the diagnosis is uncertain, onset is before age 65, or the patient or family is requesting specialist confirmation.
    </p>

    <p>
      Disclosure of a dementia diagnosis is a consultation in its own right. It should ideally happen face to face, with a support person present if the patient agrees, with enough time allocated and without other agenda items competing. The patient should be addressed directly — not talked about — and the diagnosis should be named clearly, not buried in euphemism. Most people, once told, report relief at having an explanation; avoidance rarely serves anyone. Provide written information (Alzheimers New Zealand produces excellent resources), offer a follow-up appointment specifically for questions, and document the conversation.
    </p>

    <p>
      A diagnosis of dementia triggers a series of time-sensitive practical obligations. Driving assessment should happen promptly — see the <a href="/geriatrics/driving/">fitness to drive page</a>. Enduring powers of attorney (EPA) for both property and personal care and welfare should be established while the person has capacity to appoint them — this means early, not after deterioration. If the person already has an EPA, check who holds it and whether it has been activated. Firearms, if any, should be addressed. Residential care planning conversations should begin in the moderate stages, not the severe ones.
    </p>

    <p>
      Equity matters here. Māori and Pacific peoples develop dementia at younger ages and at higher rates, present to specialist services later, and are underrepresented in clinical trials.<sup>3</sup> A culturally responsive approach means not waiting for patients or whānau to raise cognitive concerns — asking explicitly, using validated tools where available, and ensuring interpreters are present where needed. The concept of whānau-centred care is particularly relevant in dementia, where family systems often absorb enormous and largely invisible care burden.
    </p>
