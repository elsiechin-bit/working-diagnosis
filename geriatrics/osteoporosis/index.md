<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Treat based on fracture risk, not T-score alone. Use FRAX (NZ model) or GARVAN (preferred - incorporates falls history) to calculate 10-year fracture probability before initiating treatment.</li>
    <li>A minimal trauma fracture (from standing height or less) is a fragility fracture by definition. It should prompt assessment and treatment without waiting for DXA results.</li>
    <li>First-line: <strong>alendronate 70 mg weekly</strong> (fully subsidised in NZ). Contraindicated if eGFR &lt;35 mL/min. Check calcium/vitamin D, exclude secondary causes, and arrange a baseline dental review before starting.</li>
    <li>Post-fracture care is a major quality gap in NZ. If your patient has had a wrist, vertebral, or hip fracture and is not on treatment, act now.</li>
    <li>After 5 years of oral bisphosphonate: consider a holiday for moderate-risk patients; continue or switch agents for high-risk patients (prior hip/vertebral fracture, T-score below -2.5 at femoral neck).</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Osteoporosis is defined by a T-score of -2.5 or below at the lumbar spine, femoral neck, or total hip on DXA. But the decision to treat should be based on fracture risk - not T-score alone. Fewer than 20% of patients who present with a fragility fracture in NZ receive appropriate assessment and treatment within the following 12 months. This is the treatment gap, and primary care is where it needs to close.<sup>3</sup>
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Who to investigate: DXA indications</p>
  <p>DXA is indicated for:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>All women aged 65 and over</li>
    <li>Postmenopausal women under 65 with risk factors: family history of hip fracture, low body weight (BMI &lt;19), smoking, alcohol intake above 3 units/day, or corticosteroid use (&gt;5 mg prednisolone equivalent for &gt;3 months)</li>
    <li>All men aged 70 and over</li>
    <li>Any person with a minimal trauma fracture - do not wait for DXA before commencing treatment</li>
    <li>Patients on long-term corticosteroids, aromatase inhibitors, androgen deprivation therapy, or antiepileptics</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Fracture risk calculators: FRAX (sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX, country = New Zealand) and GARVAN (garvan.org.au) are both validated for NZ populations. GARVAN is preferred where falls history is relevant - it incorporates number of falls as a direct input, which FRAX does not.<sup>1</sup>
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ First-line treatment: bisphosphonates</p>
  <p>
    Alendronate 70 mg weekly is the fully subsidised first-line agent in NZ and appropriate for most patients. Before initiating:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>Check eGFR - contraindicated if below 35 mL/min</li>
    <li>Exclude and address secondary causes: TFTs, coeliac serology, calcium, PTH, testosterone in men, review medication list for corticosteroids and enzyme inducers</li>
    <li>Assess calcium and vitamin D status - correct vitamin D deficiency (target 25-OH vitamin D ≥50 nmol/L) before starting</li>
    <li>Arrange a dental review - bisphosphonate-related osteonecrosis of the jaw is rare but important to flag; planned dental extractions should occur before bisphosphonate initiation where possible</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Counsel patients that alendronate should be taken on an empty stomach with a full glass of water, remaining upright for 30 minutes afterwards. GI intolerance is the main reason for discontinuation.
  </p>
  <p>
    For patients who cannot tolerate or absorb oral bisphosphonates, or where adherence is a concern: <strong>zoledronate 5 mg IV annually</strong> is an effective alternative. It is subsidised under Special Authority in NZ. Ensure adequate vitamin D before infusion to avoid acute hypocalcaemia. Flu-like symptoms post-infusion are common (acute phase reaction) and typically resolve within 48-72 hours; paracetamol is effective.
  </p>
</div>
