<p>Driving carries profound practical, psychological, and social significance for older adults - independence, identity, and connection to community are all entangled with the ability to get behind the wheel. Raising fitness-to-drive concerns requires both clinical rigour and relational skill. It is also a legal obligation. GPs have an ethical and legal duty to advise patients who are medically unfit to drive, and to notify NZTA if the patient does not comply and poses a serious ongoing road safety risk.<sup>1</sup></p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Conditions and the assessment process</p>
  <p>The authoritative reference is NZTA's <em>Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive: A Guide for Medical Practitioners</em>, available on the NZTA website, which sets out condition-specific standards for both private and commercial vehicle licensing. Conditions that commonly trigger mandatory assessment in geriatric practice include: dementia and other cognitive impairments, poorly controlled epilepsy, significant visual field loss, severe obstructive sleep apnoea, symptomatic cardiac conditions (including recent MI, uncontrolled arrhythmias, and implanted defibrillators), and significant peripheral neuropathy or musculoskeletal impairment affecting vehicle control.</p>
  <p>Dementia deserves special attention. A diagnosis of dementia does not automatically disqualify a person from driving - early dementia with preserved insight, judgment, and motor function may be compatible with continued driving for a period. However, it triggers mandatory NZTA referral for assessment, which typically involves an on-road driving evaluation. The GP's role is to notify NZTA that the patient has a medical condition requiring assessment, not to make the final fitness determination. Document this notification and the clinical reasoning.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ When the patient refuses</p>
  <p>When a patient is unwilling to stop driving despite advice, the GP's options are: to seek support from family (with the patient's consent where possible), to request a formal on-road assessment (which can be framed as an objective test rather than a clinical opinion), and ultimately to notify NZTA directly if the safety risk is serious and the patient refuses to comply. This notification is a professional obligation under the Land Transport Act 1998 and overrides the usual duty of confidentiality.<sup>3</sup> Inform the patient of your intention to notify, and document the notification in the clinical record.</p>
  <p>The conversation itself is easier when framed around safety and an objective assessment process rather than a personal judgment.
