    <h1>Urinary <em>incontinence</em>.</h1>
    <p class="topic-dek">Among the most treatable conditions in geriatric medicine. Among the least often raised — by patients or their doctors.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="topic-body">

    <p>
      Urinary incontinence affects roughly one in three older women and one in five older men, yet the majority never raise it with their GP — and many GPs do not ask.<sup>1</sup> Shame, normalisation ("it's just part of getting old"), and the assumption that nothing can be done all contribute. The clinical consequences of not asking are real: incontinence is independently associated with falls (trips to the toilet at night, rushing to avoid leakage), pressure injuries, depression, social isolation, carer burnout, and residential care admission. It is not a minor quality-of-life issue.
    </p>

    <p>
      The diagnostic first step is typing, because the interventions diverge sharply:
    </p>

    <ul>
      <li><strong>Urgency incontinence (overactive bladder)</strong> — sudden, compelling urge to void with involuntary leakage; detrusor overactivity is the usual mechanism. First-line treatment is bladder training and pelvic floor muscle exercises, not anticholinergics.</li>
      <li><strong>Stress incontinence</strong> — leakage with increased intra-abdominal pressure (coughing, sneezing, lifting); weak urethral sphincter or pelvic floor. First-line is pelvic floor physiotherapy.</li>
      <li><strong>Mixed incontinence</strong> — features of both; treat the predominant component first.</li>
      <li><strong>Overflow incontinence</strong> — dribbling leakage from a chronically distended bladder; often painless in older adults. Caused by outlet obstruction (prostatic enlargement, pelvic organ prolapse) or detrusor underactivity (neuropathic, medication-related). Post-void residual measurement is essential — a PVR &gt;150–200 mL warrants further investigation.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>
      Before any pharmacological treatment, a medication review is mandatory. Diuretics (timing matters — a single morning dose is better tolerated than divided doses), anticholinergics, alpha-blockers, calcium channel blockers, and opioids all affect bladder function. Check for urinary tract infection (a common precipitant in older women) and consider atrophic vaginitis in postmenopausal women — it responds well to topical oestrogen, which is not contraindicated in most older women and does not carry the systemic risks of oral HRT.
    </p>

    <p>
      A three-day bladder diary (recording fluid intake, voiding frequency, volume, and episodes of leakage) provides invaluable diagnostic information and baseline data — and often surprises patients who had underestimated their fluid intake or overestimated their voiding frequency. Post-void residual can be measured with a portable bladder scanner in practices that have one, or arranged in secondary care.
    </p>

    <p>
      Pelvic floor physiotherapy is significantly underutilised and underreferred in older adults. It is effective for both stress and urgency incontinence, can be delivered in primary care or via community physiotherapy referral, and has no adverse effects. Bladder training — timed voiding with gradual extension of voiding intervals — is the behavioural treatment of choice for urgency incontinence. These should be tried before pharmacotherapy in most patients.
    </p>

    <p>
      If pharmacotherapy is needed for urgency incontinence, the beta-3 agonist mirabegron is preferred over anticholinergics (oxybutynin, solifenacin, tolterodine) in older adults because it does not carry anticholinergic cognitive burden. Mirabegron is subsidised in New Zealand under Special Authority for overactive bladder refractory to conservative management. Note the contraindication in severe uncontrolled hypertension. Faecal incontinence is addressed separately — see the <a href="/geriatrics/faecal-incontinence/">faecal incontinence page</a>.
    </p>
