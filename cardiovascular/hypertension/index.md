<h2>Confirming the diagnosis</h2>

<p>A single elevated clinic reading is not hypertension. Confirm with either ambulatory blood pressure monitoring (ABPM, daytime average) or home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM, twice daily for 7 days, discard day 1, average the rest). White-coat hypertension is common and usually does not need treatment; masked hypertension is real and worth screening for in patients with known CVD risk and normal-looking clinic BPs.</p>

<h2>Workup at diagnosis</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bloods:</strong> creatinine and eGFR, electrolytes, fasting glucose or HbA1c, lipid profile, TSH if clinically indicated</li>
  <li><strong>Urine:</strong> ACR (proteinuria), dipstick</li>
  <li><strong>ECG:</strong> baseline; consider echo if LVH on ECG, suspected heart failure, or murmur</li>
  <li><strong>CVD risk:</strong> calculate 5-year risk via PREDICT - the result drives the BP target</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to think secondary</h2>

<p>Secondary hypertension accounts for around 5 to 10% of cases and is more likely in young patients, sudden-onset hypertension, resistant hypertension, or hypertension with hypokalaemia or other unexpected biochemistry. The screen depends on the suspicion:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Renal artery stenosis</strong> - asymmetric kidneys, deteriorating renal function on ACEi/ARB, audible bruit. Renal artery duplex.</li>
  <li><strong>Primary aldosteronism (Conn's)</strong> - hypertension with low or low-normal potassium, especially if resistant. Aldosterone-renin ratio off all interfering drugs.</li>
  <li><strong>Phaeochromocytoma</strong> - paroxysmal headaches, sweating, palpitations. Plasma metanephrines.</li>
  <li><strong>Obstructive sleep apnoea</strong> - common, under-diagnosed, contributes to resistance. Epworth, refer for sleep study.</li>
  <li><strong>Cushing's, hyperthyroidism, coarctation, drug-induced (NSAIDs, COC, decongestants, liquorice)</strong> - clinical clues drive workup.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Drug choice in detail</h2>

<p>Start with the most appropriate single agent and uptitrate before adding a second. Do not combine ACEi with ARB. In NZ, candesartan and cilazapril are the most commonly used ARB and ACEi respectively (both well-funded), amlodipine is the default CCB, and indapamide or chlortalidone are preferred over older thiazides where available. Spironolactone (or eplerenone if intolerant) is the standard fourth-line agent for resistant hypertension.</p>

<h2>Special populations</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Diabetes:</strong> ACEi or ARB first regardless of age; tighter BP target in those with proteinuria</li>
  <li><strong>CKD:</strong> ACEi or ARB first; expect a small creatinine rise; reduce or stop if eGFR drops more than 25%</li>
  <li><strong>Pregnancy and pre-conception:</strong> labetalol, methyldopa, nifedipine MR; stop ACEi/ARB</li>
  <li><strong>Older adults:</strong> stand and check for postural drop; relax targets where falls risk is high</li>
</ul>

<h2>Monitoring after starting treatment</h2>

<p>Recheck BP and renal function at 2 to 4 weeks after starting or up-titrating ACEi/ARB or diuretic. Once at target, 6 to 12 monthly review is typical, more often if comorbid. Encourage HBPM to keep the patient engaged and reduce reliance on clinic readings.</p>
