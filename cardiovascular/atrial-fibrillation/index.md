<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>Atrial fibrillation confers stroke risk regardless of symptoms. Calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc score at diagnosis. All patients with score ≥1 (men) or ≥2 (women) need anticoagulation unless contraindicated. Do not rely on perceived "paroxysmal" nature to defer anticoagulation.</p>

<div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Confirm with ECG (irregular ventricular rate, absent P waves). Anticoagulate if CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥1 (men) or ≥2 (women): DOAC preferred (apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban, rivaroxaban) or warfarin. Rate control: beta-blocker, verapamil, or diltiazem (target HR <110 bpm at rest).</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>Haemodynamic instability or acute heart failure; symptomatic despite rate control; first episode of AF with rapid ventricular response; secondary AF (thyroid, PE, sepsis). Cardiology review if rhythm control strategy considered.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile tell-patient">

<p>"Atrial fibrillation is an irregular heartbeat that increases your risk of stroke. The anticoagulation medication protects your brain. Rate control medication helps your heart work efficiently."</p>

## Diagnosis

Atrial fibrillation is an irregular supraventricular arrhythmia. Diagnosis is confirmed by ECG showing irregular ventricular rate with absent P waves. Symptoms vary: some patients have palpitations, dyspnoea, or chest discomfort; others are asymptomatic. AF may present acutely or be discovered incidentally.

Categorize AF as:
- **Paroxysmal**: recurrent, self-terminating episodes
- **Persistent**: sustained >7 days and requires intervention to terminate
- **Permanent**: AF present and accepted (no further rhythm control attempted)

## Stroke risk assessment

All AF patients require stroke risk assessment using the **CHA₂DS₂-VASc score**:

- **C**: congestive heart failure (1 point)
- **H**: hypertension (1 point)
- **A₂**: age ≥75 (2 points)
- **D**: diabetes (1 point)
- **S₂**: stroke/TIA/thromboembolism history (2 points)
- **V**: vascular disease (MI, PAD, aortic plaque) (1 point)
- **A**: age 65–74 (1 point)
- **Sc**: sex (female) (1 point)

**Anticoagulation thresholds:**
- Men with score ≥1: anticoagulate
- Women with score ≥2: anticoagulate
- Score 0 (men) or 1 (women): low risk; aspirin 81 mg daily is an option if anticoagulation declined

The **HAS-BLED score** assesses bleeding risk (hypertension, abnormal renal/liver, stroke, bleeding history, labile INR, elderly, drugs/alcohol). Score ≥3 suggests high bleeding risk; do not withhold anticoagulation, but monitor more closely.

## Anticoagulation

**Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs)** are preferred: apixaban 5 mg twice daily, dabigatran 110–150 mg twice daily, edoxaban 30–60 mg daily, or rivaroxaban 20 mg daily. DOACs are more convenient than warfarin (no INR monitoring) and equally effective for stroke prevention in most AF patients.

Use **warfarin** (target INR 2–3) if DOAC is contraindicated (severe renal impairment, mechanical valve, contraindication listed for specific DOAC).

Reassess anticoagulation annually. Do not discontinue anticoagulation based on paroxysmal nature or symptom resolution; stroke risk persists.

## Rate control

Target resting ventricular rate <110 bpm (lenient rate control). Strict rate control (<80 bpm) does not improve outcomes over lenient control but increases medication burden.

- **Beta-blocker**: metoprolol, bisoprolol, or carvedilol (first-line; also reduce angina risk)
- **Calcium-channel blocker**: verapamil or diltiazem (if beta-blocker contraindicated)
- **Digoxin**: less commonly used; primarily for sedentary patients or those with heart failure

Add agents if monotherapy insufficient. Combination beta-blocker plus verapamil is common.

## Rhythm control

Rhythm control (aiming to restore and maintain sinus rhythm) is not superior to rate control for mortality or functional outcomes. Reserve for:
- First episode of AF (attempt to prevent recurrence)
- Haemodynamically unstable AF
- Significant functional impairment despite rate control
- AF secondary to an acute cause (PE, thyroid storm, sepsis)

Rhythm control requires antiarrhythmic drugs (flecainide, amiodarone, sotalol) and monitoring. Refer to cardiology for rhythm control strategy.

## Management of acute AF with rapid ventricular response

If patient is haemodynamically stable, rate control in primary care with beta-blocker IV (e.g., metoprolol 25 mg IV) or calcium-channel blocker IV (verapamil 5 mg IV) is reasonable. If unstable or ongoing symptoms, refer acutely for evaluation and possible DC cardioversion.

## Secondary AF

Identify and treat underlying causes: hyperthyroidism, pneumonia, PE, MI, severe dehydration, surgery, sepsis, or stimulant use. AF may resolve once the trigger is addressed.

## AF in older adults

AF is common in older patients. Anticoagulation reduces stroke risk despite age. Do not withhold anticoagulation due to age alone; assess CHA₂DS₂-VASc score and bleeding risk (HAS-BLED). Falls risk is commonly cited as reason to avoid anticoagulation, but data do not support withholding anticoagulation in older patients who fall without severe head trauma.

## Follow-up

Arrange cardiology review if rhythm control is considered, if symptomatic despite rate control, or if AF is secondary. Monitor rate control at 2–4 weeks and then regularly. Reassess renal function annually (affects DOAC dosing). Counsel on medication adherence, alcohol limitation (excessive alcohol triggers AF), and symptom reporting.
