<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>Palpitations with syncope, dyspnoea, chest pain, or haemodynamic instability warrant urgent evaluation. ECG is essential to rule out arrhythmia. Red flags: regular rapid rate >150 bpm, short runs of VT, or signs of structural heart disease.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Thorough history: rate, regularity, duration, triggers (caffeine, exercise, stress), associated symptoms. Examination and ECG at rest. If ECG normal and no structural disease, most palpitations are benign. Holter or event monitoring if arrhythmia suspected but ECG normal.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>Syncope or pre-syncope with palpitations, sustained tachycardia >150 bpm, ECG shows VT or other serious arrhythmia, structural heart disease (murmur, reduced EF), or family history of sudden cardiac death. Rapid AF with poor rate control also warrants urgent referral.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile tell-patient">
<h3>Tell the patient</h3>
<p>"Palpitations are an awareness of your heart beating. Most are harmless, especially if your heart rhythm is regular and you have no other symptoms. We'll check your heart to be sure."</p>

## History and characterization

Palpitations are an awareness of heart beating, usually described as a "racing," "pounding," "fluttering," or "skipping" sensation. Characterize them:

- **Rate**: slow (<60 bpm), normal (60–100), or fast (>100)
- **Regularity**: regular (suggests PSVT, atrial flutter, sinus tach) versus irregular (AF, ectopics, varying AV conduction)
- **Onset and offset**: sudden (suggests PSVT or AF) versus gradual (sinus tachycardia)
- **Duration**: seconds, minutes, hours, or all day
- **Triggers**: caffeine, alcohol, exercise, emotion, certain foods, menstrual cycle
- **Associated symptoms**: dyspnoea, chest pain, syncope, fatigue

Ask about cardiac risk factors, family history of sudden death or arrhythmias, and medications (sympathomimetics, antiarrhythmics, TCA).

## Examination

Check pulse (rate, regularity). Measure blood pressure. Auscultate heart for murmurs (structural disease). Assess for signs of heart failure (JVP, oedema, crackles). Examine thyroid (hyperthyroidism). Check for tremor (anxiety, hyperthyroid).

## Resting ECG

ECG is the essential first test. Perform while the patient is having palpitations if possible (more likely to capture arrhythmia). Look for:

- **Sinus tachycardia**: regular rate >100; usually benign if no other findings
- **Atrial fibrillation**: irregular rate, absent P waves
- **Atrial flutter**: saw-tooth baseline with regular ventricular response (2:1, 3:1)
- **Premature atrial/ventricular ectopics**: early beats
- **PSVT**: regular rapid rate (140–250 bpm), no visible P waves (buried in QRS or T wave)
- **Prolonged QT interval**: risk for torsades de pointes
- **WPW pattern**: short PR interval, delta wave (risk for rapid AF)
- **Structural abnormality**: LVH, ischaemic changes

## Differential diagnosis

**Benign causes:**
- Ectopic beats (PACs, PVCs): isolated early beats, "skipping" sensation. Usually benign if structurally normal heart.
- Anxiety or panic: palpitations during stress or panic attacks; hyperventilation.
- Sinus tachycardia: response to exercise, fever, hyperthyroidism, anaemia, caffeine, dehydration.
- Haemodynamic changes: pregnancy, postural hypotension, blood loss.

**Arrhythmias requiring assessment:**
- Atrial fibrillation
- Atrial flutter
- PSVT (atrioventricular nodal or atrioventricular reentrant tachycardia)
- VT (usually in setting of structural disease)

**Cardiac causes:**
- Structural disease: valve disease, cardiomyopathy, myocarditis
- Coronary artery disease

**Non-cardiac causes:**
- Hyperthyroidism
- Anaemia
- Hypoglycaemia
- Adrenergic excess (phaeochromocytoma, stimulant use)

## Investigations

**ECG**: essential, as above.

**Thyroid function**: if palpitations are persistent or accompanied by weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor.

**Echocardiography**: if ECG shows structural abnormality, murmur on exam, reduced EF, or recurrent palpitations with suspicion of underlying disease. Not needed for isolated ectopics in a structurally normal heart.

**Holter monitoring (24 hours) or event recording**: if arrhythmia is suspected but ECG is normal. Patient records symptoms and correlation with rhythm is assessed. Holter is useful if episodes are frequent; event monitor is better for infrequent episodes.

## Management of benign palpitations

If ECG is normal, no structural disease is detected, and symptoms are benign (no syncope, no rapid sustained tachycardia):

- Reassurance: most palpitations are benign
- Identify and eliminate triggers: reduce caffeine, alcohol, stress
- Treat anxiety if present
- Regular exercise and adequate sleep
- Beta-blocker (e.g., metoprolol 25–50 mg daily) if symptoms persist and patient is bothered

## Management of ectopic beats

Isolated ectopics (PACs or PVCs) are benign in a structurally normal heart, especially if asymptomatic or mildly bothersome. Do not treat with antiarrhythmics (which can be proarrhythmic). Reassure and suggest trigger avoidance. If very frequent or symptomatic, beta-blocker may help.

## Red flags warranting urgent assessment

Palpitations with:
- Syncope or pre-syncope
- Chest pain or dyspnoea
- Signs of heart failure
- Sustained rapid rate >150 bpm
- Family history of sudden death
- Structural heart disease

Refer acutely to emergency department or cardiology for further evaluation.

## Follow-up

Review ECG interpretation with patient. Counsel on triggers. If monitoring is arranged, schedule follow-up to discuss findings. Document reassurance provided. Most palpitations resolve with education and trigger avoidance.
