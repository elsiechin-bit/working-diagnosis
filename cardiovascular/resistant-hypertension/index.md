## Definition

Resistant hypertension is defined as blood pressure >130/80 mmHg despite concurrent use of three or more antihypertensive agents at optimal doses, including a diuretic. It affects 10–15% of hypertensive patients and is associated with higher cardiovascular risk and renal disease.

## Confirm the diagnosis

Before labelling someone as resistant, confirm with home BP monitoring over 5–7 days (average of 2–3 readings each morning for 7 days). White-coat hypertension accounts for 25% of apparent resistant cases. Once confirmed, assess adherence directly: ask about side effects, cost, complexity of regimen, or forgetfulness.

## Exclude secondary causes

Secondary hypertension occurs in 10–15% of resistant cases. Screen for:

- **Sleep apnoea**: daytime somnolence, snoring, witnessed apnoeas. If suspected, refer for sleep study.
- **Chronic kidney disease**: check eGFR and urine ACR. CKD <30 mL/min requires specialist input.
- **Primary hyperaldosteronism**: hypokalaemia despite ACE/ARB, metabolic alkalosis, or low-renin hypertension. Refer if aldosterone-to-renin ratio >30.
- **Phaeochromocytoma**: episodic sweating, tremor, palpitations, severe headache, or paroxysmal hypertension. Check 24-hour urine metanephrines or plasma free metanephrines.
- **Aortic coarctation**: blood pressure higher in arms than legs, radio-femoral delay. Rare but critical to exclude in young patients.
- **Renal artery stenosis**: sudden onset of hypertension (especially >55 years), renal bruit, or decline in renal function after ACE/ARB.

## Optimize existing therapy

Ensure triple therapy is maximal:

- ACE inhibitor (e.g., lisinopril 40 mg daily) or ARB (e.g., valsartan 160 mg daily)
- Calcium-channel blocker (e.g., amlodipine 10 mg daily)
- Thiazide diuretic (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide 25 mg daily, or chlorthalidone 25 mg, which is more potent)

Check sodium intake (target <2300 mg daily). If thiazide dose is low or furosemide is used instead, switch or increase. Loop diuretics are less effective for hypertension unless renal function is impaired.

## Fourth-agent options

If triple therapy is optimal and home BP remains uncontrolled:

- **Amlodipine (additional dose)**: some patients need 20 mg daily in divided doses, though maximal single dose is 10 mg.
- **Doxazosin 8 mg daily**: alpha-blocker; effective but can cause dizziness or hypotension.
- **Spironolactone 25–50 mg daily**: potassium-sparing diuretic; effective in resistant cases. Check potassium at baseline, 1 week, and 1 month after starting.
- **Amiloride 5 mg daily**: another potassium-sparer; use if hypokalaemia is concern.

## When to refer

Refer to cardiology or a specialist hypertension clinic if:
- Age <40 and resistant hypertension (suggests secondary cause)
- Fourth agent needed and patient is tolerating poorly
- End-organ damage (proteinuria, LVH on ECG, renal impairment)
- Hypertensive urgency or emergency
- Suspicion of secondary cause

## Follow-up

Recheck BP at 4 weeks after any adjustment. Aim for <130/80 mmHg; some data suggests <120/80 in high-risk patients, but individualize targets. Monitor electrolytes and renal function 4 weeks after adding any potassium-sparing agent.
