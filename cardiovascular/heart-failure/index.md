## Diagnosis

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome of dyspnoea, fatigue, and exercise intolerance caused by cardiac dysfunction (systolic or diastolic). Symptoms may be at rest or on exertion. Key features:

- **Dyspnoea**: exertional, orthopnea, nocturnal cough, or paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea
- **Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance**
- **Peripheral oedema**: ankles, legs, sacrum
- **Hepatomegaly and ascites**: in advanced disease
- **Signs**: elevated JVP, displaced apex beat, third heart sound (S3 gallop), pulmonary crackles

Diagnosis requires BNP >100 ng/L or NT-proBNP >125 ng/L *and* objective evidence of cardiac dysfunction (echocardiography, cardiac MRI, or stress imaging).

## Classification by ejection fraction

Categorize HF based on echocardiographic ejection fraction:

- **HFrEF** (reduced): EF ≤40%
- **HFmrEF** (mildly reduced): EF 41–49%
- **HFpEF** (preserved): EF ≥50%

All groups benefit from ACE/ARB and SGLT2 inhibitors; beta-blockers have strongest evidence in HFrEF.

## Investigations

**BNP or NT-proBNP**: supports diagnosis if >100 ng/L (BNP) or >125 ng/L (NT-proBNP). Repeat after treatment initiation to guide optimisation.

**Echocardiography**: assesses EF, wall motion abnormalities, valve disease, chamber size, and diastolic function. Essential to guide therapy choice.

**ECG**: assess for ischaemia, bundle branch block (indicates CRT eligibility), and arrhythmias.

**Chest X-ray**: pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly, pleural effusion.

**Full blood count, urea, electrolytes, creatinine, glucose**: assess renal function, anaemia, and electrolyte status before starting medications.

## Medical management of HFrEF

Start all four pillars (unless contraindicated):

**ACE inhibitor or ARB**: e.g., lisinopril 10–40 mg daily or valsartan 40–160 mg daily. Reduces mortality and HF progression. Check creatinine and potassium before starting and after 1–2 weeks.

**Beta-blocker**: bisoprolol 1.25–10 mg daily, metoprolol 25–190 mg daily, or carvedilol 3.125–25 mg twice daily. Target HR 50–70 bpm if tolerated. Start low, titrate slowly.

**SGLT2 inhibitor**: dapagliflozin 10 mg daily or empagliflozin 10 mg daily. Benefits all HF regardless of EF or diabetes status. Reduces HF hospitalisation and mortality.

**Aldosterone antagonist**: spironolactone 12.5–50 mg daily (if EF <35% and potassium <5.0 mmol/L). Check potassium at baseline, 1 week, and 1 month.

**Diuretic**: furosemide 20–80 mg daily or equivalent, adjusted to symptom control. Use cautiously to avoid hypotension and worsening renal function.

## Management of HFpEF

No drugs are proven to reduce mortality in HFpEF. Focus on:

- Control blood pressure (target <130/80)
- Manage comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, AF, obesity)
- Diuretic for symptom relief
- ACE/ARB and SGLT2 inhibitors may be considered (growing evidence)
- Cardiac rehabilitation and exercise

## Acute heart failure (decompensation)

Acute dyspnoea, orthopnoea, pulmonary oedema, or hypoxia is a medical emergency. Refer acutely to hospital for:

- Oxygen to target SpO₂ >90%
- IV diuretic (furosemide 40–80 mg IV)
- Vasodilator (nitroglycerin IV if hypertensive)
- Echocardiography if EF unknown
- Investigation of trigger (infection, AF, ischaemia, medication non-adherence)

## Triggers of decompensation

Identify and manage:
- Infection (pneumonia, UTI, sepsis)
- Non-adherence to medications or sodium restriction
- Acute coronary syndrome
- Arrhythmia (especially AF with rapid ventricular response)
- Hypertensive crisis
- New cardiac dysfunction (myocarditis, valve disease)
- Renal deterioration
- Medication (NSAIDs, some antiarrhythmics)

## Device therapy

Refer for consideration of:
- **ICD** (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator): if EF ≤35% despite optimal medical therapy and secondary prevention of sudden cardiac death is appropriate
- **CRT** (cardiac resynchronisation therapy): if EF ≤35%, QRS ≥120 ms, and left bundle branch block pattern

## Follow-up

Arrange cardiology assessment for all new HF. Primary care follow-up at 2 weeks after diagnosis or medication change, then regularly (every 3–6 months if stable). Titrate medications to target doses. Monitor renal function, electrolytes, and BNP. Screen for depression and refer to mental health services if needed. Encourage exercise, salt restriction (<3 g daily), and fluid restriction (if hyponatraemia).

## Red flags for acute assessment

Acute dyspnoea, syncope, chest pain, tachycardia >110 bpm, hypotension, or confusion warrant urgent hospital referral.
