## Diagnosis

Stable angina is reproducible chest pain or pressure triggered by exertion (walking uphill, rushing, cold weather, stress) and relieved by rest or sublingual nitrate within 15 minutes. Characteristics:

- **Location**: substernal, may radiate to left arm, neck, jaw, or back
- **Character**: pressure, tightness, heaviness, or squeezing (not sharp or pleuritic)
- **Triggers**: exertion, emotional stress, cold weather
- **Duration**: typically 5–15 minutes
- **Relief**: rest or GTN within 15 minutes
- **Associated symptoms**: dyspnoea, nausea, fatigue (without prominent GI symptoms)

Atypical presentations occur in women (more dyspnoea than pain), older adults, and diabetics (silent ischaemia). Always consider angina if exertional chest discomfort occurs in anyone with cardiovascular risk factors.

## Initial assessment

Obtain a detailed history of angina pattern, triggers, and relief. Check cardiovascular risk factors: age, smoking, diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, obesity, family history, physical activity. Perform ECG at rest (may show ST changes, T-wave inversions, or be normal). Check troponin if any doubt about stability. Assess functional limitation: is angina limiting activities of daily living or occurring with minimal exertion?

## Referral pathway

All new angina should be referred for assessment. The decision to investigate (exercise stress test, imaging, or angiography) depends on symptom severity and functional impact. High-risk features warrant urgent referral: age >60, diabetes, reduced ejection fraction, persistent symptoms despite medication, or limiting functional capacity. Low-risk stable angina may be managed conservatively with medication and regular follow-up.

## Medical management

Start one of the following:

- **Beta-blocker**: reduces heart rate and myocardial oxygen demand. Start metoprolol 25–50 mg daily, increase to 100–200 mg daily as tolerated. Monitor BP and heart rate. Contraindications: asthma, bradycardia, decompensated heart failure.
- **Calcium-channel blocker**: dilates coronary vessels and reduces afterload. Amlodipine 5–10 mg daily or diltiazem 180–240 mg daily. Use if beta-blocker contraindicated or incompletely controlling symptoms.

Add **short-acting sublingual nitrate** (GTN spray 0.3–0.6 mg) for acute episodes. Advise sitting down before using to prevent syncope. If symptoms persist despite monotherapy, add the other class (beta-blocker plus calcium-channel blocker) or a long-acting nitrate.

**Long-acting nitrate** (e.g., isosorbide mononitrate 40–60 mg daily): use if symptoms occur frequently. Advise a nitrate-free interval (12 hours off per 24 hours) to prevent tolerance. Do not confuse long-acting nitrate timing with short-acting GTN rescue use.

## Additional management

- **Aspirin 81 mg daily**: reduces secondary event risk.
- **Statin**: high-intensity statin regardless of LDL (angina itself indicates high risk).
- **ACE inhibitor or ARB**: if hypertension, diabetes, or LV dysfunction.
- **Address risk factors**: smoking cessation, weight loss, exercise (cardiac rehab if available), blood pressure and glucose control.

## Response to treatment and follow-up

Expect symptom improvement within 1–2 weeks. If angina worsens or becomes unstable (pain at rest, increasing frequency, or changing character), escalate assessment. Review adherence to medications at each visit. Reassess functional capacity every 3–6 months. If medical management is insufficient or patient is disabled by symptoms, discuss coronary intervention.

## Revascularisation

Angiography and revascularisation (PCI or CABG) are considered if angina is not controlled by medication and severely limits functional capacity, or if stress testing or imaging shows extensive ischaemia. This decision is made in secondary care based on anatomy, symptom burden, and patient preference.
