## What is absolute CVD risk?

Absolute risk is the probability of having a cardiovascular event (heart attack, stroke, or vascular death) in the next 10 years. It depends on age, blood pressure, cholesterol, and smoking status—not on symptoms or family history alone. The SCORE2 algorithm is validated for European populations and is used in NZ guidance.

## SCORE2 calculation and thresholds

SCORE2 is the European risk calculator. In NZ primary care, use it for adults aged 40–69 with no history of CVD, atrial fibrillation, or chronic kidney disease. Inputs are age, sex, systolic blood pressure, total cholesterol, and smoking status. Output is 10-year risk of fatal or non-fatal cardiovascular event.

Risk categories:
- Low risk: <5% (continue lifestyle measures, reassess periodically)
- Moderate risk: 5–10% (discuss preventive treatment; consider statin if risk factors are rising or patient is worried)
- High risk: 10–20% (offer statin; consider further investigation depending on clinical picture)
- Very high risk: ≥20% (intensive treatment; consider specialist review)

## When to calculate

Offer risk assessment in routine checks, particularly around age 40–50. Also calculate when starting someone on antihypertensive therapy, after a new diagnosis of diabetes, or if hyperlipidaemia is found. Do not delay cardiovascular preventive treatment while searching for family history.

## Who does SCORE2 not apply to?

SCORE2 is not for people with established CVD (use secondary prevention algorithms instead), diabetes (they are already at high risk), chronic kidney disease (CKD stage 3b and below increases risk), or atrial fibrillation (they need anticoagulation, not risk score alone).

## Familial hypercholesterolaemia

Suspect if total cholesterol >7.5 mmol/L, LDL >4.8 mmol/L, or premature CVD in first-degree relatives. FH is very high risk. Treat with high-intensity statin regardless of absolute risk score. Refer to lipid clinic if LDL remains high on maximum statin or if cascade screening is needed.

## Communicating risk

Explain risk in plain language. Avoid saying "you are high-risk"—instead, explain the percentage: "Out of 100 people like you, about 15 might have a cardiovascular event in the next 10 years." Offer treatment options, not directives. Revisit risk if smoking status changes, blood pressure improves, or new risk factors emerge.
