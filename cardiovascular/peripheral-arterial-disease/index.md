<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>PAD presents as exertional leg pain (claudication) or foot ulcers. Assess with clinical history and ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI). ABPI <0.9 confirms PAD. All PAD patients require aggressive cardiovascular risk reduction: antiplatelet therapy, statin, blood pressure control, and diabetes management. <div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Take claudication history: location, onset with exertion, relief at rest. Examine foot pulses (femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) and skin. Measure ABPI (systolic ankle pressure / systolic brachial pressure). ABPI 0.91–0.99 = borderline; <0.9 = PAD. Start aspirin 81 mg daily, high-intensity statin, and optimise blood pressure and glucose.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>Critical limb ischaemia (rest pain, tissue loss, gangrene); ABPI <0.5; claudication significantly limiting function and patient fit for intervention; or duplex shows significant stenosis and patient is a candidate for revascularisation (angioplasty or bypass).</p>
</div>

## Diagnosis

Peripheral arterial disease is atherosclerotic narrowing of arteries supplying the legs, aorta, or visceral vessels. Symptoms range from asymptomatic to critical limb ischaemia.

**Claudication**: exertional calf, thigh, or buttock pain (reproducible, triggered by walking, relieved by rest within 10 minutes). Characteristic location reflects vessel involved (calf = tibial/peroneal; thigh = superficial femoral; buttock = iliac/aortic). Pain is never at rest initially and resolves quickly with stopping.

**Atypical presentations**: exertional leg fatigue, weakness, or numbness. Some older patients report isolated foot pain or slowness rather than cramping.

**Critical limb ischaemia**: rest pain (typically at night, worse supine, improves with leg dependency), tissue loss (ulcers, gangrene), or infection. Medical emergency requiring urgent vascular assessment.

## Physical examination

Check both legs:
- **Peripheral pulses**: femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial. Absence suggests proximal stenosis.
- **Skin**: pallor, hair loss, thin shiny skin, temperature gradient
- **Foot ulcers or gangrene**: location (heels, toes, lateral foot)
- **Bruits**: aortic, iliac, femoral; suggests turbulent flow from stenosis
- **Blood pressure**: asymmetry between arms (suggests subclavian or aortic disease)

## Ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI)

ABPI is essential first test. Measure supine systolic pressures at both ankles and both arms using handheld Doppler and sphygmomanometer. Calculate ABPI for each leg:

ABPI = highest ankle systolic pressure (same leg) / highest arm systolic pressure (either arm)

Interpret:
- **Normal**: 0.91–1.30
- **Borderline**: 0.91–0.99 (repeat or consider duplex)
- **Mild-to-moderate PAD**: 0.50–0.90
- **Severe PAD**: <0.50
- **Non-compressible**: >1.30 (suggests medial sclerosis; duplex needed)

ABPI <0.9 in a patient with claudication confirms diagnosis.

## Further investigation

**Duplex ultrasound**: if ABPI abnormal, claudication symptoms, or critical limb ischaemia. Assesses haemodynamic significance of stenosis and locates lesions. Non-invasive, no radiation.

**CT or MR angiography**: if considering revascularisation; shows vessel anatomy and lesion morphology for surgical planning. Refer to vascular specialist.

**Exercise stress test**: if claudication diagnosis uncertain or to confirm reproducibility (walk to claudication, measure time and distance).

## Management of claudication

**Conservative first-line:**
- Supervised exercise or walking programme: improves walking distance and symptoms through collateral development. Refer for cardiac rehab if available.
- Smoking cessation: critical; smokers have worse outcomes
- Weight loss and diabetes control
- Comorbidity management

**Medications:**
- **Antiplatelet therapy**: aspirin 81 mg daily reduces risk of MI and stroke (not proven to improve claudication symptoms)
- **Statin**: high-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80 mg or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg) for cardiovascular protection regardless of cholesterol level
- **Blood pressure control**: target <130/80 (antihypertensive also reduces symptoms in some)
- **ACE inhibitor or ARB**: if hypertension or diabetes, for cardioprotection
- **Cilostazol**: 100 mg twice daily (if available; improves walking distance in claudication). Not funded universally in NZ; check local prescribing guidelines

## Critical limb ischaemia

Rest pain, tissue loss, or infection is a medical emergency requiring same-day vascular referral for possible:
- IV antibiotic (if infection)
- IV vasodilator or anticoagulation
- Urgent duplex or angiography
- Revascularisation (angioplasty, stenting, or bypass) if technically feasible
- Amputation if unsalvageable

## Revascularisation

Angioplasty (percutaneous transluminal angioplasty, PTA) or bypass surgery is considered if claudication significantly limits function/quality of life, patient is fit for intervention, and duplex shows significant stenosis with adequate run-off vessels. Decision made by vascular specialists based on anatomy and patient factors.

## Secondary prevention

All PAD patients require:
- Smoking cessation
- Diabetes and blood pressure control
- Statin therapy
- Antiplatelet therapy
- Regular review of foot health
- Annual influenza and once-lifetime pneumococcal vaccination

## Acute limb ischaemia

Sudden onset of cold, painful, pale leg with absent pulses is acute thromboembolism or graft thrombosis. Requires emergency vascular assessment and imaging. Refer immediately to hospital.

## Follow-up

Review claudication severity, functional impact, and medication adherence every 3–6 months. Repeat ABPI if symptoms change significantly. Examine feet at each visit (screen for ulcers). Reinforce exercise and cardiovascular risk factor modification. Refer to vascular surgery if claudication worsens, function deteriorates, or critical limb ischaemia develops.
