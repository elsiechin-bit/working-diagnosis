<p>Urinary incontinence affects roughly one in three women and one in ten men over 65 in NZ.[1] Most do not bring it up with their GP, often because they assume it is a normal part of ageing or feel embarrassed. It is not normal, and it is treatable. The first step is identifying which type you have, because the treatments are quite different.</p>

<h2>The four main types</h2>

<h3>Stress incontinence</h3>
<p>Leakage with cough, sneeze, laugh, lifting, or exercise. Usually small amounts. The cause is weakness of the pelvic floor muscles or, in men, after prostate surgery. Most common in women who have had children. Pelvic floor exercises are the first-line treatment and they work for the majority of cases.</p>

<h3>Urge incontinence (overactive bladder)</h3>
<p>A sudden, hard-to-resist urge to urinate, often with leakage on the way to the toilet. Frequent urination through the day and waking at night to urinate. Often described as "the keys-in-the-door" feeling - the urge intensifies as you approach a known toilet. Caused by the bladder muscle contracting when it should be relaxed.</p>

<h3>Mixed incontinence</h3>
<p>Both stress and urge components. Common, especially in women over 60. Treatment usually addresses the dominant pattern first.</p>

<h3>Overflow incontinence</h3>
<p>Frequent dribbling of small amounts. Often a sense of incomplete emptying. In men, usually caused by prostate enlargement obstructing the urethra. In women, less common but can occur with severe pelvic organ prolapse or after pelvic surgery. In either sex, severe constipation, certain neurological conditions, and some medicines can contribute.</p>

<h3>Functional incontinence</h3>
<p>The bladder works fine but the person cannot reach the toilet in time because of mobility problems, cognitive impairment, or environmental barriers. Often improves with environmental changes (commode by the bed, easier-to-undo clothing, walking aids, lighting).</p>

<h2>Causes you can fix</h2>

<p>Before reaching for medicines, look at the things that frequently cause or worsen incontinence:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Urinary tract infection</strong> - especially if symptoms started recently or worsened suddenly</li>
  <li><strong>Constipation</strong> - a loaded bowel pushes on the bladder; worth treating even if not severe</li>
  <li><strong>Caffeine, alcohol, and fizzy drinks</strong> - all bladder irritants. Try a 4-week trial cut for everyone with urge incontinence</li>
  <li><strong>Diuretic medicines (water tablets)</strong> - timing matters; morning rather than evening dosing helps</li>
  <li><strong>Anticholinergic medicines</strong> - can paradoxically cause overflow in older men with enlarged prostates</li>
  <li><strong>Untreated diabetes</strong> - high blood sugar increases urine production</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep apnoea</strong> - causes a separate cause of nocturnal urination through atrial natriuretic peptide release</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic organ prolapse</strong> - common in older women, often missed unless examined</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to try first</h2>

<h3>Pelvic floor exercises (Kegels)</h3>
<p>Effective for stress and many cases of urge incontinence in both men and women. Done correctly and consistently, around 60-70% of women see meaningful improvement.[2] Done badly, they do nothing. The most important step is being taught the correct technique by a pelvic floor physiotherapist.</p>

<p>The basic exercise: tighten the muscles you would use to stop the flow of urine, hold for 5-10 seconds, then relax. Repeat 10-15 times, three times a day. Build up gradually. You should feel the contraction in the pelvic floor, not in the abdomen, buttocks, or thighs. If you cannot tell if you are doing it right, see a pelvic floor physio.</p>

<h3>Bladder retraining</h3>
<p>For urge incontinence. The aim is to gradually extend the interval between toilet visits, retraining the bladder to hold more without urgency. Start by going to the toilet on a fixed schedule (often every hour or two) regardless of urge, then extend by 15 minutes each week as urges become more manageable. Pelvic floor squeezes during an urge often delay it long enough to walk to the toilet.</p>

<h3>Lifestyle</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Reduce caffeine, alcohol, fizzy drinks. Trial 4 weeks of cutting them out</li>
  <li>Maintain healthy weight - even modest weight loss reduces leakage</li>
  <li>Stop smoking - chronic cough worsens stress incontinence</li>
  <li>Treat constipation - target soft, easily passed stools with adequate fibre, fluid, and movement</li>
  <li>Time fluid intake - drink most of your daily fluid before 6pm if you wake at night</li>
</ul>

<h3>Containment products</h3>
<p>Modern incontinence pads are far better than they used to be. They are not a treatment but they are dignity-preserving while you work on the underlying issue. Disability Allowance through Work and Income may help with cost.</p>

<h2>Medications</h2>

<p>For urge incontinence, the main classes are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Anticholinergics</strong> - oxybutynin, solifenacin, tolterodine. Funded in NZ. They reduce bladder muscle overactivity but cause dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, and confusion in older adults. Cumulative anticholinergic burden is associated with dementia risk; many GPs are cautious about long-term use, especially after age 75</li>
  <li><strong>Mirabegron</strong> - a different mechanism (beta-3 agonist) with less anticholinergic burden. Funded in NZ with Special Authority for those who have not tolerated anticholinergics</li>
  <li><strong>Topical estrogen</strong> for postmenopausal women - low-dose vaginal estrogen cream or pessary improves urge symptoms and reduces UTIs. Fully funded; safe for almost all women including those with breast cancer history (after specialist discussion)</li>
</ul>

<p>For stress incontinence, no oral medication is first-line. Pelvic floor work, weight loss, and where appropriate referral for surgical or pessary options are the main routes.</p>

<p>For overflow in men with prostatic enlargement, alpha blockers (tamsulosin, doxazosin) and 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride) reduce obstruction.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP - and when to ask for a specialist</h2>

<p>See your GP if any of these applies:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms have lasted more than a few weeks</li>
  <li>Sudden onset of urinary symptoms (urgency, frequency, leakage) - especially with fever or back pain - rule out UTI urgently</li>
  <li>Blood in the urine (visible or detected on dipstick) - always needs investigation</li>
  <li>Difficulty starting the stream, weak stream, or sense of incomplete emptying (men)</li>
  <li>Pelvic pressure or sensation of "something coming down" in women (prolapse)</li>
  <li>Symptoms have not improved despite 8 to 12 weeks of pelvic floor work and lifestyle changes</li>
</ul>

<p>Specialist options in NZ:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pelvic floor physiotherapist</strong> - usually private (around NZ$120-180 per session), some ACC funding for postnatal and post-surgical referrals; some DHBs offer a limited number of sessions free</li>
  <li><strong>Urogynaecology</strong> - DHB referral for women with prolapse, complex incontinence, or treatment-refractory disease</li>
  <li><strong>Urology</strong> - DHB referral for men with obstruction, suspected prostate disease, or any haematuria</li>
  <li><strong>Continence advisors</strong> - nurse specialists in some DHBs and through the NZ Continence Association; community-based assessment and product advice</li>
</ul>
