<p>A cataract is a clouding of the natural lens inside the eye. It is not a film over the eye, not a growth, and not a disease in the dramatic sense. It is part of how lenses age, and almost everyone develops some lens cloudiness if they live long enough. Around half of New Zealanders over 75 have a cataract significant enough to affect vision, and cataract surgery is the most commonly performed operation in the public health system here.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The good news is that the surgery is highly effective and very safe.</p>

<h2>What you might notice</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Vision becoming gradually blurred, foggy, or "like looking through a dirty window"</li>
  <li>Glare from oncoming car headlights at night, or sunlight reflecting off water</li>
  <li>Colours looking faded or yellow-tinged</li>
  <li>Frequent changes in your glasses prescription, or a temporary improvement in close-up vision (sometimes called "second sight")</li>
  <li>Difficulty reading street signs or recognising faces in the distance</li>
</ul>

<p>Cataracts develop slowly over years. Many people only realise how much vision they had lost after one eye is fixed.</p>

<h2>Risk factors</h2>

<p>Age is by far the biggest. Other contributors include diabetes, long-term oral steroid use, previous eye injury or surgery, smoking, heavy lifetime sun exposure (relevant in NZ), and certain medical conditions. Some cataracts are congenital or appear in childhood; these are uncommon.</p>

<h2>When is surgery worth doing?</h2>

<p>The decision is based on what the cataract is stopping you doing, not how it looks on examination. Surgery is reasonable when the cataract is interfering with everyday activities - reading, driving (especially night driving), recognising people, or doing the work or hobbies that matter to you.</p>

<p>In the New Zealand public system, eligibility for cataract surgery is decided using the Clinical Priority Assessment Criteria (CPAC) score. The score combines visual acuity with a question about how much the cataract is affecting your daily life. Each Te Whatu Ora district has its own threshold, so wait times and access vary by region.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> Privately, surgery is usually arranged within a few weeks and costs in the order of $3,500 to $5,500 per eye.</p>

<h2>What the surgery involves</h2>

<p>The procedure is called phacoemulsification. It is done as a day case under local anaesthetic, with sedation if you want it. The cloudy lens is broken up and removed through a small incision (usually 2 to 3 millimetres) and replaced with a clear plastic intraocular lens (IOL). The whole operation takes about 20 minutes.</p>

<p>You can choose between a standard monofocal IOL (sharp focus at one distance, usually distance vision, with reading glasses needed for close work) and a premium IOL (multifocal or extended depth of focus, less reliance on reading glasses but with some trade-offs in night vision and clarity). Premium IOLs are not funded in the public system. Talk through the options with your surgeon - the right choice depends on your eyes, your work, and what you read.</p>

<h2>Recovery</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You go home the same day, with a protective eye shield to wear that night and for a few nights of sleep.</li>
  <li>Vision is usually noticeably clearer within a day or two, with full settling over four to six weeks.</li>
  <li>You will use anti-inflammatory and antibiotic drops for around four weeks.</li>
  <li>Avoid swimming, eye rubbing, and getting soap in the eye for two weeks. Light walking, gentle exercise, and most normal activity are fine within a day or two.</li>
  <li>Most people are back to work within a few days for desk-based jobs; longer if your work involves dust, water, or heavy lifting.</li>
  <li>If you wear glasses, you will usually need a new prescription about four to six weeks after surgery.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Risks</h2>

<p>Cataract surgery is one of the safest operations in modern medicine. Serious complications occur in well under 1 percent of operations.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> The most common ones to know about are: posterior capsule opacification (a clouding of the membrane behind the new lens), which is easily fixed with a quick laser procedure later; postoperative inflammation; and rare but serious complications like infection or retinal detachment.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor or eye clinic urgently after surgery</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sudden worsening vision after initial improvement</li>
  <li>Increasing pain rather than mild discomfort</li>
  <li>Marked redness, discharge, or photophobia</li>
  <li>New floaters, flashes, or a curtain across vision (rare, but possible)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your eye specialist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>How much is the cataract affecting my vision in measurable terms?</li>
  <li>Public or private - what would the wait look like for me?</li>
  <li>Which IOL would suit my eyes and the way I use them?</li>
  <li>What does recovery look like for someone in my work?</li>
</ul>
