Asthma affects about 1 in 7 New Zealand adults - one of the highest rates in the world. It is a chronic condition where the airways become inflamed and twitchy, narrowing in response to triggers. With the right treatment most people can be symptom-free, exercise normally, and avoid hospital admissions.

<h2>What asthma is</h2>

<p>Three things happen in an asthma attack:</p>

<ol>
<li>The muscles around the small airways tighten (bronchoconstriction)</li>
<li>The lining of the airways swells and becomes inflamed</li>
<li>Extra mucus is produced</li>
</ol>

<p>Result: less air gets in and out, particularly out, which is why wheeze and tight breathing dominate.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wheezing (whistling on breathing out)</li>
<li>Shortness of breath</li>
<li>Tight chest</li>
<li>Cough, often worse at night or early morning</li>
<li>Symptoms triggered by exercise, cold air, allergens, or viruses</li>
</ul>

<p>Cough-variant asthma exists - some people only have a cough, with no wheeze.</p>

<h2>Triggers</h2>

<ul>
<li>Viral colds (the single most common trigger)</li>
<li>Allergens - dust mite, cat, pollen, mould</li>
<li>Cigarette smoke and vaping aerosol</li>
<li>Cold or dry air</li>
<li>Exercise</li>
<li>Strong emotions, laughter</li>
<li>Air pollution and smoke from fires</li>
<li>Some medicines (NSAIDs, beta blockers)</li>
<li>Reflux</li>
<li>Hormonal changes (some women flare around periods)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What good control looks like</h2>

<ul>
<li>No daytime symptoms (or rare and mild)</li>
<li>No night-time waking</li>
<li>No reliever use beyond once a week</li>
<li>Normal activity and exercise</li>
<li>No flare-ups needing oral steroids or hospital</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these is missing, your asthma is not well controlled - see your GP.</p>

<h2>The two types of inhaler</h2>

<p><strong>Reliever (blue) inhaler.</strong> Salbutamol (Ventolin). Opens airways within minutes; lasts 4-6 hours. For symptoms only.</p>

<p><strong>Preventer (often brown, orange, or purple) inhaler.</strong> Inhaled corticosteroid (ICS) - budesonide, fluticasone, beclomethasone, or combined with a long-acting bronchodilator. Reduces inflammation. Takes weeks to reach full effect. Used every day, even when feeling well.</p>

<h2>The big shift: AIR and MART</h2>

<p>NZ guidelines have changed substantially. The current evidence-based approach for most adults:</p>

<p><strong>AIR (Anti-Inflammatory Reliever):</strong> Use a single combined budesonide-formoterol inhaler (Symbicort) only when needed for symptoms - both relieves and treats inflammation in one puff.</p>

<p><strong>MART (Maintenance and Reliever Therapy):</strong> Use Symbicort daily AND for relief. The same inhaler does both jobs.</p>

<p>This approach is preferred over traditional salbutamol-only relief because it reduces flare-ups and admissions. Talk to your GP about which approach suits you.</p>

<p>The old "blue inhaler alone" approach for mild asthma is no longer recommended for most adults. Frequent salbutamol use without a steroid is associated with more severe attacks and deaths.&sup1;</p>

<h2>How asthma is diagnosed</h2>

<ul>
<li>History (key)</li>
<li>Spirometry (a breathing test) showing reversible airflow obstruction</li>
<li>Peak flow variability over 1-2 weeks</li>
<li>Sometimes a trial of treatment</li>
<li>Allergy testing in some cases</li>
</ul>

<h2>Daily life</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Take your preventer every day</strong> - the most common reason for poor control</li>
<li><strong>Use a spacer</strong> with metered-dose inhalers - more drug reaches your lungs. See our <a href="/handouts/asthma-inhaler-spacer/">inhaler technique handout</a></li>
<li><strong>Have an asthma action plan</strong> - see our <a href="/handouts/asthma-action-plan/">action plan handout</a></li>
<li><strong>Annual flu vaccine</strong> and COVID boosters - asthma raises the risk of severe respiratory infections</li>
<li><strong>Stop smoking and vaping</strong></li>
<li><strong>Treat hayfever</strong> - poorly controlled allergic rhinitis worsens asthma</li>
<li><strong>Maintain a healthy weight</strong> - obesity worsens asthma</li>
<li><strong>Exercise</strong> - regular exercise helps asthma in the long run; warm up before, take reliever before if exercise-triggered</li>
<li><strong>Annual asthma review</strong> with your GP or practice nurse</li>
</ul>

<h2>When asthma is severe</h2>

<p>People with severe asthma despite good treatment may benefit from specialist input:</p>

<ul>
<li>Higher-dose inhalers</li>
<li>Long-acting bronchodilators added (LABA)</li>
<li>LAMA (tiotropium)</li>
<li>Leukotriene antagonist (montelukast)</li>
<li>Biologics for eosinophilic asthma (omalizumab, mepolizumab, dupilumab) - PHARMAC-funded for specific criteria</li>
</ul>

<h2>Warning signs - same-day or 111</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> immediately for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Reliever needed every 1-2 hours</li>
<li>Cannot speak full sentences</li>
<li>Lips or fingertips blue</li>
<li>Drowsiness or confusion</li>
<li>Silent chest (no wheeze - air not moving)</li>
<li>Heart rate over 130</li>
</ul>

<p>While waiting: keep using reliever - up to 6 puffs every 5 minutes, with a spacer if available.</p>

<h2>Maori and Pacific health</h2>

<p>Asthma rates and hospital admission rates are substantially higher in Maori and Pacific people, partly due to housing (cold, damp, mould), tobacco exposure, and access to care. Free preventer inhalers, asthma educator visits, and home insulation programmes are available - ask your GP what is available locally.</p>
