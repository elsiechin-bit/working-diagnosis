Hyperthyroidism happens when the thyroid produces too much hormone, putting the whole body into overdrive. It is less common than hypothyroidism but can cause significant symptoms - heart racing, weight loss, anxiety, tremor. The good news: there are several effective treatments and most people return to normal.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Heart racing or palpitations</li>
<li>Tremor (especially hands)</li>
<li>Weight loss despite normal or increased appetite</li>
<li>Heat intolerance, sweating</li>
<li>Anxiety, agitation, irritability</li>
<li>Insomnia</li>
<li>Loose stools or diarrhoea</li>
<li>Tiredness despite restlessness</li>
<li>Muscle weakness, especially in thighs</li>
<li>Light or absent periods</li>
<li>Hair thinning</li>
<li>Eye changes - dry, gritty, prominent (in Graves' disease)</li>
</ul>

<p>Older adults can have atypical presentations - mainly atrial fibrillation, heart failure, or unexplained weight loss.</p>

<h2>Causes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Graves' disease</strong> - autoimmune. The most common cause. Often eye involvement and a smooth enlarged thyroid</li>
<li><strong>Toxic nodular goitre</strong> - one or more thyroid nodules producing hormone autonomously</li>
<li><strong>Thyroiditis</strong> - inflammation of the thyroid (postpartum, viral, drug-induced); usually self-limiting</li>
<li><strong>Excess thyroid hormone intake</strong> - over-replacement of levothyroxine, or factitious thyrotoxicosis</li>
<li><strong>Iodine excess</strong> - amiodarone, contrast dye, iodine supplements</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Blood tests:</p>

<ul>
<li>TSH - low or undetectable</li>
<li>Free T4 - high</li>
<li>Free T3 - high</li>
<li>TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) - positive in Graves'</li>
</ul>

<p>Imaging:</p>

<ul>
<li>Thyroid scan and uptake (radioactive iodine or technetium) to determine the cause</li>
<li>Thyroid ultrasound for nodules</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Three main options - choice depends on cause and patient preference.</p>

<p><strong>1. Antithyroid medication.</strong> Carbimazole (or propylthiouracil) blocks hormone production.</p>

<ul>
<li>Effect within 4-8 weeks</li>
<li>Standard course in Graves' is 12-18 months; about 50% of people stay in remission afterwards</li>
<li>Side effects: rash, joint aches, sometimes liver inflammation. Rarely, agranulocytosis (severe drop in white cells) - any sore throat or fever while on carbimazole needs urgent blood test</li>
<li>Carbimazole has a small risk of birth defects in pregnancy - propylthiouracil is preferred in early pregnancy</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>2. Radioactive iodine.</strong> A single oral dose destroys overactive thyroid cells.</p>

<ul>
<li>Effective in over 90%</li>
<li>Most people become hypothyroid afterwards and need lifelong levothyroxine</li>
<li>Avoid pregnancy for 6 months after; men should avoid fathering for 4 months</li>
<li>Special precautions for a few days after (limited contact with children and pregnant women)</li>
<li>Not recommended in active Graves' eye disease (can worsen)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>3. Surgery (thyroidectomy).</strong> Removal of part or all of the thyroid.</p>

<ul>
<li>Quick definitive cure</li>
<li>Lifelong levothyroxine afterwards</li>
<li>Risks: voice change, low calcium</li>
<li>Reserved for large goitre, suspected malignancy, severe eye disease, pregnancy, or patient preference</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptom relief</h2>

<p>While waiting for definitive treatment to work, beta blockers (propranolol, metoprolol) reduce:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tremor</li>
<li>Heart racing</li>
<li>Anxiety</li>
<li>Heat intolerance</li>
</ul>

<p>Usually started on diagnosis and tapered as thyroid hormone normalises.</p>

<h2>Graves' eye disease (thyroid eye disease)</h2>

<p>About 1 in 4 people with Graves' develop eye changes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bulging eyes (proptosis)</li>
<li>Lid retraction</li>
<li>Dry, gritty, watery eyes</li>
<li>Double vision</li>
<li>In severe cases, vision loss</li>
</ul>

<p>What helps:</p>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking - smoking dramatically worsens eye disease</li>
<li>Lubricant eye drops</li>
<li>Sleep with head elevated</li>
<li>Sunglasses for sensitivity</li>
<li>Selenium supplements show modest benefit</li>
<li>Severe cases need ophthalmology specialist input - oral or IV steroids, immunosuppressants, biologics (teprotumumab in some countries), surgery</li>
</ul>

<p>Treat hyperthyroidism with caution if eye disease is active - radioiodine can worsen it.</p>

<h2>Thyroid storm</h2>

<p>A rare but serious complication where hyperthyroidism becomes severe and dangerous - high fever, very fast heart rate, agitation, vomiting, sometimes coma. Triggers include surgery, illness, childbirth. Needs hospital care immediately.</p>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most people achieve normal thyroid function or controlled hypothyroidism with levothyroxine. Cardiovascular risks (atrial fibrillation, heart failure) reduce once treated. Bone density, which thins during hyperthyroidism, partially recovers.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Hyperthyroidism in pregnancy needs careful specialist management - propylthiouracil in first trimester, switch to carbimazole later. Both cross the placenta. Untreated hyperthyroidism risks miscarriage, prematurity, and fetal hyperthyroidism.</p>
