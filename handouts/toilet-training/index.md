<p>Toilet training is a major milestone. Starting when your child is truly ready, using a positive approach, and keeping it stress-free makes the process smoother for everyone. This handout explains readiness signs and practical strategies that work.</p>

<h2>Readiness signs - when to start</h2>

<p>Most children are ready for toilet training between 18 months and 3 years, but there is a wide normal range. Start toilet training when your child shows most of these signs, not before:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Stays dry for 2 hours or more during the day</li>
  <li>Shows interest in the toilet or wearing underwear</li>
  <li>Can communicate the need to go (words, signs, or behaviour)</li>
  <li>Can follow simple instructions</li>
  <li>Shows discomfort in dirty nappies</li>
  <li>Can sit still briefly and has some control over their body</li>
  <li>Is not in a period of major change (new sibling, house move, starting childcare)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Starting toilet training positively</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Let them watch and learn.</strong> Children learn by watching others use the toilet. Let your child watch a parent or sibling (if comfortable) to normalise toilet use.</li>
  <li><strong>Get child-sized equipment.</strong> A small toilet seat or potty chair makes it less intimidating. Some children prefer a potty chair, others prefer the toilet with a step stool.</li>
  <li><strong>Make it fun and casual.</strong> Read books together on the toilet, sing songs, or chat. Avoid pressure or forcing your child to sit if they are upset.</li>
  <li><strong>Celebrate successes.</strong> Praise effort and success enthusiastically, but avoid making accidents a big deal.</li>
  <li><strong>Dress for easy removal.</strong> Use clothing that your child can easily pull down (avoid complex outfits or nappies that are hard to remove).</li>
  <li><strong>Offer privacy and dignity.</strong> Once your child is comfortable, allow them privacy (closing a door or curtain) to build independence.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Establishing routines</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Regular toilet times.</strong> Offer the toilet after meals, before bed, and at regular intervals (every 2 hours). Consistency helps establish habits.</li>
  <li><strong>Daytime first, nights later.</strong> Daytime continence usually happens first (typically by 3 years). Nighttime dryness comes later (often not until age 5 - 7).</li>
  <li><strong>Keep nappies for nights and outings initially.</strong> Many children need nappies at night for years after daytime training. This is normal and not a failure.</li>
  <li><strong>Build gradually.</strong> Some children train quickly; others take months. Pace training with your child's readiness, not arbitrary timelines.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Managing accidents</h2>

<p>Accidents are normal and universal during toilet training. They happen to all children and are not your child's fault or failure. Keep your response low-key: clean up calmly, reassure your child, and move on. Shaming or punishing accidents causes anxiety and delays training.</p>

<h2>Regression</h2>

<p>Toilet-trained children often regress (start having accidents again) during stress, illness, change, or when a new sibling arrives. This is normal. Respond calmly by returning to supportive routines without pressure. Regression usually resolves quickly with patience.</p>

<h2>Resistance and refusing the toilet</h2>

<p>If your child resists toilet training or refuses to sit on the toilet, pause and try again in a few weeks or months. Forcing a resistant child causes anxiety and makes training longer overall. Your child will train eventually - there is no benefit to pushing before they are ready.</p>

<h2>Bedwetting</h2>

<p>Nighttime bedwetting is separate from daytime training. Bedwetting is normal and involuntary until at least age 5 - 7. Avoid nighttime training pressure; use nappies, pull-ups, or waterproof mattress protection. Bedwetting eventually resolves in all children without intervention.</p>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your child is over 3 years old and showing no interest in training (consider readiness, not delay)</li>
  <li>Training started well but prolonged regression occurs</li>
  <li>Your child is anxious about the toilet or has pain with toileting</li>
  <li>You feel stressed or your child is distressed - pause and restart later</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your health visitor or GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Is my child showing signs of readiness?</li>
  <li>What approach would work best for my child?</li>
  <li>Is it normal for my child to still need nappies at night?</li>
  <li>What should I do if training is not going well?</li>
</ul>
