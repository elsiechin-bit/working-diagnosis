<p>A GP appointment is often short - usually 10 - 15 minutes. Getting the most from this time means preparing well and communicating clearly. This handout helps you make the most of your appointment.</p>

<h2>Before your appointment</h2>

<h3>Write down your concerns</h3>

<p>Before your appointment, think about what you want to discuss. Write down your main concerns or symptoms. This helps you remember everything and prioritise what is most important. If you have multiple issues, list them in order of priority - discuss the most urgent ones first if time runs short. Be specific: instead of "I feel unwell", write "I have a cough for 2 weeks, worse at night, occasional fever".</p>

<h3>Gather relevant information</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Bring any recent test results, hospital letters, or documents from other doctors</li>
  <li>Make a list of current medicines (prescription and over-the-counter) with doses</li>
  <li>Note any allergies or adverse reactions to medicines</li>
  <li>Write down when symptoms started and what makes them better or worse</li>
  <li>Note if symptoms affect your daily activities (work, sleep, exercise)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Think about what you want</h3>

<p>Be clear about what you hope to get from the appointment. Do you want a diagnosis, help managing symptoms, medicine, referral to a specialist, or reassurance? Knowing this helps your doctor understand your needs.</p>

<h2>During your appointment</h2>

<h3>Describe your symptoms clearly</h3>

<p>Give your doctor a clear picture of what is bothering you. Describe when it started, how often it happens, what makes it better or worse, and how it affects you. Use plain language - your doctor will ask questions if they need more detail. Specific information is more helpful than vague descriptions.</p>

<h3>Be honest</h3>

<p>Tell your doctor everything relevant, even if it feels embarrassing or you think it is not important. This includes alcohol use, smoking, stress, sexual or relationship problems, or mental health concerns. Your doctor is not there to judge - they need accurate information to help you properly.</p>

<h3>Mention all your medicines</h3>

<p>Tell your doctor about all medicines you are taking, including vitamins, supplements, and over-the-counter drugs. Some can interact with new medicines or affect your symptoms. If you often forget to take medicines, say so - your doctor may suggest simpler regimes.</p>

<h3>Ask questions and take notes</h3>

<p>If you do not understand something, ask your doctor to explain it again. Ask what you can do to help yourself get better. If you are unsure about how to take a new medicine, when to see them again, or what to do if symptoms persist, ask. It is better to clarify during the appointment than be confused later. Write down key points or ask for written information if you think you might forget.</p>

<h3>Discuss medicines and treatments</h3>

<p>If your doctor suggests a medicine or treatment, ask about:
  What it does and how long it takes to work
  Potential side effects and what to do if they happen
  Whether there are other options
  What happens if you do not take the medicine
  Whether it is safe if you are planning pregnancy, breastfeeding, or taking other medicines</p>

<h3>Agree on a plan</h3>

<p>Before you leave, check you understand the plan: take any new medicines, do any tests, see your doctor again when, and what to do if symptoms worsen. If you cannot follow the plan (for example, cost, side effects, lifestyle factors), discuss this with your doctor now rather than ignoring the advice later.</p>

<h2>After your appointment</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Follow the agreed plan.</strong> If you have been prescribed medicine, take it as directed. If you cannot or have concerns, ring your doctor to discuss rather than simply stopping.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep booked follow-up appointments.</strong> These allow your doctor to check if treatment is working and adjust if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Let your doctor know if things change.</strong> If symptoms get worse, improve, or you develop new symptoms, contact your doctor.</li>
  <li><strong>Book your next appointment if needed.</strong> Do not wait until an issue becomes urgent - preventive care and early intervention are more effective.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do if you did not get what you needed</h2>

<p>If you felt rushed, not listened to, or did not get answers to your questions, you can:
  Request a follow-up appointment to discuss further
  Ask for a longer appointment if you have multiple complex issues
  See a different GP in the practice if you prefer
  Request a referral to a specialist if your GP cannot help with your problem
  Switch GPs if you consistently feel the service is not right for you</p>

<h2>Preparing for specific appointment types</h2>

<h3>Preventive health checks</h3>

<p>These often check blood pressure, weight, cholesterol, and discuss lifestyle. Bring any family health history (especially heart disease, diabetes, cancer). Wear clothes that allow easy blood pressure measurement.</p>

<h3>Chronic disease management</h3>

<p>Write down how well your condition is controlled, any symptoms, side effects from medicines, and questions about your treatment. Bring recent test results and a blood pressure log if you have one.</p>

<h3>Mental health concerns</h3>

<p>Be specific about mood, sleep, appetite changes, and how symptoms affect your daily life. If you have thoughts of harming yourself, tell your doctor - they need to know to help you properly.</p>

<h3>Sexual or relationship issues</h3>

<p>Your doctor is trained to discuss these sensitively. Be honest about what is concerning you - your doctor is not there to judge, and many effective treatments exist.</p>

<h2>Bringing a support person</h2>

<p>You can bring a friend, family member, or carer to your appointment if you wish. They can help you remember important information, ask questions, or provide emotional support. Tell your receptionist when booking that you are bringing someone.</p>

<h2>Accessibility needs</h2>

<p>If you have hearing loss, vision problems, mobility limitations, or other accessibility needs, let your practice know in advance. They can arrange an interpreter, ensure the room is accessible, allow extra time, or adjust the appointment in other ways to help you communicate.</p>
