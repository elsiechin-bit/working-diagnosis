<p>Screens before sleep have become a near-universal habit in Aotearoa, including for tamariki. The popular story is that "blue light" stops melatonin and that is why everyone is sleeping badly. The actual evidence is more nuanced. Three things matter, in roughly this order: <strong>what you are doing</strong> on the screen (engaging or relaxing, social or solo, doomscrolling or reading), <strong>when you are doing it</strong> relative to your sleep window, and <strong>the brightness and content</strong>. Pure blue-light effects exist but are smaller in real-world use than the headlines suggest. The other two factors are far more important - and are also things you can change.</p>

<h2>What the evidence shows</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pre-sleep media use is consistently linked to shorter and worse-quality sleep in children and adolescents</strong>, with effect sizes that get larger when the device is in the bedroom and used at the time of sleep<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Content and arousal matter more than the screen itself.</strong> A relaxing audiobook on a tablet has very different effects from an action game or a heated group chat. Arousing content keeps cortisol and heart rate up; the screen is incidental.</li>
  <li><strong>Blue light suppresses melatonin in lab studies</strong>, but real-world device brightness is much lower than the lab conditions. Modern phones in night mode emit very little blue light. Putting your phone in night mode is fine; treating that as a complete sleep solution is not.</li>
  <li><strong>Bedroom devices reduce sleep duration in adolescents</strong> by roughly 30 to 60 minutes a night in the largest studies. Removing the device from the bedroom is the highest-yield intervention available to whanau.</li>
  <li><strong>Adults are not exempt.</strong> The mechanism shifts a little - news cycling, work email, and notification-driven anxiety play a bigger role than melatonin suppression - but the result is the same: harder to fall asleep, more night waking, less restorative sleep.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Recommendations for adults</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>One hour before bed, work and news email stops.</strong> If you cannot manage an hour, even 30 minutes is enough to break the cortisol cycle.</li>
  <li><strong>No phone in bed.</strong> If it is your alarm, charge it across the room or buy a $20 alarm clock.</li>
  <li><strong>Swap doomscrolling for something boring.</strong> A book, a podcast, an audiobook on a phone face-down, knitting, a slow podcast. The aim is to lower arousal, not stimulate it.</li>
  <li><strong>Use night mode</strong> after 8 pm if you do use a screen - small effect, but free.</li>
  <li><strong>Watch caffeine and alcohol</strong> alongside the screen habit. Caffeine after midday and a wine at 10 pm both shorten deep sleep more than the phone does.</li>
</ul>

<p>For more on adult sleep specifically, see the <a href="/lifestyle-prevention/sleep/">Sleep</a> page.</p>

<h2>Recommendations for tamariki and rangatahi</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Devices out of the bedroom overnight</strong> for primary-school-age tamariki. This is the single highest-leverage intervention available.</li>
  <li><strong>For rangatahi, a charging station outside the bedroom</strong> from a fixed time - 9 pm for younger teens, 10 pm for older - is more workable than a battle every night. Whole-whanau, not just one child.</li>
  <li><strong>Predictable sleep times</strong> on weeknights matter more than absolute lights-out times. Weekend drift of more than 1 to 2 hours is what produces "Sunday-night insomnia".</li>
  <li><strong>Talk about content, not just time.</strong> Two hours of group video chat with friends has different sleep impacts from two hours of horror videos or social-media comparison. The content drives the arousal.</li>
  <li><strong>Model what you ask of them.</strong> Adults scrolling in bed while saying "no phones in your room" is the most common reason rules collapse.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How much sleep should they actually be getting?</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Tamariki 5 to 12 years:</strong> 9 to 11 hours per night</li>
  <li><strong>Rangatahi 13 to 17 years:</strong> 8 to 10 hours per night</li>
  <li><strong>Adults:</strong> 7 to 9 hours per night, most nights</li>
  <li><strong>Older adults (65+):</strong> 7 to 8 hours, often broken into one nighttime stretch and a short afternoon nap</li>
</ul>

<p>The body clock shifts later in adolescence - rangatahi who can fall asleep at 11 pm and want to wake at 8 am are not being lazy, they are being teenagers. School start times work against this biology, which is why the after-school crash and the weekend lie-in happen.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sleep problems lasting more than 4 weeks despite better wind-down habits</li>
  <li>Loud snoring, witnessed pauses in breathing, or daytime sleepiness severe enough to affect driving - possible sleep apnoea, treatable</li>
  <li>Tamariki snoring with mouth-breathing or behavioural change - possible adenoid or tonsil issue</li>
  <li>Nightmares, sleepwalking, or violent movements during sleep</li>
  <li>Insomnia paired with low mood, racing thoughts, or anxiety</li>
  <li>Concerns about disordered or compulsive screen use - in adults or rangatahi</li>
</ul>
