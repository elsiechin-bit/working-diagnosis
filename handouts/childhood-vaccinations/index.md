<p>Vaccination is one of the clearest wins of modern medicine. Diseases that killed or disabled large numbers of NZ children just a generation ago are now rare. The NZ schedule is free for all children regardless of immigration or residency status. This handout walks through what is on the schedule and why, what to expect after a vaccine, the situations that need a doctor, and where to find good information if you are unsure.</p>

<h2>The schedule</h2>

<p>What is offered free in NZ for children, as of 2024-2025. Your Plunket nurse or GP practice will track these and remind you - keep your child's Well Child book or app updated.</p>

<h3>6 weeks</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Rotavirus (oral)</li>
  <li>DTaP-IPV-HepB-Hib (the "6-in-1") - diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough, polio, hepatitis B, Haemophilus influenzae type b</li>
  <li>Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PCV)</li>
</ul>

<h3>3 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Rotavirus (oral, second dose)</li>
  <li>6-in-1 (second dose)</li>
  <li>PCV (second dose)</li>
</ul>

<h3>5 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>6-in-1 (third dose)</li>
</ul>

<h3>12 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) - first dose</li>
  <li>PCV (third dose)</li>
  <li>Hib booster (Haemophilus influenzae type b)</li>
</ul>

<h3>15 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>MMR (second dose)</li>
  <li>Varicella (chickenpox)</li>
  <li>DTaP-IPV booster (4-in-1)</li>
</ul>

<h3>4 years</h3>
<ul>
  <li>DTaP-IPV booster</li>
  <li>MMR catch-up if not had 2 doses by then</li>
</ul>

<h3>11 years (Year 7)</h3>
<ul>
  <li>HPV (human papillomavirus) - 2 doses for under-15s</li>
  <li>Tdap booster (whooping cough)</li>
</ul>

<h3>45 and 65 years</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Tdap (free at age 45 and 65)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Annually</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Influenza - free for children under 13 since 2024, and for high-risk children of any age (asthma, chronic conditions)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Special circumstances</h3>
<ul>
  <li>BCG (tuberculosis) - free for newborns at higher risk, including those with family contacts who lived in countries with high TB rates</li>
  <li>Hepatitis A and others - some travel and condition-specific</li>
</ul>

<h2>What each vaccine prevents</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rotavirus</strong> - the commonest cause of severe gastroenteritis in young children before vaccination. Hospitalisation rates fell by around 60% in NZ after the 2014 introduction.[1]</li>
  <li><strong>Diphtheria, tetanus, whooping cough</strong> - whooping cough especially dangerous for young infants; epidemics every 3-5 years in NZ</li>
  <li><strong>Polio</strong> - paralytic, eliminated from NZ but still present in some countries</li>
  <li><strong>Hepatitis B</strong> - chronic infection causes liver disease and cancer</li>
  <li><strong>Hib</strong> - meningitis and severe pneumonia in young children, now rare</li>
  <li><strong>Pneumococcal</strong> - meningitis, pneumonia, severe ear infections</li>
  <li><strong>Measles</strong> - one of the most contagious diseases known. Real risk of pneumonia, encephalitis, death. NZ has had outbreaks regularly when vaccination rates drop.</li>
  <li><strong>Mumps</strong> - meningitis, deafness, infertility</li>
  <li><strong>Rubella</strong> - mild in children but devastating in pregnancy</li>
  <li><strong>Varicella (chickenpox)</strong> - usually mild but can cause serious complications</li>
  <li><strong>HPV</strong> - prevents cervical, anal, throat, and other cancers</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to expect after vaccinations</h2>

<h3>Common, normal, expected</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Sore arm or leg at injection site for 1-2 days</li>
  <li>Mild fever within 24-48 hours</li>
  <li>Mild rash at the injection site</li>
  <li>A bit grumpy, off food, sleepy</li>
  <li>For MMR: a faint rash and mild fever sometimes 7-10 days later as the live vaccine takes effect - normal, not contagious</li>
</ul>

<p>Treat with comfort, fluids, paracetamol if needed (do not pre-medicate before vaccination - reduces antibody response). Babies often nap longer than usual on vaccination day. Cuddles, breastfeeding, calm time.</p>

<h3>Rare but worth knowing</h3>
<ul>
  <li>High-pitched persistent crying for over 3 hours in young infants</li>
  <li>Hypotonic-hyporesponsive episode (very pale, floppy, unresponsive briefly) - rare, usually resolves, report to GP</li>
  <li>Allergic reaction - very rare; clinics keep adrenaline on hand</li>
  <li>Febrile convulsion in genetically predisposed children</li>
</ul>

<h3>What is not caused by vaccines</h3>

<p>Several claims have been thoroughly investigated and disproved. They are worth knowing because you will encounter them online:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Autism.</strong> The 1998 Wakefield study claiming a link to MMR was retracted, found fraudulent, and the author lost his medical licence. Multiple very large studies since have shown no link. Children who develop autism would have done so without vaccination.</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden infant death.</strong> SUDI rates have fallen as vaccination rates have risen. Vaccination does not cause SUDI.</li>
  <li><strong>Immune system overload.</strong> Children encounter thousands of antigens every day from food, dust, and environment. Vaccines add a tiny fraction.</li>
  <li><strong>Long-term harm from "too many vaccines too young".</strong> The schedule is built around when vulnerability is highest and protection most needed.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common questions</h2>

<h3>Can my child still be vaccinated if they are unwell?</h3>
<p>Mild illness with no fever - yes, vaccinations can usually proceed. Significant fever or moderately unwell - usually best to defer a week or two. Your nurse will check at the appointment.</p>

<h3>What if we missed a vaccination?</h3>
<p>It is never too late to catch up. The NZ schedule has catch-up provisions; your GP or Plunket nurse can map a plan. Catch-up vaccinations are free.</p>

<h3>What about side effects I have read about?</h3>
<p>Worth a conversation with someone you trust. Bring specific concerns to your GP or Plunket nurse and ask. The NZ Immunisation Advisory Centre (IMAC) handbook is the most rigorous local source.</p>

<h3>Are there extra vaccines I might consider paying for?</h3>
<p>Some that are not currently funded but are reasonable: meningococcal B (Bexsero) - particularly important if there has been a meningococcal cluster in your area; meningococcal ACWY (Nimenrix); rotavirus (already free); BCG (TB) for unspecified-risk children.</p>

<h2>Where to get vaccinated</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your GP - all routine schedule vaccinations</li>
  <li>Plunket nurses - some, varies by region</li>
  <li>Hauora services for Maori and Pacific - free, often outreach</li>
  <li>Some pharmacies - influenza, some travel vaccines</li>
  <li>School-based programmes for HPV, Tdap, Year 7 boosters</li>
</ul>
