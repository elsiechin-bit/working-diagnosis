<p>Anxiety is the most common mental health condition in New Zealand. It is a normal human response to stress or perceived danger - the problem arises when anxiety is persistent, out of proportion, or gets in the way of daily life. Understanding what is happening in your body and mind is the first step toward managing it.</p>

<h2>What anxiety feels like</h2>

<p>Anxiety affects people physically and mentally. Common experiences include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Constant or excessive worry that is hard to control</li>
  <li>Restlessness, feeling on edge or wound up</li>
  <li>Fatigue - anxiety is exhausting</li>
  <li>Difficulty concentrating; your mind going blank</li>
  <li>Irritability</li>
  <li>Muscle tension, especially in the shoulders, neck, or jaw</li>
  <li>Sleep problems - difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep</li>
  <li>Physical symptoms: racing heart, shortness of breath, chest tightness, stomach upset, dizziness, tingling</li>
</ul>

<p>It is very common for anxiety to cause physical symptoms that feel real and worrying - but are caused by the anxiety itself. This can create a cycle where physical symptoms increase worry, which increases symptoms.</p>

<h2>Why this happens</h2>

<p>Your brain has a threat-detection system (sometimes called the "fight or flight" response) designed to protect you from danger. In anxiety, this system becomes overactive or triggered by situations that are not genuinely dangerous. Your body responds as if you are facing a physical threat - heart rate goes up, breathing quickens, muscles tense. This is not weakness: it is your nervous system trying to protect you.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Talking therapy.</strong> Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is the most effective treatment for anxiety. It helps you understand how your thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are connected, and teaches you practical skills to change unhelpful patterns. Your GP can refer you to a psychologist or counsellor. Subsidised and free therapy options exist in NZ - ask about Primary Mental Health funding in your area.</li>
  <li><strong>Controlled breathing.</strong> Slow, deliberate breathing can directly calm the nervous system. Try breathing in for 4 counts, holding for 1-2 counts, and breathing out slowly for 6-7 counts. This works best when practised regularly, not just in moments of anxiety.</li>
  <li><strong>Physical activity.</strong> Exercise significantly reduces anxiety. Regular aerobic exercise (brisk walking, running, swimming, cycling) 3-5 times a week is as effective as medication for many people with mild-to-moderate anxiety.</li>
  <li><strong>Reducing avoidance.</strong> Anxiety often leads to avoiding situations that feel scary. While avoidance reduces anxiety in the short term, it makes anxiety worse over time. Gradually facing feared situations (with support from a therapist if needed) is a core part of overcoming anxiety.</li>
  <li><strong>Reducing caffeine.</strong> Caffeine directly stimulates the nervous system and can significantly worsen anxiety symptoms, particularly heart pounding and restlessness.</li>
  <li><strong>Reducing alcohol.</strong> Alcohol may briefly reduce anxiety but causes a rebound increase in anxiety as it wears off, and worsens anxiety over time.</li>
  <li><strong>Mindfulness.</strong> Regular mindfulness practice helps reduce the tendency to catastrophise and builds tolerance for uncertainty. Apps like Headspace or Smiling Mind are good starting points.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Medicines can help for moderate-to-severe anxiety, particularly when used alongside therapy. SSRIs (such as sertraline or escitalopram) are the most commonly prescribed and are safe for long-term use. They take several weeks to take effect. Benzodiazepines (such as diazepam or lorazepam) may be used short-term for acute episodes but are not recommended long-term due to risk of dependence.</p>

<h2>When to get help</h2>

<p>It is worth seeing your GP if anxiety is:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Affecting your work, relationships, or daily activities</li>
  <li>Causing you to avoid things you used to be able to do</li>
  <li>Not improving despite self-help strategies</li>
  <li>Accompanied by depression, panic attacks, or thoughts of self-harm</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are in crisis or having thoughts of suicide, call <strong>1737</strong> (free, 24/7) or <strong>Lifeline 0800 543 354</strong>, or go to your nearest emergency department.</p>
