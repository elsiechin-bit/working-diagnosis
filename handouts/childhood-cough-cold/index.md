<p>Children get an average of 6-8 viral upper respiratory infections (colds) a year, more if they are at daycare or have older siblings. A child who has had three colds back-to-back through winter is not unusual or unhealthy - they are just busy doing what their immune system is meant to do. This handout covers what to expect, what helps, what does not, and the relatively rare situations that need a doctor's eye.</p>

<h2>What a cold actually does</h2>

<p>A typical viral cold lasts 7-10 days. The pattern in most children:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Day 1-2:</strong> sore throat, mild fever, off food, miserable</li>
  <li><strong>Day 3-5:</strong> runny nose (clear, then yellow-green - colour does not mean bacterial), cough, fever settling</li>
  <li><strong>Day 6-10:</strong> cough lingering, energy returning, appetite back</li>
</ul>

<p>The cough is often the last symptom to go. A post-viral cough lasting 3-4 weeks is normal in children. It is not a sign that antibiotics are needed.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Fluids</h3>
<p>Often offered, often refused. Keep offering. Breast milk, formula, water, soup, jelly, ice blocks if old enough. Small frequent sips work better than large drinks. Hydration thins mucus and makes the cough more productive.</p>

<h3>Saline nose drops or spray</h3>
<p>For young babies who cannot blow their nose, saline drops and a gentle bulb syringe before feeds and sleep make a real difference. Available without prescription. Use as often as needed.</p>

<h3>Humid air</h3>
<p>Steamy bathroom (close door, run hot shower) for 10 minutes before bed. A cool-mist humidifier in the bedroom. Avoid hot-mist humidifiers near children - burn risk.</p>

<h3>Honey</h3>
<p>For children over 12 months: a teaspoon of honey at bedtime reduces overnight cough as effectively as over-the-counter cough syrups, with fewer side effects.[1] Do not give honey to babies under 12 months - botulism risk.</p>

<h3>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</h3>
<p>For discomfort, fever, sore throat, headache. Use the same doses as for fever (see fever handout). Do not exceed the labelled doses.</p>

<h3>Rest, cuddles, time off if needed</h3>
<p>Children recover faster when they are not pushed back into childcare or school too soon. The general rule: 24 hours fever-free, eating and drinking normally, before returning.</p>

<h2>What does not help</h2>

<h3>Cough syrups for children under 6</h3>
<p>Do not use over-the-counter cough and cold medicines in children under 6. The evidence does not show meaningful benefit, and there are real risks - including drowsiness, paradoxical excitation, and overdose. NZ Medsafe and Healthify both advise against them. Honey is more effective and safer.</p>

<h3>Antibiotics</h3>
<p>Colds are caused by viruses. Antibiotics do nothing to viruses. They will not shorten a cold, will not prevent complications in a routine cold, and will not turn green snot back to clear faster. They will, however, give your child stomach upset and help bacteria become resistant. NZ has reasonably high but rising antibiotic resistance rates - your child will need antibiotics that work some day. Save them for when they are needed.</p>

<h3>Suppressing a cough is rarely useful</h3>
<p>The cough is doing the work of clearing the airway. Codeine-containing cough syrups should not be used in children under 12 - serious risk of respiratory depression.</p>

<h2>When the picture changes - see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Fever lasting more than 3 days, or returning after a clear period</li>
  <li>Increasing rather than improving on day 5-7</li>
  <li>Working hard to breathe (chest sucking in between ribs, grunting, fast breathing)</li>
  <li>Wheeze or stridor (high-pitched noise on breathing in)</li>
  <li>Severe earache, especially with discharge</li>
  <li>Severe sore throat with difficulty swallowing or drooling</li>
  <li>Cough that has not started to improve at all by 3 weeks</li>
  <li>Coughing fits ending in a "whoop" or vomit (consider whooping cough, especially if vaccinations are not up to date)</li>
  <li>Any of the urgent warning signs from the fever handout</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specific things to watch for</h2>

<h3>Bronchiolitis</h3>
<p>Cold-like symptoms followed by wheeze, cough, and laboured breathing in a baby (especially under 12 months). See the bronchiolitis handout. This is the commonest reason for a baby to be hospitalised in NZ winter.</p>

<h3>Otitis media (middle ear infection)</h3>
<p>Pulling at ears, fever, miserable, sometimes ear discharge. Common after a cold. Most cases settle with pain relief alone. See your GP, especially if symptoms persist or recur.</p>

<h3>Streptococcal sore throat</h3>
<p>Most sore throats are viral. Streptococcal sore throat (matters because of rheumatic fever risk in NZ) is more likely in:</p>

<ul>
  <li>School-aged children</li>
  <li>Maori or Pacific children, or children of any ethnicity living in lower-decile areas</li>
  <li>Sore throat without runny nose or cough</li>
  <li>White spots on tonsils, swollen neck glands, fever</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ has free throat swabbing services in many areas (Mana Kidz, school-based programmes). Ask your GP, school nurse, or local Maori health provider.</p>

<h3>Asthma flare</h3>
<p>Children with asthma often have asthma exacerbations triggered by viral illnesses. If your child has asthma and a cold, follow your written asthma action plan. If they do not have one, ask your GP at the next non-acute appointment.</p>

<h2>How to reduce illnesses (a little)</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Handwashing</strong> - the single most evidence-based intervention. Teach it like brushing teeth.</li>
  <li><strong>Vaccinations</strong> - influenza vaccine annually for children with chronic conditions and asthma; routine NZ schedule prevents whooping cough, measles, and several other infections that can present as severe respiratory illness</li>
  <li><strong>Smoke-free home</strong> - second-hand smoke increases respiratory illness rates significantly</li>
  <li><strong>Breastfeeding</strong> reduces severity and duration of viral illnesses in the first year</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep, balanced diet, time outdoors</strong> all play a role</li>
</ul>

<p>Vitamin C, zinc, echinacea, and most "immune boosters" have very limited evidence in children. Probiotics may slightly reduce the duration of illnesses but the effect is small.</p>
