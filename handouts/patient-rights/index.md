<p>As a patient in Aotearoa New Zealand, you have legal rights that protect you and give you control over your healthcare. Understanding these rights helps you advocate for yourself, ask questions, and make informed decisions about your care. This handout outlines what you are entitled to.</p>

<h2>The right to be treated with respect and dignity</h2>

<p>You have the right to be treated with respect and manaakitanga (care and hospitality). This means healthcare providers must listen to you, respect your cultural beliefs and values, and communicate in a way you understand. You can ask for an interpreter at any appointment if English is not your first language, and this service is free. You also have the right to involve a support person (whanau, friend, carer) in your appointments if you wish.</p>

<h2>The right to information</h2>

<p>You have the right to clear, honest information about your health condition, treatment options, and likely outcomes. Your doctor should explain things in plain language, give you time to ask questions, and answer them truthfully. If you do not understand something, ask them to explain it again. You also have the right to ask for written information about your condition and treatment.</p>

<h2>The right to make decisions about your care</h2>

<p>You have the right to choose or refuse treatment, even if your doctor recommends it. This is called informed consent. Before any treatment, your doctor must explain what it involves, the benefits, risks, and alternatives. You can take time to decide. If you lack decision-making capacity (for example, due to unconsciousness or severe dementia), decisions are made according to the Health and Disability Commissioner Code of Consumer Rights and relevant laws.</p>

<h2>The right to privacy and confidentiality</h2>

<p>Your health information is confidential. Your doctor cannot share it with others without your permission, except in very limited circumstances (such as safety risks or legal obligations). You can ask to see your medical records. You can also ask your doctor to limit who has access to your information.</p>

<h2>The right to access your medical records</h2>

<p>You can request a copy of your medical records from your GP or hospital. They must provide them within 20 working days, usually for a small fee (around $10 - 25). You can also ask for corrections if information is wrong or outdated.</p>

<h2>The right to complain</h2>

<p>If you feel your care was not good enough, you have the right to complain. You can complain to your doctor or health service first. If you are not satisfied, you can complain to the Health and Disability Commissioner (HDC), which is free and independent. The HDC investigates complaints and can order remedies or compensation.</p>

<h2>The right to continuity of care</h2>

<p>You have the right to see the same healthcare provider if possible, or at least have good communication between providers if you see different doctors. Your information should be shared safely between services so you do not have to repeat your story multiple times.</p>

<h2>The right to cultural safety</h2>

<p>You have the right to healthcare that respects your culture, language, and beliefs. This is especially important for Maori and Pasifika patients, who have the right to ask for culturally appropriate care and services. You can request a Maori or Pasifika health worker if you wish.</p>

<h2>The right to free or affordable care</h2>

<p>In New Zealand, many healthcare services are free or subsidised through the public health system. You have the right to know the cost of private services upfront. If cost is a barrier to your care, discuss this with your doctor or practice - there may be community services or support available.</p>

<h2>How to advocate for yourself</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ask questions.</strong> If you do not understand something, ask your doctor to explain again.</li>
  <li><strong>Take notes.</strong> Write down key points from your appointments so you remember them later.</li>
  <li><strong>Bring a support person.</strong> Having a friend or family member present can help you remember information and feel supported.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask about options.</strong> There may be different ways to manage your condition - ask what alternatives exist.</li>
  <li><strong>Request time to decide.</strong> You do not have to decide on treatment immediately. Ask to take information home and think about it.</li>
  <li><strong>Be honest about concerns.</strong> If you are worried about side effects or cannot follow a treatment plan, tell your doctor. Together you can find a better approach.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to complain</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You felt disrespected or your cultural beliefs were ignored</li>
  <li>You were not given proper information to make a decision</li>
  <li>You were not listened to or your concerns were dismissed</li>
  <li>You believe your care fell below an acceptable standard</li>
  <li>You were treated in a way that harmed you physically or emotionally</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What are all my treatment options?</li>
  <li>What are the benefits and risks of each option?</li>
  <li>What happens if I choose not to have this treatment?</li>
  <li>Can I have more time to think about this?</li>
  <li>Can I have written information about my condition?</li>
  <li>Can I see my medical records?</li>
</ul>
