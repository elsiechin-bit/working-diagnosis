Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the deeper skin and the tissue just below it. It usually follows a small break in the skin (a cut, insect bite, athlete's foot, eczema crack), although sometimes no entry point is obvious. Treated promptly with antibiotics, most cellulitis settles within 5-10 days. Treated late, it can become serious. Knowing the signs and acting on them matters.

<h2>What cellulitis looks like</h2>

<ul>
<li>An area of skin that is red, hot, swollen, and tender</li>
<li>The redness has a poorly defined edge that gradually fades into normal skin</li>
<li>Skin may look shiny or stretched</li>
<li>The area is usually painful, sometimes throbbing</li>
<li>Often on a leg, face, or arm</li>
<li>Sometimes with a small entry wound, blister, or weeping patch</li>
<li>Lymph glands upstream (e.g. groin if leg, armpit if arm) may be tender and swollen</li>
<li>Often with feeling unwell, fever, or chills</li>
</ul>

<p>Erysipelas is a related infection that affects the more superficial skin - it has a sharper, raised red border. Treatment is similar.</p>

<h2>What to do</h2>

<p>See your GP <strong>same-day</strong> for any spreading red painful area, especially with fever or feeling unwell.</p>

<p>While waiting for your appointment:</p>

<ul>
<li>Mark the edge of the redness with a pen so you and your GP can see if it is spreading</li>
<li>Elevate the affected limb above heart level when possible</li>
<li>Take paracetamol for pain</li>
<li>Drink plenty of fluids</li>
<li>Rest</li>
</ul>

<p>Avoid ibuprofen and other NSAIDs - some studies suggest they can mask early signs of more serious deep infection.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Most cellulitis is treated with oral antibiotics. In NZ the usual first-line is flucloxacillin 500 mg four times a day for 7 days. If you are penicillin-allergic, alternatives include erythromycin or cefalexin.</p>

<p>Hospital admission and intravenous antibiotics are needed if:</p>

<ul>
<li>You are very unwell, with high fever or sepsis signs</li>
<li>The infection is spreading rapidly despite oral antibiotics</li>
<li>It is on the face or in someone immunosuppressed</li>
<li>There are concerns about a deeper infection (necrotising fasciitis)</li>
<li>Oral antibiotics have failed</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to expect with treatment</h2>

<ul>
<li>Pain and warmth start to settle within 24-48 hours</li>
<li>Redness can take 5-10 days to clear, sometimes longer</li>
<li>Skin may peel as it heals</li>
<li>Some swelling can persist for weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>It is normal for the redness to look briefly worse in the first 24 hours after starting antibiotics - bacteria are dying and triggering more inflammation. As long as the redness is not actively spreading and you are starting to feel better, this is fine.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - act urgently</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> or go to the emergency department for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe and rapidly worsening pain, especially out of proportion to the visible redness</li>
<li>Skin turning dark, purple, black, or developing blisters or fluid-filled bullae</li>
<li>Crackling under the skin (gas)</li>
<li>High fever, shaking chills, confusion, fast heart rate, very low blood pressure</li>
<li>Vomiting or unable to keep antibiotics down</li>
<li>Spreading redness despite 48 hours of antibiotics</li>
</ul>

<p>These features suggest necrotising fasciitis or sepsis - serious conditions that need immediate hospital care.</p>

<h2>Why does cellulitis come back?</h2>

<p>About 1 in 4 people who have had cellulitis develop another within a few years. Common reasons:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Athlete's foot (tinea pedis)</strong> with cracks between the toes - the single most common entry point. See our <a href="/handouts/tinea/">tinea handout</a></li>
<li><strong>Chronic leg swelling</strong> from heart failure, varicose veins, or after a previous DVT</li>
<li><strong>Lymphoedema</strong> - chronic swelling of a limb after lymph node surgery, radiotherapy, or recurrent infection</li>
<li><strong>Eczema, psoriasis, or other chronic skin conditions</strong> with cracks</li>
<li><strong>Chronic ulcers</strong> on the lower leg</li>
<li><strong>Diabetes</strong> with foot ulcers or fissured skin</li>
<li><strong>Obesity</strong> - skin folds and reduced circulation</li>
<li><strong>Pet bites and scratches</strong> - dog and cat bites have high infection rates</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing recurrence</h2>

<ul>
<li>Treat athlete's foot promptly and keep it controlled</li>
<li>Moisturise dry skin daily</li>
<li>Treat eczema flares before they crack</li>
<li>Wear well-fitting shoes and avoid going barefoot in public showers</li>
<li>Use insect repellent in summer</li>
<li>Look after diabetic feet - daily inspection, podiatrist review</li>
<li>Wear compression stockings if your GP recommends them for chronic swelling</li>
<li>For lymphoedema, refer to a specialist lymphoedema therapist</li>
<li>Lose weight if overweight - reduces leg swelling and recurrence rates</li>
</ul>

<p>For people with two or more episodes per year despite these measures, your GP may consider <strong>preventive antibiotics</strong> (low-dose penicillin V or erythromycin daily) for 6-12 months. This roughly halves recurrence in trials.&sup1;</p>

<h2>Special situations</h2>

<p><strong>Facial cellulitis</strong> needs same-day GP review and is sometimes admitted, particularly if around the eye (orbital cellulitis is sight-threatening) or on the upper lip.</p>

<p><strong>Periorbital and orbital cellulitis</strong> - any swelling around an eye with pain on eye movement, double vision, or reduced vision is an emergency.</p>

<p><strong>Cellulitis after dental procedures, body piercing, or surgery</strong> - tell your GP about the procedure; sometimes different antibiotics are needed.</p>

<p><strong>Cellulitis in immunosuppressed people</strong> (chemotherapy, HIV, biologics, transplant) - often needs intravenous antibiotics and broader cover.</p>

<h2>What is not cellulitis</h2>

<p>Several things mimic cellulitis and are commonly confused with it:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Stasis dermatitis</strong> - chronic redness on lower legs from venous disease, often itchy rather than painful, usually both legs</li>
<li><strong>Contact dermatitis</strong> - itchy rather than tender, with a clearer edge</li>
<li><strong>Deep vein thrombosis</strong> - swelling and pain, usually less redness, calf often tight</li>
<li><strong>Insect bite reaction</strong> - itchy, less painful</li>
<li><strong>Acute gout</strong> - very tender hot red joint, often big toe</li>
</ul>

<p>If your "cellulitis" affects both legs and is not getting better with antibiotics, the diagnosis may be wrong - see your GP.</p>
