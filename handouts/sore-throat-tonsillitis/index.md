A sore throat is one of the most common reasons for seeing a GP. The big question is usually: do I need antibiotics? In New Zealand the answer depends on age, ethnicity, and where you live, because of our high rate of acute rheumatic fever in some communities.

<h2>Most sore throats are viral</h2>

<p>Around 80-90% of adult sore throats and 70-80% of childhood sore throats are caused by viruses. These get better on their own in 5-7 days. Antibiotics do nothing useful for them.</p>

<p>Viral throat clues:</p>

<ul>
<li>Runny nose</li>
<li>Cough</li>
<li>Hoarseness or laryngitis</li>
<li>Mouth ulcers</li>
<li>Conjunctivitis</li>
</ul>

<h2>Strep throat (group A streptococcus)</h2>

<p>About 10-30% of sore throats in children and 5-15% in adults are caused by Streptococcus pyogenes ("strep"). Classic features:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden onset</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Tender, swollen lymph nodes in the neck</li>
<li>Red tonsils, sometimes with white pus spots</li>
<li>No cough</li>
</ul>

<p>Strep usually settles within a week even without antibiotics. The reason we treat it is to prevent complications.</p>

<h2>The rheumatic fever risk</h2>

<p>NZ has the highest rate of acute rheumatic fever in the developed world, especially in Maori and Pacific children aged 4-19. Untreated strep throat can trigger rheumatic fever 2-3 weeks later, leading to permanent heart valve damage.</p>

<p>The Ministry of Health recommends antibiotics for sore throat in:</p>

<ul>
<li>All Maori and Pacific children and young people aged 3-35 in higher-risk areas</li>
<li>Anyone aged 3-35 with a personal or family history of rheumatic fever</li>
<li>Anyone with severe symptoms, very enlarged tonsils with pus, or suspected scarlet fever</li>
</ul>

<p>For others, watch and wait is reasonable - or a throat swab to confirm strep before treating.</p>

<h2>Treatment for confirmed strep</h2>

<p>Penicillin V (amoxicillin in young children) for 10 days. The full course matters - stopping early loses the rheumatic fever protection. Symptoms usually settle in 24-48 hours but the antibiotics keep working in the background.</p>

<p>If penicillin allergic: erythromycin or roxithromycin.</p>

<h2>Self-care for any sore throat</h2>

<ul>
<li>Paracetamol or ibuprofen regularly for pain and fever</li>
<li>Plenty of fluids - cool, warm, soup, ice blocks all fine</li>
<li>Salt-water gargles (half a teaspoon of salt in a cup of warm water)</li>
<li>Throat lozenges (over age 4)</li>
<li>Honey for cough (over age 1)</li>
<li>Rest</li>
<li>Avoid smoking and second-hand smoke - both prolong the irritation</li>
</ul>

<h2>Tonsillitis</h2>

<p>Tonsillitis is just a more localised, severe sore throat. Often viral. Bacterial tonsillitis is treated as strep above. Big tonsils alone do not need surgery - tonsillectomy is reserved for very frequent severe episodes (typically 7+ episodes a year for 1 year, or 5+ per year for 2 years), sleep apnoea from huge tonsils, or quinsy (peritonsillar abscess).</p>

<h2>When to seek urgent care</h2>

<p>See a doctor same-day for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Difficulty swallowing your own saliva</li>
<li>Difficulty breathing or noisy breathing</li>
<li>Severe pain, especially one-sided, with fever and inability to open the mouth wide (suggests quinsy)</li>
<li>Drooling in a child</li>
<li>Severe headache, neck stiffness, or rash with fever</li>
<li>Very swollen face or neck</li>
<li>Sore throat lasting more than a week despite treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for true breathing difficulty.</p>

<h2>Free throat swabs in NZ</h2>

<p>Free sore throat clinics and school-based swabbing programmes operate in many higher-risk areas. Pharmacies in some regions also offer free throat checks for eligible groups. Ask your local pharmacy.</p>
