Urticaria, or hives, are itchy raised wheals that appear suddenly on the skin and usually disappear within hours. They are common - around 1 in 5 people will have at least one episode in their lifetime. Most hives clear quickly. Some keep coming back for months. Almost always they are uncomfortable rather than dangerous, but a few clues should prompt urgent care.

<h2>What hives look like</h2>

<ul>
<li>Raised, often pink or pale, sometimes red</li>
<li>Round, oval, or map-like shapes</li>
<li>Each individual hive lasts under 24 hours and fades without leaving a mark</li>
<li>Itchy or stinging</li>
<li>Often appear on different parts of the body in waves</li>
</ul>

<p>Sometimes the deeper version, <strong>angioedema</strong>, causes swelling around eyes, lips, hands, or genitals. This usually settles over a day or two and is not dangerous unless it affects the throat - see urgent help below.</p>

<h2>Acute (short) vs chronic (long) urticaria</h2>

<p><strong>Acute urticaria</strong> lasts under 6 weeks. Often triggered by a viral infection (especially in children), a medicine, a food, or sometimes nothing identifiable. Usually self-limiting.</p>

<p><strong>Chronic urticaria</strong> lasts 6 weeks or more, with hives appearing on most days. Around half of cases are autoimmune (the body's own antibodies activate the histamine-releasing cells). It is not a sign of underlying serious disease in most people, though your GP may screen for thyroid problems and other clues.</p>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<p>Things that can cause or aggravate hives:</p>

<ul>
<li>Viral infections (the most common cause of acute hives, especially in children)</li>
<li>Medicines: penicillin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac, aspirin), opioids, contrast dyes</li>
<li>Foods: shellfish, peanuts, tree nuts, eggs, milk, fish, sesame, kiwifruit</li>
<li>Insect stings</li>
<li>Heat, cold, pressure, vibration, sweating, stress (physical urticarias)</li>
<li>Latex</li>
<li>Some illnesses (autoimmune disease, rarely lymphoma)</li>
</ul>

<p>For most people with chronic urticaria, no specific trigger is ever identified - and that is OK. The treatment is the same.</p>

<h2>What to do at home</h2>

<p>For mild hives:</p>

<ul>
<li>Take a non-sedating antihistamine - cetirizine 10 mg, loratadine 10 mg, or fexofenadine 180 mg. Available over the counter</li>
<li>Cool compresses or a tepid shower</li>
<li>Avoid scratching - it spreads the rash and can break the skin</li>
<li>Avoid alcohol, hot showers, and tight clothing while active - they worsen itch</li>
<li>Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac) during a flare; paracetamol is fine</li>
</ul>

<p>For chronic hives, regular daily antihistamine - sometimes at higher than label dose - is often needed. Your GP can guide you up to 4 times the standard dose if standard doses are not enough; this is well established and safe with modern antihistamines.</p>

<h2>When to seek urgent help</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> or go to the emergency department for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Difficulty breathing or wheezing</li>
<li>Tightness in the throat or trouble swallowing</li>
<li>Tongue or throat swelling</li>
<li>Faintness, dizziness, or rapid pulse</li>
<li>Hives spreading rapidly across the whole body in someone who has just eaten a known allergen, taken a new medicine, or been stung</li>
</ul>

<p>This is anaphylaxis until proven otherwise and needs adrenaline. If the person has an EpiPen, use it now and still call 111.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP soon</h2>

<ul>
<li>Hives lasting more than a few days, or coming back repeatedly</li>
<li>Hives associated with joint pain, fever, or weight loss</li>
<li>Individual hives lasting longer than 24 hours, or leaving bruises (suggests urticarial vasculitis - different management)</li>
<li>Suspected food, medicine, or insect-sting trigger you want assessed</li>
</ul>

<h2>Investigations</h2>

<p>Most acute urticaria needs no tests. For chronic hives, your GP may check:</p>

<ul>
<li>Full blood count and CRP</li>
<li>Thyroid function and antibodies</li>
<li>Total IgE</li>
<li>Other tests only if pointed by history (e.g. coeliac, autoimmune)</li>
</ul>

<p>Allergy testing is rarely useful for chronic urticaria - the cause is usually internal.</p>

<h2>Treatment beyond antihistamines</h2>

<p>For chronic urticaria not controlled by high-dose antihistamines:</p>

<ul>
<li>H2 antihistamines (ranitidine, famotidine) - sometimes added</li>
<li>Montelukast - an asthma tablet that can help</li>
<li>Short courses of oral steroid for severe flares (not for long-term use)</li>
<li>Omalizumab (Xolair) - a monthly injection, very effective for refractory chronic urticaria. Specialist-supervised. PHARMAC-funded for chronic urticaria not responding to high-dose antihistamines</li>
<li>Ciclosporin - in specialist hands</li>
</ul>

<h2>The good news</h2>

<p>Acute urticaria almost always settles. Chronic urticaria, while frustrating, eventually resolves in most people - often within 1-5 years.&sup1;</p>
