<p>Being told you or someone you love has dementia is a hard day. The diagnosis usually comes after months of half-noticed changes, and it raises more questions than any single appointment can answer. This handout is a starting point: what dementia actually is, what it is not, what to expect from the system in NZ, and the things worth doing in the first months after diagnosis.</p>

<h2>What dementia is</h2>

<p>Dementia is an umbrella term for diseases of the brain that cause progressive problems with memory, thinking, behaviour, and the ability to do daily tasks. It is not a single disease, and it is not a normal part of getting older. Roughly 70,000 New Zealanders are currently living with dementia; that number is projected to roughly double by 2050.[1]</p>

<p>Two things distinguish dementia from normal age-related forgetfulness:</p>

<ul>
  <li>It is <strong>progressive</strong> - it gets gradually worse, not better and worse and better.</li>
  <li>It interferes with <strong>everyday function</strong> - paying bills, following a recipe, getting dressed, managing medicines.</li>
</ul>

<p>Forgetting where you put your keys is normal. Forgetting what keys are for is not.</p>

<h2>The common types</h2>

<h3>Alzheimer's disease</h3>
<p>The most common form, around 60-70% of cases. Memory of recent events tends to go first; older memories last longer. Word-finding difficulties, getting lost in familiar places, and difficulty managing money are typical. Slow gradual decline over years.</p>

<h3>Vascular dementia</h3>
<p>Caused by small or large strokes affecting brain tissue. Can be sudden in onset (after a recognised stroke) or stepwise (gradual decline punctuated by small drops). Often coexists with Alzheimer's disease. Cardiovascular risk factor management is part of the treatment.</p>

<h3>Lewy body dementia</h3>
<p>Often features early visual hallucinations (people, animals), fluctuating cognition (clearer some days than others), and parkinsonian features (slow movement, tremor, falls). Sensitivity to antipsychotic medicines is a real safety concern.</p>

<h3>Frontotemporal dementia</h3>
<p>Tends to present younger (50s and 60s) with personality and behaviour changes (disinhibition, apathy, loss of empathy, language change) before memory decline. Frequently misdiagnosed as depression or a midlife crisis at first.</p>

<h2>How dementia is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Diagnosis in NZ usually starts in primary care and may be confirmed in a memory clinic, neurology, or geriatric medicine service. The process typically includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A careful history, ideally with a family member or close friend who has noticed the changes</li>
  <li>Cognitive testing - usually a structured test like the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) or Mini-ACE</li>
  <li>Blood tests to exclude reversible causes - thyroid problems, B12 deficiency, electrolyte disturbances, infection</li>
  <li>A CT or MRI scan of the brain to look for stroke, tumour, or other structural cause</li>
  <li>Sometimes a referral for specialist memory assessment</li>
</ul>

<p>It is worth being clear: there is no single test that diagnoses dementia. The diagnosis is made by putting all of the above together.</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>Dementia progresses at very different speeds in different people. A common pattern is years of mild stage (forgetfulness, occasional confusion) before noticeable change, then months to years of moderate stage (more help with daily tasks needed), then severe stage (most daily activities require help). The trajectory is rarely smooth - infections, hospital admissions, and bereavement often cause a step-down that does not fully recover.</p>

<p>What changes over time, in roughly the order people notice:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Short-term memory and learning new information</li>
  <li>Word-finding</li>
  <li>Getting lost in familiar places</li>
  <li>Managing finances and medicines</li>
  <li>Recognising people in unexpected contexts</li>
  <li>Behavioural changes (apathy, irritability, sometimes disinhibition)</li>
  <li>Sleep changes (sundowning - more confusion in late afternoon and evening)</li>
  <li>Eventually, basic self-care</li>
</ul>

<h2>What about medications?</h2>

<p>For Alzheimer's disease, two classes of medicine can help with symptoms (not cure):</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cholinesterase inhibitors</strong> - donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine. Funded in NZ with Special Authority criteria (mild to moderate Alzheimer's, baseline cognitive testing, regular review). Roughly 30-40% of patients have a noticeable benefit; the effect is modest and slows decline rather than reversing it.</li>
  <li><strong>Memantine</strong> - used for moderate to severe disease, sometimes added to a cholinesterase inhibitor. Funded with Special Authority.</li>
</ul>

<p>For vascular dementia, control of blood pressure, diabetes, and atrial fibrillation reduces further damage. For Lewy body dementia, the same cholinesterase inhibitors often help and antipsychotics need particular caution. New disease-modifying treatments (lecanemab, donanemab) are not yet funded in NZ as of late 2025; that will likely change in coming years.</p>

<h2>Living well with dementia</h2>

<p>The most important interventions are not pharmacological:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay engaged.</strong> Social connection, mental activity (reading, music, gardening, conversation), and gentle physical activity all slow decline.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep, mood, and pain.</strong> Untreated depression, sleep disturbance, or pain make cognition worse. Treating them gives back ground.</li>
  <li><strong>Hearing and vision.</strong> Both contribute disproportionately to confusion. Get them checked and treated.</li>
  <li><strong>Routine and familiar environment.</strong> Predictability reduces anxiety and disorientation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Driving</h2>

<p>The legal position in NZ is that any driver with cognitive impairment that affects driving safety must inform NZTA. In practice, your GP is required to alert NZTA if your driving is unsafe due to medical reasons. Most people with mild dementia can continue to drive for some time with periodic on-road assessment; moderate dementia is usually incompatible with safe driving. There is a separate handout on driving and older age.</p>

<h2>The legal and practical things to do early</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Enduring power of attorney (EPOA)</strong> - both for property and for personal care and welfare. Must be set up while you still have the mental capacity to do so. See a lawyer or Public Trust.</li>
  <li><strong>Will</strong> - if you have not made one or want to update it, do it now.</li>
  <li><strong>Advance care plan</strong> - record your wishes about future medical care while you can speak for yourself. See the separate handout on advance care planning.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell your bank</strong> - ask about safeguards and joint or supervised accounts.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell the people who matter</strong> - family, employer if relevant, GP, pharmacist.</li>
</ul>
