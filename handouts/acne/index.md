Acne affects most New Zealanders at some point - 85% of teenagers and many adults too. It is not caused by poor hygiene or eating chocolate. It is a real medical condition, it can leave scars, and there are effective treatments. The earlier you start, the less likely you are to be left with marks.

<h2>What is going on under the skin</h2>

<p>Four things combine in acne:</p>

<ol>
<li>Sebaceous (oil) glands produce more sebum, especially under hormonal influence</li>
<li>Dead skin cells block the pore</li>
<li>The bacterium <em>Cutibacterium acnes</em> grows in the blocked pore</li>
<li>Inflammation produces redness, pus, and swelling</li>
</ol>

<p>Most acne is on face, chest, upper back, and shoulders - where oil glands cluster.</p>

<h2>Types of spots</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Whiteheads</strong> - closed plugged pores</li>
<li><strong>Blackheads</strong> - open plugged pores (the dark colour is oxidised oil, not dirt)</li>
<li><strong>Papules</strong> - small red bumps</li>
<li><strong>Pustules</strong> - pus-filled spots</li>
<li><strong>Nodules and cysts</strong> - deeper, painful lumps that are most likely to scar</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does not work</h2>

<ul>
<li>Scrubbing - makes acne worse by irritating skin</li>
<li>Toothpaste, lemon juice, baking soda - all damaging</li>
<li>Picking and squeezing - drives infection deeper and causes scars</li>
<li>Avoiding all moisturiser - many treatments dry the skin and need a non-comedogenic moisturiser to keep them tolerable</li>
<li>Most expensive cosmetics - unless they contain proven ingredients</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does work - over the counter</h2>

<p><strong>Benzoyl peroxide</strong> (2.5% to 5%) - kills bacteria and unblocks pores. Apply once daily, increase if tolerated. Bleaches towels and pillowcases - use white ones. Available without prescription.</p>

<p><strong>Salicylic acid</strong> washes - help with blackheads and whiteheads.</p>

<p><strong>Adapalene 0.1%</strong> gel (Differin) - a topical retinoid, now available without prescription. Excellent for blackheads, whiteheads, and prevention. Apply at night to whole acne-prone area, not just spots.</p>

<p>Use one or two of these consistently for at least 6-12 weeks before judging.</p>

<h2>What does work - prescription</h2>

<p>If over-the-counter products are not enough after 2-3 months, see your GP:</p>

<p><strong>Stronger topical retinoids</strong> - tretinoin, adapalene 0.3%</p>

<p><strong>Topical antibiotics</strong> (clindamycin, erythromycin) - usually combined with benzoyl peroxide to reduce resistance</p>

<p><strong>Oral antibiotics</strong> - doxycycline, lymecycline, erythromycin. Used for moderate inflammatory acne, usually 3-6 months. Always combined with topical benzoyl peroxide or retinoid</p>

<p><strong>Combined oral contraceptive pill</strong> - effective for women whose acne is hormonally driven. Some pills (Diane-35, Ginet, Estelle) are particularly helpful</p>

<p><strong>Spironolactone</strong> - off-label for adult women with hormonal acne. Often very effective</p>

<p><strong>Isotretinoin (Roaccutane)</strong> - the most powerful acne treatment. Used for severe, scarring, or treatment-resistant acne. Funded in NZ for prescribed criteria. Requires careful monitoring (blood tests, contraception for women, mood) but cures acne in most people permanently</p>

<h2>Diet and acne</h2>

<p>The evidence is modest:</p>

<ul>
<li>High-glycaemic diet (sugary food, white bread, sugary drinks) - some studies show worsening</li>
<li>Skim milk in particular - small effect on acne in some studies&sup1;</li>
<li>Whey protein supplements - implicated in some young men with new-onset acne</li>
<li>Chocolate - mixed evidence, probably negligible</li>
</ul>

<p>If you suspect a personal trigger, eliminate it for 6-8 weeks and see. Otherwise, a balanced diet is enough.</p>

<h2>Skin care basics</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wash twice daily with a gentle cleanser - not soap</li>
<li>Avoid harsh scrubs and exfoliating brushes</li>
<li>Use non-comedogenic moisturiser (look for "non-comedogenic" or "oil-free" on the label)</li>
<li>Use SPF 30+ daily, especially if on retinoids or doxycycline</li>
<li>Wash pillowcases weekly</li>
<li>Avoid touching the face</li>
</ul>

<h2>Scarring</h2>

<p>Active inflammation that lasts weeks-months is what causes scars. Treating early prevents them. Once scars exist:</p>

<ul>
<li>Red marks (post-inflammatory erythema) fade over months</li>
<li>Brown marks (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation) can take a year or more, especially on darker skin. SPF helps</li>
<li>True scars (icepick, rolling, boxcar) are permanent. Treatments include microneedling, laser, subcision, fillers - mostly private and expensive</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mental health</h2>

<p>Acne carries real psychological weight. Many young people experience low self-esteem, social anxiety, and depression. Severe acne is associated with increased suicide risk.&sup2; Tell your GP if you are struggling - effective acne treatment helps mood, and counselling support is available.</p>
