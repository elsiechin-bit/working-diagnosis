<p>Around one in six women and one in ten men experience clinically significant depression or anxiety in the year after a baby is born.[1] In a country where new parents are stretched thin, sleep-deprived, and physically recovering, mental illness in this period is common, treatable, and routinely missed because parents (and clinicians) attribute it to "just being tired". This handout covers what postnatal mental illness is, what is normal, what is not, and where to get help in NZ.</p>

<h2>The difference between baby blues and postnatal illness</h2>

<h3>Baby blues</h3>
<p>Affect around 70-80% of women in the first 1-2 weeks after birth. Tearfulness, mood swings, irritability, anxiety. Caused by hormonal changes plus exhaustion. Settle on their own within 2 weeks. Common, normal, do not need treatment beyond support and rest.</p>

<h3>Postnatal depression and anxiety</h3>
<p>Persist beyond 2-3 weeks, get worse rather than better, and interfere with day-to-day functioning. Can start at any point in the first year (sometimes second). Need active treatment.</p>

<h2>What postnatal depression looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Persistent low mood, tearfulness, hopelessness</li>
  <li>Loss of interest or pleasure in things you usually enjoy</li>
  <li>Difficulty bonding with the baby</li>
  <li>Feelings of guilt, inadequacy, or worthlessness</li>
  <li>"Going through the motions" of caring for the baby without feeling much</li>
  <li>Withdrawal from friends, family, partner</li>
  <li>Difficulty sleeping even when the baby sleeps</li>
  <li>Loss of appetite or comfort eating</li>
  <li>Fatigue out of proportion to baby care demands</li>
  <li>Poor concentration, indecisiveness</li>
  <li>Thoughts of self-harm, or that the family would be better off without you</li>
</ul>

<h2>What postnatal anxiety looks like</h2>

<p>Often overlooked because new parents are "expected" to be anxious. The signs of clinically significant anxiety:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Constant worry that you cannot switch off, even when there is no apparent reason</li>
  <li>Repeatedly checking the baby is breathing, even when you have just checked</li>
  <li>Intrusive disturbing thoughts about harm coming to the baby (these are very common and almost never lead to action - but tell your GP because they are treatable)</li>
  <li>Physical symptoms: racing heart, tight chest, shortness of breath, digestive upset</li>
  <li>Avoiding places or activities because of anxiety</li>
  <li>Inability to sleep when the baby is sleeping due to vigilance</li>
  <li>Panic attacks</li>
</ul>

<h2>Postnatal mental illness in fathers and partners</h2>

<p>Around 1 in 10 fathers and non-birthing partners experience clinically significant depression in the first year. Often missed because no one asks. Symptoms can look slightly different - irritability, withdrawal, working long hours, drinking more, conflict in the relationship. Worth screening too.</p>

<h2>Why is it not "just being tired"?</h2>

<p>Tiredness alone usually responds to a few good nights' sleep. Postnatal depression and anxiety persist even when the baby is sleeping. The mental fog, the loss of pleasure, the constant worry - those are not relieved by rest. They need treatment.</p>

<h2>Risk factors</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Personal or family history of depression or anxiety</li>
  <li>Previous postnatal depression</li>
  <li>Difficult pregnancy or birth, NICU admission for the baby</li>
  <li>Premature or unwell baby</li>
  <li>Lack of social support</li>
  <li>Relationship stress or conflict</li>
  <li>Financial stress, housing instability</li>
  <li>Recent significant loss</li>
  <li>History of trauma</li>
  <li>Multiples (twins, triplets)</li>
  <li>Significant difficulty breastfeeding when planned</li>
  <li>Discordance between expectations and reality of motherhood</li>
</ul>

<p>Having risk factors does not mean it will happen. Not having them does not mean it will not.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Talk to someone</h3>
<p>Your GP, midwife (free up to 6 weeks), Plunket nurse, Tipu Ora kaiawhina, family member, friend, or 1737. The hardest part is often the first conversation. After that, the system in NZ is reasonably good at picking up the thread.</p>

<h3>Therapy</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Just A Thought</strong> - free NZ online CBT including a perinatal mental health course</li>
  <li><strong>Brief intervention counselling at GP</strong> - free, often available within 1-2 weeks</li>
  <li><strong>Maternal mental health services</strong> - DHB-based specialist services for moderate to severe disease, free, GP referral</li>
</ul>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>Antidepressants and anti-anxiety medications are safe in pregnancy and breastfeeding for most women. The medicines that have the most safety data in lactation are:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sertraline (Zoloft) - first choice in breastfeeding</li>
  <li>Citalopram, escitalopram</li>
  <li>Fluoxetine (longer half-life so passes more to baby; usually second-line in breastfeeding)</li>
</ul>

<p>Untreated depression or anxiety in a parent has measurable negative effects on baby development. The right approach to medication is not "avoid all medication" but "use the safest effective medication". Discuss with your GP.</p>

<h3>Practical support</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Sleep where possible - even split nights with a partner make a difference</li>
  <li>Get out of the house once a day</li>
  <li>Accept offers of help - food, baby holding, doing the dishes</li>
  <li>Postnatal yoga and exercise classes - movement plus social contact</li>
  <li>New parent groups - Plunket runs them, also community groups</li>
  <li>Reduce decisions - meal plan, batch cook, simplify everything you can</li>
</ul>

<h2>When it is more urgent</h2>

<p>See your GP urgently or call a crisis line if any of these apply:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Thoughts of self-harm or suicide</li>
  <li>Thoughts of harming the baby</li>
  <li>Significant difficulty caring for or bonding with the baby</li>
  <li>Inability to sleep at all (not just baby-related)</li>
  <li>Symptoms in the first 1-2 weeks that are getting worse rather than better</li>
  <li>Hallucinations, paranoia, or strange beliefs - rare but possible postpartum psychosis is a medical emergency</li>
</ul>

<h2>NZ services</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mothers' Helpers</strong> 0800 002 717 - peer support for mothers with postnatal depression</li>
  <li><strong>PADA</strong> (Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Aotearoa) - <a href="https://pada.nz/">pada.nz</a></li>
  <li><strong>Plunket</strong> 0800 933 922 - 24/7 free for under-5s; PlunketLine nurses know the local services</li>
  <li><strong>1737</strong> free call or text</li>
  <li><strong>Maternal Mental Health</strong> services - DHB-based, GP referral</li>
  <li><strong>Tipu Ora</strong> - kaupapa Maori parenting support, regional</li>
  <li><strong>Pacific antenatal and postnatal services</strong> - varies by region</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/courses/perinatal-mental-health">Just A Thought - Perinatal mental health course</a></li>
</ul>

<h2>To partners and whanau</h2>

<p>If you are reading this for someone you love who has just had a baby:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Notice. Persistent low mood, withdrawal, anxiety, exhaustion that is not relieved by sleep are signs.</li>
  <li>Ask gently. "How are you actually doing?" Not "are you OK?".</li>
  <li>Listen. Resist fixing.</li>
  <li>Practical help is mental health help. Doing the dishes, cooking dinner, taking the baby for a walk so they can sleep all reduce risk.</li>
  <li>Help them to the GP if they are reluctant. Offer to come.</li>
  <li>Look after yourself - postnatal mental illness in partners is common too.</li>
</ul>
