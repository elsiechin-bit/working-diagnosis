<p>Erectile dysfunction (ED) is the persistent inability to get or keep an erection firm enough for sex. It is common, it is not a sign of weakness, and it is very treatable. It is also one of the most under-discussed problems in general practice, because men often wait years before raising it. Two things are worth knowing up front: occasional difficulty is normal at any age, and persistent ED is often the body's earliest warning that something else is going on, especially in the blood vessels.</p>

<h2>What is normal at different ages</h2>

<p>Erectile function is a vascular event - blood needs to flow into the penis quickly and stay there. Like any vascular function, it changes with age. Around 40 percent of men in their forties report some degree of ED, rising to about 70 percent by their seventies.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Most of the change is gradual: erections taking longer to develop, needing more direct stimulation, or being less firm than they used to be. None of that on its own is a problem. ED becomes worth treating when it is consistent, distressing, or interfering with your relationship.</p>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Most persistent ED in men over 40 has a vascular cause - the same processes that narrow the arteries to the heart also narrow the much smaller arteries to the penis. ED can appear three to five years before a heart attack or stroke in men with otherwise silent cardiovascular disease.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> Other common contributors include diabetes, smoking, high blood pressure, raised cholesterol, low testosterone (less common than the marketing suggests), pelvic surgery, certain medicines (SSRIs, beta-blockers, finasteride, opioids), and psychological factors.</p>

<p>Psychogenic ED tends to come on suddenly, varies with situation, and preserves morning erections. Vascular ED tends to be gradual, consistent, and erodes morning erections over time. Most men have a mix.</p>

<h2>The cardiovascular link</h2>

<p>Because ED is often the first sign of vascular disease, NZ guidance recommends that men presenting with ED have a full cardiovascular risk assessment - blood pressure, cholesterol, HbA1c, and a 5-year cardiovascular risk score.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> Treating ED without checking the underlying vasculature misses the point.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking is one of the strongest reversible causes of ED. Improvement can be seen within weeks of quitting.</li>
  <li><strong>Move more, lose weight if relevant.</strong> Aerobic exercise (30 to 40 minutes of moderate activity, most days) reliably improves erectile function.</li>
  <li><strong>Limit alcohol.</strong> Heavy or habitual drinking dampens both erectile function and libido.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage diabetes, blood pressure, and cholesterol.</strong> Good control of all three protects the small vessels that erections depend on.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep.</strong> Untreated obstructive sleep apnoea is a common, missed cause. If you snore loudly or wake unrefreshed, ask your GP about a sleep study.</li>
  <li><strong>Talk to your partner.</strong> Performance anxiety amplifies almost every cause. Brief psychosexual counselling helps when there is a strong psychological component.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>The first-line treatments are PDE5 inhibitors: sildenafil (Viagra), tadalafil (Cialis), and vardenafil (Levitra). They work for around 70 percent of men, with similar effectiveness across the three.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup> Sildenafil and tadalafil are funded in NZ only under specific Special Authority criteria; most men buy them privately, and the cost from a NZ pharmacy is usually $30 to $60 per month depending on dose and quantity. They are taken on demand (sildenafil, vardenafil) or daily at a low dose (tadalafil). They cannot be combined with nitrates, which is why your GP will ask about your heart medicines first.</p>

<p>Second-line options include vacuum erection devices (effective, low risk, modest cost), intracavernosal injection therapy, and intraurethral pellets. Penile prostheses are a last resort but are very effective when other treatments fail. Your GP can refer you to a urologist for these options.</p>

<p>Testosterone replacement is only useful if testosterone is genuinely low (see the testosterone in midlife handout). It will not help ED in men with normal testosterone, and the marketing of "low T" treatment for ED has run well ahead of the evidence.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>ED that has been present for more than three months and is bothering you</li>
  <li>ED with chest pain, breathlessness on exertion, or new leg cramps - these need cardiovascular review</li>
  <li>Sudden complete loss of erections, especially after a pelvic injury or surgery</li>
  <li>Painful or curved erections (Peyronie's disease)</li>
  <li>ED with low libido, fatigue, and loss of muscle mass - testosterone is worth checking</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Could this be the first sign of cardiovascular disease in my case?</li>
  <li>Are any of my current medicines contributing?</li>
  <li>Which PDE5 inhibitor would suit me, and what does it cost?</li>
  <li>If the first medicine does not work, what are the next steps?</li>
</ul>
