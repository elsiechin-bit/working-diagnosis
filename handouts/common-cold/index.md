<p>A cold (upper respiratory tract infection, or URTI) is caused by a virus - usually a rhinovirus. Viruses cannot be treated with antibiotics. Most healthy adults recover within 7-10 days without any prescription treatment. The main goal of management is to ease symptoms while your immune system does its job.</p>

<h2>Typical symptoms and timeline</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Days 1-2:</strong> Sore throat, runny nose, feeling run down</li>
  <li><strong>Days 3-5:</strong> Nasal congestion, mucus (which may turn yellow or green - this is normal and does not mean you need antibiotics), cough begins</li>
  <li><strong>Days 6-10:</strong> Symptoms gradually improve; cough may linger for 2-3 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>A mild fever (up to about 38.5°C) is common and is part of your body's immune response. It is not dangerous in healthy adults.</p>

<h2>Why antibiotics do not help</h2>

<p>Antibiotics kill bacteria - not viruses. Taking antibiotics for a cold will not help you recover faster, will not prevent a bacterial infection developing afterward, and carries real downsides: side effects, antibiotic resistance, and disruption to your gut bacteria. Unnecessary antibiotic prescriptions are one of the biggest public health challenges in New Zealand and globally.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest.</strong> Give your body the energy it needs to fight the infection. Take time off work if you can - you will also reduce the risk of spreading the virus to others.</li>
  <li><strong>Fluids.</strong> Stay well hydrated. Water, clear broths, herbal teas, and diluted fruit juice all count. Warm drinks can be soothing for a sore throat.</li>
  <li><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for fever, sore throat, or body aches. Follow the dosing instructions. Ibuprofen is slightly more effective for sore throat; avoid it if you have stomach, kidney, or heart problems.</li>
  <li><strong>Saline nasal spray or drops</strong> help clear congestion and rinse out the nasal passages. Available from any pharmacy. Safe to use several times a day.</li>
  <li><strong>Decongestants</strong> (such as pseudoephedrine or oxymetazoline) can relieve blocked nose. Nasal sprays should not be used for more than 3-5 days in a row. Oral decongestants should be avoided in people with high blood pressure or heart conditions.</li>
  <li><strong>Honey</strong> (for adults and children over 12 months) has modest evidence for soothing cough - a teaspoon in warm water or herbal tea can help.</li>
  <li><strong>Throat lozenges</strong> containing local anaesthetic (such as benzocaine) can temporarily ease sore throat pain.</li>
</ul>

<p>Vitamin C, zinc, and echinacea have modest evidence for slightly shortening the duration of a cold if started early. They will not prevent a cold that has already started. They are not harmful.</p>

<h2>When to see a doctor</h2>

<p>Most colds do not need a GP visit. But see your doctor if you have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>A fever over 39°C that is not coming down with paracetamol or ibuprofen</li>
  <li>Symptoms that are getting significantly worse after day 5, rather than improving</li>
  <li>Severe or one-sided ear pain, or any change in hearing</li>
  <li>Severe facial pain or headache (suggesting sinusitis that may need treatment)</li>
  <li>Cough producing brown or blood-stained mucus</li>
  <li>Shortness of breath or chest pain</li>
  <li>Difficulty swallowing or drooling (which could indicate a throat abscess)</li>
  <li>Symptoms lasting more than 10 days without improvement</li>
  <li>You are immunocompromised, pregnant, or have significant underlying health conditions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing spread</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Wash hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds</li>
  <li>Cover coughs and sneezes with your elbow or a tissue (not your hand)</li>
  <li>Avoid close contact with vulnerable people - newborns, the elderly, and those with weakened immune systems</li>
  <li>Stay home from work or school until you have had no fever for 24 hours</li>
</ul>
