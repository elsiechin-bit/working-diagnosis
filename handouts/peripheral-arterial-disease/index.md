Peripheral arterial disease, or PAD, means the arteries supplying the legs are narrowed, usually by the same plaque process that causes heart disease. Pain on walking is the classic symptom. PAD also signals that the heart and brain arteries are likely affected too, so treatment helps the whole body.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>The classic symptom is "intermittent claudication":</p>

<ul>
<li>Cramping or aching pain in the calves, sometimes thighs or buttocks, when walking</li>
<li>Comes on after a predictable distance</li>
<li>Eases within a few minutes of stopping</li>
<li>Returns at the same distance next time</li>
</ul>

<p>More advanced disease can cause:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pain in the foot at rest, often worse at night, eased by hanging the leg over the bed</li>
<li>Cold, pale, or bluish foot</li>
<li>Slow-healing sores or ulcers, usually on toes or heel</li>
<li>Hair loss on the lower legs, brittle toenails</li>
</ul>

<p>Some people have no pain at all but still have significant disease - especially people with diabetes (where nerve damage hides the pain).</p>

<h2>Why it matters beyond the legs</h2>

<p>People with PAD have around 2-4 times the risk of heart attack and stroke compared with the general population.&sup1; PAD is often the first sign of widespread artery disease.</p>

<h2>How it is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Your GP will:</p>

<ul>
<li>Feel for foot pulses</li>
<li>Compare blood pressures in arms and ankles (the ankle-brachial index, or ABI). An ABI under 0.9 confirms PAD</li>
<li>Sometimes order Doppler ultrasound or specialist imaging</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - the heart and brain matter most</h2>

<p>For mild to moderate PAD, the main aim is to prevent heart attacks and strokes. The medicines are familiar:</p>

<p><strong>Antiplatelet</strong> - aspirin or clopidogrel.</p>

<p><strong>High-intensity statin</strong> - atorvastatin 40-80 mg or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg, regardless of starting cholesterol.</p>

<p><strong>Blood pressure control</strong> - usually below 130/80.</p>

<p><strong>Diabetes control</strong> - if present.</p>

<p><strong>Stop smoking</strong> - the single most powerful intervention. Smokers have far worse PAD outcomes.</p>

<h2>Treatment - the legs</h2>

<p><strong>Supervised exercise.</strong> Walking until pain comes on, then resting, then walking again. Done several times a week, this can double walking distance over a few months. Cardiac/vascular rehabilitation programmes provide supervised sessions.</p>

<p><strong>Medications</strong> for symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cilostazol - improves walking distance for some people; not suitable with heart failure</li>
<li>Pentoxifylline - older, modest benefit</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Procedures</strong> if symptoms remain disabling or if there is rest pain or tissue loss:</p>

<ul>
<li>Angioplasty with stent</li>
<li>Bypass surgery</li>
</ul>

<p>These are reserved for people with severe disease, not those with mild claudication.</p>

<h2>Foot care</h2>

<p>People with PAD (especially with diabetes) need to look after their feet daily:</p>

<ul>
<li>Check feet every day for cuts, blisters, redness, swelling</li>
<li>Wash and dry carefully, including between toes</li>
<li>Cut nails straight across; see a podiatrist for tough nails or callus</li>
<li>Wear well-fitting shoes; never go barefoot outdoors</li>
<li>See a podiatrist - free under most DHB diabetes programmes</li>
<li>See your GP same day for any new sore, redness, or change in colour</li>
</ul>

<h2>Walking through pain - is it safe?</h2>

<p>Yes. Walking until you feel claudication pain, then resting briefly, then continuing, is safe and is the recommended exercise programme. The pain in claudication is not damaging the leg.</p>

<p>Stop and seek help if you develop sudden severe pain at rest, a cold/pale/numb foot, or a sore that will not heal.</p>
