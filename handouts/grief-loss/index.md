<p>Grief is the body and mind's response to losing someone or something that mattered to us. It is not a problem to be solved or a process to be completed. It is, more often, a reorientation - learning to carry the loss while still living the life that comes next. This handout is a starting point: what is normal, what tends to help, and the situations where extra support is genuinely useful.</p>

<h2>The myth of "stages"</h2>

<p>Elisabeth Kubler-Ross's "five stages" (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) were originally observations of dying patients, not bereaved ones. They got popularised as a tidy sequence that grief is supposed to follow. It does not. Real grief is messier:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Comes in waves, not stages</li>
  <li>Is not linear - "good days" and "bad days" alternate, often unpredictably</li>
  <li>Includes more than sadness - anger, relief, guilt, numbness, even occasional moments of joy</li>
  <li>Has no fixed timeline</li>
  <li>Does not "complete" - more often it changes shape</li>
</ul>

<p>If your grief does not look like the stages, you are not grieving wrong.</p>

<h2>What is normal in the first year</h2>

<h3>Physical</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Exhaustion that does not lift with rest</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance - either insomnia, or sleeping a lot and waking unrested</li>
  <li>Appetite change - up or down</li>
  <li>Heart palpitations, chest tightness, sighing breath ("air hunger")</li>
  <li>Reduced immunity - more colds, slower healing</li>
  <li>Worsening of any chronic condition you have</li>
  <li>"Brain fog" - memory and concentration problems</li>
  <li>Heaviness in the chest - the actual physical sensation that the word "grief" is named for</li>
</ul>

<h3>Emotional</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Waves of sadness, sometimes with no warning, triggered by small things</li>
  <li>Anger - at the person who died, at the world, at yourself, at people who try to help</li>
  <li>Guilt - real or imagined</li>
  <li>Relief, especially after long illness; often followed by guilt about the relief</li>
  <li>Anxiety - about your own mortality, or other people you love</li>
  <li>Loneliness even when surrounded by people</li>
  <li>Numbness, especially in the first weeks</li>
  <li>Sense of unreality, "this cannot be happening"</li>
</ul>

<h3>Cognitive and behavioural</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Hearing or sensing the person's presence - very common, not pathological</li>
  <li>Wanting to talk about them, or wanting not to</li>
  <li>Avoiding things that remind you, or seeking them out</li>
  <li>Difficulty with decisions, planning, work concentration</li>
  <li>Withdrawal from friends and family</li>
  <li>Or the opposite - keeping busy to avoid feeling</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Time and patience with yourself</h3>
<p>Grief in the first year is often described as "in the room with you" - a constant presence. By the second year for most people, the grief has moved into a different relationship - still present, but more like a companion than an occupier. The general rule of thumb: be careful about big decisions in the first year (selling the house, leaving the job, new relationships); time-tested decisions in the second.</p>

<h3>Talk about them</h3>
<p>Many bereaved people fear "moving on" because it feels like leaving the person behind. The opposite tends to be more true: the people who do best long-term keep the dead person present in some form - through stories, photos, anniversaries, ongoing ways the person continues to matter to them.</p>

<h3>Routine</h3>
<p>Sleep, regular meals, daily walks. Boring, foundational. Grief is exhausting; the body and mind cope better with rhythm than with chaos.</p>

<h3>Move your body</h3>
<p>Walking, swimming, gardening, gentle exercise. Lifts mood, improves sleep, reduces the physical heaviness.</p>

<h3>Other people</h3>
<p>Bereaved people often withdraw at the time they most need company. Counter-intuitively, the company that helps most is often someone willing to sit with you and not try to fix anything. Grief support groups can be valuable - other bereaved people understand things others do not.</p>

<h3>Permission to feel what you feel</h3>
<p>People will tell you to "be strong", "stay positive", "focus on the good times". Some of that is helpful, much of it is not. There is no right way to feel.</p>

<h2>When grief becomes more than grief</h2>

<p>Most people grieve and gradually integrate the loss without needing professional help. A minority develop:</p>

<h3>Major depression</h3>
<p>Around 1 in 7 bereaved people develop clinically significant depression in the first year. Signs that grief has tipped into depression:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Persistent low mood that does not lift even briefly</li>
  <li>Loss of interest in everything, including the person who died</li>
  <li>Pervasive guilt or feeling worthless</li>
  <li>Thoughts of self-harm, suicide, or wanting to "join" the person who died</li>
  <li>Inability to function in basic tasks for many weeks</li>
  <li>Symptoms not improving at all over months</li>
</ul>

<p>This is treatable. See your GP.</p>

<h3>Prolonged grief disorder</h3>
<p>Persistent intense grief beyond 12 months that disrupts daily functioning. Affects around 7-10% of bereaved adults.[1] Signs:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Intense longing or yearning for the person, persistent</li>
  <li>Preoccupation with the person to the exclusion of other relationships and activities</li>
  <li>Identity disruption - "I do not know who I am without them"</li>
  <li>Avoidance of reminders, or compulsive seeking out of reminders</li>
  <li>Inability to engage with new people or activities</li>
  <li>Numbness or emptiness</li>
  <li>Feeling that life is meaningless</li>
</ul>

<p>Specific therapies (Complicated Grief Therapy, prolonged exposure for grief) help.</p>

<h3>PTSD</h3>
<p>If the death was traumatic - witnessed, sudden, violent, suicide - PTSD is more common. See the PTSD handout.</p>

<h2>Specific situations</h2>

<h3>Sudden death</h3>
<p>Often more disorienting and traumatic. Seek support earlier - both grief support and trauma-focused care if needed.</p>

<h3>Suicide</h3>
<p>Specific support exists in NZ. Skylight, Victim Support, and Mental Health Foundation all offer resources for those bereaved by suicide. The complications of suicide bereavement (guilt, anger, social stigma) are recognised - support that understands them helps.</p>

<h3>Loss of a baby - miscarriage, stillbirth, neonatal death</h3>
<p>Often invisible to the wider world but profoundly significant. Sands NZ provides support. Many midwives and GPs can provide ongoing follow-up.</p>

<h3>Anticipatory grief</h3>
<p>Grieving before the death, common with terminal illness, dementia, or significant decline. Real grief, deserves the same support as bereavement.</p>

<h3>Non-death losses</h3>
<p>Divorce, miscarriage, infertility, loss of a pet, loss of health, loss of a homeland or identity. The grief response is similar even though the world may not name it as grief.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Persistent symptoms beyond 12 months that are not gradually settling</li>
  <li>Thoughts of self-harm, or thoughts of joining the person who died</li>
  <li>Inability to function in basic tasks (eating, dressing, work) for many weeks</li>
  <li>Significant alcohol or drug use to cope</li>
  <li>Significant new physical symptoms</li>
  <li>You want help but do not know where to start</li>
  <li>Sudden, traumatic, or stigmatised death (sudden cardiac, suicide, violent)</li>
</ul>
