<p>Alcohol is the most-used drug in NZ. It is also one of the most underrated contributors to mental health problems. The link is bidirectional: alcohol worsens mental illness, and mental illness drives alcohol use. Many people who try to address their depression or anxiety hit a wall because they have not addressed the drinking. This handout walks through why, what cutting back actually does, and how to do it in NZ.</p>

<h2>What alcohol does to mental health</h2>

<h3>The pharmacology</h3>
<p>Alcohol is a central nervous system depressant. The first one or two drinks reduce anxiety and produce mild euphoria - which is why people drink. After that, several effects accumulate:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Disrupts sleep architecture.</strong> Alcohol gets you to sleep faster but reduces REM sleep and fragments the second half of the night. You sleep worse on alcohol than off it, even if it does not feel that way.</li>
  <li><strong>Worsens anxiety the next day.</strong> The brain compensates for alcohol's depressant effect by producing more excitatory chemicals; when alcohol clears, you are left with rebound anxiety. This is "hangxiety".</li>
  <li><strong>Lowers mood over days to weeks.</strong> Sustained drinking depletes serotonin and dopamine, producing depressive symptoms even in people without underlying depression.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduces stress resilience.</strong> Long-term drinkers have a less effective stress response, even when sober.</li>
  <li><strong>Disinhibits.</strong> Increases risk of impulsive decisions, conflict, accidents, self-harm.</li>
  <li><strong>Increases suicide risk.</strong> Alcohol is involved in around half of all NZ suicides.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The two-way relationship</h3>

<p>People drink to manage anxiety, low mood, sleep, social discomfort, trauma. The drinking provides short-term relief, then makes the underlying condition worse, which drives more drinking. The cycle is reliable.</p>

<p>This pattern is particularly clear with:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Anxiety disorders</strong> - alcohol provides the most reliable short-term relief; produces rebound anxiety on withdrawal</li>
  <li><strong>Depression</strong> - alcohol makes depression worse, sometimes dramatically</li>
  <li><strong>PTSD</strong> - drinking blunts intrusive memories temporarily, prevents the natural processing the trauma needs</li>
  <li><strong>ADHD</strong> - many adults with undiagnosed ADHD self-medicate with alcohol</li>
  <li><strong>Bipolar</strong> - destabilises mood and reduces medication effectiveness</li>
  <li><strong>Insomnia</strong> - the most common bad sleep prescription in NZ</li>
</ul>

<p>The mental health condition is rarely fully treatable while the drinking continues at problem levels.</p>

<h2>What "problem drinking" actually means</h2>

<p>You do not need to be physically dependent for alcohol to be a mental health problem. NZ guidance:</p>

<h3>Low-risk drinking guidelines</h3>
<ul>
  <li>No more than 10 standard drinks a week (women)</li>
  <li>No more than 15 standard drinks a week (men)</li>
  <li>No more than 4 standard drinks on any one occasion</li>
  <li>At least 2 alcohol-free days a week</li>
  <li>None during pregnancy</li>
  <li>None or much less for those with mental illness, taking certain medications, or in recovery</li>
</ul>

<p>A standard drink in NZ is 10g of alcohol - roughly a small (100ml) glass of wine, a 330ml beer at 4%, or a 30ml shot of spirits. Most home pours are 1.5-2 standard drinks despite looking like one.</p>

<h3>Signs alcohol is contributing to mental health</h3>

<ul>
  <li>You drink to cope with anxiety, low mood, stress, or sleep</li>
  <li>Your mood is worse the day after drinking</li>
  <li>You wake at 3am after drinking</li>
  <li>You think about your next drink during the day</li>
  <li>You drink more than you intended</li>
  <li>You have tried to cut back and not succeeded</li>
  <li>Your therapy or medication is not working as well as it should</li>
  <li>You have rebound anxiety in the morning</li>
</ul>

<h2>What changes when you cut back</h2>

<p>Most people who cut alcohol significantly notice changes within 1-4 weeks:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sleep improves - especially the second half of the night</li>
  <li>Morning anxiety reduces or stops</li>
  <li>Mood lifts - sometimes dramatically in those who had been drinking heavily</li>
  <li>Energy increases</li>
  <li>Concentration improves</li>
  <li>Weight often drops</li>
  <li>Liver enzymes normalise</li>
  <li>Skin clears</li>
  <li>Existing mental health treatments work better</li>
</ul>

<p>The first 1-2 weeks can be uncomfortable - rebound anxiety, sleep disturbance, irritability. This passes. After that, most people feel better than they did when drinking, often unrecognisably so.</p>

<h2>How to cut back</h2>

<h3>For most people</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Take a structured break.</strong> 4 weeks is enough to feel the difference. The "Dry July" or "Sober October" frame is useful for many.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan alcohol-free days.</strong> 3-4 a week to start. Make them specific: "I will not drink Mon, Tues, Wed".</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce the dose.</strong> Smaller pours, lower-alcohol options, alcohol-free beers (which have improved a lot).</li>
  <li><strong>Notice triggers.</strong> What time of day, what feeling, what social context. Plan an alternative for each.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell someone.</strong> A partner, friend, GP, peer group. Accountability matters.</li>
  <li><strong>Apps</strong> - Try Dry, Reframe, I Am Sober track sober days and provide structure.</li>
</ul>

<h3>If physically dependent on alcohol</h3>
<p>Important: if you drink daily and significantly, abrupt cessation can be medically dangerous. Alcohol withdrawal can cause seizures, delirium tremens, and rarely death. Signs of dependence:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Drink every day</li>
  <li>Drink first thing in the morning</li>
  <li>Hands shake until you have a drink</li>
  <li>Withdrawal symptoms (sweating, anxiety, tremor) when not drinking</li>
  <li>History of seizures during attempts to stop</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these apply, see your GP before stopping. Medical detox - sometimes outpatient, sometimes inpatient - is safe and effective. Diazepam-tapered withdrawal is the standard approach. Doing it alone is dangerous.</p>

<h2>Treatment options in NZ</h2>

<h3>Free or low-cost</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Alcohol Drug Helpline</strong> 0800 787 797 - 24/7 free, by phone or online chat</li>
  <li><strong>Brief intervention</strong> at your GP - many practices have on-site counsellors</li>
  <li><strong>Community alcohol and drug services</strong> - DHB-funded, free, GP referral</li>
  <li><strong>AA - Alcoholics Anonymous NZ</strong> - free, peer-led, in-person and online meetings</li>
  <li><strong>SMART Recovery</strong> - science-based group programme, free</li>
</ul>

<h3>Medications</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Naltrexone</strong> - reduces craving and reward from alcohol; useful for those wanting to cut down or remain abstinent. Funded in NZ. Often underused.</li>
  <li><strong>Acamprosate</strong> - reduces post-cessation discomfort and craving. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Disulfiram (Antabuse)</strong> - causes unpleasant reaction if alcohol consumed; supervised use. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Treatment of co-existing depression, anxiety, sleep</strong> - often improves drinking outcomes.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Specialist services</h3>
<p>Residential rehabilitation, structured outpatient programmes, intensive day programmes - generally for those with significant dependence or repeated failed attempts. Mostly funded; some private. GP refers.</p>

<h2>For Maori and Pacific peoples</h2>

<p>Kaupapa Maori AOD services exist in most regions and incorporate Te Whare Tapa Wha and tikanga in the recovery approach. Te Rau Matatini, regional iwi providers. Pacific services through Vaka Tautua, Le Va, MoanaConnect.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You suspect alcohol is contributing to your mental health</li>
  <li>You want to cut down and have not been able to</li>
  <li>You are physically dependent (see signs above) - do not stop alone</li>
  <li>Co-existing depression, anxiety, or other mental illness</li>
  <li>Family or partner concerned</li>
  <li>You are in recovery and want medication support</li>
  <li>Drinking is affecting work, relationships, parenting</li>
</ul>
