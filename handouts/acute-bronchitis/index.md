Acute bronchitis is inflammation of the larger airways, most often after a viral upper respiratory infection. The hallmark is a cough that hangs around long after the rest of the cold has cleared. It is one of the most common reasons for asking for antibiotics - and one of the conditions where antibiotics have been most clearly shown not to help.

<h2>Typical course</h2>

<ul>
<li>Starts as a cold (runny nose, sore throat, mild fever) for a few days</li>
<li>Cough develops, initially dry, then often producing clear, yellow, or green sputum</li>
<li>Cough lasts on average 2-3 weeks</li>
<li>Wheezing or chest tightness is common</li>
<li>Mild breathlessness with exertion</li>
<li>Fatigue</li>
</ul>

<p>By the second week, the cold has gone but the cough lingers. This is normal and does not mean antibiotics are needed.</p>

<h2>Why is the cough so persistent?</h2>

<p>The lining of the airways is shed and slowly regrows over weeks. Inflammation makes airways twitchy and easily irritated by talking, laughing, cold air, or exercise. The cough reflex stays sensitive long after the virus has been cleared.</p>

<h2>Why antibiotics rarely help</h2>

<p>The cause is almost always viral - rhinovirus, influenza, RSV, parainfluenza, coronaviruses. Bacteria account for fewer than 10% of cases. Cochrane reviews show antibiotics shorten the cough by about half a day on average and have a 1 in 5 risk of side effects (diarrhoea, thrush, rash). Most people do not get any benefit at all.&sup1;</p>

<p>Coloured sputum is not a useful guide to bacterial infection. Yellow and green sputum can come from inflammation alone.</p>

<h2>What helps the cough</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Time.</strong> Most coughs settle within 3 weeks; some last 4-6 weeks</li>
<li><strong>Honey</strong> (over age 1) - small but real benefit, especially for nighttime cough in children</li>
<li><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for chest discomfort and fever</li>
<li><strong>Plenty of fluids</strong> - thins secretions</li>
<li><strong>Warm drinks and steam</strong> - soothing</li>
<li><strong>Rest</strong> - and avoid pushing through</li>
<li><strong>Stop smoking</strong> - the single most important thing for any cough</li>
<li><strong>Cough suppressants</strong> (dextromethorphan, pholcodine) - help some people for short-term relief, especially for sleep. Use sparingly</li>
<li><strong>Bronchodilator inhaler</strong> (salbutamol) - useful if there is wheeze and tightness; ask your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>What rarely helps despite the marketing</h2>

<ul>
<li>Most over-the-counter cough mixtures - mostly placebo</li>
<li>Vitamin C in normal doses</li>
<li>Echinacea</li>
<li>Antibiotics (above)</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<p>See a GP for cough that:</p>

<ul>
<li>Lasts more than 3 weeks without improvement</li>
<li>Is associated with high fever, severe chest pain, or feeling very unwell</li>
<li>Produces blood</li>
<li>Causes increasing breathlessness</li>
<li>Wakes you repeatedly at night</li>
<li>Triggers vomiting or fainting</li>
<li>Occurs in someone with COPD, asthma, heart failure, or immunosuppression</li>
<li>Comes with weight loss, night sweats, or a long history of smoking</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP may consider chest X-ray, spirometry, or other tests if the picture does not fit simple bronchitis.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - same-day or 111</h2>

<ul>
<li>Severe breathlessness, especially at rest</li>
<li>Lips, tongue, or fingertips turning blue</li>
<li>Confusion or drowsiness</li>
<li>Coughing up large amounts of blood</li>
<li>Chest pain that feels crushing or radiates to arm or jaw</li>
<li>Fever above 39°C with severe symptoms</li>
</ul>

<h2>Bronchitis vs pneumonia</h2>

<p>Pneumonia is an infection of the lung tissue itself. It usually has higher fever, chest pain, breathlessness, and looks abnormal on X-ray. Pneumonia needs antibiotics. See our <a href="/handouts/pneumonia/">pneumonia handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Repeated bronchitis</h2>

<p>If you have a cough lasting weeks several times a year, see your GP. The diagnosis may be:</p>

<ul>
<li>Asthma (especially cough-variant)</li>
<li>COPD</li>
<li>Postnasal drip from chronic rhinitis</li>
<li>Reflux (GORD)</li>
<li>Smokers' bronchitis</li>
<li>Bronchiectasis</li>
</ul>

<p>These need specific treatment, not repeated courses of antibiotics.</p>
