Melanoma is a cancer of the pigment-producing cells in the skin. New Zealand has the highest rate in the world. The crucial fact: when caught early, melanoma is almost always curable with simple surgery. When found late, it is one of the most serious cancers. Knowing your skin and showing your GP any new or changing spot saves lives.

<h2>What melanoma looks like</h2>

<p>Most melanomas are pigmented (brown, black, sometimes red, blue, or even skin-coloured). They may arise from an existing mole or as a new spot. See the <a href="/handouts/skin-cancer-abcde/">ABCDE rule</a> handout for the classic warning signs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Asymmetry</li>
<li>Border irregular</li>
<li>Colour mixed</li>
<li>Diameter over 6 mm</li>
<li>Evolving (changing) - the most important</li>
</ul>

<p>Less common but important patterns:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Nodular melanoma</strong> - a fast-growing, often dark or skin-coloured raised lump that bleeds or oozes</li>
<li><strong>Acral melanoma</strong> - on palms, soles, or under nails. More common in Maori, Pacific, and Asian skin</li>
<li><strong>Mucosal melanoma</strong> - in mouth, genitals, or rectum (rare)</li>
<li><strong>Lentigo maligna</strong> - a slow-growing brown patch on sun-damaged skin in older people</li>
</ul>

<h2>Risk factors</h2>

<ul>
<li>Fair skin, fair eyes, red or blonde hair</li>
<li>Many moles (over 50) or atypical moles</li>
<li>History of sunburn, especially severe and in childhood</li>
<li>Family history of melanoma</li>
<li>Personal history of skin cancer</li>
<li>Sunbed use</li>
<li>Immunosuppression (transplant, certain medicines)</li>
<li>Older age</li>
</ul>

<p>You can have none of the obvious risk factors and still develop a melanoma. Always take a changing spot seriously.</p>

<h2>How melanoma is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Your GP examines the spot, usually with a dermatoscope. If suspicion remains, the spot is excised under local anaesthetic with a 2 mm safety margin and sent to the lab.</p>

<p>The pathology report tells you:</p>

<ul>
<li>Whether it is melanoma or something else</li>
<li>Breslow thickness (depth in millimetres) - the single most important number</li>
<li>Whether the surface is ulcerated</li>
<li>The cell division rate</li>
<li>Whether it has reached the cut margins</li>
</ul>

<h2>Staging and what it means</h2>

<p>Staging combines the depth, ulceration, and any spread to lymph nodes or distant sites:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Stage 0 (in situ):</strong> top layer only. Cure rate effectively 100%</li>
<li><strong>Stage I-II:</strong> confined to skin. 5-year survival 80-99%</li>
<li><strong>Stage III:</strong> spread to local lymph nodes. 5-year survival 50-80%, with treatment</li>
<li><strong>Stage IV:</strong> spread to distant sites. Modern immunotherapy and targeted therapy have transformed outcomes - 5-year survival now 50%+ in some groups, where it was once 5%.&sup1;</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Wider excision.</strong> Once melanoma is confirmed, a wider excision is done to remove a margin of normal skin around the original site (5-20 mm depending on depth).</p>

<p><strong>Sentinel lymph node biopsy.</strong> For melanomas thicker than about 0.8-1 mm, the first draining lymph node is checked. This guides further treatment.</p>

<p><strong>Adjuvant treatment.</strong> For higher-risk melanomas (stage III), immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) for 12 months reduces relapse risk substantially. PHARMAC funds these for eligible patients.</p>

<p><strong>Advanced melanoma.</strong> For stage IV, immunotherapy (combination ipilimumab plus nivolumab) and targeted therapy (BRAF/MEK inhibitors) have changed outcomes dramatically.</p>

<h2>Follow-up</h2>

<p>After diagnosis, you will be seen regularly for skin checks - the schedule depends on stage:</p>

<ul>
<li>Stage 0: GP skin check yearly</li>
<li>Stage I-II: every 6 months for 2-5 years, then yearly</li>
<li>Stage III+: more frequent specialist follow-up, often with scans</li>
</ul>

<p>The risk of a second primary melanoma is around 5-10% over 10 years, so vigilance is for life.&sup2;</p>

<h2>Self-monitoring after a melanoma</h2>

<p>Check your skin head-to-toe monthly. Show your GP any new or changing spot. Photographs taken at routine reviews help track moles. Apps and mole-mapping services exist (mostly private in NZ).</p>

<h2>The emotional side</h2>

<p>A melanoma diagnosis is frightening, even for early stage. Many people feel anxious for months afterwards, especially before each follow-up. Talk to your GP - support, counselling, and peer support groups (through Melanoma NZ) all help.</p>

<h2>Family</h2>

<p>If you have melanoma, first-degree relatives have around 2-3 times the population risk. Encourage them to be SunSmart and to have a baseline skin check. Genetic testing is rarely indicated - usually only if there are several family members with melanoma or pancreatic cancer.</p>
