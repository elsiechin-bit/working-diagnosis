<p>Poor sleep is extremely common and has real effects on your health, mood, concentration, and risk of long-term conditions. The good news is that most sleep problems respond well to consistent changes in your habits - without needing sleeping pills.</p>

<h2>How much sleep do adults need?</h2>

<p>Most adults need seven to nine hours of sleep per night. Some people function well on slightly less; very few people genuinely thrive on less than six hours. If you regularly feel tired, irritable, or struggle to concentrate during the day, you are probably not getting enough.</p>

<h2>The single most important habit</h2>

<p>Set a consistent wake time - and stick to it, seven days a week, no matter how badly you slept the night before. This is the most powerful thing you can do to reset your body clock. Do not try to "catch up" by sleeping in on weekends - it makes the problem worse over time.</p>

<h2>Building a sleep-friendly routine</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Wind down for 30-60 minutes before bed.</strong> Dim the lights, avoid screens, and do something calm - reading a physical book, gentle stretching, or listening to quiet music.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep screens out of the bedroom.</strong> The blue light from phones, tablets, and TVs delays the release of melatonin (your sleep hormone) and keeps your brain alert. If you use your phone as an alarm, switch on aeroplane mode.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid caffeine after midday.</strong> Caffeine has a half-life of around five to six hours - a coffee at 3pm still has half its caffeine in your body at 8pm. This includes energy drinks, strong tea, and some soft drinks.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid alcohol near bedtime.</strong> Alcohol can help you fall asleep but disrupts the deep stages of sleep, leading to lighter, more broken sleep in the second half of the night.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet.</strong> A room temperature around 18-19°C is ideal for most people. Blackout curtains or an eye mask can help if light is an issue.</li>
  <li><strong>Get natural light in the morning.</strong> Even 10 minutes outside soon after waking helps reset your body clock and improves sleep the following night.</li>
  <li><strong>Use your bed only for sleep (and sex).</strong> If you work, watch TV, or scroll from bed, your brain learns to associate the bed with being awake. This makes it harder to switch off when you want to sleep.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you cannot fall asleep</h2>

<p>If you have been lying awake for more than 20-30 minutes, get up. Go to another room, do something quiet and not too stimulating, and return to bed only when you feel genuinely sleepy. Lying in bed frustrated reinforces the problem.</p>

<h2>Exercise and sleep</h2>

<p>Regular physical activity - especially in the morning or afternoon - significantly improves sleep quality. Vigorous exercise late at night can make it harder to fall asleep for some people, though this varies between individuals.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<p>Good sleep habits help most people, but some sleep problems need professional support. See your GP if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sleep difficulties have lasted more than three months and are affecting your daily life</li>
  <li>You or your partner has noticed loud snoring, gasping, or pauses in breathing during sleep - this may be sleep apnoea, which is treatable</li>
  <li>You are relying on sleeping pills regularly</li>
  <li>You feel your sleep problems are linked to anxiety or depression</li>
</ul>

<p>Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the most effective treatment for long-term insomnia. Your GP can refer you to a psychologist trained in CBT-I, or recommend a validated online programme.</p>
