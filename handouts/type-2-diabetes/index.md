<p>Type 2 diabetes is a condition where the body has difficulty keeping blood glucose (sugar) at a healthy level. It develops when the body either does not make enough insulin or does not use insulin effectively. Insulin is the hormone that helps move glucose from your blood into your cells for energy.</p>

<p>Type 2 diabetes is very common in New Zealand - around 250,000 people have been diagnosed, and many more are living with it undiagnosed. It is not your fault. Genetics, family history, and many factors outside your control play a significant role.</p>

<h2>The key number: HbA1c</h2>

<p>Your HbA1c (pronounced "H-B-A-one-C") is a blood test that shows your average blood glucose over the past 2-3 months. It is measured as a percentage or in mmol/mol. Your doctor will explain your result and your personal target, but as a general guide:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Below 50 mmol/mol (6.7%) - good control for most people with type 2 diabetes</li>
  <li>50-64 mmol/mol - room for improvement; review medicines and lifestyle</li>
  <li>Above 64 mmol/mol - higher risk of complications; your doctor will want to act</li>
</ul>

<p>HbA1c is usually checked every 3-6 months when your diabetes is being established, then every 6-12 months once things are stable.</p>

<h2>Why glucose control matters</h2>

<p>Consistently high blood glucose damages blood vessels and nerves over time. This can lead to complications affecting the eyes (diabetic eye disease), kidneys (diabetic kidney disease), feet and legs (nerve damage and circulation problems), and heart and brain (heart attack and stroke). Most of these complications can be prevented or significantly delayed by good glucose control, along with managing blood pressure and cholesterol.</p>

<h2>What helps - lifestyle</h2>

<p>Lifestyle changes are the foundation of type 2 diabetes management and can make a significant difference, especially early on:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Eat well.</strong> There is no single "diabetes diet" but the evidence favours whole foods, plenty of vegetables, legumes, and minimally processed foods. Reducing refined carbohydrates (white bread, white rice, sugary drinks, pastries) helps keep blood glucose steadier. A registered dietitian can help you make a personalised plan - ask your GP for a referral.</li>
  <li><strong>Move more.</strong> Physical activity directly lowers blood glucose and improves insulin sensitivity. Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week, spread across most days. Even a 15-minute walk after meals helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Reach a healthier weight.</strong> Even modest weight loss (5-10% of body weight) can improve blood glucose significantly. In some people, substantial weight loss can lead to remission of type 2 diabetes.</li>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking dramatically worsens diabetes complications. Free support: Quitline 0800 778 778.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Many people with type 2 diabetes need medicines in addition to lifestyle changes. Metformin is usually the first medicine prescribed. It is safe, well-proven, and inexpensive. Other medicines may be added over time as needed. Some newer medicines (such as SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 agonists) also protect the heart and kidneys, which is an important extra benefit for some people.</p>

<p>Insulin may be needed eventually if other medicines are not enough. This is not a sign of failure - it is just how the condition progresses for some people.</p>

<h2>Your annual review</h2>

<p>Every year your GP should check: your HbA1c, blood pressure, kidney function, cholesterol, your feet, and refer you for an eye check. These checks catch problems early, when they are easiest to treat. Make sure you attend.</p>

<h2>When to get help quickly</h2>

<p>Go to urgent care or call your GP if you have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms of very high blood sugar: extreme thirst, frequent urination, blurred vision, fatigue, fruity-smelling breath, feeling very unwell</li>
  <li>Symptoms of low blood sugar (if you take insulin or certain tablets): shakiness, sweating, confusion, heart racing - treat immediately with 15 g of fast sugar (4 glucose tablets, 150 ml fruit juice, or 3-4 jelly beans), then eat something containing carbohydrate</li>
  <li>Any foot wound, ulcer, or infection that is not healing - seek same-day assessment</li>
</ul>
