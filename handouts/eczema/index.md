<p>Eczema (also called atopic dermatitis) is a chronic skin condition that causes dry, itchy, inflamed skin. It affects around 1 in 5 New Zealand children and 1 in 10 adults. It is not contagious, not caused by being unclean, and is not "cured" - but it can be managed very well with the right skin care routine.</p>

<p>Eczema tends to come in flares - periods when the skin is worse - separated by times when it is calm or clear. The goal of treatment is to keep the skin as calm as possible, reduce the frequency and severity of flares, and manage them quickly when they occur.</p>

<h2>The foundation: moisturising daily</h2>

<p>Regular moisturising is the single most important part of eczema management. It is not optional, and it is not just for flares. Use it every day, all over, whether or not the skin looks bad.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Use an emollient (moisturiser), not a regular body lotion.</strong> Emollients are thicker and more effective at repairing the skin barrier. Aqueous cream, sorbolene cream, or a prescribed emollient all work. Some people do better with ointments (thicker, greasier) than creams.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply immediately after bathing</strong> while the skin is still slightly damp, to lock in moisture. Pat skin dry gently - do not rub.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply generously.</strong> A child with moderate eczema may use 250 g of emollient per week. Using small amounts is one of the most common reasons eczema does not improve.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid fragranced moisturisers, bubble baths, and scented soaps</strong> - these dry and irritate the skin barrier.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Bathing</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Lukewarm (not hot) baths or showers - once daily is usually fine</li>
  <li>Use a soap-free wash - ask your pharmacist for a suitable product</li>
  <li>Keep baths short (5-10 minutes)</li>
  <li>Pat (do not rub) dry, then apply emollient immediately</li>
</ul>

<h2>Using topical steroid creams</h2>

<p>Topical steroid creams are safe and effective when used correctly for flares. Many people are worried about steroids, but when used as directed, they are appropriate and important.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Use the steroid your GP has prescribed</strong> - different strengths are used for different body parts and severity levels. Weaker steroids are used on the face and skin folds; stronger ones on thicker skin areas like the arms and legs.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply once daily to the inflamed areas only</strong> (not to clear skin). Apply a thin layer and rub gently in.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply before your emollient</strong>, or wait 30 minutes after applying emollient before applying the steroid cream.</li>
  <li><strong>Use for 5-7 days at the start of a flare</strong>, or as directed. Stop when the flare settles. Do not stop too soon if the skin is still inflamed - incomplete treatment is a common reason flares persist.</li>
  <li><strong>The "fingertip unit"</strong> is a useful guide to amount: one fingertip unit (from tip to first crease of an adult index finger) covers the hand and wrist. Your GP or pharmacist can explain the right amount for different areas.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Identifying and avoiding triggers</h2>

<p>Common eczema triggers include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sweating (during exercise or in warm weather)</li>
  <li>Synthetic fabrics - cotton is usually better tolerated</li>
  <li>Fragranced products: soaps, detergents, shampoos, fabric softeners</li>
  <li>Dust mites</li>
  <li>Pet dander (cat and dog saliva)</li>
  <li>Certain foods (in young children with severe eczema, food allergies can be a trigger - discuss with your GP if suspected)</li>
  <li>Stress</li>
  <li>Dry or cold air</li>
</ul>

<p>Keeping a simple diary of flares and possible triggers can help identify patterns specific to you.</p>

<h2>Managing the itch</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Keep nails short to reduce damage from scratching</li>
  <li>Cool the skin with a damp cloth or emollient kept in the fridge during flares</li>
  <li>Non-sedating antihistamines (such as loratadine or cetirizine) do not treat eczema directly but may reduce itching enough to break the itch-scratch cycle</li>
  <li>Sedating antihistamines (such as promethazine) may help at night if itching is preventing sleep - discuss with your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A flare that is not improving after 1 week of correct topical steroid use</li>
  <li>Weeping, crusting, or very red and hot skin - this may indicate a bacterial infection (impetigo) requiring antibiotic treatment</li>
  <li>Blistering or painful rash - this may indicate a viral infection (eczema herpeticum), which needs urgent treatment</li>
  <li>Eczema significantly affecting sleep or daily activities</li>
  <li>You are unsure whether you are using the right products or the right amount</li>
</ul>
