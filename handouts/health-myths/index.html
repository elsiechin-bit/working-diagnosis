❍ Myth vs Evidence

Common health myths explained

Not everything that circulates online about health is wrong, but the parts that are wrong matter. Here is what the evidence actually shows.

Myths
Persist
Medical myths survive because they contain a grain of truth or fit our intuitions
Source

"You need to drink 8 glasses of water a day" ›
The claim: You must drink exactly 8 glasses (about 2 litres) of water daily or you will become dehydrated.
Grain of truth: Hydration is important and most people do not drink enough fluid.
What the evidence actually shows: The "8 glasses" figure is not from scientific research. It appears to trace back to a 1945 US dietary recommendation that suggested 2.5 litres of water daily, but included water from all food sources (fruits, vegetables, soup, coffee all contain water).2 Thirst is a reliable indicator of hydration status in most healthy adults. People with certain conditions (heart failure, kidney disease, some medications) need specific fluid guidance from their doctor. Dark urine is a practical sign of dehydration. Heavy exercise and heat increase fluid requirements.
Bottom line: Drink when you are thirsty. Do not ignore thirst. Dark urine is a sign to drink more. Eight glasses is a reasonable approximation for many people but has no rigorous evidence base.
"Feed a cold, starve a fever" ›
The claim: Eat more when you have a cold, eat less when you have a fever.
Grain of truth: There is some lab-based research suggesting immune responses to bacterial versus viral infections may differ in glucose metabolism.
What the evidence actually shows: No clinical trial has shown that restricting food during fever improves outcomes. The study often cited (Van den Berghe glucose-insulin trial) was in critically ill ICU patients, not people with ordinary fevers. During illness, the body's metabolic demands actually increase. Most people eat less when unwell due to reduced appetite, which is fine. Forcing food or restricting it based on symptom type has no good evidence basis.
Bottom line: Eat what you can tolerate when you are sick. Do not force food, but do not restrict it either. Stay hydrated. The saying is not backed by evidence.
"Vitamin C prevents colds" ›
The claim: Taking vitamin C supplements will prevent you from catching a cold, or reduce its severity.
Grain of truth: Vitamin C is essential for immune function, and severe deficiency (scurvy) does impair immunity.
What the evidence actually shows: The Cochrane review on vitamin C and the common cold found that regular supplementation does not reduce the incidence of colds in the general population.1 It may modestly reduce the duration of a cold by about half a day. There is some evidence for benefit in people under extreme physical stress (marathon runners, soldiers in Arctic conditions). Doses above 200mg daily are not better absorbed.
Bottom line: Vitamin C will not stop you catching a cold. It might shorten one very slightly. Get it from food (kiwifruit, capsicum, citrus) rather than supplements if possible.
"Cracking your knuckles causes arthritis" ›
The claim: If you crack your knuckles regularly, you will develop arthritis in your hands.
Grain of truth: The cracking sound is real (caused by rapid joint space expansion and cavitation of dissolved gases in synovial fluid) and it does not sound pleasant to people nearby.
What the evidence actually shows: A classic study by Dr Donald Unger (who cracked the knuckles of one hand only for 60 years and found no difference in arthritis rates) and larger observational studies have found no association between knuckle cracking and osteoarthritis. The habit may cause minor soft tissue irritation over decades, but arthritis development is not a consequence.
Bottom line: Knuckle cracking does not cause arthritis. It may be annoying to people nearby. That is probably reason enough to stop.
"Reading in dim light ruins your eyes" ›
The claim: Reading or doing close work in dim light will permanently damage your eyes.
Grain of truth: Reading in dim light does cause eye strain, fatigue, and temporary difficulty focusing.
What the evidence actually shows: Eye strain from poor lighting is real and uncomfortable, but it is transient. There is no evidence that it causes permanent damage to the eye or increases myopia (short-sightedness). Myopia development in children is influenced by genetics and time spent outdoors, not by reading in dim light. The eye recovers fully after rest.
Bottom line: Read in good light because it is more comfortable, not because dim light is permanently damaging.
"Detox diets cleanse your body of toxins" ›
The claim: Juice cleanses, lemon water, activated charcoal, or special diets can "detoxify" your body and remove accumulated poisons.
Grain of truth: The liver and kidneys do filter and remove waste products continuously. Diet can support this.
What the evidence actually shows: "Detox" is not a recognised physiological process that can be accelerated by specific foods or drinks. The liver metabolises and excretes fat-soluble toxins; the kidneys excrete water-soluble waste products. A systematic review of detox diets found no rigorous evidence of benefit.4 Juice cleanses, lemon water, and activated charcoal do not enhance the natural function of these organs. Adequate hydration, avoiding excessive alcohol, and eating enough fibre and antioxidant-rich foods genuinely support liver and kidney function, but that is not a "detox."
Bottom line: Your liver and kidneys detox you continuously and effectively. No product or protocol enhances this. Save your money.
"Breakfast is the most important meal of the day" ›
The claim: You must eat breakfast every morning or your metabolism will suffer and you will gain weight.
Grain of truth: Some studies have shown associations between breakfast consumption and lower BMI and better metabolic markers.
What the evidence actually shows: The phrase was popularised by cereal companies in early 20th-century advertising. The observational associations between breakfast and health outcomes likely reflect reverse causation (people who eat breakfast tend to have generally healthier lifestyles) rather than a direct causal benefit of breakfast itself. Randomised trials of breakfast consumption have not consistently shown weight loss benefit. Intermittent fasting, which involves skipping breakfast, has reasonable evidence for metabolic benefit in some people. Whether you eat breakfast is much less important than what you eat across the day.
Bottom line: Breakfast is not uniquely important. Eat when you are hungry. Total daily food quality matters far more than when the first meal occurs.
"You only use 10% of your brain" ›
The claim: Most people use only 10% of their brain, and unlocking the remaining 90% would enable superhuman abilities.
Grain of truth: Not all neurons fire simultaneously; different tasks use different brain regions.
What the evidence actually shows: Brain imaging studies consistently show that virtually all brain regions are active over the course of a day. Even during sleep, large portions of the brain are active. The myth appears to have multiple possible origins, including misquoted comments by William James and perhaps misinterpretations of early neuroscience. Damage to virtually any brain region produces detectable deficits, which would not be the case if 90% were unused. This myth is included here to illustrate how medical myths often arise and persist because they are a comforting idea that implies untapped human potential.
Bottom line: You use all of your brain. The 10% myth is firmly false, and it has survived largely because it appeals to our sense that we might do more.
A note on evaluating health claims online
  • Ask: is this a single study, or is it backed by systematic reviews of multiple studies? Single studies often do not replicate.
  • Ask: who funded the research? Industry-funded studies have well-documented bias.
  • Ask: does the claim have a plausible biological mechanism? Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.
  • Ask: is the person making the claim selling something? Follow the incentives.
  • Your GP or pharmacist can help you evaluate specific claims. They know your health context and can point you to reliable sources.
