<p>Glue ear (otitis media with effusion) occurs when fluid builds up and remains in the middle ear, causing temporary hearing loss. It is extremely common in young children and usually resolves on its own. This handout explains what causes glue ear, when to watch and wait, and when treatment is needed.</p>

<h2>What it is and how it affects your child</h2>

<p>Glue ear is fluid in the middle ear that causes temporary hearing loss. Children may seem inattentive, speak less clearly, or not respond to quiet sounds. It differs from acute ear infection - there is usually no ear pain or fever. Hearing improves once the fluid drains, which often happens without treatment.</p>

<h2>What it feels like and signs</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Temporary hearing loss - child does not seem to hear well</li>
  <li>Speaking less clearly or using simpler language</li>
  <li>Inattention or seeming distracted</li>
  <li>Turning up the television volume</li>
  <li>Balance problems (ear fluid affects balance in some children)</li>
  <li>Usually no pain or fever</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Glue ear usually follows repeated colds when the Eustachian tube (which normally drains fluid from the middle ear) becomes blocked or does not work properly. The middle ear fills with sticky fluid that prevents sound waves from reaching the inner ear. Most glue ear develops in winter during cold and flu season and resolves as Eustachian tube function improves.</p>

<h2>Watch and wait approach</h2>

<p>Most glue ear resolves on its own within 3 months without treatment. The recommended approach is to watch and wait, as many children recover spontaneously. During this time:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Monitor for improvement.</strong> Hearing usually improves gradually as the fluid drains.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain good communication.</strong> Face your child when speaking so they can lip-read if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Support speech development.</strong> Talk to your child frequently, use clear speech, and engage in conversation and reading.</li>
  <li><strong>Consider hearing aids temporarily if severe.</strong> If hearing loss is significantly affecting learning and your child is of school age, temporary hearing aids may help while waiting for natural resolution.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When treatment may be needed</h2>

<p>If glue ear persists beyond 3 months and is significantly affecting your child's hearing, speech, or school performance, your doctor may discuss treatment options including ear ventilation tubes (grommets). Grommets are small tubes placed through the eardrum to allow fluid to drain and air to enter the middle ear. They are temporary and usually stay in place for 6 - 12 months.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis and hearing testing</h2>

<p>Your GP can diagnose glue ear by examining the eardrum with an otoscope. If glue ear is suspected, your GP may refer your child for hearing tests to assess the degree of hearing loss. Children with school-related concerns should have formal audiometry testing.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You notice your child seems to have hearing loss</li>
  <li>Your child's speech development seems delayed</li>
  <li>You are concerned about school performance or learning</li>
  <li>Your child has had repeated ear infections followed by prolonged hearing loss</li>
  <li>You want hearing testing to assess the degree of loss</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Does my child definitely have glue ear?</li>
  <li>Should my child have hearing tests?</li>
  <li>How long does it usually take to resolve?</li>
  <li>What can I do to support my child's hearing and speech?</li>
  <li>When would grommets be considered?</li>
  <li>Is there anything that can prevent glue ear?</li>
</ul>
