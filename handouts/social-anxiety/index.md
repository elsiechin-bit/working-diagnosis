<p>Social anxiety is the fear of being judged, embarrassed, or humiliated in social or performance situations. It is one of the most common mental health conditions in NZ, affecting around 8% of adults at any time and around 13% over a lifetime.[1] It is also one of the most under-recognised - sufferers tend to attribute it to being "shy" or "an introvert" and miss that effective treatment exists.</p>

<h2>What it is - and what it is not</h2>

<h3>What it is</h3>
<p>Persistent, excessive fear of one or more social situations where you might be judged. The fear feels out of proportion to the actual situation. It interferes with what you want to do.</p>

<p>Common feared situations:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Speaking in groups, meetings, presentations</li>
  <li>Eating or drinking in front of others</li>
  <li>Using public toilets</li>
  <li>Phone calls, especially to strangers</li>
  <li>Parties, social events</li>
  <li>Dating, romantic interactions</li>
  <li>Authority figures</li>
  <li>Being the centre of attention</li>
</ul>

<p>Physical symptoms when in (or anticipating) the feared situation: blushing, sweating, trembling, rapid heart rate, dry mouth, nausea, mind going blank.</p>

<h3>What it is NOT</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Shyness</strong> - shyness is a temperament, often pleasant or quiet. It does not interfere with what you want to do.</li>
  <li><strong>Introversion</strong> - introverts find social events tiring. Socially anxious people fear them, often avoid them, and feel terrible if they cannot.</li>
  <li><strong>Being self-aware</strong> - everyone gets a bit nervous before a presentation. Social anxiety is when the nervousness escalates to incapacitating, the avoidance is significant, and your life shrinks around it.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The cycle that keeps it going</h2>

<p>Social anxiety is fundamentally a vicious cycle. It works like this:</p>

<ol>
  <li>You fear that you will be judged in a situation</li>
  <li>You avoid the situation, or push through with intense distress</li>
  <li>Avoidance feels like relief in the moment</li>
  <li>The brain "learns" that the situation IS dangerous, since you escaped it</li>
  <li>The next time the situation appears, the fear is bigger</li>
  <li>The avoidance becomes more entrenched, and your life shrinks</li>
</ol>

<p>Within feared situations, "safety behaviours" play a similar role - holding the cup with two hands so it does not shake, rehearsing every word in your head, looking at your phone to avoid eye contact. They feel like they help. They prevent the brain from learning the situation was actually OK.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)</h3>
<p>The treatment with the strongest evidence. Around 60-75% of people are significantly improved after a course (10-15 sessions).[2] Two main parts:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cognitive work</strong> - identifying and updating beliefs that drive anxiety. Common beliefs: "people are watching me", "if I blush they will judge me", "I will say something stupid". Therapist helps you test these against reality.</li>
  <li><strong>Behavioural exposure</strong> - graduated practice in feared situations, ideally without safety behaviours. Starts small (saying hello to a stranger), builds to bigger (giving a 2-minute speech to a friendly group, then a less friendly one). The key is repetition - the brain needs to see the feared outcome NOT happen, repeatedly, before fear extinguishes.</li>
</ul>

<p>Available free in NZ via:</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/courses/social-anxiety">Just A Thought - Social Anxiety course</a> - 8 modules, NZ-funded, free</li>
  <li>Brief intervention counselling at most NZ GP practices</li>
  <li>Private therapists - around NZ$120-200 per session</li>
</ul>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram, paroxetine) are first-line. Around 50-60% of people respond meaningfully. Used alongside or in place of CBT, depending on severity and preference. Effects build over 4-6 weeks. Talk to your GP.</p>

<p>Benzodiazepines and beta-blockers are sometimes used as needed for performance situations (a single propranolol 30 minutes before a presentation, for instance). Useful as a bridge but not a long-term solution because they prevent the brain from learning the situation was OK.</p>

<h3>Lifestyle</h3>
<p>Less powerful than CBT or medication, but adjuncts:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Caffeine reduction - amplifies physical anxiety</li>
  <li>Alcohol reduction - reduces anxiety in the moment but worsens it over hours and reinforces avoidance</li>
  <li>Regular exercise - reduces baseline anxiety</li>
  <li>Mindfulness practice - shifts attention from internal monitoring to external focus</li>
</ul>

<h2>The role of avoidance</h2>

<p>The most useful single insight in social anxiety: avoidance is the engine. Every time you avoid a feared situation, the fear gets bigger. Every time you go in despite the fear, the fear gets a little smaller.</p>

<p>This means the path out of social anxiety is the opposite direction from where the anxiety wants you to go. It is uncomfortable. It works.</p>

<p>You do not have to do the most feared thing first. Build a hierarchy from "mildly uncomfortable" (asking a stranger for the time) to "deeply feared" (giving a public speech). Work up the ladder. Each rung is repeated until it stops being scary, then move on.</p>

<h2>Variations that are worth knowing</h2>

<h3>Performance-only social anxiety</h3>
<p>Public speaking, music performance, sport. Rest of social life is fine. Often responds well to short courses of CBT plus performance strategies (breathing, beta-blockers as needed).</p>

<h3>Generalised social anxiety</h3>
<p>Most or all social situations are difficult. More likely to need extended treatment. Often co-occurs with depression, alcohol use, or avoidant personality traits.</p>

<h3>Selective mutism in children</h3>
<p>Child speaks comfortably at home but is silent in school or other settings. Specific to socially anxious children. Treatable; specialist referral worth pursuing.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Anxiety in social situations that interferes with work, study, relationships, or things you want to do</li>
  <li>Avoiding situations that matter to you</li>
  <li>Using alcohol to manage social events</li>
  <li>Symptoms have lasted more than 6 months</li>
  <li>Co-existing low mood, panic, or other concerns</li>
  <li>Children or adolescents with significant withdrawal or selective mutism</li>
</ul>
