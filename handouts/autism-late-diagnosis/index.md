<p>For most of the last fifty years, autism was understood as a condition of children, especially boys, with significant impairment. The understanding has shifted dramatically. Many adults - particularly women, people who learned to mask their differences, and people whose autism overlapped with other conditions - reach 30, 40, 50, even 70 before recognising they are autistic. This handout is for those people: those who suspect, those just diagnosed, those whose family member has just been diagnosed. It walks through what late diagnosis is like, how to get assessed in NZ, and what tends to follow.</p>

<h2>What autism is</h2>

<p>Autism is a neurodevelopmental difference - a different way the brain takes in, processes, and responds to information about the social world, sensory environment, and patterns. Two main areas of difference are required for diagnosis:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Social communication and interaction</strong> - including reciprocal social-emotional exchange, non-verbal communication, developing relationships</li>
  <li><strong>Restricted, repetitive patterns of behaviour, interests, or activities</strong> - including stereotyped movements, insistence on sameness, intense special interests, sensory differences</li>
</ul>

<p>The features must be present from early development and cause clinically significant impact. Autism is not a disease and there is no "cure" - it is a different neurology. The aim of diagnosis and support is enabling autistic adults to live well as themselves, not to make them less autistic.</p>

<h2>What late diagnosis often looks like</h2>

<p>Adults coming to autism assessment in mid-life often share patterns:</p>

<ul>
  <li>"I have always felt different but could not name how"</li>
  <li>"Social situations exhaust me in a way that confuses people"</li>
  <li>"I have intense interests and lose myself in them, sometimes to the exclusion of other things"</li>
  <li>"I can mask in short bursts but pay for it with days of recovery"</li>
  <li>"Sensory things matter to me a lot - lights, sounds, fabrics, smells"</li>
  <li>"I prefer routine. Disruption is destabilising."</li>
  <li>"I find unwritten social rules confusing - what is meant rather than said"</li>
  <li>"I have always struggled with executive function - planning, transitions, starting things"</li>
  <li>"I have a history of anxiety, depression, eating disorders, or burnout that did not fully fit"</li>
  <li>"I had a child diagnosed and recognised myself in the assessment"</li>
</ul>

<p>Common backgrounds: high academic achievement masking the differences; or, conversely, school refusal or social isolation that was attributed to other things. Many have had multiple previous mental health diagnoses (anxiety, depression, OCD, EUPD/BPD) that captured part but not all of the picture.</p>

<h2>Why late diagnosis matters</h2>

<p>Some people ask if it is worth pursuing diagnosis as an adult. Reasons people give for valuing it:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Self-understanding.</strong> Recognition of a lifelong pattern often re-frames a lifetime of unexplained struggle.</li>
  <li><strong>Self-compassion.</strong> "I was not lazy or weak; I was working harder than people knew."</li>
  <li><strong>Better support.</strong> Workplace adjustments, therapy that takes autism into account, communication with healthcare that fits.</li>
  <li><strong>Different relationship with limits.</strong> Permission to honour sensory needs, downtime, and pace.</li>
  <li><strong>Community.</strong> Connection with autistic adults online and in NZ.</li>
  <li><strong>Help for co-existing conditions.</strong> Many anxiety and depression presentations respond differently when autism is recognised.</li>
</ul>

<p>Reasons people say no: cost of formal assessment, fear of disclosure, comfort with self-identification without formal diagnosis. Self-identification is increasingly recognised as legitimate by the autistic community when formal assessment is inaccessible.</p>

<h2>Getting assessed in NZ</h2>

<p>Adult autism assessment is currently fragmented in NZ. As of 2024-2025:</p>

<h3>Public services</h3>
<p>Most DHB mental health services do not assess autism in adults unless there is significant co-existing mental illness. Some regions have neurodevelopmental services that include adults; many do not. Your GP can check what is available locally.</p>

<h3>Private assessment</h3>
<p>Most adult diagnoses in NZ are made privately. Common providers:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Clinical psychologists with specific autism training - around NZ$1,800-3,500 for full assessment</li>
  <li>Psychiatrists - particularly useful where co-existing mental health conditions need consideration</li>
  <li>Some occupational therapists provide autism-informed assessments</li>
</ul>

<p>Wait times vary widely; some clinicians have 12+ month lists, others can see new clients within weeks. Online or hybrid assessments are now common.</p>

<h3>What the assessment involves</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Detailed developmental history (childhood, school, social development)</li>
  <li>Family interview where possible</li>
  <li>Structured assessments such as ADOS-2 (observation), ADI-R (parent interview), or AAA</li>
  <li>Self-report screening measures (AQ, RAADS-R, CAT-Q for masking)</li>
  <li>Cognitive testing in some cases</li>
  <li>Screening for co-existing conditions - ADHD, anxiety, depression, OCD, eating disorders</li>
  <li>Multiple sessions usually, totalling 4-8 hours of contact</li>
</ul>

<h2>What comes next after diagnosis</h2>

<h3>The first months</h3>
<p>Many newly-diagnosed adults describe a period of re-evaluation - revisiting school memories, relationship patterns, work history through a new lens. Common emotions: relief, grief, anger, validation, confusion. Therapy with an autism-informed therapist can help. The autistic adult community (online groups, NZ Autistic NZ) is often a powerful resource.</p>

<h3>Practical things</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Workplace adjustments</strong> - protected breaks, sensory accommodations, written-rather-than-verbal instructions, flexible schedule. NZ Human Rights Act includes neurodiversity in disability discrimination protection.</li>
  <li><strong>Healthcare communication</strong> - tell your GP, dentist, anaesthetist. Many will adjust how they communicate. Some people use written communication or scripts.</li>
  <li><strong>Sensory environment</strong> - permission to manage noise, lights, fabrics, smells</li>
  <li><strong>Social rules clarified</strong> - "decoding" books and resources are useful, as is working with a therapist who can name unwritten rules</li>
  <li><strong>Co-existing conditions addressed</strong> - many improve when autism context is acknowledged</li>
</ul>

<h2>Important things to know</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Autistic women, gender-diverse people, and people of colour have historically been under-diagnosed because most diagnostic criteria were derived from young white boys.</li>
  <li>"Masking" - the conscious or unconscious suppression of autistic traits to fit in - is exhausting and is a major contributor to burnout, anxiety, and depression in autistic adults. Reducing masking is part of recovery.</li>
  <li>Autism does not get worse with age, but autistic burnout is real - extended period of significant fatigue, increased difficulty with sensory and social demands, often after a sustained period of masking. Recovery requires reduced demand.</li>
  <li>Co-existing ADHD is very common (estimates 30-50%). Both can be addressed.</li>
  <li>Autism does not mean lower intelligence. The conflation of autism with intellectual disability is historic and inaccurate.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is NOT a sign of autism on its own</h2>

<p>Social media has expanded awareness usefully but also produced a lot of "if you do this you might be autistic" content. Single traits (preferring routine, being introverted, finding parties tiring, having focused interests) are common in non-autistic people too. Autism is a constellation of traits across multiple domains, present from early development, with persistent significant impact. If you suspect, formal assessment is the way to find out.</p>
