<p>Adult ADHD is one of the most under-diagnosed mental health conditions in NZ. International estimates suggest around 4-5% of adults meet criteria, and most of them have not been diagnosed.[1] The picture in NZ is similar. Awareness has shifted dramatically in the last few years - which is good news (more people getting help) but has also produced long waitlists, a lot of confusing information online, and a real distinction between adult ADHD as a clinical condition and "ADHD content" as social media wellness category. This handout walks through what adult ADHD looks like, how to get assessed in NZ in 2024-2025, and what to expect.</p>

<h2>What adult ADHD actually is</h2>

<p>ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition involving differences in executive function - the brain's system for prioritising, planning, sustaining attention, regulating impulses, and managing emotion. It is not a deficit of attention; it is a difficulty with regulating attention. The brain is plenty active, just often in directions that are not strategically chosen.</p>

<p>It starts in childhood (by definition) but presents differently in adults than children:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Hyperactivity often becomes internal restlessness rather than visible movement</li>
  <li>Attention problems are masked by intelligence and effort, until demands exceed capacity</li>
  <li>Emotional dysregulation is often the most prominent adult feature</li>
  <li>Time blindness, difficulty starting tasks, and chronic lateness are common</li>
  <li>"Rejection sensitive dysphoria" - intense reaction to perceived criticism or rejection - is a recognised feature</li>
</ul>

<h2>How it shows up in adults</h2>

<h3>The internal experience</h3>
<ul>
  <li>"I cannot start things even though I want to."</li>
  <li>"I lose interest the moment something stops being novel."</li>
  <li>"My brain is louder than other people's."</li>
  <li>"I can hyper-focus on the wrong things and not the right ones."</li>
  <li>"My emotions are bigger and faster than the situation."</li>
  <li>"I fail at things I clearly know how to do."</li>
  <li>"I have lived my whole life feeling like I am working three times as hard as other people for the same result."</li>
</ul>

<h3>Common life patterns</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Chronic underachievement relative to potential</li>
  <li>Multiple unfinished projects, half-renovations, abandoned hobbies</li>
  <li>Job changes every 2-3 years</li>
  <li>Relationship friction around time, attention, follow-through</li>
  <li>Chronic lateness or, conversely, manic over-preparation to avoid being late</li>
  <li>Money problems out of proportion to income (impulse spending, missed bills)</li>
  <li>Higher rates of anxiety, depression, addiction</li>
  <li>Higher rates of car accidents, injuries, ED visits</li>
</ul>

<p>Many adults manage well in their 20s through coping strategies and structure provided by external systems (school, parents). The real cracks often appear when those external structures fall away (university, parenthood, self-employment, leadership roles).</p>

<h2>Getting assessed in NZ</h2>

<p>The system as of 2024-2025:</p>

<h3>Step 1: see your GP</h3>
<p>Tell your GP what you are noticing. They will check for things that look like ADHD (anxiety, depression, sleep apnoea, thyroid, alcohol use, side effects of medication, postnatal cognitive change). For most adults, ADHD is not the only explanation - it is a layer alongside other things.</p>

<p>Bring with you: a written list of symptoms, examples of how they affect your life, anything you remember from childhood, family history of ADHD, autism, or anxiety/depression.</p>

<h3>Step 2: formal assessment</h3>

<p>In NZ, ADHD diagnosis must be made by a psychiatrist (or, for children, a paediatrician). Three pathways:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Public mental health services</strong> via your DHB - free, but only available for people with significant impairment AND co-existing major mental health concerns. Many DHBs decline ADHD-only referrals. Wait times where accepted are typically 6-18 months.</li>
  <li><strong>Private psychiatry</strong> - around NZ$1,200-2,500 for a comprehensive assessment. Wait times currently 6-12 months for established services. Some clinicians do online assessments via video.</li>
  <li><strong>Some workplace EAP</strong> programmes will fund psychological assessment and refer for psychiatric review.</li>
</ul>

<p>The assessment includes structured interview, often a self-report questionnaire (DIVA-5 or CAADID), childhood corroboration where possible (school reports, family interview), and screening for differential diagnoses.</p>

<h3>Step 3: treatment plan</h3>

<p>If diagnosed, your psychiatrist sets up the treatment plan and writes the initial scripts. Stimulant medication (methylphenidate, dexamphetamine, lisdexamfetamine) is "Restricted Specialist Authority" in NZ - meaning the initial prescription must come from a psychiatrist or paediatrician. Once stabilised, your GP can typically continue the prescriptions, sometimes with annual psychiatry review.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>Stimulants are first-line and effective in 60-70% of adults with ADHD, with effect sizes among the largest in psychiatry.[2]</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Methylphenidate</strong> - immediate-release (Rubifen) and long-acting (Concerta, Ritalin LA). Funded in NZ.</li>
  <li><strong>Dexamphetamine</strong> - immediate-release. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Lisdexamfetamine</strong> (Vyvanse) - long-acting prodrug. Funded since recent years.</li>
  <li><strong>Atomoxetine</strong> (Strattera) - non-stimulant, funded with Special Authority. Useful when stimulants are not tolerated or contraindicated.</li>
  <li><strong>Guanfacine</strong> - non-stimulant, funded for children, off-label for adults.</li>
</ul>

<p>Most people start low and titrate up over weeks. Side effects to watch: appetite reduction, sleep disturbance if dosed too late, increased heart rate, occasionally mood changes. Cardiovascular history should be reviewed before starting; ECG is sometimes performed.</p>

<h3>Non-medication strategies</h3>

<p>Medication is the most effective single intervention but works best alongside structural and skill-based supports:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>External structure</strong> - calendars, alarms, body doubling (working alongside someone), accountability partners</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce environmental friction</strong> - layout, visible reminders, automation</li>
  <li><strong>CBT for ADHD</strong> - specific therapy targeting executive function difficulties</li>
  <li><strong>ADHD coaching</strong> - structured support around goal-setting and follow-through</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep, exercise, food</strong> - significantly affect symptoms; not optional</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specific things people ask</h2>

<h3>I scored high on an online ADHD test - does that mean I have it?</h3>
<p>No. Self-report questionnaires (ASRS, ADHD-RS) are screening tools - they identify who needs proper assessment. They do not diagnose. Many other things produce similar scores - anxiety, depression, autism, sleep disorders, hypothyroidism, perimenopause.</p>

<h3>What about ADHD content on TikTok / Instagram?</h3>
<p>Social media has been good for awareness and bad for diagnostic accuracy. The list of "things that might mean you have ADHD" online includes most experiences common to all humans. If your symptoms have caused real, persistent, significant impairment since childhood, ADHD is plausible. If they appeared recently, are mild, or fluctuate, other things are more likely.</p>

<h3>Should I just self-medicate to see if it helps?</h3>
<p>No - and a stimulant trial without a diagnosis does not actually tell you what you think it does. Stimulants improve focus, energy, and motivation in nearly everyone. Whether they help with ADHD is more about whether they normalise function than whether they "work".</p>

<h3>Will medication change who I am?</h3>
<p>Most people describe medication as turning down the noise so they can be themselves. Some describe it as making the things they wanted to do possible. A minority do not tolerate it. The effect is meant to be subtle - if you feel transformed or zombied, the dose is wrong.</p>
