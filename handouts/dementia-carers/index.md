<p>Looking after someone with dementia is one of the hardest jobs there is, and most carers do it without training. This handout is a practical companion: things that tend to help, things that tend to make it worse, what to expect, and how to get the help you are entitled to in NZ. None of it replaces the conversations you will have with your GP, the dementia adviser, and the family. It is a starting point.</p>

<h2>Communication</h2>

<p>The single most useful skill is to slow down. Dementia narrows the bandwidth of what someone can take in at once. The following helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>One question at a time. Wait for an answer before adding the next one.</li>
  <li>Short sentences. Familiar words. The point first, the qualifier second.</li>
  <li>Eye contact, calm voice, slow pace.</li>
  <li>Use names, not pronouns. "Your daughter Sarah is on the phone" is clearer than "she is on the phone".</li>
  <li>Reduce competing input. Turn down the radio. Turn the TV off. Move to a quieter room.</li>
  <li>Closed questions become easier than open ones. "Would you like tea or coffee?" rather than "What would you like?"</li>
</ul>

<h2>The hardest moments</h2>

<h3>Memory loss with confabulation</h3>
<p>People with dementia often fill in the gaps in their memory with stories that feel real to them. Arguing about whether something happened is rarely helpful. The general rule is: respond to the emotion, not the factual error. "You sound worried about your mother" rather than "Your mother died in 1998".</p>

<h3>Repetition</h3>
<p>The same question, sometimes every minute. They are not testing you. They have no memory of having asked. Answer the same way each time, calmly. Some carers write a single answer on a card and gently point to it.</p>

<h3>Sundowning</h3>
<p>Increased confusion, restlessness, and agitation in late afternoon and early evening. Common; not yet fully understood. What helps: keep a steady routine, ensure good light through the day, reduce stimulation in the evening, address pain or hunger or full bladder, screen for urinary infection if sudden change.</p>

<h3>Refusing personal care</h3>
<p>Bathing, dressing, and toileting can become flashpoints. Often the issue is not the task but the loss of dignity, the cold, the strangeness of the bathroom, or pain when moving. Try: warmth, towel modesty, a slower pace, music, a simpler routine, doing the task at the time of day when they are most settled. Sometimes a different carer or a paid caregiver they have built rapport with can succeed where family cannot.</p>

<h3>Aggression</h3>
<p>Often a symptom of unmet need - pain, fear, hunger, full bladder, an environment that does not make sense. Ask: is something hurting? Is the place strange? Has the routine changed? Has there been a recent infection or new medication? Walk away briefly if it is safe to do so. If aggression is new or escalating, see your GP - check for delirium, urinary infection, constipation, or a medication side effect.</p>

<h3>Wandering and getting lost</h3>
<p>Common in mid-stage dementia. Practical safeguards: identification (a wallet card, a medical alert bracelet), GPS-enabled phones or trackers, telling neighbours, latches that are not in the line of sight at the front door, an absent-minded purposeful walk routine that you do together each day so the urge is met. WanderSearch is a free national service in NZ that registers vulnerable people for rapid response.</p>

<h2>Safety at home</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Remove or store medicines, sharp objects, and harmful chemicals where they cannot be reached or mistaken for food</li>
  <li>Turn the hot water cylinder thermostat down to prevent scalds</li>
  <li>Stove safety: gas isolation valves or induction cooktops are safer than gas hobs left on</li>
  <li>Front door locks that need a key from the inside (in some jurisdictions; check fire safety advice)</li>
  <li>Good lighting, especially at night</li>
  <li>Falls safety - see the separate falls prevention handout</li>
  <li>Driving conversation - see the separate driving handout</li>
</ul>

<h2>Daily routine</h2>

<p>Predictable rhythm is the single most calming intervention. The same wake time, the same meal times, the same activities at the same time of day. Big changes - holidays, hospital admissions, moving house - often cause a noticeable drop in function that takes weeks to recover.</p>

<h2>Looking after yourself</h2>

<p>Carer burnout is real and predictable. Roughly half of dementia carers will develop clinically significant depression at some point during their caring role.[1] You cannot care for someone else if your own tank is empty.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Build in regular breaks. Even an hour a week. Build them in before you need them.</li>
  <li>Accept respite care offers. Your relationship with the person you care for is more important than being the only one who can do the caring.</li>
  <li>Carer support groups - the Alzheimers NZ regional offices run them, in person and online. Other carers understand things others do not.</li>
  <li>Tell your GP how you are coping. Carer mental health is part of their job too.</li>
  <li>Sleep, eating, exercise, friendships - protect them.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The NZ support system</h2>

<h3>Needs Assessment Service Coordination (NASC)</h3>
<p>The gateway to government-funded home-based support and respite care. Your GP can refer or you can self-refer. NASC will assess what services the person can access and at what level. Worth doing earlier rather than waiting for crisis.</p>

<h3>Home-based support services</h3>
<p>Personal care (showering, dressing), household management (cleaning, meal preparation), and respite care can all be funded depending on the assessment. Providers are contracted by the local DHB.</p>

<h3>Respite care</h3>
<p>Either day respite (someone comes to you, or the person attends a day programme), or short-stay respite (the person stays in a rest home for a few nights to two weeks while you have a break). See the separate handout on respite care.</p>

<h3>Carer Support subsidy</h3>
<p>A government subsidy that pays for someone else to take over for some hours per year. The amount is set by NASC. Often underused because people do not know about it.</p>

<h3>Disability Allowance</h3>
<p>Through Work and Income, can help with the extra costs that come with caring (transport, equipment, additional household help).</p>

<h2>Looking ahead</h2>

<p>At some point, home care may no longer be safe or sustainable. Knowing the signs that this point is approaching helps you plan rather than react in crisis. They include: worsening physical care needs that exceed what you can provide; multiple falls; significant aggression or wandering; the carer becoming exhausted, ill, or socially isolated. The separate handout on moving into a rest home covers what to expect from that step.</p>
