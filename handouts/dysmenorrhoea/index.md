<p>Roughly half to three-quarters of women experience painful periods at some point, and around 10-20% experience pain severe enough to interfere with daily life.[1] Period pain that responds to ibuprofen is annoying but normal. Period pain that has you off school, off work, in tears, vomiting, or sending you to the emergency department is not normal and deserves investigation. This handout walks through what causes period pain, what to try, and when to push for more.</p>

<h2>Two main types</h2>

<h3>Primary dysmenorrhoea</h3>
<p>Period pain that is not caused by another condition. The pain comes from the uterus contracting strongly during menstruation, driven by chemicals called prostaglandins. Typically:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Starts within 1-2 years of first period</li>
  <li>Begins shortly before or with the period and lasts 1-3 days</li>
  <li>Cramping pain in the lower abdomen, sometimes radiating to back or thighs</li>
  <li>May come with nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, headache, fatigue</li>
  <li>Tends to settle with age and after childbirth</li>
  <li>Responds to anti-inflammatory medicines (ibuprofen, naproxen, mefenamic acid)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Secondary dysmenorrhoea</h3>
<p>Period pain caused by something specific. Suspected when:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pain starts later in life (after years of normal periods)</li>
  <li>Pain is severe, getting worse, or different in character</li>
  <li>Pain occurs at other times of the cycle (not just during the period)</li>
  <li>There are other symptoms - heavy bleeding, painful sex, painful bowel motions, infertility</li>
  <li>Standard pain relief stops working</li>
</ul>

<p>The most common causes of secondary dysmenorrhoea are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Endometriosis</strong> - tissue similar to the uterine lining growing elsewhere in the pelvis. Often diagnosed late. See the separate endometriosis handout.</li>
  <li><strong>Adenomyosis</strong> - similar tissue growing INTO the wall of the uterus. Can cause heavy and painful periods.</li>
  <li><strong>Fibroids</strong> - benign muscle growths in the uterus. Particularly common as women approach 40.</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic inflammatory disease</strong> - infection of the upper genital tract.</li>
  <li><strong>Ovarian cysts</strong> - usually benign but sometimes painful.</li>
  <li><strong>IUD</strong> - copper IUDs commonly increase period pain in the first 3-6 months; if persistent and severe, consider removal.</li>
  <li><strong>Cervical stenosis</strong> - narrowed cervical opening, sometimes after surgery or treatment.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to try at home</h2>

<h3>Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)</h3>
<p>The most effective non-prescription option. NSAIDs block the prostaglandins that cause uterine cramping. They work best when started before pain becomes severe - either at the first sign of pain or, even better, the day before bleeding is expected.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mefenamic acid (Ponstan)</strong> - 500mg three times a day with food. Works particularly well for period pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Naproxen</strong> - 250-500mg twice a day. Longer-lasting than ibuprofen.</li>
  <li><strong>Ibuprofen</strong> - 400-600mg every 6-8 hours with food. Widely available.</li>
</ul>

<p>NSAIDs are not safe for everyone - avoid if you have asthma flared by NSAIDs, peptic ulcer, kidney disease, are pregnant, or have certain other conditions. Check with your pharmacist or GP if unsure. Do not take NSAIDs for more than the days of your period unless directed.</p>

<h3>Heat</h3>
<p>Hot water bottle or heat pack on the lower abdomen. Genuinely effective - similar evidence to ibuprofen in some trials.[2]</p>

<h3>Movement</h3>
<p>Gentle movement, walking, yoga, light stretching often helps more than rest. Vigorous exercise during peak pain is uncomfortable for many but in general, women who are physically active have less period pain on average.</p>

<h3>TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)</h3>
<p>Small portable device delivering mild electrical impulses through skin patches. Effective for many. Available from pharmacies for around NZ$80-150.</p>

<h3>Diet and supplements</h3>
<p>Modest evidence for:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Magnesium 200-400mg daily for the few days before and during period</li>
  <li>Omega-3 fatty acids</li>
  <li>Reducing caffeine, alcohol, and salt around your period</li>
</ul>

<p>Less robust evidence for: vitamin B1, vitamin E, ginger, fennel. Worth knowing: some popular dietary advice for period pain has weak evidence. Try one thing at a time and see what helps you.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is severe enough to take you off school, work, or normal activities</li>
  <li>Standard pain relief is not enough</li>
  <li>Pain is getting worse over time</li>
  <li>Pain has started after years of normal periods</li>
  <li>Pain occurs at other times in the cycle (not just during periods)</li>
  <li>Pain with sex (deep dyspareunia)</li>
  <li>Pain with bowel motions, especially around periods</li>
  <li>Heavy bleeding (changing protection more than every 2 hours; clots; bleeding through clothes)</li>
  <li>Difficulty conceiving</li>
  <li>Symptoms suggesting infection - fever, smelly discharge, pelvic tenderness</li>
</ul>

<h2>What your GP might suggest</h2>

<h3>Hormonal contraception</h3>
<p>Often dramatically helpful. Options include:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Combined pill, used continuously</strong> (skip the placebo week) - reduces period frequency and severity</li>
  <li><strong>Hormonal IUD (Mirena)</strong> - often makes periods very light or stops them; especially useful if heavy bleeding too. Fully funded in NZ.</li>
  <li><strong>Progestogen-only methods</strong> - depot, mini-pill, implant</li>
</ul>

<h3>Investigations</h3>
<p>If pain pattern suggests secondary dysmenorrhoea, expect:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pelvic examination</li>
  <li>STI screen (chlamydia, gonorrhoea)</li>
  <li>Pelvic ultrasound - looking for fibroids, adenomyosis, ovarian cysts, endometriomas</li>
  <li>Sometimes MRI for deep endometriosis</li>
  <li>Sometimes laparoscopy (specialist referral) - the only way to definitively diagnose endometriosis</li>
</ul>

<h3>Specialist referral</h3>
<p>For pain that has not responded to combined hormonal management, suspected endometriosis or adenomyosis, fertility issues, or for surgical evaluation.</p>

<h2>What is normal vs not</h2>

<p>This is the question patients most often want answered. Roughly:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Normal:</strong> mild to moderate cramping for the first 1-2 days, controlled by ibuprofen or paracetamol; able to continue your usual activities</li>
  <li><strong>Borderline:</strong> need stronger pain relief, take a day off school or work, recover in a day or two</li>
  <li><strong>Not normal:</strong> regularly bedridden, vomiting from pain, going to ED for pain, missing more than a day or two each cycle, pain getting worse rather than better, pain at other times of the cycle</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are in the "not normal" range, please do not accept "it is just period pain". Most secondary causes are diagnosable and treatable, and the earlier you start, the better.</p>
