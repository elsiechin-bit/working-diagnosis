<p>Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a condition where your immune system attacks the insulation around nerve fibres, causing inflammation and damage. It can cause widely varying symptoms and disability. The good news is that disease-modifying medicines can slow progression, and many strategies help manage symptoms and maintain quality of life.</p>

<h2>What MS is and how it develops</h2>

<p>MS occurs when your immune system attacks myelin (the insulation around nerve fibres). This causes inflammation and disrupts nerve signals. MS can relapse and remit (symptoms come and go), or progress gradually. Most people experience relapsing-remitting MS initially, where symptoms flare unpredictably then improve. Some transition to secondary progressive MS, where progression is more gradual. A small number have primary progressive MS from onset.</p>

<h2>Symptoms vary widely between people</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Vision problems - blurred vision, eye pain, loss of colour vision (optic neuritis)</li>
  <li>Weakness and numbness - affecting limbs, typically one side initially</li>
  <li>Balance problems and vertigo - dizziness, loss of coordination</li>
  <li>Fatigue - often the most disabling symptom, not relieved by rest</li>
  <li>Cognitive changes - difficulty concentrating, memory problems, brain fog</li>
  <li>Bladder and bowel problems - urgency, frequency, or retention</li>
  <li>Pain - neuropathic pain, spasticity, muscle tightness</li>
  <li>Mood changes - depression, anxiety, emotional lability</li>
</ul>

<h2>Disease-modifying medicines - they matter</h2>

<p>Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) reduce relapse frequency and slow disease progression. Starting early makes a significant difference. DMTs include interferons, glatiramer acetate, fingolimod, natalizumab, and ocrelizumab. Newer medicines are often more effective but require closer monitoring. Your neurologist will choose based on disease activity, your relapse history, and side effect profile. The goal is to get to no evidence of disease activity (NEDA).</p>

<h2>Managing relapses</h2>

<p>A relapse is new symptoms or worsening of existing symptoms lasting more than 24 hours. Most relapses improve partially or fully over weeks to months. High-dose intravenous methylprednisolone speeds recovery of vision and neurological function. If you have sudden vision loss, weakness, or neurological changes, contact your neurologist urgently. Early treatment of relapses reduces long-term disability.</p>

<h2>Symptom management - practical strategies</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Address fatigue specifically.</strong> Fatigue is different from tiredness. Pacing activity, rest periods, sleep optimisation, and specific medicines (amantadine, modafinil) can help. Physiotherapy and occupational therapy teach energy-conservation techniques.</li>
  <li><strong>Physical activity and physiotherapy.</strong> Despite fatigue, regular gentle activity helps prevent deconditioning and improves mood. Swimming, tai chi, or walking are often well tolerated. Physiotherapy helps with balance, strength, and spasticity.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage spasticity and pain.</strong> Stretching, heat, and medicines like baclofen reduce muscle tightness. Neuropathic pain responds to gabapentin or pregabalin.</li>
  <li><strong>Bladder and bowel management.</strong> Intermittent self-catheterisation, timed toileting, and stool softeners manage these troublesome symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Cognitive support.</strong> Simplify routines, use lists and reminders, and address sleep problems (which worsen cognitive function).</li>
  <li><strong>Address mood and anxiety.</strong> Depression is common and treatable. Counselling and antidepressants help.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain social and occupational role.</strong> Staying engaged helps quality of life and mood. Adjust rather than abandon activities.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Planning long-term</h2>

<p>MS is unpredictable. Some people remain relatively stable for decades; others accumulate disability more quickly. Planning includes discussing mobility aids early, home modifications, workplace adjustments, and career planning. Occupational therapists help with practical strategies. Psychological support helps adapt to uncertainty and ongoing disability.</p>

<h2>When to see your neurologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>To establish diagnosis and start disease-modifying therapy</li>
  <li>During relapses for urgent treatment</li>
  <li>To review and adjust disease-modifying medicines annually</li>
  <li>If new symptoms develop</li>
  <li>To manage symptoms that worsen despite DMT</li>
  <li>To discuss relapse frequency and disease progression</li>
  <li>If side effects from medicines develop</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your neurologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What type of MS do I have?</li>
  <li>Which disease-modifying medicine should I start?</li>
  <li>How will we know if the medicine is working?</li>
  <li>What are the side effects and monitoring requirements?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist or occupational therapist?</li>
  <li>What triggers relapses, and what should I do if I suspect a relapse?</li>
  <li>How will MS likely affect my work, family, and activities?</li>
</ul>
