<p>In September 2023, NZ moved to a new way of screening for cervical cancer. The old smear test (looking at cells under a microscope) is being replaced by an HPV test, which checks for the virus that causes nearly all cervical cancers. The HPV test is more accurate, can be done less often, and has a self-test option that does not require a speculum examination. This handout walks through what is now offered, what your result means, and the things people most often ask.</p>

<h2>Why we screen at all</h2>

<p>Cervical cancer is one of the most preventable cancers we know of. It is caused, in nearly all cases, by persistent infection with high-risk types of human papillomavirus (HPV). Most people who get HPV clear it without ever knowing they had it. A small minority do not, and over years that persistent infection can cause cell changes that, if untreated, become cancer.</p>

<p>Screening catches this process at the precancer stage, when treatment is simple. NZ has had cervical screening since 1990, and the cervical cancer death rate has fallen by more than 60% since then.[1] The 2023 change is expected to prevent another 400 cases of invasive cancer over the next 20 years.</p>

<h2>Who is eligible</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Anyone with a cervix aged 25 to 69 (this includes trans and non-binary people who have a cervix)</li>
  <li>Free for Maori, Pacific peoples, community service card holders, those previously unscreened or under-screened, and several other groups. Check the eligibility list at <a href="https://www.timetoscreen.nz/cervical-screening/">timetoscreen.nz</a></li>
  <li>Free for everyone aged 30 to 69 since the new programme launched (regardless of group)</li>
  <li>If you are not in a free-eligibility group, the cost is around NZ$30-60 depending on your practice</li>
</ul>

<p>Screening starts at age 25 even if you have had the HPV vaccine. The vaccine reduces but does not eliminate cervical cancer risk.</p>

<h2>How often</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Every 5 years</strong> if your test is negative for HPV - this is most people, most of the time</li>
  <li><strong>Sooner</strong> if your last test detected HPV but cell changes were low-grade (you will be told the next step)</li>
  <li><strong>More often</strong> if you have had a high-grade abnormality treated, or if you are immunosuppressed (HIV, transplant, on certain medicines)</li>
</ul>

<p>If your previous test was a smear (cytology), you can move to the HPV test at your next screening interval.</p>

<h2>The self-test option</h2>

<p>Since the change in 2023, you can do the HPV test yourself with a vaginal swab. This is the same accuracy as a clinician-taken sample for HPV detection.[2]</p>

<ul>
  <li>You take the swab in private (in the clinic toilet, or sometimes at home with a postal kit)</li>
  <li>It takes about 30 seconds</li>
  <li>The swab goes into a vial, which the clinic sends to the lab</li>
  <li>Result usually back within 2-3 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>The self-test is the same NZ-funded test as a clinician-taken one. If your self-test detects HPV, the next step usually requires a speculum examination to look at the cervix directly, but the screening test itself is your choice.</p>

<h2>What the test looks for</h2>

<p>The lab tests for high-risk HPV types. If HPV is not found, no further action is needed for 5 years. If HPV is found, the lab tests further:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>HPV 16 or 18</strong> - the highest-risk types - automatic referral to colposcopy (closer look at the cervix in a clinic)</li>
  <li><strong>Other high-risk HPV types</strong> - the lab also looks at the cells (reflex cytology). If cell changes are present, colposcopy. If not, repeat HPV test in 12 months.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What the results mean</h2>

<h3>HPV not detected</h3>
<p>The most common result. Means none of the high-risk HPV types are present. Your next test is in 5 years. Your risk of cervical cancer in the next 5 years is very low - lower than the equivalent risk after a normal smear under the old programme.</p>

<h3>HPV detected, low-grade or no cell changes</h3>
<p>HPV is present but the cells look normal or only mildly abnormal. Most HPV infections clear on their own within 1-2 years. The recommendation is usually to repeat the HPV test in 12 months. If the virus has cleared, back to 5-yearly. If it is still there, colposcopy.</p>

<h3>HPV 16 or 18 detected, or high-grade cell changes</h3>
<p>Direct referral to colposcopy. This is not a cancer diagnosis - it means your cervix needs a closer look to see if there are precancer cells that need treatment.</p>

<h2>Colposcopy - what to expect</h2>

<p>A specialist appointment in a hospital clinic. The doctor or nurse uses a colposcope (a magnifier with a light) to look at the cervix. They may apply a vinegar-based solution to highlight any abnormal areas. If anything looks abnormal, they may take a small biopsy. Most people find it like a longer cervical screening test, with mild cramping at most.</p>

<p>If precancer cells (CIN, cervical intraepithelial neoplasia) are found, the treatment is usually a LLETZ procedure - a 15-minute outpatient procedure that removes the abnormal cells, done with local anaesthetic. Cure rates for CIN treated this way are around 95%.[3]</p>

<h2>Common questions</h2>

<h3>I have had the HPV vaccine. Do I still need to be screened?</h3>
<p>Yes. The vaccine protects against the most common high-risk HPV types but not all of them. Screening fills the gap.</p>

<h3>I am over 70. Do I still need it?</h3>
<p>If your last HPV test was negative and you have no symptoms, screening usually stops at 70. If your last test detected HPV or had any abnormality, screening continues.</p>

<h3>I have had a hysterectomy. Do I still need it?</h3>
<p>If your hysterectomy was for benign reasons (fibroids, heavy bleeding) and your previous screening was always normal, no. If your hysterectomy was for cervical or endometrial cancer or precancer, follow-up is different.</p>

<h3>What if I have symptoms - bleeding, pain, discharge?</h3>
<p>See your GP. Symptoms are not a screening question - they need clinical assessment regardless of when your last screen was.</p>

<h3>Is the test painful?</h3>
<p>The self-test is usually only mildly uncomfortable. The clinician-taken test involves a speculum, which most people describe as uncomfortable rather than painful, lasting around a minute. Talk to your GP or nurse if you have anxiety, a history of trauma, or pain - they can adjust the technique, use a smaller speculum, or use other approaches.</p>
