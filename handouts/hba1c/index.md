HbA1c (glycated haemoglobin) is the most useful single number in diabetes care. It shows what your blood sugar has been on average over the past 8-12 weeks - a marathon view rather than a snapshot. It does not require fasting, and it guides almost every treatment decision.

<h2>What the units mean</h2>

<p>NZ uses mmol/mol. Older or American documents may use percent (%). Conversion:</p>

<ul>
<li>Normal: under 41 mmol/mol (under 5.9%)</li>
<li>Prediabetes: 41-49 mmol/mol (5.9-6.6%)</li>
<li>Diabetes: 50 mmol/mol or higher (6.7% or higher)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Targets in diabetes</h2>

<p>The "right" target depends on you. General guidance:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Most adults with type 2 diabetes:</strong> 53 mmol/mol or lower (about 7%)</li>
<li><strong>Younger, otherwise healthy, low hypo risk:</strong> 48 mmol/mol or lower</li>
<li><strong>Older, frail, or hypo-prone:</strong> 58-64 mmol/mol may be safer</li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy:</strong> 42 mmol/mol or lower</li>
<li><strong>Type 1 diabetes:</strong> 53 mmol/mol or lower for most</li>
</ul>

<p>Going lower is not always better. In older people, very tight control raises hypo risk and reduces quality of life without clear benefit.</p>

<h2>How HbA1c relates to average glucose</h2>

<p>Approximate equivalents:</p>

<ul>
<li>HbA1c 42 = average glucose 6.5 mmol/L</li>
<li>HbA1c 53 = 8.5 mmol/L</li>
<li>HbA1c 64 = 10.0 mmol/L</li>
<li>HbA1c 75 = 12.0 mmol/L</li>
<li>HbA1c 86 = 13.5 mmol/L</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>Each 11 mmol/mol (1%) reduction in HbA1c is associated with substantial reduction in long-term complications - eye disease, kidney disease, neuropathy, and cardiovascular events.&sup1; The biggest gains come from improving very high HbA1c (e.g. 90 to 75); the gains are smaller as you approach normal.</p>

<h2>How often to check</h2>

<ul>
<li>At diagnosis</li>
<li>Every 3 months when adjusting treatment or above target</li>
<li>Every 6 months when stable and at target</li>
<li>Annually if very stable on simple treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>What can throw off HbA1c</h2>

<p>Conditions that shorten red blood cell life can <em>lower</em> HbA1c falsely:</p>

<ul>
<li>Iron deficiency (after iron treatment)</li>
<li>Recent blood loss</li>
<li>Haemolytic anaemia</li>
<li>Sickle cell disease and other haemoglobinopathies</li>
<li>Pregnancy (later trimesters)</li>
</ul>

<p>Conditions that lengthen red cell life can <em>raise</em> HbA1c falsely:</p>

<ul>
<li>Iron deficiency anaemia (untreated)</li>
<li>Splenectomy</li>
<li>Some kidney disease</li>
</ul>

<p>Tell your GP if any of these apply - alternative tests (fasting glucose, fructosamine, CGM data) may be more useful for you.</p>

<h2>Improving HbA1c by 5-15 mmol/mol</h2>

<p>What works (typical gains):</p>

<ul>
<li>Losing 5-10% of body weight: 5-15 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Quitting sugary drinks: 3-10 mmol/mol</li>
<li>150 minutes per week of exercise: 3-7 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Adding metformin: 10-15 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Adding an SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin): 5-10 mmol/mol plus heart and kidney protection</li>
<li>Adding a GLP-1 agonist (dulaglutide): 10-15 mmol/mol plus weight loss</li>
<li>Starting insulin: variable but can drop HbA1c 20-30 mmol/mol</li>
</ul>

<h2>The 80/20 rule</h2>

<p>For most people with high HbA1c, four habits matter most:</p>

<ol>
<li>Cut sugary drinks completely</li>
<li>Halve the rice/bread/potato portion</li>
<li>Add vegetables to every meal</li>
<li>Walk after dinner (10-20 minutes)</li>
</ol>

<p>This combination produces measurable HbA1c drops within weeks.</p>

<h2>HbA1c is not the only thing</h2>

<p>Diabetes care also includes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Blood pressure (target usually below 130/80)</li>
<li>LDL cholesterol (target usually below 1.8)</li>
<li>Annual eye check</li>
<li>Annual foot check</li>
<li>Annual urine albumin/creatinine ratio (kidney check)</li>
<li>Cardiovascular risk assessment</li>
<li>Weight, smoking, exercise</li>
</ul>

<p>A normal HbA1c with high blood pressure and untreated cholesterol is not good diabetes care.</p>
