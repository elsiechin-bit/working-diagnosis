<p>Constipation means having bowel motions less often than usual, needing to strain to pass them, or passing hard, dry, or small stools. What counts as "normal" varies widely - anywhere from three times a day to once every three days can be normal. What matters is whether your pattern has changed, and whether you have discomfort.</p>

<p>Constipation is very common, particularly in older adults, during pregnancy, and in people who are less active or eat a low-fibre diet. In most cases, it is straightforward to treat.</p>

<h2>Common causes</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Low-fibre diet</li>
  <li>Not drinking enough fluid</li>
  <li>Inactivity</li>
  <li>Ignoring the urge to go (common in busy lifestyles or when toilets are not accessible)</li>
  <li>Medicines - particularly iron supplements, opioid painkillers (codeine, tramadol), some antidepressants, calcium channel blockers, and antacids containing calcium or aluminium</li>
  <li>Pregnancy</li>
  <li>Irritable bowel syndrome</li>
  <li>Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid)</li>
  <li>Anxiety and depression</li>
</ul>

<h2>Dietary changes - first line</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Increase fibre gradually.</strong> Aim for 25-30 g of fibre per day. Good sources include wholegrain bread and cereals, oats, legumes (lentils, chickpeas, beans), vegetables, fruit with skin on, and nuts and seeds. Increase slowly over 1-2 weeks to avoid bloating and gas.</li>
  <li><strong>Drink more water.</strong> Fibre works by absorbing water to bulk up and soften stools. Without enough fluid, extra fibre can make constipation worse. Aim for at least 6-8 glasses (1.5-2 litres) of fluid per day.</li>
  <li><strong>Prune juice or prunes.</strong> These contain sorbitol, a natural laxative. 1-2 glasses of prune juice or 5-6 prunes per day can help significantly.</li>
  <li><strong>Regular meals and breakfast.</strong> Eating stimulates movement through the gut. Do not skip meals.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Bowel habits</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Respond to the urge.</strong> When you feel the need to go, go. Regularly ignoring the urge makes constipation worse over time.</li>
  <li><strong>Try after breakfast.</strong> The gastrocolic reflex - the gut movement triggered by eating - is strongest after the first meal of the day. Sitting on the toilet for 5-10 minutes after breakfast (even without urgency) can help retrain the bowel.</li>
  <li><strong>Good posture on the toilet.</strong> Sitting with your feet elevated on a small step (so your knees are higher than your hips) is the most natural position for passing a bowel motion. It relaxes the pelvic floor muscles and reduces straining.</li>
  <li><strong>Exercise.</strong> Physical activity stimulates bowel movement. Even regular walking helps.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Laxatives</h2>

<p>If dietary changes are not enough, laxatives are safe and effective for most adults. There are several types:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bulk-forming laxatives</strong> (such as psyllium husk/Metamucil) absorb water and increase stool bulk. They work slowly (1-3 days) and must be taken with plenty of water. Good for long-term use.</li>
  <li><strong>Osmotic laxatives</strong> (such as lactulose or macrogol/Movicol) draw water into the bowel to soften stools. Macrogol is particularly effective and well-tolerated. Lactulose can cause bloating.</li>
  <li><strong>Stimulant laxatives</strong> (such as senna or bisacodyl) stimulate bowel movement more actively. They work within hours and are useful for short-term or occasional use. Suitable for before-bed dosing with a result the next morning.</li>
  <li><strong>Stool softeners</strong> (such as docusate) have limited evidence as a standalone treatment but may help in combination with other laxatives.</li>
</ul>

<p>Long-term use of stimulant laxatives is generally safe when needed (for example, in people on regular opioid pain medicines). The old idea that laxatives are "habit-forming" in a dangerous sense is largely not supported by evidence for the types listed above.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Constipation that has come on suddenly after a long period of normal bowel habits, particularly in people over 50</li>
  <li>Blood in the stools or on the toilet paper (not clearly from haemorrhoids)</li>
  <li>Unintentional weight loss</li>
  <li>Constipation alternating with diarrhoea</li>
  <li>Severe pain with bowel motions</li>
  <li>No improvement after 2 weeks of dietary changes and over-the-counter laxatives</li>
</ul>
