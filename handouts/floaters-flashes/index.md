<p>Almost everyone notices floaters at some point - the cobwebs, dots, or thread-like shapes that drift across your vision and seem to move when you look at them. Most are completely harmless and live with you for life. A small number of patterns of floaters and flashes point to something more serious going on at the back of the eye, and the difference matters because retinal tears and detachments are highly treatable when caught quickly and devastating when missed. The aim of this handout is to give you a clear sense of what to ignore, what to mention at your next eye check, and what to act on the same day.</p>

<h2>What floaters and flashes are</h2>

<p>The eye is filled with a clear gel called the vitreous. With age, the vitreous becomes more liquid and gradually pulls away from the back of the eye - a process called posterior vitreous detachment (PVD). This is a normal part of ageing and happens to most people in their 50s, 60s, or 70s, and earlier in people who are short-sighted. The shadows cast by clumps in the gel are what you see as floaters. As the gel pulls on the retina during a PVD, it can stimulate the retina mechanically, which the brain interprets as flashes of light - often described as brief, lightning-streak flashes, more obvious in dim light or when you move your eyes quickly.</p>

<h2>Mostly benign patterns</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Floaters that have been there for years and have not changed</li>
  <li>The occasional floater that drifts past and then resolves</li>
  <li>A handful of new floaters appearing over weeks, then settling, with no flashes and no curtain over your vision (a typical uncomplicated PVD)</li>
  <li>Brief, occasional flashes early in a PVD that fade over a few weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these is an emergency on its own. Most are worth mentioning at your next routine optometry appointment but do not need to interrupt your week.</p>

<h2>The patterns that need same-day assessment</h2>

<p>About 10 to 15 percent of people with a symptomatic acute PVD turn out to have a retinal tear, and a smaller proportion go on to a retinal detachment if the tear is not treated.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The point of urgent assessment is to find tears early, before they progress.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>A sudden shower of new floaters</strong>, especially with flashes - far more than the usual one or two drifting through</li>
  <li><strong>New flashes of light</strong>, especially repeated and persistent, particularly to one side of your vision</li>
  <li><strong>A dark curtain, shadow, or cloud</strong> coming across part of your vision, from the side, top, or bottom - this is the classic symptom of retinal detachment</li>
  <li><strong>A sudden change in vision</strong> in one eye, especially in someone who is short-sighted, has had cataract surgery, or has had a recent eye injury</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these is happening, you need to be seen the same day. Options:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Phone an optometrist or eye clinic near you and ask for an urgent appointment - most will fit you in</li>
  <li>Phone your GP for an urgent referral</li>
  <li>If neither is available within hours, go to an emergency department - the ophthalmology team or on-call ophthalmologist can be contacted from there</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not wait for a regular GP appointment, and do not wait to see if it settles overnight.</p>

<h2>What the assessment involves</h2>

<p>An optometrist or ophthalmologist will check your visual acuity, look for signs of bleeding in the eye, and dilate your pupils with drops to examine the retina in detail with a special lens. A scan of the retina (OCT) and sometimes a wide-field photograph may be done. The drops blur your near vision for a few hours, so do not plan to drive immediately afterward.</p>

<p>If a retinal tear is found and there is no detachment yet, treatment is usually a quick laser procedure (laser retinopexy) that seals the tear in the office or clinic. If a retinal detachment is present, you will be referred urgently for surgery - this is usually done within 24 to 72 hours and is highly effective at restoring vision when caught early.</p>

<h2>Higher-risk groups</h2>

<ul>
  <li>People who are short-sighted (myopic), especially highly myopic</li>
  <li>People who have had cataract surgery, even years ago</li>
  <li>People with a previous retinal tear or detachment in either eye</li>
  <li>People with a family history of retinal detachment</li>
  <li>Recent eye trauma</li>
</ul>

<p>If you fall into one of these groups and you notice any of the warning patterns above, treat them as urgent until proven otherwise.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor or optometrist</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Same day:</strong> sudden new floaters, persistent new flashes, a curtain or shadow over part of your vision, or sudden change in vision in one eye</li>
  <li><strong>This week:</strong> a few new floaters that have settled, no curtain, no major flashes - reasonable for a routine optometry check soon</li>
  <li><strong>At next routine check:</strong> floaters that have been stable for years and are not bothering you</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your optometrist or eye specialist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Is this a posterior vitreous detachment, or is something else happening?</li>
  <li>Is there a retinal tear that needs laser?</li>
  <li>What changes mean I should come back urgently?</li>
  <li>Should the other eye be monitored more closely from now on?</li>
</ul>
