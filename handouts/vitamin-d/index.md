<p>Vitamin D is unusual: it is technically a hormone, and the great majority of it comes from sunlight on bare skin rather than from food. Aotearoa is at high latitudes (Auckland 37 degrees south, Invercargill 46 degrees south), with summer UV among the strongest in the world and winter UV that drops too low at the southern end of the country to make any vitamin D between May and August. The result is that most NZers do not have a vitamin D problem in summer, and most who do are in specific groups in winter. The advice has to balance two real risks: too little vitamin D, and too much sun.</p>

<h2>What vitamin D does, and how much you need</h2>

<p>Vitamin D mainly helps the gut absorb calcium, which keeps bones, teeth, and muscles strong. Severe deficiency in tamariki causes rickets; severe deficiency in adults causes osteomalacia and contributes to falls and fractures. Outside of these clear deficiency states, the supplements industry has made bigger health claims (cancer prevention, infection prevention, mood) than the evidence actually supports. The NZ blood test cut-offs<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Deficient:</strong> serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D below 25 nmol/L</li>
  <li><strong>Insufficient:</strong> 25 to 50 nmol/L</li>
  <li><strong>Sufficient:</strong> 50 nmol/L and above</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to get enough from the sun, safely</h2>

<p>NZ guidance, jointly issued by the Cancer Society, MoH, and Health Promotion Agency, hinges on the season<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>September to April (peak UV):</strong> protect your skin. Most people get enough vitamin D incidentally - a few minutes of sun on the face and arms in the early morning or late afternoon, outside the 10 am to 4 pm peak window. Sunscreen, shade, hats, and clothing during peak hours.</li>
  <li><strong>May to August (low UV):</strong> a daily walk at midday with face, hands, and forearms exposed (no sunscreen) is the simplest way to keep levels up. Around 30 minutes is plenty in the upper North Island and longer in the South.</li>
  <li><strong>South Island in winter:</strong> the sun does not produce useful vitamin D regardless of how much you stand in it. Supplementation, or building stores in summer, is the answer for at-risk groups.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who is at risk and might need a test</h2>

<p>Routine population testing is not recommended in NZ - it is low-yield in the general population and the result rarely changes management for someone otherwise well. Testing is reasonable when you have a specific reason to suspect deficiency:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Naturally darker skin tones</strong> (skin types V and VI), especially when combined with mostly-indoor lifestyles or modest dress</li>
  <li><strong>Housebound or in residential care</strong>, including older adults who do not get outdoors</li>
  <li><strong>Fully covered for cultural or religious reasons</strong> when outside</li>
  <li><strong>Coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or post-bariatric surgery</strong> - all reduce absorption</li>
  <li><strong>On medication</strong> that affects vitamin D metabolism (some anticonvulsants, long-term steroids)</li>
  <li><strong>Bone problems</strong> already - osteoporosis, recurrent fractures, or muscle pain consistent with osteomalacia</li>
  <li><strong>Babies who are exclusively breastfed</strong>, especially with darker skin or born in winter - daily supplementation from birth is recommended</li>
</ul>

<h2>What supplements look like in NZ</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> colecalciferol (vitamin D3) 1.25 mg (50,000 IU) tablets, taken monthly. One tablet a month is the standard adult replacement dose<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Over-the-counter:</strong> daily 1,000 IU tablets are widely available and reasonable for at-risk people who prefer a daily routine.</li>
  <li><strong>Tamariki:</strong> infant drops are available; dose by weight - your GP, midwife, or pharmacist will advise.</li>
  <li><strong>Toxicity is rare</strong> at usual doses, but very high doses over months can cause high blood calcium - do not stack supplements on top of monthly prescribed doses without checking.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you have low levels on a blood test</h2>

<p>Mild insufficiency (25 to 50 nmol/L) usually responds to a few months of monthly tablets plus sensible sunlight. Frank deficiency (under 25 nmol/L) often needs a loading regimen first - your GP will guide. Repeat testing 3 to 6 months later is usually enough to confirm replenishment; lifelong testing is rarely warranted unless you have an ongoing reason for low levels.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Bone or muscle pain that is unexplained, especially with weakness on standing from a chair</li>
  <li>Recurrent fractures or new diagnosis of osteoporosis</li>
  <li>You are in one of the higher-risk groups above and have not had levels checked</li>
  <li>You are caring for a baby exclusively breastfed, particularly through winter</li>
  <li>You have been taking high-dose vitamin D supplements long-term and are unsure whether to continue</li>
</ul>
