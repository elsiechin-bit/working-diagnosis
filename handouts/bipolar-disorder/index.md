<p>Bipolar disorder is a long-term mood condition characterised by episodes of significantly elevated mood (mania or hypomania) and episodes of depression. It affects around 2-3% of NZ adults across the spectrum.[1] It is highly treatable. Most people with bipolar live full lives with the right combination of medication, psychological tools, and self-management. This handout is a starting point: what bipolar is, what to expect, and what tends to help.</p>

<h2>The spectrum</h2>

<p>"Bipolar" is an umbrella for several patterns:</p>

<h3>Bipolar I</h3>
<p>At least one episode of mania - a period (lasting a week or more, or any duration if hospitalisation needed) of elevated, expansive, or irritable mood with significantly increased energy and goal-directed activity. Plus typically episodes of major depression. Around 1% of adults.</p>

<h3>Bipolar II</h3>
<p>At least one episode of hypomania (lasting at least 4 days, less severe than mania, no psychotic features, no significant impairment in functioning during the episode itself) and at least one major depressive episode. Often more depressive than manic over time. Around 1-2% of adults.</p>

<h3>Cyclothymia</h3>
<p>Numerous periods of hypomanic symptoms and depressive symptoms over at least 2 years, never quite reaching threshold for full episodes. Around 0.4-1% of adults.</p>

<h3>Bipolar with rapid cycling, mixed features, or seasonal pattern</h3>
<p>Specifiers that change treatment approach.</p>

<h2>What mania looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Reduced need for sleep without feeling tired (this is often the earliest signal)</li>
  <li>Elevated, irritable, or expansive mood</li>
  <li>Inflated self-esteem, grandiosity</li>
  <li>Pressured speech, hard to interrupt</li>
  <li>Racing thoughts, jumping between ideas</li>
  <li>Distractibility</li>
  <li>Increased goal-directed activity (starting many projects, working long hours)</li>
  <li>Impulsive risk-taking - financial, sexual, driving, substance use</li>
  <li>In severe cases, psychotic features (hallucinations, delusions of grandeur)</li>
</ul>

<p>Hypomania is the milder version. People often feel productive, creative, charismatic during hypomania - which is why it can be hard to recognise as illness from the inside. The downside often comes after, in the depressive phase or in the consequences of impulsive decisions.</p>

<h2>What bipolar depression looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Depressed mood most of the day, most days</li>
  <li>Loss of interest or pleasure</li>
  <li>Significant weight or appetite change</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance - either insomnia or hypersomnia</li>
  <li>Slowed movement and thinking, or agitation</li>
  <li>Fatigue, low energy</li>
  <li>Feelings of worthlessness, excessive guilt</li>
  <li>Difficulty concentrating, indecisiveness</li>
  <li>Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide</li>
</ul>

<p>Bipolar depression often differs from unipolar depression in being more sleepy/heavy/slowed (atypical pattern), with more mood reactivity and seasonal variation. The treatment also differs - antidepressants used alone in bipolar can trigger mania.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Diagnosis is made by a psychiatrist (or sometimes a senior GP with experience). The pattern requires:</p>

<ul>
  <li>History of clear mood episodes meeting criteria</li>
  <li>Exclusion of substance use, medical conditions, or medications causing similar features</li>
  <li>Family history is supportive (bipolar is highly heritable)</li>
  <li>Often takes years - the average gap between first episode and diagnosis is 5-10 years internationally</li>
</ul>

<p>If you suspect bipolar in yourself or a loved one, your GP can do an initial assessment and refer to community mental health services or psychiatry.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<h3>Medication is foundational</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mood stabilisers</strong> - lithium (the gold standard for bipolar I, requires blood monitoring), sodium valproate (effective but contraindicated in pregnancy), lamotrigine (best for bipolar depression and bipolar II)</li>
  <li><strong>Atypical antipsychotics</strong> - quetiapine, olanzapine, aripiprazole, risperidone. Effective for both mania and bipolar depression in different combinations.</li>
  <li><strong>Antidepressants</strong> - used cautiously, almost always alongside a mood stabiliser, never alone, because they can trigger mania.</li>
  <li><strong>Benzodiazepines</strong> - short-term during acute episodes, especially for sleep.</li>
</ul>

<p>Medication is usually long-term (years to lifelong). Stopping when feeling well is the main reason for relapse. Most people require 2-3 medication changes before finding the right combination.</p>

<h3>Psychological therapies</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Psychoeducation - understanding the illness, recognising early warning signs</li>
  <li>Cognitive Behavioural Therapy adapted for bipolar</li>
  <li>Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT) - protecting daily routines, especially sleep</li>
  <li>Family-Focused Therapy - families learn to recognise and respond to early signs</li>
</ul>

<h3>Self-management</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sleep is medication.</strong> Reduced sleep is the most reliable trigger for mania. Protect it.</li>
  <li><strong>Routine.</strong> Same wake time, regular meals, consistent activity. Disruption to routine destabilises mood.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid alcohol and recreational drugs.</strong> Both worsen the illness and the medication.</li>
  <li><strong>Mood diary.</strong> Especially in the first years, tracking mood, sleep, and triggers helps spot patterns.</li>
  <li><strong>Early warning signs plan.</strong> Identify your personal first signs of an episode (less sleep, more shopping, project obsession, irritability) and have a written plan for what to do.</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular health.</strong> People with bipolar have higher cardiovascular risk; the medications can contribute. Regular GP review.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Living well with bipolar</h2>

<p>Many people with bipolar live full lives - careers, relationships, family. The patterns that protect long-term outcomes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Consistent medication, even when feeling well</li>
  <li>Regular psychiatric review (annual at minimum once stable)</li>
  <li>Strong relationships with people who know your warning signs</li>
  <li>Honest communication with employers about needed accommodations (flexible hours, ability to take leave when needed)</li>
  <li>Acceptance of the illness as part of life, neither defining nor minimised</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do if you suspect mania in yourself or someone you love</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do not let them drive long distances or make major financial decisions</li>
  <li>Try to protect sleep - the most concrete intervention available</li>
  <li>Reduce stimulation - quiet environment, fewer demands</li>
  <li>Contact their psychiatrist, mental health team, or GP today</li>
  <li>If significantly unwell, contact community mental health crisis team or take to ED</li>
  <li>If they are at risk to themselves or others, call 111 - police can assist with mental health emergencies</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy and breastfeeding</h2>

<p>Specialist consultation needed - usually involves a perinatal psychiatrist. Some medications are safer in pregnancy (lamotrigine), others contraindicated (valproate). Stopping medication abruptly in pregnancy raises risk of relapse, which is itself bad for mother and baby. Joint planning with psychiatry and obstetrics is the right path.</p>
