<p>Gout is a type of arthritis caused by uric acid crystals building up in a joint. It causes sudden, intense pain - often overnight - in one joint, most commonly the big toe, ankle, or knee. The joint becomes hot, swollen, and extremely tender to touch. Even the weight of a bedsheet can be unbearable.</p>

<p>Gout is one of the most common forms of arthritis in New Zealand and is especially prevalent in Maori and Pacific peoples. It is very treatable - both the acute attack and the underlying condition.</p>

<h2>What to do right now</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest the joint.</strong> Keep weight off it as much as you can while the attack is active.</li>
  <li><strong>Elevate the affected limb.</strong> Raising your foot or leg above heart level helps reduce swelling.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply ice.</strong> Wrap a bag of ice or frozen peas in a cloth and apply to the joint for 20-30 minutes at a time, several times a day. Never apply ice directly to skin.</li>
  <li><strong>Drink plenty of water.</strong> Staying well hydrated helps your kidneys clear uric acid. Aim for 2-3 litres of water per day during an attack. Avoid alcohol and sugary drinks.</li>
  <li><strong>Take your anti-inflammatory medicine as prescribed.</strong> See below.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines for an acute attack</h2>

<p>The sooner you start anti-inflammatory treatment, the faster the attack resolves. Three options are commonly used - your doctor will choose based on your other health conditions:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Colchicine</strong> is the preferred first-line treatment in NZ. Take it as directed by your doctor - usually 1 mg immediately, then 0.5 mg one hour later, and then 0.5 mg twice daily until the attack settles. Do not exceed the prescribed dose. It commonly causes diarrhoea and nausea at higher doses.</li>
  <li><strong>NSAIDs</strong> (such as ibuprofen or naproxen) work quickly when taken at anti-inflammatory doses. They are less suitable if you have kidney disease, stomach ulcers, heart failure, or take blood thinners. Take with food.</li>
  <li><strong>Prednisone</strong> (a steroid) is used when colchicine and NSAIDs cannot be taken. It is very effective and usually given as a short course of 5-7 days.</li>
</ul>

<p>Paracetamol alone is not effective for gout. If your pain is severe and your prescription medicines are not controlling it, contact your GP.</p>

<h2>What to avoid during an attack</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Do not start allopurinol</strong> during an attack. If you are already taking it, continue as normal - but do not start it for the first time until the attack has fully settled.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid aspirin</strong> (other than low-dose 100 mg prescribed for heart disease) - full-dose aspirin can make gout worse.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid alcohol</strong>, especially beer and spirits, which raise uric acid levels.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How long does an attack last?</h2>

<p>With treatment, most attacks settle within 3-7 days. Without treatment, they can last 1-2 weeks. The joint should return to normal once the attack has passed, though you may notice some skin peeling as it heals.</p>

<h2>Preventing future attacks</h2>

<p>Gout attacks tend to come back. After this attack has settled, talk to your doctor about long-term uric acid-lowering treatment (usually allopurinol). This can prevent future attacks, protect your joints, and protect your kidneys. Diet changes alone are rarely enough - but reducing alcohol, red meat, seafood, and sugary drinks does help.</p>

<h2>When to get help quickly</h2>

<p>See your GP or go to urgent care if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>You have a fever alongside the joint pain - this may suggest a joint infection, which is a medical emergency</li>
  <li>Your medicines are not working after 48-72 hours</li>
  <li>You have never had gout diagnosed before - the first attack should always be confirmed by a doctor</li>
  <li>Multiple joints are affected at the same time</li>
</ul>
