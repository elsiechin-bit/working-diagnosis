<p>Diabetes can damage the small blood vessels at the back of the eye, in the retina. This damage is called diabetic retinopathy. It is one of the leading preventable causes of vision loss in working-age New Zealanders.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The thing that makes it dangerous is also the thing that makes it manageable: it usually has no symptoms until it is well advanced. By the time you notice your vision changing, you have lost something that cannot easily be put back. That is why every adult with diabetes in New Zealand is offered a free retinal screen, and why the screen is one of the most worthwhile half-hours in your year.</p>

<h2>How diabetes affects the eyes</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Diabetic retinopathy.</strong> The capillaries of the retina become weakened and leaky. Mild changes (background retinopathy) cause no symptoms. As it progresses, new fragile vessels can grow (proliferative retinopathy) and bleed into the eye, causing sudden vision loss.</li>
  <li><strong>Diabetic macular oedema.</strong> Fluid leaks into the macula (the part of the retina responsible for central vision), making vision blurred or distorted. This can happen at any stage of retinopathy.</li>
  <li><strong>Cataracts.</strong> Develop earlier in people with diabetes.</li>
  <li><strong>Glaucoma.</strong> Risk is roughly doubled in people with diabetes.</li>
</ul>

<p>About one in three people with diabetes has some form of retinopathy.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> Maori, Pacific, and South Asian peoples are at higher risk, both of diabetes itself and of more advanced eye complications.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup></p>

<h2>The NZ Diabetic Retinal Screening Programme</h2>

<p>Every person in New Zealand with type 1 or type 2 diabetes is eligible for a free retinal photograph screen, organised regionally by Te Whatu Ora. Screening is offered every two years for most people, more often if changes are present. You do not need a referral; your GP or diabetes nurse will enrol you. The appointment takes about 30 minutes - drops are put in to dilate your pupils, photographs are taken of the back of each eye, and the images are reviewed by a specialist.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup></p>

<p>If the screen is normal, you will simply be invited back when you are next due. If changes are seen, you will be referred to an ophthalmology service for follow-up.</p>

<h2>What helps protect your vision</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Get the screen, every time you are invited.</strong> The single most important thing.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep your HbA1c on target.</strong> Your GP or diabetes nurse will agree a target with you. Tight control reduces the risk of new retinopathy and slows existing changes.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat blood pressure.</strong> High blood pressure independently accelerates retinopathy. Most people with diabetes need their BP under 130/80.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat cholesterol.</strong> Statins reduce cardiovascular events and may also slow retinopathy progression.</li>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking worsens every diabetic complication, eyes included. Free support is available through Quitline (0800 778 778).</li>
  <li><strong>Tell your screening team if you are pregnant or planning pregnancy.</strong> Retinopathy can progress quickly in pregnancy, and screening intervals are shortened.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment if changes are found</h2>

<p>Most diabetic eye disease found early on screening needs no treatment beyond optimising glucose, blood pressure, and lipid control, with closer monitoring. When treatment is needed, the main options are anti-VEGF injections into the eye (for macular oedema and proliferative retinopathy), laser treatment to seal leaking vessels, and rarely, vitrectomy surgery for advanced bleeding or retinal traction.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup> All are available in NZ, and the injections used in NZ for diabetic eye disease are PHARMAC-funded for confirmed indications.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor or optometrist between screens</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Any sudden change in vision in one or both eyes</li>
  <li>A new dark spot, floaters, or "cobwebs" in your vision</li>
  <li>Blurring that does not clear with cleaning your glasses or blinking</li>
  <li>Pain or redness in one eye</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor or screening team</h2>

<ul>
  <li>When is my next retinal screen due?</li>
  <li>What is my current HbA1c, and what target should we be aiming for?</li>
  <li>What is my blood pressure target?</li>
  <li>If I am referred, what does that involve and how long is the wait in my district?</li>
</ul>
