<p>Hip osteoarthritis develops when the cartilage in your hip joint gradually wears down. This causes pain and stiffness, particularly with activities like walking, climbing stairs, or getting out of a chair. The good news is that many people manage hip OA effectively at home and stay active for years. This handout explains what helps.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain in the hip, groin, or outer hip (sometimes felt in the knee or lower back)</li>
  <li>Stiffness after resting or first thing in the morning</li>
  <li>Difficulty with specific movements: getting out of chairs, climbing stairs, putting on shoes or socks</li>
  <li>A grinding or clicking sensation in the hip</li>
  <li>Pain that worsens with prolonged activity and improves with rest</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Hip osteoarthritis occurs when the smooth cartilage protecting the hip joint wears down over time. Contributing factors include age, previous hip injuries, and hip problems from childhood (such as developmental dysplasia). Unlike some joint diseases, osteoarthritis is not inflammatory - it is purely mechanical wear and tear. The condition usually progresses slowly.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay active with low-impact exercise.</strong> Walking, swimming, and cycling are excellent choices that build strength without overloading your hip. Aim for at least 150 minutes per week of activity.</li>
  <li><strong>Strengthen your hip and core muscles.</strong> Stronger muscles around your hip take stress off the joint and reduce pain. Specific exercises (like clamshells, side-lying leg lifts, and bridges) are very effective. A physiotherapist can show you the right technique.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage your weight.</strong> Extra weight increases stress on your hip joint. Losing 5 - 10% of body weight if overweight can significantly improve symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Use regular pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen taken regularly helps you stay active. Ibuprofen may be particularly helpful for inflammatory flares. Check with your GP if you have stomach or heart concerns.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply ice after activity.</strong> 15 - 20 minutes of ice after exercise can reduce pain and any swelling.</li>
  <li><strong>Use walking aids if needed.</strong> A walking stick in the hand opposite your affected hip significantly reduces stress on the joint and can ease pain during walking.</li>
  <li><strong>Modify your environment.</strong> Raised toilet seats, bath rails, and firm chairs make daily activities easier. A mattress that is not too soft helps with getting out of bed.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief is usually the first approach. If this is not enough, your GP may recommend a topical anti-inflammatory cream, stronger pain relief, or steroid injections. Talk with your GP about what is appropriate for you.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is not improving with self-care</li>
  <li>Your hip is significantly swollen, hot, or red</li>
  <li>You develop severe sudden pain in your hip</li>
  <li>Pain is affecting your quality of life or ability to walk</li>
  <li>You have trouble sleeping due to pain</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most people manage hip osteoarthritis well with exercise, activity modification, and regular pain relief. If symptoms become severe and significantly limit your life despite conservative measures, hip replacement surgery is very effective and is an option worth discussing with your doctor.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Would physiotherapy help? Can you refer me?</li>
  <li>Do I need any imaging?</li>
  <li>What should I avoid?</li>
  <li>When should I consider surgery?</li>
</ul>
