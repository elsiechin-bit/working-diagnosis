Glandular fever (also called infectious mononucleosis or "mono") is a viral infection most commonly caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). It mostly affects teenagers and young adults. The acute illness typically lasts 2-4 weeks, but fatigue can linger for months. There is no specific cure - rest, time, and patience are the main treatments. Most people recover completely.

<h2>How you catch it</h2>

<p>EBV spreads through saliva - hence its old nickname "the kissing disease". You can also catch it from sharing drinks, food, or utensils. Casual contact (sharing a room, brief conversation) is unlikely to pass it on.</p>

<p>By age 30, around 90% of adults have antibodies to EBV - most childhood infections are mild or symptomless. People who first encounter EBV as teenagers or young adults tend to get the classical, more severe illness.</p>

<p>The incubation period (time from exposure to first symptoms) is 4-6 weeks.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<p>Classic symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Severe sore throat</strong> - often the first and most striking feature. Tonsils swollen, red, sometimes with thick white coating</li>
<li><strong>Fever</strong> - often high (38-40°C), lasting 5-14 days</li>
<li><strong>Swollen lymph nodes</strong> - especially in the neck, but also armpits and groin</li>
<li><strong>Profound fatigue</strong> - far beyond a normal flu tiredness</li>
<li><strong>Headache and muscle aches</strong></li>
<li><strong>Loss of appetite</strong></li>
<li><strong>Spleen enlargement</strong> - usually painless but felt by the doctor on exam (and important for activity advice, see below)</li>
</ul>

<p>Less common but characteristic:</p>

<ul>
<li>Rash - often after taking amoxicillin (some doctors prescribe it for the sore throat before realising it is glandular fever; the rash that follows is harmless but striking)</li>
<li>Mild jaundice (yellow eyes) from liver involvement</li>
<li>Eye puffiness</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Your GP will examine your throat, neck nodes, and abdomen. Useful tests:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Monospot or heterophile antibody test</strong> - quick blood test, often positive within 1-2 weeks</li>
<li><strong>EBV serology</strong> - more specific, useful if Monospot is negative</li>
<li><strong>Full blood count</strong> - shows characteristic atypical lymphocytes</li>
<li><strong>Liver function tests</strong> - often mildly raised</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - rest and time</h2>

<p>There is no antiviral cure. Management is supportive:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Rest.</strong> Listen to your body. Pushing through during the acute phase prolongs recovery</li>
<li><strong>Fluids.</strong> Plenty of water, soup, smoothies. Sore throats make solids difficult; cold or soft foods often help</li>
<li><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for fever, headache, and sore throat</li>
<li><strong>Salt-water gargles or lozenges</strong> for sore throat</li>
<li><strong>Avoid alcohol</strong> for at least 4 weeks - the liver is inflamed and recovers more slowly with alcohol</li>
<li><strong>Stop or avoid amoxicillin</strong> - causes a rash in up to 95% of glandular fever patients (not a true allergy)</li>
</ul>

<h2>The spleen and contact sport</h2>

<p>Glandular fever enlarges the spleen in around half of people. A swollen spleen can rupture from a blow or hard contact - rare but serious.</p>

<p>Avoid:</p>

<ul>
<li>Contact sports (rugby, football, hockey, martial arts) for at least <strong>4 weeks</strong> from symptom onset, often longer</li>
<li>Heavy lifting</li>
<li>High-impact gym work</li>
</ul>

<p>If the GP can no longer feel the spleen (or an ultrasound shows it is normal-sized), and you feel well, return is reasonable. If in doubt, ask. Some sports physicians will check at 4 weeks.</p>

<h2>School and work</h2>

<p>You will likely need 1-3 weeks off, sometimes longer. Once the fever has gone and you feel up to it, gradual return is fine. Most people are back at full activities by 4-6 weeks, but tiredness can persist for months.</p>

<h2>Pacing through recovery</h2>

<p>Many people feel better, then push too hard, then crash for a week. This is the most common mistake. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Build up activity in 10-20% increments, not jumps</li>
<li>Plan rest periods through the day</li>
<li>Skip non-essential commitments for a while</li>
<li>Sleep at least 8-9 hours</li>
<li>Cut alcohol and limit caffeine</li>
</ul>

<p>Most fatigue settles within 1-3 months. About 1 in 10 people develop more prolonged post-glandular-fever fatigue.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - see your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>Severe abdominal pain (especially left upper area, where the spleen is) - could indicate splenic rupture</li>
<li>Difficulty breathing or swallowing - severe tonsil swelling can block the airway</li>
<li>Severe headache, neck stiffness, confusion, drowsiness - rare but serious neurological complications</li>
<li>Fever lasting more than 2 weeks, or returning</li>
<li>Increasing yellowing of the eyes</li>
<li>Swelling not getting better over 4 weeks</li>
<li>Severe fatigue beyond 3 months</li>
</ul>

<p>Splenic rupture: severe sudden abdominal pain, especially the left side, possibly spreading to the left shoulder, with feeling faint - call <strong>111</strong>.</p>

<h2>Possible complications</h2>

<ul>
<li>Hepatitis (mild liver inflammation) - common, usually settles in weeks</li>
<li>Splenic rupture - rare but serious</li>
<li>Airway obstruction from very large tonsils - sometimes needs short-course steroids</li>
<li>Neurological - rare encephalitis or Guillain-Barre syndrome</li>
<li>Persistent fatigue lasting months</li>
<li>Reactivation - rare; usually only in immunosuppressed people</li>
</ul>

<h2>Is it linked to long-term illness?</h2>

<p>EBV has been linked to higher risk of multiple sclerosis decades later, and to a small minority of cancers (e.g. nasopharyngeal carcinoma, some lymphomas).&sup1; For most people, glandular fever is a self-limiting illness with no long-term consequences.</p>

<p>Recent research links EBV infection to chronic fatigue syndrome and ME/CFS in some patients. If your fatigue persists beyond 3 months and you struggle to function, see your GP for assessment.</p>

<h2>Family</h2>

<p>You can return to normal activity once feeling well. The virus continues to be shed in saliva intermittently for many months after recovery, so close family members may catch it - though by adulthood, most have already had it without knowing.</p>
