<p>Gastroenteritis - often called a tummy bug or gastro - is inflammation of the stomach and bowel, usually caused by an infection. Most cases are viral, settle in a few days, and do not need antibiotics. The main risk is dehydration, especially in young tamariki and older adults. In Aotearoa, campylobacter (mostly from undercooked chicken) is the most commonly notified bacterial cause, but viral bugs like norovirus and rotavirus account for the majority of cases overall.</p>

<h2>Typical symptoms and timeline</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Days 1-2:</strong> Sudden nausea, vomiting, watery diarrhoea, cramping tummy pain, sometimes a low-grade fever</li>
  <li><strong>Days 2-3:</strong> Vomiting often settles first; diarrhoea continues; appetite is poor</li>
  <li><strong>Days 3-5:</strong> Most symptoms easing, energy slowly returning, bowels still loose</li>
  <li><strong>Days 5-10:</strong> Loose stools may linger, particularly in young children - this is normal as the gut lining heals</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Most tummy bugs are caused by viruses - norovirus is the classic "winter vomiting bug", and rotavirus mainly affects infants (the rotavirus vaccine is on the NZ schedule at 6 weeks and 3 months). Bacterial causes are less common but matter here: campylobacter is NZ's most-notified foodborne illness, with rates roughly twice those of comparable countries, and the biggest single source is fresh chicken meat. Salmonella, shigella, yersinia, and certain strains of E. coli can also cause gastroenteritis. Less commonly, parasites such as giardia and cryptosporidium are picked up from untreated bore, tank, or stream water, or from contact with farm animals.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sip fluids often.</strong> Small amounts every few minutes work better than big drinks, which can trigger more vomiting. Water, weak tea, clear broth, and diluted juice (1 part juice to 5 parts water) are all fine.</li>
  <li><strong>Use an oral rehydration solution (ORS)</strong> if vomiting and diarrhoea are happening together, or if symptoms last more than a day. Gastrolyte and Hydralyte are available from any pharmacy without prescription, and are a much better match for what your body is losing than water alone.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat when you feel like it.</strong> There is no need to fast. Start with bland foods - toast, rice, banana, plain crackers - and build up as your appetite returns.</li>
  <li><strong>Paracetamol</strong> can help with fever or aches. Be cautious with ibuprofen and other anti-inflammatories during the acute phase if you are dehydrated, as they are harder on the kidneys.</li>
  <li><strong>Rest</strong>, and wash your hands well after the toilet and before handling food.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to avoid</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sports drinks and full-strength fruit juice</strong> are too sugary and can make diarrhoea worse. An oral rehydration solution is a better choice.</li>
  <li><strong>Anti-diarrhoeal medicines</strong> like loperamide (Imodium) are sometimes useful for adults with mild illness, but should not be used in children, or by anyone with blood in the stool, a high fever, or signs of severe illness.</li>
  <li><strong>Antibiotics</strong> are not helpful for the great majority of cases - including campylobacter, where they can actually prolong how long you carry and shed the bug. Your GP will only prescribe them for specific confirmed infections.</li>
  <li><strong>Milk and fizzy drinks</strong> while you are acutely unwell, as they can be hard to tolerate.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Do I need tests?</h2>

<p>For an otherwise well adult who has had symptoms for only a few days and no blood in the stool, no tests are needed - the diagnosis is made from your symptoms, and the treatment is the same regardless of which bug is causing it. Your GP may ask for a stool sample if your diarrhoea has lasted more than 7 days, if there is blood in your stool, after recent overseas travel or a recent hospital stay, if you are immunocompromised, or if you work in food handling, healthcare, or early-childhood care.</p>

<h2>Stopping the spread</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay home from work, school, or daycare for 48 hours</strong> after the last episode of diarrhoea or vomiting</li>
  <li><strong>Wash hands with soap and water</strong> after the toilet and before food - alcohol gels do not work as well against norovirus</li>
  <li><strong>Do not prepare food for others</strong> while you are unwell, or for 48 hours afterwards</li>
  <li><strong>Clean toilets, taps, and high-touch surfaces</strong> with a bleach-based cleaner; wash soiled clothing and linen separately on a hot wash</li>
  <li><strong>In the kitchen, do not wash raw chicken</strong> - it splashes bacteria around the bench. Use separate boards for raw meat, and cook chicken until the juices run clear</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<p>Contact your GP, after-hours clinic, or call Healthline (<strong>0800 611 116</strong>, free, 24/7) if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>You cannot keep any fluids down for more than a few hours</li>
  <li>There is blood in your vomit or stool</li>
  <li>Severe abdominal pain, or pain in one specific spot</li>
  <li>Fever over 39°C, or any fever lasting more than 48 hours</li>
  <li>Diarrhoea lasting more than 7 days</li>
  <li>Signs of dehydration - very dark urine, no urine for 8 hours or more, dizziness, dry mouth, sunken eyes, lethargy, or confusion</li>
  <li>You are pregnant, immunocompromised, frail, or have a long-term condition like diabetes or kidney disease</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> or go straight to the emergency department if a baby under 6 months has vomiting or diarrhoea, if a child is floppy or very drowsy or hard to wake, if there are no wet nappies for 6 hours or more in a baby, if there is severe ongoing tummy pain or vomiting that is bright green, or if anyone is confused, fainting, or unable to stand because of dehydration.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I need a stool test, or is it reasonable to wait and see?</li>
  <li>Should I pause any of my regular medicines while I am unwell - for example blood pressure tablets, diuretics, or metformin?</li>
  <li>When can I safely return to work, or send my child back to daycare?</li>
  <li>Does this need to be reported to public health, and do my close contacts need to do anything?</li>
</ul>
