<p>Testicular cancer is rare but it is the most common cancer in men aged 15 to 40 in New Zealand. Around 140 NZ men are diagnosed each year.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The good news is that it is one of the most treatable cancers we have. Five-year survival is over 95 percent overall and remains around 70 percent even when it has spread by the time of diagnosis.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Catching it early matters because early treatment is shorter, less aggressive, and more likely to preserve fertility. A monthly self-check takes about a minute.</p>

<h2>When to do it</h2>

<p>Once a month is plenty. Pick a regular time you will remember (the first of the month, the start of a shift cycle, your birthday date). The best moment is just after a warm shower or bath, when the scrotal skin is relaxed and the testicles sit lower and are easy to feel.</p>

<h2>How to do it</h2>

<ol>
  <li>Stand in front of a mirror and look. Note any new swelling, asymmetry, or change in the skin.</li>
  <li>Cup the scrotum gently in your hand. Each testicle should feel firm but not hard, smooth, and slightly springy. One sitting a little lower than the other is completely normal.</li>
  <li>Roll one testicle at a time between thumb and fingers of both hands. You are feeling the whole surface.</li>
  <li>At the back of each testicle you will feel a soft, slightly tubular structure - the epididymis. This is normal anatomy, not a lump. Above the epididymis you will feel the spermatic cord running upward.</li>
  <li>Compare sides. The two testicles should feel similar in texture, even if not identical in size.</li>
</ol>

<h2>What is normal</h2>

<ul>
  <li>One testicle slightly larger than the other</li>
  <li>One sitting lower than the other (usually the left)</li>
  <li>The epididymis at the back of the testicle as a soft, slightly tubular structure</li>
  <li>A dull awareness or slight ache after long hours of sitting or after intense exercise, that settles</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to take to your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A new lump or hard area on the testicle itself, even if it is small and painless</li>
  <li>A change in the size, shape, or feel of one testicle compared with the other</li>
  <li>A feeling of heaviness, fullness, or a dull ache in the scrotum, lower abdomen, or groin that does not settle</li>
  <li>A noticeable enlargement of one testicle that is not from injury</li>
  <li>Fluid build-up that has appeared without injury</li>
  <li>Any breast tissue tenderness or enlargement (rare, but a known presenting feature)</li>
</ul>

<p>Most lumps in the scrotum are not cancer. Cysts of the epididymis, varicoceles (varicose veins of the cord), and hydroceles (fluid around the testicle) are all common and benign. But you cannot tell the difference at home, and the consequence of waiting on a real cancer is much worse than the consequence of a quick GP check on a benign lump.</p>

<h2>Why men sometimes wait</h2>

<p>Embarrassment, fear, and the assumption that a young, fit man does not get cancer all delay presentation. New Zealand GPs see scrotal complaints constantly, with no judgement and no fuss. The exam takes a minute. An ultrasound, if needed, takes about fifteen.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<p>Book an appointment for any new lump on the testicle itself, any change in size or texture, or any persistent dull ache. Do not wait for it to get bigger. If there is sudden severe pain in one testicle - especially if it is acute, with nausea or vomiting - this can be testicular torsion, which is a surgical emergency: go to an emergency department or call <strong>111</strong>.</p>
