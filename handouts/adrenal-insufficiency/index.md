Adrenal insufficiency means your body cannot make enough cortisol - the stress hormone that keeps blood pressure, blood sugar, and many body systems working through illness. People with adrenal insufficiency take cortisol replacement (usually hydrocortisone) every day. The crucial extra knowledge is what to do during illness, injury, or surgery, when normal cortisol needs would surge but the replacement does not adjust automatically.

<h2>Who has adrenal insufficiency?</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Primary (Addison's disease)</strong> - usually autoimmune destruction of the adrenal glands. Less common</li>
<li><strong>Secondary</strong> - the pituitary gland cannot signal the adrenals. Caused by pituitary disease, surgery, or radiation</li>
<li><strong>Tertiary</strong> - long-term steroid treatment (prednisone over 5 mg daily for several weeks) suppresses the natural axis. The most common cause overall</li>
</ul>

<p>People being weaned off long-term steroid treatment may need months for their own cortisol production to return.</p>

<h2>Why daily cortisol matters</h2>

<p>Cortisol is essential for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Maintaining blood pressure</li>
<li>Maintaining blood glucose</li>
<li>Responding to stress and illness</li>
<li>Many metabolic processes</li>
</ul>

<p>Without enough cortisol, blood pressure can crash and blood glucose can fall during illness, leading to <strong>adrenal crisis</strong> - a medical emergency.</p>

<h2>Daily replacement</h2>

<p>Most people take:</p>

<ul>
<li>Hydrocortisone 10-30 mg daily, split into 2-3 doses (e.g. 10 mg morning, 5 mg lunchtime, 5 mg evening), OR</li>
<li>Prednisone 3-5 mg once daily, OR</li>
<li>Plenax-Plenadren (sustained release hydrocortisone), where available</li>
</ul>

<p>People with primary adrenal insufficiency also need fludrocortisone for salt balance.</p>

<h2>The sick day rules</h2>

<p>The basic principle: more stress = more cortisol needed. If your body cannot make extra, you must take it.</p>

<p><strong>Mild illness</strong> (cold, mild flu, minor procedure):</p>

<ul>
<li>Double your usual hydrocortisone for 2-3 days, or until clearly better</li>
<li>Continue normal eating and drinking</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Moderate illness</strong> (fever 38°C+, more significant infection, vomiting, dental procedures, broken bone):</p>

<ul>
<li>Triple your hydrocortisone for the duration of illness</li>
<li>Increase fluids - water with salt or electrolyte drink</li>
<li>Contact your GP same-day</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Severe illness, vomiting, or surgery:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>If unable to keep oral medicines down: use injectable hydrocortisone (100 mg IM/SC immediately)</li>
<li>Call 111 or go to ED</li>
</ul>

<p>People with adrenal insufficiency should always have an emergency injection kit at home and know how to use it. Family members should also know.</p>

<h2>Steroid emergency card and Medic Alert</h2>

<p>Carry an up-to-date steroid emergency card or wear a Medic Alert bracelet showing:</p>

<ul>
<li>Your diagnosis</li>
<li>Your daily dose</li>
<li>"In emergency, give 100 mg hydrocortisone IM"</li>
<li>Phone number for your endocrine specialist</li>
</ul>

<p>The NZ Endocrine Society and Hormone Foundation produce these cards.</p>

<h2>Adrenal crisis - the emergency</h2>

<p>Symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe weakness or collapse</li>
<li>Severe abdominal or back pain</li>
<li>Vomiting and diarrhoea</li>
<li>Confusion or drowsiness</li>
<li>Low blood pressure, fast heart rate</li>
<li>Low blood sugar</li>
<li>Sometimes fever</li>
</ul>

<p>What to do:</p>

<ol>
<li>Inject 100 mg hydrocortisone immediately (subcutaneous or intramuscular)</li>
<li>Call 111</li>
<li>Lie flat and elevate legs while waiting</li>
<li>Family or others present should give the injection if the patient cannot</li>
</ol>

<p>This is a true emergency. Adrenal crisis can be fatal within hours if untreated.</p>

<h2>Surgery and procedures</h2>

<p>Tell every surgeon, anaesthetist, dentist, and endoscopist about your adrenal insufficiency. They will arrange:</p>

<ul>
<li>Minor procedures (dental work, simple endoscopy): double or triple oral dose around the procedure</li>
<li>Major surgery: intravenous hydrocortisone before, during, and after</li>
<li>Childbirth: extra cover during labour and delivery</li>
</ul>

<h2>Travel</h2>

<ul>
<li>Carry plenty of hydrocortisone tablets (more than you need)</li>
<li>Carry the injection kit in hand luggage</li>
<li>Carry a doctor's letter explaining your condition</li>
<li>Travel insurance noting your condition</li>
<li>Identify hospitals at your destinations</li>
<li>Time zones do not matter much - stick to roughly the same schedule</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Most women with adrenal insufficiency have normal pregnancies. Doses often need adjusting in late pregnancy and labour. Plan ahead with your endocrinologist and obstetrician.</p>

<h2>Day-to-day life</h2>

<ul>
<li>Take all doses on schedule. Set phone alarms</li>
<li>Eat regular meals</li>
<li>Stay hydrated, especially in hot weather</li>
<li>Annual endocrinology review</li>
<li>Annual flu vaccine, COVID boosters as recommended</li>
<li>Know your sick day plan and refresh it yearly</li>
</ul>

<p>People with adrenal insufficiency live full, normal lives - the trick is knowing what to do when stress goes up.</p>
