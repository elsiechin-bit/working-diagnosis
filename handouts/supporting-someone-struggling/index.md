<p>One of the strongest predictors of recovery from mental illness is having someone in your corner. If you are reading this, you are probably already that person for someone. This handout is a practical guide: what to do, what to say, what to avoid, and the things that protect you while you support them.</p>

<h2>Notice</h2>

<p>The first part of supporting someone is recognising they are struggling. Common signs:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Withdrawal from things they normally enjoy</li>
  <li>Mood changes that persist - sad, anxious, flat, irritable</li>
  <li>Sleep changes - too much, too little, disrupted</li>
  <li>Appetite or weight changes</li>
  <li>Drinking more or using more</li>
  <li>Forgetting things, lower productivity at work or study</li>
  <li>Increased physical complaints - headaches, stomach issues, fatigue</li>
  <li>Talking about themselves negatively, hopelessness, "everyone would be better off without me"</li>
  <li>Risk-taking, self-neglect, or unusual behaviour</li>
</ul>

<p>Trust your sense if something is off. People who know someone well are often better at noticing than the person themselves.</p>

<h2>Open the conversation</h2>

<p>The hardest part. A few openings that work:</p>

<ul>
  <li>"I have noticed you have seemed [tired/quiet/down/stretched] recently. How are you actually doing?"</li>
  <li>"I have been thinking about you. Want to grab a coffee?"</li>
  <li>"I am here if there is anything going on you want to talk about."</li>
  <li>"You have been on my mind - is there anything happening for you?"</li>
</ul>

<p>The phrase "how are you actually doing" lands differently than "how are you". The "actually" gives permission to drop the polite answer.</p>

<p>Pick a time when you are both unhurried. Walking together is often better than face-to-face - reduces the intensity. A drive, a walk, a quiet meal.</p>

<h2>Listen, not fix</h2>

<p>The temptation is to solve, fix, advise. The thing most struggling people actually need first is to feel heard. The hierarchy:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Listen.</strong> Reflect back what you are hearing.</li>
  <li><strong>Acknowledge.</strong> "That sounds really hard." "I am sorry you are going through this."</li>
  <li><strong>Ask, don't tell.</strong> "What do you think would help?" "Is there anything I can do?"</li>
  <li><strong>Only suggest if they want suggestions.</strong> "Would it be useful to think about who you might talk to?"</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps to say</h2>

<ul>
  <li>"I am here for you."</li>
  <li>"This is not your fault."</li>
  <li>"Lots of people go through this."</li>
  <li>"You do not have to figure it out alone."</li>
  <li>"What can I do that would actually help?"</li>
  <li>"It makes sense that you feel this way."</li>
  <li>"Take your time. I am not going anywhere."</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to avoid saying</h2>

<ul>
  <li>"At least..." (minimises)</li>
  <li>"Everyone goes through this." (dismissive)</li>
  <li>"You just need to..." (judgemental, unhelpful)</li>
  <li>"Have you tried [exercise / meditation / supplements]?" (well-intentioned but lands as fixing)</li>
  <li>"Why do not you just leave / quit / move on / let it go?" (oversimplifies)</li>
  <li>"You are too young / too old / too lucky / too anything to feel this way."</li>
  <li>"Cheer up, it will pass."</li>
  <li>Comparing to your own or others' worse situations</li>
</ul>

<p>Most of these come from a place of caring. They land badly anyway.</p>

<h2>Practical help</h2>

<p>Specific, concrete help often matters more than emotional support:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Bring a meal, do their dishes, take the kids for an afternoon</li>
  <li>Drive them to a GP appointment</li>
  <li>Sit with them while they make a phone call they have been avoiding</li>
  <li>Run errands - groceries, chemist, post office</li>
  <li>Take care of small things they have lost the energy for - paying a bill, washing</li>
  <li>Walk with them daily - movement plus company</li>
  <li>Watch a movie together - not every interaction needs to be about the problem</li>
</ul>

<p>"Let me know if you need anything" is well-meaning but rarely takes up. People who are struggling do not have the energy to identify what they need and ask. Specific offers work better: "I am bringing dinner Tuesday at 6, that work?"</p>

<h2>If they need professional help</h2>

<p>You can encourage but not force (in most cases). What helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>"It might help to talk to your GP. Want me to help you book?"</li>
  <li>Offer to come with them. Many people are intimidated by GP appointments for mental health.</li>
  <li>If they are unsure, suggest 1737 first - free, confidential, low-stakes</li>
  <li>Help them research options - therapists, online programmes, peer support</li>
  <li>If cost is a barrier, mention free options - Just A Thought, GP brief intervention, 1737</li>
</ul>

<p>Some people will refuse help repeatedly. Continuing to be present, without pressure, often eventually opens the door.</p>

<h2>If they mention suicide or self-harm</h2>

<p>Take it seriously. Always. The myth that asking about suicide can plant the idea is wrong - asking directly is one of the most protective things you can do.</p>

<ul>
  <li>"Are you having thoughts of ending your life?" (direct, calm, non-judgemental)</li>
  <li>If yes, ask: "Do you have a plan?" "Do you have access to means?" "What is keeping you safe right now?"</li>
  <li>Do not promise to keep it secret. Their safety comes first.</li>
  <li>Help them connect with support - 1737, 0508 828 865, GP, ED</li>
  <li>If immediate danger, call 111 or take them to ED</li>
  <li>Help reduce access to means if possible (medications, firearms)</li>
  <li>Stay with them until they are with another support person or service</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are unsure, call 1737 yourself - they support people supporting others.</p>

<h2>Look after yourself</h2>

<p>Supporting someone with mental illness is exhausting. The patterns that protect you:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>You cannot pour from an empty cup.</strong> Sleep, eat, exercise, see other people. The basics matter more, not less, when you are caregiving.</li>
  <li><strong>You are not their therapist.</strong> Be a friend, partner, family member - encourage them toward professional help, do not try to be it.</li>
  <li><strong>Set limits.</strong> "I cannot talk after 10pm but I am here in the morning." "I can come Saturday but not Sunday." Your limits are not abandonment.</li>
  <li><strong>Have your own people.</strong> Talk to friends, your own therapist, peer support groups for family of people with mental illness. The Mental Health Foundation has resources for supporters.</li>
  <li><strong>Know the warning signs in yourself.</strong> Burnout, depression, anxiety, drinking more, withdrawing - all common in carers. Get help early.</li>
  <li><strong>Recognise when professional help is needed.</strong> Some situations are beyond what a non-clinician can manage. Severe psychosis, mania, severe eating disorder, suicidal crisis - these need professionals.</li>
</ul>

<h2>For families of people with serious mental illness</h2>

<p>If you are supporting someone with bipolar, schizophrenia, major depression, severe eating disorder, or significant addiction, the stakes and demands are higher. NZ services for family:</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.supportingfamilies.org.nz/">Supporting Families NZ</a> - peer support and education for families</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.balance.org.nz/">Balance NZ</a> - bipolar family support</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.ed.org.nz/">EDANZ</a> - eating disorder family support</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.al-anon.org.nz/">Al-Anon</a> - for families affected by someone's drinking</li>
  <li><strong>Gam-Anon</strong> - for families affected by problem gambling</li>
</ul>
