<p>A steroid (corticosteroid) injection into a painful joint is a common treatment in NZ general practice. It is used for conditions such as osteoarthritis of the knee, hip, or shoulder, frozen shoulder, trigger finger, and some inflammatory joint problems. The injection delivers anti-inflammatory medicine directly into the joint, where it works locally to reduce swelling and pain. It will not change the underlying joint, but it can give you a useful window of relief to get on top of exercise, weight management, or physiotherapy.</p>

<h2>What to expect in the first 48 hours</h2>

<p>Most people feel fine to drive home and carry on with their day. Some experience a brief flare of pain in the first 24 to 48 hours - this is called a post-injection flare and is usually due to the steroid crystals irritating the joint before they dissolve. It settles on its own.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest the joint relatively.</strong> Avoid heavy use, sport, and prolonged standing or walking for the first 24 to 48 hours. Normal walking and gentle movement is fine and helpful.</li>
  <li><strong>Use ice if it is sore.</strong> A bag of frozen peas wrapped in a tea towel for 15 to 20 minutes at a time, several times a day. Never apply ice directly to skin.</li>
  <li><strong>Paracetamol or anti-inflammatories</strong> (such as ibuprofen) are fine to take if you need them. Neither interferes with the steroid.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep the injection site dry for 24 hours.</strong> A normal shower the next day is fine. Avoid baths, spa pools, and swimming until at least 48 hours have passed.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When the steroid actually starts working</h2>

<p>If local anaesthetic was mixed with the steroid, you may notice the joint feels numb or pain-free for the first few hours and then sore again as the anaesthetic wears off. The steroid effect itself builds gradually over 3 to 7 days, and most people notice clear improvement by week two. Maximum benefit is usually felt around 4 to 6 weeks. Pain relief commonly lasts 2 to 3 months, sometimes longer; for some people the effect is shorter, especially in advanced osteoarthritis.</p>

<h2>Use the window well</h2>

<p>The injection is most useful as a stepping stone, not a destination. While the joint is calmer:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Strengthen the muscles around the joint.</strong> A physiotherapist (often subsidised through ACC if your joint pain followed an injury) can give you specific exercises for the knee, hip, or shoulder.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep moving.</strong> Walking, swimming, and cycling are generally well tolerated. Build up gradually rather than going hard early.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce joint loading where possible.</strong> For lower-limb joints, even a few kilograms of weight loss meaningfully reduces force across the knee with every step.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan ahead.</strong> If you have an event or holiday coming up, talk with your GP about timing future injections to coincide with peak benefit.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Repeat injections</h2>

<p>Steroid joint injections are not limited to a fixed number in a lifetime, but most NZ GPs follow guidance suggesting no more than three to four injections into the same joint per year, with at least three months between them. This spacing reflects safety advice rather than a hard rule. If your relief is short-lived, or each injection works less well than the last, that is useful information - it usually means the underlying joint problem needs a different plan. That can include further physiotherapy, weight and activity changes, specialist referral, or considering joint replacement surgery.</p>

<h2>Side effects and what to watch for</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A short-lived flare of joint pain in the first 1 to 2 days, as above.</li>
  <li>A small bruise or pinprick mark at the injection site.</li>
  <li>Facial flushing or warmth in the day or two afterwards (uncommon, settles on its own).</li>
  <li>A temporary rise in blood sugar for a few days if you have diabetes - worth checking your readings more often during this time.</li>
  <li>A small, temporary dimple or paler patch in the skin over the injection site (rare, more common with smaller joints).</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to contact your GP urgently</h2>

<p>Joint infection after an injection is rare but serious. Contact your GP, an after-hours clinic, or Healthline (<strong>0800 611 116</strong>, free 24/7) if you have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Increasing redness, heat, or swelling at the joint after 48 hours, especially if it is spreading</li>
  <li>Severe pain that is getting worse rather than easing</li>
  <li>Fever, chills, or feeling unwell</li>
  <li>Pus or discharge from the injection site</li>
  <li>Inability to move the joint</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>How long do you expect this injection to help, given the state of my joint?</li>
  <li>What is the longer-term plan if it works well, or if it does not?</li>
  <li>Am I a candidate for physiotherapy through ACC, or under the public system?</li>
  <li>Should I be referred to an orthopaedic specialist now or later?</li>
</ul>
