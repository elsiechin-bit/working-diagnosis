<p>Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is an autoimmune condition where your immune system attacks the lining of your joints, causing inflammation, pain, and joint damage. The good news is that modern medicines called disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs) can control inflammation and prevent permanent damage if started early. This handout explains what helps.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Symmetrical joint pain and swelling (usually both sides of the body - both wrists, both hands)</li>
  <li>Morning stiffness lasting more than an hour</li>
  <li>Warmth and tenderness in affected joints</li>
  <li>Fatigue and sometimes low-grade fever</li>
  <li>Gradual swelling and deformity of joints if untreated</li>
  <li>May feel generally unwell</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why early treatment matters</h2>

<p>RA is progressive and can cause permanent joint damage and disability if inflammation is not controlled. Starting disease-modifying medicines (DMARDs) early - ideally within 3 months of symptom onset - significantly reduces long-term joint damage. Early aggressive treatment offers the best outcome.</p>

<h2>Disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs)</h2>

<p><strong>DMARDs are the cornerstone of RA treatment.</strong> The most commonly prescribed first-line DMARD is methotrexate, which suppresses the immune system to reduce inflammation. Other DMARDs include sulfasalazine and leflunomide. Your rheumatologist will often combine DMARDs or add biologic medicines (which target specific immune cells) for better control. DMARDs take several weeks to work, so initial pain relief with anti-inflammatories is important while DMARDs take effect.</p>

<h2>What actually helps alongside medicines</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest and protect inflamed joints.</strong> Use splints or braces during flares to reduce stress on swollen joints and manage pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Stay active within pain limits.</strong> Gentle exercise maintains joint function and muscle strength. Swimming and range-of-motion exercises are often well tolerated.</li>
  <li><strong>Physiotherapy.</strong> A physiotherapist can teach you exercises and techniques to protect joints and maintain function as your disease is controlled.</li>
  <li><strong>Use heat and ice.</strong> Heat before exercise helps tissues relax; ice after activity reduces inflammation.</li>
  <li><strong>Pain relief while DMARDs work.</strong> Paracetamol and non-steroidal anti-inflammatories (NSAIDs) help manage pain in early disease while waiting for DMARDs to take effect.</li>
  <li><strong>Adapt your environment.</strong> Use ergonomic tools, lever handles, jar openers, and other aids to reduce stress on small joints.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage fatigue.</strong> Pacing activity, regular rest periods, and good sleep help manage the fatigue that often comes with RA.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Monitoring and adjustments</h2>

<p>Your doctor will monitor inflammation with blood tests (inflammatory markers like CRP and ESR, plus rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP antibodies). Treatment is adjusted based on how well your disease is controlled and your tolerance of medicines. The goal is remission or low disease activity - essentially controlling inflammation so you feel well and prevent joint damage.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor or rheumatologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You develop joint pain and stiffness suggestive of RA</li>
  <li>Your symptoms are not controlled with current medicines</li>
  <li>You develop side effects from medicines</li>
  <li>You have signs of infection (fever, chills) while on DMARDs</li>
  <li>Your joints are swelling more or showing signs of damage</li>
  <li>You are pregnant or planning pregnancy (some DMARDs need adjustment)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor or rheumatologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What DMARD will I start, and how long before it works?</li>
  <li>What blood tests will be monitored?</li>
  <li>What side effects should I watch for?</li>
  <li>Can I have children while taking these medicines?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist?</li>
  <li>Will I need biologic medicines?</li>
</ul>
