<p>Lower back pain is one of the most common reasons people visit a GP in New Zealand. The great majority of episodes - even when the pain feels severe - improve significantly within 4-6 weeks. This handout explains what is usually happening, and what you can do to recover as quickly as possible.</p>

<h2>What is usually causing the pain?</h2>

<p>In most cases of sudden lower back pain, there is no serious underlying disease. The pain comes from muscles, tendons, or joints in the spine being strained or irritated. Imaging (x-rays or MRI scans) often shows very little, and the findings frequently do not match how much pain a person feels. This can be frustrating to hear, but it is actually reassuring - it means you are not "broken" and recovery is the rule, not the exception.</p>

<h2>The most important thing: keep moving</h2>

<p>Staying active is the most important thing you can do for your recovery. Bed rest makes back pain worse, not better. Muscles weaken quickly when they are not used, and the spine needs movement to stay healthy.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Try to continue normal activities as much as the pain allows</li>
  <li>Walking is excellent - start with short distances and build up gradually</li>
  <li>Avoid activities that make the pain significantly worse, but gentle movement throughout the day helps</li>
  <li>Avoid prolonged sitting - get up and move briefly every 30-40 minutes</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pain relief</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Heat.</strong> A heat pack or warm shower can relax tight muscles and ease pain. Use for 15-20 minutes at a time.</li>
  <li><strong>Paracetamol</strong> can help with pain and is safe for most people. Take at regular intervals (rather than only when it is very bad) while the pain is acute.</li>
  <li><strong>Anti-inflammatories</strong> such as ibuprofen can be more effective for back pain than paracetamol, especially if there is muscle spasm. Take with food and avoid if you have stomach, kidney, or heart problems. Check with your pharmacist or GP first.</li>
  <li><strong>Topical gels</strong> (anti-inflammatory creams rubbed into the skin) can help and have fewer side effects than tablets.</li>
</ul>

<p>Strong opioid medicines (such as codeine or tramadol) are generally not recommended for lower back pain - they do not work well for this type of pain and carry significant risks including dependence.</p>

<h2>What else helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Physiotherapy</strong> is helpful for back pain that is not settling or for learning exercises to prevent recurrence. Your GP can refer you, or ACC may cover treatment if the injury was work-related or from an accident.</li>
  <li><strong>Exercise.</strong> Swimming, walking, yoga, and Pilates are all good for back health in the longer term. Core strengthening exercises (which your physiotherapist can prescribe) help protect the spine.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep position.</strong> If sleeping is difficult, try lying on your side with a pillow between your knees, or on your back with a pillow under your knees.</li>
  <li><strong>Stress and mood.</strong> Anxiety, low mood, and stress genuinely increase back pain. If you are struggling emotionally, address this alongside the physical pain - it makes a real difference.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Warning signs - see your GP urgently</h2>

<p>Most lower back pain is not serious, but some symptoms need prompt assessment. Contact your GP the same day or go to emergency if you have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Numbness, tingling, or weakness in one or both legs</li>
  <li>Loss of bladder or bowel control, or numbness in the groin or inner thighs - this is a medical emergency, call 111</li>
  <li>Pain that is constant and not relieved at all by rest or position change</li>
  <li>Significant pain after a fall or injury, especially if you are older or take steroids</li>
  <li>Unexplained weight loss alongside back pain</li>
  <li>Back pain with fever</li>
  <li>Back pain that has not improved at all after 4-6 weeks of self-care</li>
</ul>

<h2>ACC cover</h2>

<p>If your back pain started from a specific injury - at work, in an accident, or during sport - you may be covered by ACC. Talk to your GP or physiotherapist about lodging a claim. ACC cover can significantly reduce the cost of treatment.</p>
