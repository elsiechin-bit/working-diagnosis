<p>Functional constipation is one of the most common gastrointestinal problems of childhood, accounting for approximately 3-5% of all paediatric primary care visits.<sup>1</sup> It causes significant distress to children and families, is frequently under-treated, and has a high rate of relapse if laxative treatment is stopped too early. The majority of childhood constipation is functional (no underlying organic cause), but red flags for organic causes must be excluded.</p>

<h2>Presentation and diagnosis</h2>
<p>Functional constipation is a clinical diagnosis using the Rome IV criteria: two or more of the following for at least 4 weeks in a child under 4 years (or for at least 2 months in older children): two or fewer defaecations per week; at least one episode per week of incontinence after toilet training; a history of retentive posturing or excessive voluntary stool retention; history of painful or hard bowel motions; presence of a large faecal mass in the rectum; history of large diameter stools that obstruct the toilet.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>The clinical picture often involves a cycle of painful defaecation leading to stool withholding, which leads to harder, larger stools, which leads to more pain and more withholding. Once a faecal mass (impaction) accumulates in the rectum, overflow incontinence can develop - parents may describe frequent small liquid or paste-like accidents that look like diarrhoea. This overflow faecal incontinence is not diarrhoea and treating it as such (by stopping laxatives) makes the underlying constipation worse.</p>

<p>The Bristol Stool Chart is a useful objective tool for describing stool consistency in the clinic and for families at home. Type 1-2 stools (hard lumps, sausage with lumps) indicate constipation; type 3-4 are normal; type 5-7 indicate loose stools.</p>

<h2>Red flags requiring investigation</h2>
<p>Organic causes account for under 5% of childhood constipation but must not be missed. Red flags warranting further investigation include: onset in the first month of life (especially if associated with delayed passage of meconium beyond 48 hours - consider Hirschsprung's disease); ribbon stools; failure to thrive; abdominal distension; sacral dimple or skin changes at the base of the spine (suggest spinal dysraphism); neurological signs in the lower limbs; blood in the stool without a clear anorectal cause.<sup>1</sup></p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>In uncomplicated functional constipation, investigation is not required. Plain abdominal X-ray is not recommended as a routine tool for diagnosing constipation or guiding management - clinical assessment is sufficient. It may be considered if rectal examination is not possible or the history is atypical. Blood tests (thyroid function, coeliac antibodies, serum calcium) are only indicated if the history or examination suggests an organic cause.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p><strong>Disimpaction first if faecal impaction is present:</strong> impaction (a palpable abdominal or rectal mass of hard stool) must be cleared before maintenance laxative treatment is effective. Macrogol (polyethylene glycol with electrolytes) is the agent of choice for disimpaction in NZ. The NZF for Children recommends escalating doses of macrogol over 6-8 days (using 13.8 g sachets of Movicol Paediatric Plain or equivalent): start with 2 sachets per day in children 1-5 years and increase by 2 sachets every 2 days up to a maximum of 8 sachets per day, until the impaction clears.<sup>3</sup> The goal is soft, liquid-ish stools for 24 hours, indicating the impaction has passed. Parents should be warned that the high doses used in disimpaction may produce cramping and watery stools.</p>

<p><strong>Maintenance laxative therapy:</strong> once disimpaction is achieved, reduce to a maintenance dose sufficient to produce soft, comfortable stools (Bristol 3-4) once daily. Macrogol is first-line: typically 1 sachet per day in children 1-5 years, adjusted to response. Lactulose is an alternative but is less effective than macrogol and causes more bloating. Stimulant laxatives (senna, bisacodyl) may be added as a second agent if macrogol alone is insufficient.</p>

<p>Maintenance treatment should continue for a minimum of 6 weeks after successful bowel habit is established, and often for 3-6 months or longer. Relapse is common if laxatives are stopped too early. Taper the dose gradually rather than stopping abruptly.</p>

<p><strong>Toilet training and behaviour:</strong> regular toilet sits after meals (utilising the gastrocolic reflex), a footstool to support the feet, and positive reinforcement are important behavioural components. Toilet anxiety is common in children who have experienced painful defaecation; a calm, non-pressured approach with praise (not punishment) is essential. A bowel diary can help monitor progress.</p>

<p><strong>Diet and fluid:</strong> increase fluid intake and dietary fibre (fruit, vegetables, whole grains). Diet modification alone is rarely sufficient to resolve established constipation but is an important adjunct to laxative therapy. Cow's milk protein intolerance is occasionally associated with functional constipation in toddlers; a 2-4 week cow's milk elimination trial may be worth attempting if macrogol response is poor and there are associated GI symptoms.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to paediatric gastroenterology or general paediatrics for: any red flag features (see above); failure to respond to adequate macrogol therapy after 3 months; severe faecal incontinence requiring specialist behavioural support; suspected Hirschsprung's disease (rectal suction biopsy in secondary care); constipation associated with significant failure to thrive.</p>
