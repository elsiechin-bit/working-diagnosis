<p>Developmental surveillance is the continuous, longitudinal process of monitoring a child's progress across developmental domains through clinical observation, structured assessment, and parental concern. The GP is frequently the integration point for a picture distributed across Plunket, early childhood education, and specialist services. Early identification of developmental problems changes outcomes - the earlier intervention begins, the better the prognosis for children with developmental delay, autism spectrum disorder (ASD), and communication disorders.</p>

<h2>The Well Child Tamariki Ora (WCTO) schedule</h2>
<p>The WCTO programme provides funded structured developmental surveillance contacts from birth to age 5, delivered primarily by Plunket nurses, community nurses, and GPs.<sup>1</sup> Core contacts occur at:</p>

<ul>
  <li>3-5 days (midwife or LMC), 4-6 weeks (GP), 3 months, 5 months, 7 months, 9-10 months, 12 months (Plunket/community nurse)</li>
  <li>15 months, 2 years, 3.5 years, 4-5 years (Plunket/community nurse; B4 School Check at 4 years is GP or practice nurse in many areas)</li>
</ul>

<p>Each contact includes growth monitoring (weight, length/height, head circumference plotted on WHO growth charts), developmental screening across domains (gross motor, fine motor, language, social/emotional), hearing and vision checks, immunisation review, and family wellbeing assessment including parental mental health. The B4 School Check at age 4 specifically includes a validated developmental questionnaire (Ages and Stages Questionnaire, ASQ-3) and vision and hearing screening.</p>

<h2>Developmental domains and age-banded milestones</h2>
<p>Key milestones by domain (approximate ages; there is normal variation within the ranges below):</p>

<p><strong>Social and communication:</strong> social smile by 6-8 weeks; babble by 4-6 months; waves bye-bye by 9-10 months; first words (3-5 meaningful words) by 12-15 months; two-word phrases by 18-24 months; short sentences by 2-3 years; understood by strangers approximately 75% of the time by age 3.</p>

<p><strong>Gross motor:</strong> head control by 3-4 months; rolls by 5-6 months; sits unsupported by 7-9 months; pulls to stand by 9-10 months; walks independently by 9-15 months; runs by 18 months; up and down stairs by 2-2.5 years.</p>

<p><strong>Fine motor:</strong> reaches and grasps by 4-5 months; transfers objects between hands by 6-7 months; pincer grip by 9-10 months; stacks 2-3 blocks by 15 months; draws a line by 2 years; draws a circle by 3 years.</p>

<p><strong>Vision and hearing:</strong> follows a face by 6-8 weeks; fixes and follows a toy by 3 months. Any parental concern about hearing warrants audiological assessment regardless of age.</p>

<h2>Red flags warranting prompt referral at any age</h2>
<p>The following should prompt urgent developmental paediatric referral, regardless of the child's current age or prior developmental surveillance results:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Developmental regression</strong> - loss of any previously acquired skill, at any age, is never benign without a clear cause. This is the most important red flag in developmental medicine.</li>
  <li>No social smile by 8 weeks</li>
  <li>No babble by 9 months</li>
  <li>No joint attention (pointing, showing, sharing gaze) by 12 months</li>
  <li>No single words by 16 months</li>
  <li>No two-word spontaneous phrases by 24 months</li>
  <li>Any loss of language or social skills at any age</li>
  <li>No independent walking by 18 months</li>
  <li>Parental concern about possible ASD at any age</li>
</ul>

<p>Parental instinct has strong positive predictive value. "Something feels off" from a parent who knows their child is a significant data point. Take it seriously.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Hearing test: a formal audiological assessment should be arranged for any child with language delay or parental concern about hearing. Glue ear (OME) is a treatable cause of apparent language delay and should be actively excluded. Vision: formal ophthalmic assessment if visual concern; referral to orthoptist or ophthalmology for strabismus or suspected visual impairment.</p>

<p>The B4 School Check includes a validated hearing screen (Welch Allyn AudioScope or pure tone audiometry) and vision screen (Leicester chart or Snellen equivalent). Results feed back to the GP and any concerns require follow-up audiological or ophthalmological assessment.</p>

<p>Routine blood tests, MRI, and metabolic screening are not required in uncomplicated developmental delay. Investigations are guided by the clinical picture in secondary care. A paediatrician will often arrange chromosomal microarray in a child with global developmental delay, and thyroid function and FBC are reasonable baseline tests in a child with global delay presenting to the GP without a prior workup.</p>

<h2>Referral and pathways</h2>
<p>In NZ, referral for developmental assessment typically goes to: community paediatrics (developmental paediatrician), child development teams (in larger centres), or directly to speech-language therapy for isolated communication concerns. Waiting lists for paediatric developmental assessment are long across NZ; early referral is essential. Early intervention services (such as Ministry of Education specialist support) can be engaged before a formal diagnosis and can provide meaningful support during the wait.</p>

<p>For suspected ASD: in NZ, diagnosis currently requires assessment by a multidisciplinary team (paediatrician and psychologist, with input from speech-language therapist where indicated). The Autism Spectrum Disorder Indicators (MCHAT-R at 18-24 months, or similar screening tools) can support the decision to refer but are not diagnostic. The GP letter referring for ASD assessment should document the specific behaviours of concern, their duration, and the setting in which they occur.</p>
