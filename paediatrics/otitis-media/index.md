<p>Acute otitis media (AOM) is one of the most common bacterial infections of childhood and the most frequent reason children receive antibiotics in primary care. New Zealand has among the highest rates of childhood ear disease in the developed world, with marked disparities affecting Maori and Pacific children. Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) - persistent discharge through a perforated eardrum - affects approximately 4% of Maori and Pacific children, compared with under 1% of NZ European children.<sup>1</sup> Hearing loss from chronic ear disease is a driver of language delay and educational disadvantage.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>AOM presents with rapid onset of ear pain (otalgia), often accompanied by fever and irritability, frequently following a few days of viral upper respiratory illness. In infants and pre-verbal children, non-specific signs predominate: fever, irritability, pulling at the ear, disturbed sleep, poor feeding, and vomiting. Otalgia in a verbal child points to the diagnosis, but ear pain also occurs in otitis externa, referred pain from tonsillitis, and dental infection.</p>

<p>Diagnosis requires: (a) rapid onset of symptoms, (b) evidence of middle ear effusion on otoscopy (bulging tympanic membrane, reduced mobility on pneumatic otoscopy, visible air-fluid level), and (c) signs of middle ear inflammation (TM erythema, distinct otalgia, or perforation with discharge). A red tympanic membrane alone, without bulging, is insufficient for diagnosis of AOM; it may simply reflect a crying child.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>AOM with perforation is identified by visible discharge through the tympanic membrane. This represents a more severe form and antibiotics are indicated regardless of age.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>AOM is a clinical diagnosis. Routine investigations (FBC, CRP) are not required unless systemic infection or complications are suspected. Tympanometry is useful for detecting middle ear effusion but does not distinguish AOM from OME and is not available in most NZ GP practices. Audiological assessment is indicated if hearing loss is suspected or if the child has recurrent episodes with concern about developmental impact.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>NZ guidelines (following BPAC NZ) recommend a "watchful waiting" approach for most children aged 2 years and over with mild-to-moderate AOM, with adequate analgesia as the priority.<sup>3</sup> The rationale is that 80% of uncomplicated AOM episodes resolve without antibiotics within 72 hours, and antibiotic treatment reduces the duration of pain by less than one day while contributing to resistance and adverse effects.</p>

<p><strong>Analgesia first:</strong> paracetamol 15 mg/kg 4-6 hourly and/or ibuprofen 5-10 mg/kg 6-8 hourly (in children over 3 months) provides meaningful pain relief and should be the initial recommendation in all cases.</p>

<p><strong>Antibiotics are indicated</strong> in the following situations:<sup>3</sup></p>
<ul>
  <li>Age under 2 years (higher risk of complications, less likely to resolve spontaneously)</li>
  <li>Bilateral AOM (higher rate of bacterial aetiology and complications)</li>
  <li>AOM with perforation and discharge</li>
  <li>Severe symptoms (high fever, severe pain, very unwell child)</li>
  <li>AOM in a child with significant comorbidities (immunodeficiency, craniofacial abnormality, cochlear implant)</li>
  <li>Failure to improve after 48-72 hours of watchful waiting</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Antibiotic choice:</strong> amoxicillin 40 mg/kg/day in 3 divided doses (max 1.5 g/day) for 5 days. Where amoxicillin-resistant organisms are suspected (prior antibiotic treatment, failure to respond after 48-72 hours), amoxicillin-clavulanate (Augmentin) is appropriate. A "delayed prescription" strategy - where a prescription is provided but parents are instructed to fill it only if symptoms worsen or fail to improve after 48 hours - is as effective as immediate prescribing and reduces antibiotic use.</p>

<p><strong>CSOM management:</strong> topical ciprofloxacin ear drops are first-line (aminoglycosides are ototoxic and should not be used with a perforated eardrum). Keep the ear dry (cotton ball plug when bathing; no swimming while discharging). ENT referral for all persistent CSOM. Systemic antibiotics are not routinely indicated unless mastoiditis or systemic infection is present.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Urgent/same-day referral for: mastoiditis (post-auricular swelling, erythema, and tenderness; protruding ear); facial nerve palsy; suspected intracranial complication (meningism, severe headache, altered consciousness); severe systemic illness.</p>

<p>Routine ENT referral for: recurrent AOM (3 or more episodes in 6 months, or 4 or more in 12 months); persistent AOM not responding to antibiotics; all persistent CSOM; any child with AOM and significant associated hearing loss or developmental concern.</p>
