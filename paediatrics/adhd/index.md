<p>Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is the most common neurodevelopmental condition diagnosed in NZ children, affecting approximately 5-8% of school-age children.<sup>1</sup> It is characterised by persistent, impairing patterns of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity that are inconsistent with developmental level. ADHD is frequently under-diagnosed in girls (who more often present with the inattentive subtype without hyperactivity) and over-diagnosed in children whose difficulties are better explained by anxiety, trauma, or learning disabilities.</p>

<h2>Presentation and diagnosis</h2>
<p>DSM-5 criteria require: six or more symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity (five or more in adolescents aged 17 and over); symptoms present in at least two settings; evidence of functional impairment; symptom onset before age 12; symptoms not better explained by another mental disorder.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p><strong>Inattentive symptoms</strong> include: difficulty sustaining attention in tasks, frequent careless errors, not seeming to listen when spoken to directly, failing to follow through on instructions, difficulty organising tasks, avoiding tasks requiring sustained mental effort, losing things necessary for tasks, being easily distracted, and forgetfulness in daily activities.</p>

<p><strong>Hyperactive-impulsive symptoms</strong> include: fidgeting, leaving seat in situations where remaining seated is expected, running or climbing inappropriately, unable to play quietly, "on the go" as if driven by a motor, excessive talking, blurting out answers, difficulty waiting for a turn, and interrupting or intruding on others.</p>

<p>The clinical assessment involves: structured interview with parents and the child; review of school information (teacher report, school reports); use of validated rating scales - the Conners 3 or Vanderbilt ADHD Rating Scales are commonly used in NZ, with both parent and teacher forms; assessment of comorbidities (anxiety, learning difficulties, ASD, oppositional defiant disorder, sleep disorder); review of developmental history; hearing test (if not recently documented) to exclude hearing impairment as a contributor to inattention.</p>

<p>Important: ADHD symptoms must be present across settings and represent a persistent pattern, not a situational response to stress, family disruption, or school-based difficulties. A child who is attentive at home but distractible at school, or vice versa, may have a situational rather than neurodevelopmental explanation for the presentation.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>There is no diagnostic blood test, imaging, or neuropsychological test that confirms ADHD. The diagnosis is clinical, based on history, observation, and standardised rating scales. Routine blood tests are not indicated prior to diagnosis; thyroid function and FBC may be relevant if the clinical picture suggests an underlying medical cause for the symptoms. Formal neuropsychological testing is useful if learning disability is suspected alongside ADHD but is not required for the ADHD diagnosis itself.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Management combines pharmacological and non-pharmacological approaches and should be individualised. The NZ clinical pathway for ADHD diagnosis in children is typically led by a paediatrician or child and adolescent psychiatrist; in NZ, stimulant prescribing in children under 16 requires specialist initiation, after which GPs manage ongoing prescribing and monitoring.</p>

<p><strong>Non-pharmacological:</strong> parent training programmes (e.g., Triple P, Circle of Security) have good evidence for improving behaviour and reducing family stress. School-based accommodations (extra time, seated near the front, task chunking, movement breaks) are important and the GP can support the family in advocating for these. Regular physical activity has a meaningful effect on ADHD symptoms and should be encouraged. Adequate sleep is critical; many children with ADHD have sleep difficulties which exacerbate inattention.</p>

<p><strong>Pharmacological:</strong> methylphenidate (Ritalin, Rubifen, Concerta) is first-line and is subsidised in NZ under Special Authority criteria, which requires specialist initiation in children under 16. It is a dopamine and noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor and has robust evidence for improving attention, reducing hyperactivity, and improving academic and social functioning.<sup>3</sup> Starting doses are low (typically 2.5-5 mg for immediate-release, titrated slowly); most children require dose adjustment over the first weeks to months. Appetite suppression and sleep disturbance are common and dose-dependent. Regular monitoring of height, weight, blood pressure, and heart rate is required.</p>

<p>Dexamfetamine (Dexedrine) and atomoxetine (Strattera) are alternatives where methylphenidate is not tolerated or is insufficiently effective; these are initiated by specialists. Guanfacine (Intuniv) has a role in children with prominent hyperactivity and impulsivity, particularly where stimulants are not tolerated.</p>

<p><strong>GP monitoring:</strong> once stable on treatment, the GP typically manages 3-6 monthly reviews covering: symptom control (parent and teacher feedback), growth (plot on chart), blood pressure and heart rate, sleep quality, mood and appetite, and any emerging side effects. Annual specialist review is recommended but may be less frequent once stable.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to paediatrics or child psychiatry for: initial ADHD assessment where the diagnosis is uncertain, the child is under 5, there are significant comorbidities (ASD, anxiety, conduct disorder, learning disability), or stimulant prescribing is being considered. In NZ, referral is required before initiating stimulant medication in children under 16. Once stable on treatment and with specialist guidance, ongoing monitoring and prescribing can be managed in general practice.</p>
