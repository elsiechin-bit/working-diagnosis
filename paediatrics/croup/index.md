<p>Croup (laryngotracheobronchitis) is the most common cause of acute upper airway obstruction in children aged 6 months to 5 years, with a peak incidence at age 1-2. It is predominantly caused by parainfluenza virus (types 1 and 3) and typically presents with the characteristic barking cough and inspiratory stridor, often beginning at night. The illness is usually mild and self-limiting, but a small proportion of children develop significant airway compromise requiring hospital management.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>The classic presentation is a child aged 6 months to 5 years with a 1-2 day coryzal prodrome followed by the abrupt onset (often at night) of a seal-like barking cough, hoarse voice, and inspiratory stridor. The stridor of croup is characteristically audible at rest in moderate-severe cases; in mild cases it may only be present with agitation or crying.</p>

<p>Severity assessment guides management. A practical framework:</p>

<p><strong>Mild croup:</strong> occasional barking cough, no stridor at rest, no or mild suprasternal recession, child alert and active. The child can be distressed by the cough but is not in respiratory difficulty between episodes.</p>

<p><strong>Moderate croup:</strong> frequent barking cough, stridor at rest, sternal recession, no or little agitation. The child is visibly working harder to breathe.</p>

<p><strong>Severe croup:</strong> frequent barking cough, prominent inspiratory (and sometimes expiratory) stridor, marked sternal recession, significant agitation or altered consciousness, pallor or cyanosis. This is a medical emergency.</p>

<p>Important differentials not to miss: epiglottitis (toxic appearance, drooling, muffled voice, absent cough - do not examine the throat or distress the child), bacterial tracheitis (high fever, toxic, stridor does not respond to adrenaline), peritonsillar or retropharyngeal abscess, foreign body inhalation (sudden onset without prodrome, history of choking episode).</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Croup is a clinical diagnosis. Investigations are not required in straightforward presentations. Neck X-ray (which classically shows the "steeple sign" of subglottic narrowing) is not required and should not delay treatment. Do not examine the throat of a child with suspected severe croup or epiglottitis; distressing the child can precipitate complete airway obstruction.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Corticosteroids are the cornerstone of management at all severities. A single oral dose of dexamethasone is highly effective, reduces the severity and duration of illness, and decreases the hospitalisation and reintubation rate.<sup>1</sup></p>

<p><strong>Dexamethasone dose:</strong> 0.15 mg/kg to 0.6 mg/kg orally (maximum 10 mg). In NZ practice, 0.15-0.3 mg/kg is commonly used for mild croup and 0.6 mg/kg for moderate-severe croup; the higher dose has evidence for greater reduction in hospital admission rates.<sup>2</sup> Oral dexamethasone can be given as the 4 mg/mL injectable solution given orally - it is not palatable but is rapidly effective. A single dose is sufficient; repeated doses are not required. Prednisolone (1 mg/kg oral, max 40 mg) is an alternative if dexamethasone is unavailable but is less potent for croup.</p>

<p>For <strong>moderate-severe croup</strong> or any child in significant respiratory distress: nebulised adrenaline (1 mg/mL solution, 5 mL via nebuliser, or 1:1000 adrenaline 0.5 mL/kg up to 5 mL) provides rapid but temporary (1-2 hours) relief of airway oedema and buys time while dexamethasone takes effect.<sup>1</sup> Any child given nebulised adrenaline must be observed for at least 3-4 hours after the dose for rebound before discharge is considered; hospital admission is usually appropriate.</p>

<p>Keep the child calm and with their parent; agitation worsens stridor and airway obstruction. Cool night air (sitting outside briefly) may provide mild symptomatic relief; the mechanism is unclear. Humidified air and steam have no evidence of benefit.<sup>3</sup></p>

<h2>Discharge and safety-netting</h2>
<p>Most children with mild croup who have received dexamethasone and are comfortable after an observation period can be safely discharged home. Discharge advice: the cough typically peaks at night and may worsen before improving over the following 2-3 nights; dexamethasone takes up to 6 hours for full effect; return immediately if the child develops stridor at rest, significant increased work of breathing, pallor or cyanosis, drooling, or appears very unwell.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to secondary care for: moderate-severe croup at presentation; any child given nebulised adrenaline (observe minimum 3-4 hours, admission usually indicated); oxygen saturations below 92% in air; suspected epiglottitis or bacterial tracheitis (999/111 call); age under 6 months (narrow airway, higher risk); failure to respond to dexamethasone and adrenaline; parental concern or difficulty accessing emergency care if the child deteriorates.</p>
