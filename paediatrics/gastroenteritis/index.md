<p>Acute gastroenteritis is one of the most common paediatric presentations in NZ general practice and emergency departments. The majority of cases are viral (rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus) and self-limiting. The clinical task is accurate assessment of dehydration severity, because it is dehydration - not the gastrointestinal symptoms themselves - that determines whether a child can be managed safely at home or needs hospital care.</p>

<h2>Presentation and dehydration assessment</h2>
<p>Gastroenteritis presents with acute onset of vomiting, diarrhoea, or both, often with low-grade fever and abdominal cramps. Children under 2 and infants are at highest risk of significant dehydration because of their higher fluid-to-body-mass ratio and limited ability to self-regulate intake.</p>

<p>Assess dehydration using clinical signs. The NICE/WHO classification is a practical framework:</p>

<p><strong>No clinically detectable dehydration:</strong> alert and active, moist mucous membranes, normal skin turgor, normal urine output, normal capillary refill. Management: oral rehydration solution (ORS) at home, with clear parental safety-netting.</p>

<p><strong>Clinical dehydration (approximately 3-8%):</strong> appears unwell or irritable, dry mucous membranes, reduced skin turgor, eyes slightly sunken, reduced urine output (fewer than 3 wet nappies per day or no urine for over 8 hours), capillary refill 2-3 seconds. Management: oral rehydration in the surgery or community; hospital if unable to tolerate ORS.</p>

<p><strong>Severe dehydration (approximately 8-10% or above):</strong> lethargic, very sunken eyes, absent tears, very dry mucous membranes, markedly reduced skin turgor (skin returns slowly after pinching), capillary refill over 3 seconds, tachycardia, cool peripheries, weak pulse. Management: urgent IV access and fluid resuscitation; hospital admission.<sup>1</sup></p>

<p>Serum electrolytes are required in children with clinical or severe dehydration (sodium disturbance is common with prolonged diarrhoea or exclusive water intake), and in any child with severe vomiting, altered consciousness, or signs of shock. Stool culture is rarely indicated in uncomplicated gastroenteritis; consider if there is blood in the stool, high fever, or recent overseas travel.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p><strong>Oral rehydration solution (ORS)</strong> is the cornerstone of treatment for mild-to-moderate dehydration. Commercial preparations (Pedialyte, Hydralyte) or freshly-made sachets (Gastrolyte) provide the optimal glucose-electrolyte ratio to promote intestinal co-transport. Plain water should not be used as the sole rehydration fluid because it does not replace electrolytes and can cause hyponatraemia. Sports drinks and fruit juices are too concentrated in sugar and too low in sodium to be appropriate ORS alternatives.</p>

<p>Rehydration schedule: give 5-10 mL by spoon or syringe every 1-2 minutes; children are often able to tolerate small frequent volumes even when vomiting. For mild dehydration: 50 mL/kg ORS over 4 hours in addition to usual milk or solid intake. For clinical dehydration: 100 mL/kg ORS over 4 hours with close monitoring.</p>

<p><strong>Ondansetron</strong> (a 5-HT3 receptor antagonist) reduces the frequency of vomiting and decreases the hospitalisation rate in children with moderate gastroenteritis.<sup>2</sup> It is used in NZ emergency departments and increasingly in primary care. Dosing: 0.15 mg/kg orally (max 8 mg), single dose; may be repeated once after 8 hours. Ondansetron is not funded for outpatient use in NZ as of 2024; families may need to purchase it at cost. It is not routinely recommended in infants under 6 months or in children with suspected surgical abdomen.</p>

<p>Loperamide should not be used in children under 12 years. Probiotics have inconsistent evidence; the most studied strains (Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG, Saccharomyces boulardii) may modestly reduce duration of diarrhoea but are not a substitute for ORS.<sup>3</sup></p>

<p><strong>Feeding:</strong> continue breastfeeding throughout illness. Resume a normal age-appropriate diet as soon as tolerated; do not withhold solid food for more than 4-6 hours once oral rehydration is established. The BRAT diet (bananas, rice, apple, toast) has no evidence basis and is unnecessarily restrictive. Full-fat dairy and fruit are well tolerated in most cases.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to secondary care for: any child with severe dehydration (signs of shock, significantly altered consciousness, no urine for over 12 hours); clinical dehydration that fails to improve after 4 hours of supervised ORS; inability to tolerate any oral fluids despite ondansetron; age under 3 months with significant symptoms; bloody diarrhoea with high fever or toxic appearance (consider haemolytic uraemic syndrome); suspicion of a surgical cause (persistent bilious vomiting, abdominal distension, right iliac fossa tenderness).</p>

<p>Safety-net all children managed at home: parents should return if the child develops: any of the above red features, reduced level of consciousness, more than 8 episodes of diarrhoea per day, high fever not settling, persistent vomiting despite ondansetron, or parental concern that the child is not improving.</p>
