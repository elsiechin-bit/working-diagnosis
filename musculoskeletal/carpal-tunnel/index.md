<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Night-time pain and paraesthesia in the thumb, index, and middle fingers (± ring finger), relieved by shaking or dangling the hand, is the classic presentation. Symptoms are worse with repetitive wrist flexion - typing, gripping, driving.</li>
    <li>Thenar wasting means advanced, long-standing compression with risk of permanent denervation - this warrants urgent surgical referral. Do not wait for nerve conduction studies if wasting is visible.</li>
    <li>Nerve conduction studies are the gold standard and are typically required before elective surgical referral. Screen for underlying causes: TSH, HbA1c or fasting glucose, FBC.</li>
    <li>Conservative first-line: wrist splints at night (neutral position) plus corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel. PHARMAC funds methylprednisolone for injection. Surgical carpal tunnel release is definitive for constant symptoms or failed conservative management.<sup>3</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common peripheral nerve entrapment, affecting women three times more frequently than men with peak incidence in the fourth to sixth decades. Median nerve compression at the wrist causes nocturnal paraesthesia in the thumb, index, and middle fingers, often waking patients who shake or dangle their hand for relief. Advanced disease presents with constant paraesthesia, thenar atrophy, and weakness of thumb opposition. Risk factors include pregnancy (often resolves postpartum), hypothyroidism, diabetes, obesity, and rheumatoid arthritis. Examination includes Tinel's test, Phalen's test, and assessment of two-point discrimination and thenar strength.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>Screen for underlying causes: TSH, fasting glucose or HbA1c, FBC. Nerve conduction studies confirm diagnosis and grade severity; they are typically required before surgical referral. Clinical diagnosis is reliable but objective confirmation guides prognosis and specialist referral.</p>

  <p>Conservative management is first-line: wrist splints in neutral position at night reduce nocturnal symptoms. Corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel provides good short- to medium-term relief. PHARMAC funds methylprednisolone for injection. Treat underlying causes. Surgical decompression (carpal tunnel release) is appropriate for thenar wasting, constant rather than intermittent symptoms, or failed conservative management.<sup>3</sup> In pregnancy, splinting is preferred; corticosteroid injection with methylprednisolone is relatively safe if needed.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Thenar wasting or constant symptoms warrant urgent surgical referral given the risk of permanent denervation. For routine cases, refer after a trial of splinting and injection with ongoing symptoms limiting function - arrange nerve conduction studies before referral. Pregnancy-related carpal tunnel: splinting first; refer to obstetric or hand surgery if severe and unresponsive to conservative management.</p>
</div>
