<p>Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common peripheral nerve entrapment, affecting women three times more frequently than men. Peak incidence occurs in the fourth to sixth decades of life. Early recognition and conservative management prevent permanent nerve damage and disability. Median nerve compression at the wrist causes a characteristic pattern of pain and paraesthesia that often awakens patients at night.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Classic presentation is night-time pain and paraesthesia in the thumb, index, and middle fingers (sometimes including the ring finger), often prompting patients to shake their hand or change position to gain relief. Advanced cases present with constant paraesthesia, weakness of thumb opposition (thenar atrophy indicates significant duration and severity), and permanent sensory loss. Symptoms are worse with repetitive wrist flexion and may be triggered by activities such as typing, gripping, or driving. Examination includes Tinel's test (tapping over the carpal tunnel), Phalen's test (sustained wrist flexion), and assessment of two-point discrimination and thenar muscle strength. Risk factors include pregnancy (most common in the third trimester and often resolves postpartum), hypothyroidism, diabetes, obesity, and rheumatoid arthritis.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Screen for underlying causes: thyroid function (TSH), fasting glucose or HbA1c, full blood count (for anaemia, which can increase fluid retention). Nerve conduction studies are the gold standard for diagnosis and assess severity; these are typically required before surgical referral. Clinical diagnosis is reliable, but objective confirmation ensures appropriate specialist referral and guides prognosis.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Conservative management is first-line. Wrist splints in neutral position, particularly worn at night, reduce nocturnal symptoms and prevent involuntary wrist flexion during sleep. Corticosteroid injection into the carpal tunnel provides good short- to medium-term relief and often delays or prevents the need for surgery. PHARMAC funds methylprednisolone for injection. Treat underlying causes (thyroid hormone replacement, glycaemic control, weight reduction). Surgical decompression (carpal tunnel release) is definitive and appropriate for: thenar wasting (evidence of long-standing compression), constant rather than intermittent symptoms, or failed conservative management. Early surgery prevents permanent neurological damage in those with advanced disease.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Arrange nerve conduction studies to confirm diagnosis and grade severity. Thenar wasting or constant symptoms warrant urgent surgical referral given the risk of permanent denervation. Routine cases: refer after trial of splinting and injection (if appropriate) with ongoing symptoms limiting function. Pregnancy: splinting is preferred; corticosteroid injection with methylprednisolone is relatively safe if analgesia is needed.</p>
