  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Nursemaid's elbow (radial head subluxation) peaks at age 2-3 years, caused by longitudinal traction on the extended, pronated arm. Classic presentation: child holds arm in pronation and slight flexion and refuses to use it. Refusal to supinate is nearly pathognomonic. The mechanism may not be recalled by parents - absent history does not exclude the diagnosis.</li>
    <li>This is a clinical diagnosis. X-rays are not indicated for typical presentations and will appear normal (the radial head is not visibly displaced on standard views). X-rays are appropriate only for: age over 4, atypical mechanism (direct trauma or fall from height), signs of fracture (swelling, deformity), or persistent symptoms after two reduction attempts.</li>
    <li>Hyperpronation technique (approximately 95% first-attempt success): stabilise the elbow, supinate the forearm to neutral, then pronate fully. A subtle click may be felt. The supination-flexion technique (about 77% success) is an alternative. After successful reduction, the child should use the arm spontaneously within 15-30 minutes; no immobilisation is needed.</li>
    <li>Recurrence occurs in 26-39% of children before age 5.<sup>5</sup> Counsel parents: do not swing the child by one arm, do not pull the child up by a single arm, avoid jerking the arm during dressing. Subsequent reductions are straightforward. Recurrence is not parental negligence - it reflects anatomical vulnerability before the annular ligament matures.</li>
  </ul>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Nursemaid's elbow results from subluxation of the radial head from the annular ligament following longitudinal traction on the extended, pronated arm. It affects children aged 1-4, peaking at age 2-3. The classic presentation is a child who holds the arm in pronation and slight flexion and refuses to use it - will not reach for toys, eat with the affected arm, or perform any movement requiring supination. There is typically no visible swelling, erythema, or deformity. Attempting passive supination causes marked resistance and distress. Importantly, parents may have no clear recollection of a traction event; the subluxation can occur during innocent play, swinging, or dressing. An absent or vague history does not exclude the diagnosis if the clinical presentation is typical.<sup>2</sup></p>

  <p>X-rays are not indicated for typical presentations and will appear normal - the radial head is not visibly subluxed on standard views and a normal X-ray does not exclude the diagnosis. X-rays are appropriate for: age over 4 years (when other pathology is more probable), mechanism not fitting radial head subluxation (direct trauma, fall from height), signs of fracture (swelling, deformity), or persistent symptoms after two failed reductions.</p>

  <p>Two reduction techniques exist. The hyperpronation technique has approximately 95% first-attempt success compared to the supination-flexion method (approximately 77%)<sup>1</sup> and is preferred. Hyperpronation: hold the child comfortably, grasp the wrist, stabilise the elbow, supinate to neutral then pronate fully with gentle pressure over the radial head. A subtle click may be felt, though absence of the click does not mean reduction failed. Supination-flexion alternative: gentle traction while extending the arm, then supinate and flex the forearm. After successful reduction, the child should use the arm spontaneously within 15-30 minutes. If no improvement after 30 minutes, attempt a second reduction. If two attempts fail, obtain X-rays to exclude fracture and arrange urgent orthopaedic review. No immobilisation is needed after successful reduction; simple analgesia for comfort. Counsel parents thoroughly on avoidance and the high recurrence risk before the annular ligament matures around age 5.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Routine referral is not needed for successfully reduced nursemaid's elbow. Urgent referral to paediatrics or orthopaedics is indicated if: the presentation is atypical (age over 4, direct trauma, absence of refusal to supinate); two reduction attempts have failed and fracture cannot be excluded; or significant swelling, deformity, or lack of arm use persists after reduction. Recurrent uncomplicated nursemaid's elbow does not require orthopaedic referral and can be managed by repeated primary care reduction until the child's age makes recurrence unlikely. Parents can be taught the reduction technique for home management of uncomplicated recurrences if appropriate.<sup>3</sup></p>
