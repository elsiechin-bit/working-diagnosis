<div class="topic-body">

<p>Nursemaid's elbow, also known as pulled elbow or radial head subluxation, is a common paediatric condition seen in children aged 1 to 4 years, peaking around age 2 to 3. It results from subluxation (partial dislocation) of the radial head from the annular ligament - a fibrous band that normally holds the radial head in place during forearm rotation. The condition is typically caused by longitudinal traction applied to the extended, pronated arm, though the mechanism may not be recalled by parents and is often benign in nature. Nursemaid's elbow is a clinical diagnosis; imaging is not required for typical presentations and can lead to unnecessary delay in treatment and parental anxiety. The key skill in primary care is recognising the classic presentation (arm held in pronation and flexion, child refuses to use arm, history of traction), performing safe and effective reduction, and counselling parents about the high recurrence risk before the annular ligament thickens around age 5.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>The classic presentation is a young child (aged 1 - 4 years) who presents with a history of sudden pull or swing by the arm, following which the child holds the arm in pronation and slight flexion and refuses to use it. Pain is often minimal or absent, and the child may be more distressed by parental anxiety or the clinical encounter than by the condition itself. Importantly, parents frequently have no clear recollection of a specific traction event; the subluxation may occur during innocent play, a swing, or even during dressing. This absent or vague history does not exclude the diagnosis if the clinical presentation is typical.</p>

<p>On examination, the child is noted to be holding the arm in the classic position: pronated (palm facing backwards), slightly flexed at the elbow, and held close to the body. The child refuses to use the arm for function - they will not reach for toys, eat with the affected arm, or perform any movement that requires supination (turning the palm upward). There is typically no visible swelling, erythema, or obvious deformity. Palpation over the radial head may elicit discomfort, but tenderness is usually mild and non-specific. Attempting passive supination of the forearm (turning the palm upward) causes marked resistance and distress.</p>

<p>The key clinical feature is the refusal to supinate - this is nearly pathognomonic for nursemaid's elbow. The arm holds its pronated position as if "stuck" in that position. Importantly, X-rays do not show the subluxation; the radial head is not displaced on imaging and standard radiographs appear normal. This normal appearance on X-ray is a source of confusion - it does not exclude the diagnosis. The diagnosis is entirely clinical and does not depend on imaging.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>

<p>Nursemaid's elbow should be diagnosed clinically and reduced without prior imaging in typical presentations. X-rays are not indicated and can delay treatment unnecessarily. However, X-rays are appropriate in the following circumstances: age greater than 4 years (when the diagnosis becomes much less likely and other pathology is more probable), a mechanism that does not fit radial head subluxation (e.g., direct trauma, fall from height), signs of fracture (marked swelling, deformity, or inability to move the arm at all after attempted reduction), or persistent symptoms after two attempted reductions (to exclude an occult fracture).</p>

<p>If X-rays are obtained before reduction, they will typically appear normal - the radial head is not visibly subluxed on standard views. This normal appearance does not exclude nursemaid's elbow and should not prevent reduction from being attempted. Ultrasound can demonstrate radial head displacement and is used in some centres to confirm the diagnosis and guide reduction, but it is not essential in the typical case managed in primary care.</p>

<p>In primary care, no investigation is routinely needed. If clinical doubt exists about the diagnosis (atypical age, atypical mechanism, no refusal to supinate), referral to a paediatrician or orthopaedics for assessment and possible imaging is appropriate rather than attempting reduction in the uncertain case.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>Management is reduction of the subluxation, which can be performed in the clinic or office setting by trained primary care practitioners. Two reduction techniques exist: the supination-flexion technique and the hyperpronation technique. The hyperpronation technique is preferred as it has a higher first-attempt success rate (approximately 95%) compared to the supination-flexion method (77%).</p>

<p>Hyperpronation technique: The child should be held comfortably on a parent's lap or sitting upright. The clinician grasps the child's wrist and hand, stabilising the elbow with one hand whilst the other hand supinates the forearm to neutral, then pronates the forearm fully (turning the palm to face downward). Gentle pressure may be applied to the radial head as pronation is completed. Many clinicians report feeling a subtle click or clunk, though absence of this click does not mean reduction has failed. A click, if present, indicates successful reduction.</p>

<p>Supination-flexion technique: Alternative method: gentle traction is applied to the arm whilst it is extended, then the forearm is supinated and flexed. This is less commonly used now due to lower success rates.</p>

<p>After reduction, the child should use the arm spontaneously within 15 - 30 minutes. If the child resists use of the arm after 30 minutes, a second reduction attempt may be considered. If two reduction attempts fail, or if the child has significant swelling or deformity making nursemaid's elbow less likely, X-rays should be obtained to exclude fracture and urgent orthopaedic review arranged.</p>

<p>After successful reduction, no immobilisation is necessary. The child can return to normal activities as pain and comfort allow. Simple analgesia (paracetamol or ibuprofen) may be used for any discomfort, though pain is typically minimal after successful reduction.</p>

<p>A critical aspect of management is parental counselling. Parents must be warned that recurrence occurs in 26 - 39% of children before the annular ligament matures (usually by age 5). Parents should be instructed on how to avoid traction on the arm: do not swing the child by one or both arms, do not pull the child up by a single arm, and avoid jerking the arm during dressing. Despite counselling, recurrence is common and should not be viewed as parental negligence - it reflects the anatomical vulnerability of the young arm. Subsequent reductions are straightforward and parents can be taught the technique if recurrence occurs.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Routine referral is not necessary for successfully reduced nursemaid's elbow. However, urgent referral to a paediatrician or orthopaedics is indicated if: the presentation is atypical for nursemaid's elbow (age > 4, direct trauma, absence of refusal to supinate); reduction attempts have failed twice and fracture cannot be excluded; or significant swelling, deformity, or lack of arm use persists after reduction.</p>

<p>Referral for teaching of the reduction technique is appropriate if the child has had multiple recurrences and parents wish to manage uncomplicated reductions at home, though some primary care practitioners may feel comfortable with this approach already. Referral to orthopaedics is not needed for uncomplicated recurrent nursemaid's elbow; these can be managed by repeated primary care reduction until the child's age and ligament maturation make recurrence unlikely.</p>
