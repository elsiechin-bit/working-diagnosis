<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Immediate haemarthrosis within minutes of injury is characteristic of ACL rupture and distinguishes it from meniscal injury, which swells over hours. The Lachman test (30 degrees flexion, anterior tibial translation) has the highest sensitivity (approximately 85%).</li>
    <li>ACL ruptures do not heal spontaneously. Young athletes in pivoting sports (football, netball, rugby) typically benefit from reconstruction. Arrange MRI within 2 weeks to confirm rupture, assess meniscal injury, and grade other ligamentous damage.<sup>1</sup></li>
    <li>Surgery is best timed once swelling has resolved and range of motion has improved (typically 2-3 weeks post-injury). Prehabilitation before surgery improves post-operative outcomes.</li>
    <li>Return to sport requires 9-12 months of post-operative rehabilitation. ACC funds physiotherapy, investigations, and surgical reconstruction for acute sports injuries.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>ACL injury typically occurs with non-contact deceleration or pivoting movements. Patients often describe a feeling of the knee giving way or hearing a pop. Immediate haemarthrosis within minutes distinguishes ACL rupture from meniscal injury. On examination, the Lachman test has the highest sensitivity; the pivot shift test, if positive, is highly specific for complete ACL rupture. Test both knees for comparison. Assess for associated injuries: MCL injury presents with valgus tenderness; posterolateral corner injury with posterolateral instability; medial meniscal injury often coexists with ACL rupture.</p>

  <p>Plain radiographs (AP, lateral, axial views) are obtained to exclude fracture or Segond fracture (small avulsion of the lateral tibial plateau, highly associated with ACL injury). The Ottawa Knee Rules guide whether X-rays are indicated. MRI is the gold standard for soft tissue diagnosis and should be arranged within 2 weeks of injury.</p>

  <p>Management depends on whether the patient wants to return to pivoting sports. Non-surgical management is an option for non-pivoting activities and older or less active patients. For pivoting sport athletes, ACL reconstruction (ACLR) is typically indicated. Prehabilitation (quadriceps and hamstring strengthening, proprioceptive training) before surgery improves post-operative outcomes. Post-operative rehabilitation is intensive.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Haemarthrosis in a young athlete with suspected ACL injury warrants urgent orthopaedic referral within 1-2 weeks. MCL injuries are typically managed conservatively with physiotherapy unless combined with other ligament injury. Confirmed ACL rupture in an adolescent athlete should be referred to orthopaedic surgery for discussion of reconstruction timing and rehabilitation strategy. PCL injuries are less common and usually managed conservatively unless there is multiligamentous instability.</p>
</div>
