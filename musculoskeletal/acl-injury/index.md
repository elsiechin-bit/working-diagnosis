<p>Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injuries are common in adolescent athletes and require timely assessment and appropriate referral. Unlike meniscal injuries, ACL ruptures do not typically heal spontaneously. The decision between surgical reconstruction and conservative management depends on the patient's age, sport, activity aspirations, and associated injuries. Accurate early diagnosis and prehabilitation are critical for optimal outcomes.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Acute ACL injury typically occurs with non-contact deceleration or pivoting movements. Patients often describe a feeling of the knee "giving way" or hearing a "pop." Immediate haemarthrosis (blood in the knee) within minutes is characteristic and distinguishes ACL rupture from meniscal injury, which swells over hours. Acute pain and inability to weight-bear are common.</p>

<p>On examination, the Lachman test (30 degrees knee flexion, anterior tibial translation) has the highest sensitivity (85%) for ACL injury. The anterior drawer test is less sensitive but may be positive. The pivot shift test, if positive, is highly specific for complete ACL rupture. Test both knees for comparison. Assess for associated injuries: medial collateral ligament (MCL) injury presents with valgus tenderness; posterolateral corner injury with posterolateral instability. Medial meniscal injury often coexists with ACL rupture.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain radiographs (AP, lateral, axial views) should be obtained to exclude fractures or Segond fractures (small avulsion fracture of the lateral tibial plateau, highly associated with ACL injury). The Ottawa Knee Rules guide whether X-rays are indicated. MRI is the gold standard for soft tissue diagnosis and should be arranged within 2 weeks of injury to confirm ACL rupture, assess meniscal injury, and grade other ligamentous damage.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Management depends on whether the patient wants to return to pivoting sports. Young athletes engaged in pivoting sports (football, netball, rugby) typically benefit from ACL reconstruction (ACLR), though non-surgical management is an option for non-pivoting activities. Timing of surgery is important: acute haemarthrosis and pain should be managed first with physiotherapy and cryotherapy. Surgery is typically performed once swelling has resolved and range of motion improved (usually 2-3 weeks post-injury).</p>

<p>Prehabilitation before surgery (quadriceps and hamstring strengthening, proprioceptive training) improves post-operative outcomes. Post-operative rehabilitation is intensive and requires 9-12 months before return to sport is considered. ACC funds physiotherapy, investigations, and surgical reconstruction for acute sports injuries.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Haemarthrosis in a young athlete with suspected ACL injury warrants urgent orthopaedic referral within 1-2 weeks. MCL injuries are typically managed conservatively with physiotherapy unless there is combined injury. Confirmed ACL rupture in an adolescent athlete should be referred to an orthopaedic surgeon for discussion of reconstruction timing and rehabilitation strategy. Posterior cruciate ligament injuries are less common and usually managed conservatively unless there is multiligamentous instability.</p>
