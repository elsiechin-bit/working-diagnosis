<div class="topic-body">

<p>Hip osteoarthritis presents insidiously with groin or anterior thigh pain, often radiating to the medial knee - a pattern that may mislead patients into seeking knee evaluation. The hip joint is deep, and pain is frequently felt in the groin, buttock, or referred to the knee. Functional limitation includes difficulty putting on shoes, rising from sitting, and walking. Older adults with hip OA have reduced mobility and increased falls risk.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Pain is typically worse with weight-bearing, particularly with walking and stairs. Morning stiffness is mild and brief. Examination reveals loss of internal rotation (the earliest and most sensitive sign), hip flexion contracture, Trendelenburg gait (weakness of hip abductors), and anterolateral hip pain on palpation. A positive FADIR test (flexion-adduction-internal rotation) indicates intra-articular pathology. Differentiate from trochanteric pain syndrome (lateral hip pain, worse on lying on the affected side, point tenderness over greater trochanter), meralgia paraesthetica (purely neuropathic anterolateral thigh numbness), and referred lumbar spine pain (radicular pattern, positive straight leg raise).</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>X-ray is the initial imaging modality: standing anteroposterior pelvis plus lateral hip view. Classic OA changes include joint space narrowing (superior joint space loss is most common in hip OA), osteophytes, and subarticular sclerosis. MRI is not routine unless avascular necrosis, labral pathology, or occult fracture is suspected. Blood work (FBC, renal function, inflammatory markers) helps guide pharmacotherapy.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Weight loss and load reduction are foundational. Physiotherapy emphasizing hip abductor strengthening, hip flexor stretching, and activity modification improves pain and function. Water-based exercise is particularly beneficial. Topical NSAIDs have limited efficacy for hip pain due to depth; oral NSAIDs or paracetamol are more appropriate, used cautiously in older adults. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (20 - 40 mg triamcinolone) under ultrasound guidance provides short-term pain relief and can be repeated 3 - 4 times yearly. Total hip arthroplasty (THA) is highly effective with 15-year survivorship exceeding 90%. Selection criteria include significant pain, functional limitation, radiographic OA, and optimised conservative care. Age is not a contraindication; medical fitness and motivation matter.</p>

<p>Trochanteric pain syndrome, often coexisting with hip OA, responds to physiotherapy (hip abductor strengthening, iliotibial band stretching), load modification, and corticosteroid injection (peritrochanteric or intra-bursal, ultrasound-guided). Most patients improve over 3 - 6 months with conservative management.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to orthopaedic surgery when conservative management has been optimised (3 - 6 months) and the patient has significant functional limitation and wishes to discuss surgery. Referral urgency is routine; waiting times vary by region and sector (public vs private). Consider referral to physiotherapy early in the course.</p>
