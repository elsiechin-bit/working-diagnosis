<p>Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic systemic autoimmune inflammatory arthritis with peak onset between 40 and 60 years, three times more common in women. The disease causes irreversible joint damage early if left untreated; therefore, prompt recognition, investigation, and urgent rheumatology referral are critical to prevent permanent disability. Early disease-modifying antirheumatic drug (DMARD) initiation significantly improves long-term outcomes.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Symmetrical polyarticular synovitis is the hallmark: metacarpophalangeal (MCP) and proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joints of the hands and wrists are most commonly affected. Morning stiffness lasting >1 hour is characteristic and reflects inflammatory activity. Constitutional symptoms (fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever) may accompany joint symptoms. Important: DIP joint involvement is NOT typical of RA; suspect osteoarthritis or psoriatic arthritis if DIP joints are predominantly affected. Extra-articular manifestations include rheumatoid nodules (elbows, fingers), dry eyes and mouth (sicca), pleuritis, interstitial lung disease, vasculitis, and anaemia of chronic disease.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), rheumatoid factor (RF, positive in 70 - 80%), and anti-CCP antibodies (more specific, 95% specificity, predictive of erosive disease) are essential. Hand and foot X-rays assess for erosions and periarticular osteopenia. Antinuclear antibody (ANA) may be positive. The 2010 ACR/EULAR classification criteria requires a score ≥6/10 for diagnosis. Do not diagnose or manage new suspected RA in general practice alone; investigation should be initiated by the GP, but management requires rheumatology input.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Do not delay referral while awaiting test results - refer urgently with initial blood work. NSAIDs provide symptomatic relief while awaiting specialist assessment. Disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) must be initiated early - every month of delay increases risk of permanent joint damage. Methotrexate is the first-line DMARD in New Zealand and is PHARMAC-funded. Hydroxychloroquine, sulfasalazine, and leflunomide are alternative first-line agents. Biological therapies (anti-TNF inhibitors, other biologics) are PHARMAC-funded with Special Authority criteria for those failing conventional DMARDs or with high disease activity. Treat to remission or low disease activity is the modern target. Rheumatology specialists will coordinate DMARD selection, dose titration, monitoring, and escalation to biologics as required.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Urgent rheumatology referral (target: assessment within 2 - 4 weeks) is indicated for new suspected RA. Do not delay referral awaiting test results. The window for early DMARD initiation is critical - outcomes significantly worsen if DMARDs are started >3 months after symptom onset. GPs should have a low threshold for referral; rheumatology will confirm diagnosis and initiate appropriate therapy.</p>
