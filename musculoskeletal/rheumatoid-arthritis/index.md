<p>
  Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic systemic autoimmune inflammatory arthritis with peak onset between 40 and 60 years, and is three times more common in women. The disease causes irreversible joint damage early if untreated. Prompt recognition and urgent specialist referral are the key GP contributions - disease modification requires rheumatology input from the start.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Recognising RA: presentation and examination</p>
  <p>
    Symmetrical polyarticular synovitis is the hallmark. Metacarpophalangeal (MCP) and proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joints of the hands and wrists are most commonly affected. Morning stiffness lasting over 1 hour is characteristic and reflects active inflammation - it is not a feature of mechanical joint pain, where stiffness is brief and improves within minutes. Constitutional symptoms (fatigue, malaise, low-grade fever) are common.
  </p>
  <p>
    Important pattern recognition: distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint involvement is not typical of RA. If DIP joints are predominantly involved, consider osteoarthritis (Heberden's nodes) or psoriatic arthritis. Psoriatic arthritis can also cause asymmetric oligoarthritis, dactylitis ("sausage digit"), and enthesitis - check for psoriasis on the scalp and nails if this pattern is present.
  </p>
  <p>
    Extra-articular manifestations occur in established disease and include: rheumatoid nodules (elbows, fingers, pressure points), dry eyes and mouth (secondary Sjogren's), pleuritis, interstitial lung disease, vasculitis, and anaemia of chronic disease.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Investigation</p>
  <p>
    Order in parallel with referral, not before it. Waiting for results before referring loses the narrow window for early DMARD initiation.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>FBC, ESR, CRP:</strong> assess inflammation and anaemia of chronic disease</li>
    <li><strong>Rheumatoid factor (RF):</strong> positive in 70-80% - a useful but imperfect test. RF-negative RA is not uncommon, particularly early</li>
    <li><strong>Anti-CCP antibodies:</strong> more specific (95% specificity), positive earlier in disease course, and predictive of erosive disease. The more useful test</li>
    <li><strong>ANA:</strong> often mildly positive; helps with differential diagnosis</li>
    <li><strong>Hand and foot X-rays:</strong> assess for periarticular osteopenia and early erosions; may be normal in early disease</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The 2010 ACR/EULAR classification criteria assigns points across four domains: joint involvement, serology (RF/anti-CCP), acute phase reactants, and symptom duration. A score of 6 or more out of 10 classifies RA.<sup>1</sup> Use it as a structure for thinking about the diagnosis, not as a threshold to cross before referring.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Management: the GP role</p>
  <p>
    The GP role in new suspected RA is: diagnose early, investigate in parallel, refer urgently, and manage symptoms while waiting. Do not initiate DMARDs in primary care - dose selection, monitoring requirements, and escalation planning require specialist input.
  </p>
  <p>
    NSAIDs provide symptomatic relief while awaiting rheumatology assessment. Use with standard caution regarding renal function, cardiovascular risk, and GI protection. Low-dose prednisolone is sometimes used as a bridging measure before DMARD initiation, but this should be at rheumatology's direction.
  </p>
