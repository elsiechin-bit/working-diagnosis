<p>Osteoarthritis is the most common articular joint disease, affecting knees, hips, and hands. Early to mid-stage disease presents with mechanical pain (worse with activity, improves with rest) and morning stiffness usually under 1 hour. Risk factors include age, obesity, prior joint injury, family history, and female sex. Diagnosis is clinical combined with radiographic findings; imaging does not correlate well with symptoms, and findings must not drive unnecessary treatment. Conservative, function-focused management optimises outcomes and delays or prevents the need for surgical intervention.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Mechanical pain is the hallmark - worse with weight-bearing and activity, improving with rest. Morning stiffness is present but typically resolves within 30 minutes (distinguishing it from inflammatory arthropathies lasting >1 hour). Heberden's nodes (DIP joint) and Bouchard's nodes (PIP joint) are common in hand osteoarthritis. Examination reveals joint crepitus, restricted range of motion, and sometimes mild effusion. Radiographic changes include joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and cysts. Importantly, the presence and severity of radiographic changes do not predict symptom severity - some patients with mild symptoms have significant X-ray changes, and vice versa.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Clinical presentation plus X-ray findings (joint space narrowing, osteophytes, sclerosis) establish diagnosis. X-ray should not be done routinely but reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or when symptoms suggest alternative diagnoses. Inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) should be normal; elevated values suggest inflammatory arthropathy (rheumatoid arthritis, seronegative spondyloarthropathy) requiring different management. Negative rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP antibodies help exclude rheumatoid arthritis when inflammatory features are prominent.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Non-pharmacological interventions are most effective: weight loss (5 kg weight loss produces meaningful pain reduction in knee OA), physiotherapy (quadriceps strengthening for knee OA, proprioceptive training), regular exercise, walking aids, and appropriate footwear. These should be offered to all patients and form the foundation of management. Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel) are first-line for knee and hand OA, PHARMAC-funded. Oral NSAIDs (with gastroprotection if age >65 or prior GI disease) are used judiciously given cardiovascular and renal risks. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection provides short-term relief (4 - 8 weeks) and is useful for acute flares. Hyaluronic acid injection has modest evidence but is not PHARMAC-funded. Opioids should be avoided in OA - they increase falls risk and do not improve long-term outcomes. Paracetamol is ineffective for moderate to severe OA and should not be relied upon as sole analgesic.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Surgical intervention (joint replacement) is considered when: significant impact on quality of life despite optimised conservative management, radiographic evidence of severe disease, and documentation of genuine attempts at weight loss, physiotherapy, and pharmacological management. No absolute radiological or Kellgren-Lawrence score threshold exists - referral is symptom and function-driven, not imaging-driven. Rheumatology referral is appropriate if inflammatory features suggest underlying inflammatory arthropathy.</p>
