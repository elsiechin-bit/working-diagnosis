<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Mechanical pain (worse with activity, improves with rest) and morning stiffness under 30 minutes is OA until proven otherwise. Stiffness lasting over 1 hour points to inflammatory arthropathy.</li>
    <li>X-ray changes do not correlate with symptom severity. Some patients with severe X-ray changes have mild symptoms, and vice versa. Imaging findings must not drive treatment decisions independently of functional impact.</li>
    <li>Non-pharmacological management is most effective and should be offered to every patient: weight loss (5 kg produces meaningful pain reduction in knee OA), physiotherapy, exercise. These are first-line, not adjuncts.</li>
    <li>Topical diclofenac gel is first-line pharmacotherapy for knee and hand OA (PHARMAC-funded). Oral NSAIDs with gastroprotection for moderate-severe symptoms. Avoid opioids - they increase falls risk and do not improve long-term OA outcomes.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Osteoarthritis is the most common articular joint disease, affecting knees, hips, and hands. It is primarily a disease of cartilage degradation, with secondary bone remodelling, synovial inflammation, and periarticular muscle weakness. Early to mid-stage management in primary care focuses on function and conservative measures - joint replacement is the endpoint of a well-managed conservative pathway, not a substitute for one.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Diagnosis and investigation</p>
  <p>
    Mechanical pain is the hallmark: worse with weight-bearing and activity, improving with rest. Morning stiffness is present but typically resolves within 30 minutes - this distinguishes OA from inflammatory arthropathies (rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis) where stiffness typically lasts over 1 hour and improves with activity.
  </p>
  <p>
    Examination findings: joint crepitus, restricted range of motion, bony enlargement at joint margins, and sometimes mild effusion. Heberden's nodes (DIP joints) and Bouchard's nodes (PIP joints) are classic in hand OA. Muscle weakness and wasting around the affected joint, particularly quadriceps in knee OA, both result from and worsen the disease.
  </p>
  <p>
    X-ray: indicated for diagnostic uncertainty or when symptoms suggest an alternative diagnosis. Classic findings include joint space narrowing, osteophytes, subchondral sclerosis, and subchondral cysts. Do not order X-rays routinely for typical presentations - imaging adds nothing to management and the findings do not predict symptom severity. Importantly: radiographic changes and symptoms correlate poorly.
  </p>
  <p>
    Inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) should be normal in OA. Elevated values suggest inflammatory arthropathy requiring different management. RF and anti-CCP help exclude rheumatoid arthritis when inflammatory features are present.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Non-pharmacological management: the foundation</p>
  <p>
    Non-pharmacological interventions are the most effective management strategy for OA and should be offered to all patients as the primary intervention. They are first-line, not adjuncts to pharmacotherapy.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Weight loss:</strong> in patients with overweight or obesity, 5 kg weight loss produces a clinically meaningful reduction in knee pain. Weight loss reduces joint load and systemic inflammation. It should be part of every OA consultation in relevant patients. Exercise programmes improve outcomes independently of weight loss.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Physiotherapy and exercise:</strong> quadriceps strengthening reduces knee OA pain and improves function. Proprioceptive training reduces falls risk. Regular aerobic exercise (walking, swimming, cycling) reduces pain and disability in hip and knee OA. Refer to physiotherapy for an individualised programme.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Occupational and practical measures:</strong> walking aids (stick on the contralateral side for knee and hip OA), appropriate footwear (cushioned, supportive soles), activity modification to reduce high-impact loading. Knee splints and bracing for patellofemoral OA.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Pharmacological management</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Topical diclofenac gel:</strong> first-line pharmacotherapy for knee and hand OA. PHARMAC-funded. Effective with minimal systemic absorption, making it the preferred option for older patients and those with cardiovascular, renal, or GI risk factors. Apply to the affected joint 3-4 times daily.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Oral NSAIDs:</strong> use judiciously. Effective for moderate-to-severe symptoms but carry cardiovascular, renal, and GI risks.
