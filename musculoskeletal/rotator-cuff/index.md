<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Rotator cuff pathology represents a spectrum from tendinopathy and partial-thickness tears through to full-thickness tears. Subacromial impingement - compression of the rotator cuff tendons beneath the acromion - presents with pain on overhead activities, internal rotation, and hand-behind-back positioning, with a characteristic painful arc between 60 and 120 degrees. The Neer impingement sign (passive forward flexion causing pain) and Hawkins-Kennedy test (internal rotation with forward flexion) are the key provocative tests. Assess each muscle individually: supraspinatus (empty can test for weakness on abduction), infraspinatus (external rotation weakness), subscapularis (belly press or Gerber lift-off), and biceps (Speed's and O'Brien's tests). Full-thickness tears may cause weakness without pain if chronic. Age, mechanism, and functional demands guide urgency.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>Plain X-rays identify calcific tendinopathy, acromial morphology, signs of superior humeral migration (large rotator cuff tear), and glenohumeral joint pathology. Ultrasound is widely available in NZ, operator-dependent but accurate for partial and full-thickness tears, and useful for identifying calcific deposits suitable for guided needling. MRI provides superior soft tissue detail and is used when surgical planning is contemplated. Imaging should be guided by clinical suspicion rather than performed routinely.</p>

  <p>Physiotherapy targeting rotator cuff strengthening and scapular stabilisation is the cornerstone of management for both impingement and rotator cuff tears. Subacromial corticosteroid injection provides short-term pain relief and facilitates physiotherapy - however, repeated injections weaken tendon and should be avoided. NSAIDs provide short-term analgesia. Calcific tendinopathy may benefit from ultrasound-guided needling and barbotage. Full-thickness tears in young active patients with acute traumatic onset may warrant surgical repair; chronic degenerative tears in older patients are often managed conservatively with good functional outcomes.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Full-thickness rotator cuff tears with functional impairment in active patients warrant orthopaedic assessment for surgical repair. Calcific tendinopathy unresponsive to conservative management: refer for radiology-guided needling and barbotage. Glenohumeral instability or persistent symptoms despite optimised conservative management: orthopaedic consultation. Progressive neurological deficits or suprascapular nerve entrapment require urgent assessment. ACC covers physiotherapy and surgery for acute injuries.</p>
