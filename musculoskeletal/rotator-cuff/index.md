<p>Rotator cuff pathology represents a spectrum from tendinopathy and partial-thickness tears through to full-thickness rotator cuff tears. Subacromial impingement - compression of the rotator cuff tendons within the space beneath the acromion - is a common painful condition that may or may not be associated with structural tears. Accurate clinical examination, judicious imaging, and physiotherapy-first management distinguish this condition from other causes of shoulder pain and improve outcomes.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Subacromial impingement typically presents with pain on overhead activities, internal rotation, and hand-behind-back positioning. A painful arc is characteristic (pain between 60 and 120 degrees of abduction). Clinical examination includes the Neer impingement sign (passive forward flexion causing pain) and Hawkins-Kennedy test (internal rotation with forward flexion causing pain). Rotator cuff weakness is assessed by testing individual muscles: supraspinatus (empty can test, weakness on abduction), infraspinatus (external rotation weakness), subscapularis (belly press or Gerber lift-off test), and biceps (Speed's test, O'Brien's test). Full-thickness tears may cause weakness without pain if chronic. Age, mechanism (traumatic vs gradual onset), and functional demands guide management urgency.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain X-rays identify calcific tendinopathy, acromial morphology, signs of superior humeral migration (suggestive of large rotator cuff tear), and glenohumeral joint pathology. Ultrasound is widely available in New Zealand, operator-dependent but accurate for partial and full-thickness tears, and helps identify calcific deposits suitable for ultrasound-guided needling. MRI provides superior soft tissue detail and articular surface involvement, useful when surgical planning is contemplated. Imaging should be guided by clinical suspicion rather than performed routinely.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Physiotherapy targeting rotator cuff strengthening and scapular stabilisation is the cornerstone of management for both impingement and rotator cuff tears. Subacromial corticosteroid injection can provide short-term pain relief and facilitate engagement with physiotherapy; however, repeated injections weaken tendon and should be avoided. NSAIDs provide short-term analgesia. Calcific tendinopathy may benefit from ultrasound-guided needling and barbotage. Full-thickness tears in young, active patients with acute traumatic onset may warrant surgical repair discussion, while chronic degenerative tears in older patients are often managed conservatively. ACC funds physiotherapy for acute musculoskeletal injury.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Full-thickness rotator cuff tears with functional impairment in active patients warrant orthopedic assessment for surgical repair. Calcific tendinopathy unresponsive to conservative management may benefit from radiology-guided needling and barbotage. Glenohumeral instability or persistent symptoms despite optimised conservative management merit orthopedic consultation. Progressive neurological deficits or suprascapular nerve entrapment require urgent assessment.</p>
