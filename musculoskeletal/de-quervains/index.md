<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>De Quervain's tenosynovitis is a stenosing tenosynovitis affecting the first dorsal compartment of the wrist, which contains the abductor pollicis longus (APL) and extensor pollicis brevis (EPB) tendons. Radial-sided wrist pain and swelling over the radial styloid process, aggravated by thumb activities and lifting, are the hallmarks. The Finkelstein test - grasping the thumb within a closed fist and deviating the wrist ulnarward - is highly sensitive and reproduces characteristic pain.<sup>3</sup> Important differentials include CMC joint osteoarthritis (pain at the base of the thumb with a positive grind test) and intersection syndrome (pain 4-6 cm proximal to the wrist where the second extensor compartment crosses the APL and EPB). Duration, mechanism, and functional impact guide management intensity.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>De Quervain's is a clinical diagnosis. Ultrasound can confirm tenosynovitis, identify fluid around the tendons, and guide corticosteroid injection, but is not essential when the diagnosis is clear. Plain X-rays are not indicated unless CMC joint pathology is suspected.</p>

  <p>Activity modification and avoidance of repetitive thumb pinching and lifting are essential. A thumb spica splint immobilising the thumb and wrist (leaving fingers free) reduces symptoms. NSAIDs provide short-term pain relief. Corticosteroid injection directly into the first dorsal compartment achieves success in more than 70% with a single injection and is the most effective non-operative treatment.<sup>2</sup> In pregnancy and postpartum, splinting is preferred; methylprednisolone injection is relatively safe if pain significantly limits function. Surgery (division of the first dorsal compartment retinaculum) is definitive and indicated after two failed injections or persistent functional impairment beyond 3 months.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to hand surgery if two corticosteroid injections have failed or symptoms causing significant functional disability persist beyond 3 months despite conservative management. Most cases respond to conservative treatment and do not require surgical review.</p>
