<p>De Quervain's tenosynovitis is a stenosing tenosynovitis affecting the first dorsal compartment of the wrist, which contains the abductor pollicis longus (APL) and extensor pollicis brevis (EPB) tendons. Classic presentations include new parents lifting infants repeatedly, middle-aged women (peak 40 - 50 years), and manual workers. Early recognition and conservative management prevent progression to severe functional impairment.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Radial-sided wrist pain and swelling over the radial styloid process, aggravated by thumb activities and lifting, are the hallmarks. Pain is often sharp and well-localised. Finkelstein test - grasping the thumb within a closed fist and deviating the wrist ulnarward - is highly sensitive and reproduces pain characteristic of the condition. Differentiation from other conditions is important: carpometacarpal (CMC) joint osteoarthritis causes pain at the base of the thumb with positive grind test; intersection syndrome presents with pain 4 - 6 cm proximal to the wrist where the second extensor compartment crosses the APL and EPB; initial radiocarpel wrist sprain affects the dorsal wrist more generally. Duration, mechanism (acute repetitive activity versus gradual), and functional impact guide management intensity.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>De Quervain's is a clinical diagnosis. Ultrasound can confirm tenosynovitis and help identify fluid around the tendons, and is useful for guiding corticosteroid injection, but is not essential if clinical diagnosis is clear. Plain X-rays are not indicated unless CMC joint pathology is suspected.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Activity modification and avoidance of repetitive thumb pinching and lifting movements are essential. A thumb spica splint immobilising the thumb and wrist (leaving fingers free) reduces symptoms. NSAIDs provide short-term pain relief. Corticosteroid injection directly into the first dorsal compartment has a high success rate (>70% with single injection) and is the most effective non-operative treatment. In pregnancy and postpartum lactation, splinting is preferred; methylprednisolone injection is relatively safe if pain significantly limits function. Surgery (division of the first dorsal compartment retinaculum) is definitive and indicated if two corticosteroid injections have failed or symptoms persistently impair function.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to hand surgery if two corticosteroid injections have failed or symptoms causing significant functional disability persist beyond 3 months despite conservative management. Most cases respond to conservative treatment; surgery is selectively used in refractory cases.</p>
