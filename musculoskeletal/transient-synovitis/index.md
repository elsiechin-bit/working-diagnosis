<div class="topic-body">

<p>Transient synovitis of the hip is the most common cause of acute hip pain and limp in children aged 3 to 10 years, accounting for up to 60% of all cases of acute hip pain in this age group. It is a self-limiting inflammatory condition of the hip joint, usually following a viral upper respiratory tract infection. The condition typically resolves within 1 to 4 weeks without specific treatment, but its importance lies in confidently excluding septic arthritis, which requires urgent intervention. The challenge in primary care is distinguishing transient synovitis from septic arthritis using clinical acumen and judicious investigation, particularly the Kocher criteria which quantify the risk of septic joint based on simple, readily available variables.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>Transient synovitis presents with acute or subacute onset of hip, groin, or referred thigh pain in a child who develops an antalgic gait - a pain-avoidance gait characterised by reduced weight bearing on the affected side and shortened stride. Pain onset may be abrupt or evolve over 24 - 48 hours, and many children recall a viral illness in the preceding 1 - 2 weeks. The child may refuse to bear weight or walk entirely in acute cases, though some children continue to mobilise with obvious discomfort.</p>

<p>On examination, the child is typically afebrile or mildly febrile; however, high fever (above 38.5 degrees Celsius) suggests septic arthritis and warrants urgent investigation. The hip is held in flexion, external rotation, and abduction - the position of comfort that minimises intra-articular pressure. Internal rotation and abduction of the hip are painful and restricted, whilst external rotation is relatively preserved. The child resists active hip flexion. Passive range of motion, particularly internal rotation, reproduces pain. Notably, the child appears less systemically unwell than would be expected in septic arthritis - there is no marked lethargy, poor feeding, or systemic toxicity, though mild malaise may be present.</p>

<p>The key distinguishing feature from septic arthritis is the degree of toxicity: children with transient synovitis are generally well-appearing between pain episodes, whilst those with septic arthritis appear systemically unwell. Pain with internal rotation is common to both conditions; the presence or absence of other Kocher criteria helps quantify risk.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>

<p>Investigation is aimed at quantifying the risk of septic arthritis and deciding whether urgent imaging and possibly joint aspiration are needed. The Kocher criteria have high sensitivity and specificity for predicting septic arthritis: the criteria are fever above 38.5 degrees Celsius, non - weight bearing status, ESR greater than 40 mm/hour, and WBC greater than 12 x 10 to the 9 per litre.</p>

<p>Risk stratification: 0 criteria present = less than 0.2% risk of septic arthritis; 1 criterion = 3% risk; 2 criteria = 40% risk; 3 criteria = 93% risk; 4 criteria = 99% risk. If 0 or 1 Kocher criteria are present, septic arthritis is very unlikely and the child can be managed as an outpatient without imaging. If 2 or more criteria are present, urgent orthopaedic assessment, hip X-ray, ultrasound, and possibly magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) are indicated to assess for joint effusion and rule out septic arthritis.</p>

<p>First-line investigation in primary care should include: FBC (to measure WBC), ESR and/or CRP, and assessment of fever and weight - bearing status. Hip X-ray is often normal in transient synovitis but may show mild joint space widening from effusion; X-rays are useful mainly to exclude fracture or structural pathology. Hip ultrasound is more sensitive for detecting hip joint effusion (present in 95% of transient synovitis cases but also present in septic arthritis). If ultrasound shows a substantial effusion (greater than 10 mm) and Kocher criteria are >= 2, urgent orthopaedic referral for possible joint aspiration under ultrasound guidance is indicated to obtain fluid for culture and exclude septic arthritis.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>For children with 0 - 1 Kocher criteria (low risk), management is conservative: rest, analgesia with paracetamol or ibuprofen, and weight - bearing as tolerated. Most children can be managed entirely in the community with review within 1 week. Parents should be counselled that symptoms typically improve over days to weeks, and that activity restriction is not necessary - children should be allowed to bear weight as pain permits.</p>

<p>For children with >= 2 Kocher criteria, urgent orthopaedic assessment is needed. Depending on local pathways, this may occur in the emergency department or via urgent outpatient orthopaedic review. Management remains largely supportive (rest, analgesia), but joint aspiration may be considered to exclude septic arthritis if clinical suspicion remains high after imaging.</p>

<p>Whilst antibiotics are not indicated for transient synovitis alone, they are essential if septic arthritis cannot be excluded and would typically be started pending joint culture results. NSAIDs such as ibuprofen 10 mg/kg may provide better analgesia than paracetamol alone for hip synovitis.</p>

<p>A critical part of management is follow - up: children should be reviewed at 4 - 6 weeks to confirm complete resolution and to screen for the development of Perthes disease, which occurs in 1 - 2% of children following transient synovitis. Parents should be warned to return if the child develops a persistent limp, pain, or loss of hip range at follow - up, as these may indicate early Perthes disease requiring orthopaedic review.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Urgent referral to orthopaedics (same day or next day) is indicated if Kocher criteria >= 2 are present, or if clinical suspicion for septic arthritis is high for any reason. Urgent assessment is essential to exclude septic arthritis, which may require joint aspiration and possibly arthrotomy if infection is confirmed.</p>

<p>Routine paediatric or orthopaedic follow - up at 4 - 6 weeks is advisable even in uncomplicated cases managed in the community, to document full resolution and rule out Perthes disease. If the child has not fully recovered by 6 - 8 weeks, or if a limp persists at follow - up, re - referral to orthopaedics is warranted to assess for Perthes disease with imaging (X-ray with frog-leg views to visualise the femoral head).</p>
