<p>Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) is a lateral spinal curvature affecting 2-3% of the population but requiring treatment in less than 1%. It is diagnosed when the Cobb angle exceeds 10 degrees on standing radiographs. Idiopathic scoliosis accounts for 85% of all cases. There is a marked female predominance, especially for larger curves requiring treatment (female to male ratio approximately 4:1). Early detection and appropriate management can prevent progression and the need for surgery.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Most adolescents with scoliosis are asymptomatic and detected on routine screening at school or during examination for another reason. Physical signs include shoulder asymmetry, unequal waist creases, and an asymmetric rib hump (best seen on the Adam's forward bend test). Pain is atypical in idiopathic scoliosis and should prompt investigation for other underlying causes.</p>

<p>The Adam's forward bend test is the key screening tool: the adolescent bends forward with feet together and knees straight. The examiner views from behind looking for rib and/or lumbar prominence on one side. A visible rib hump warrants X-ray referral. Adolescents aged 10-16 should be screened opportunistically during routine clinic visits.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Standing posteroanterior and lateral full-spine radiographs are the standard initial investigation. The Cobb angle (the angle between the most tilted vertebrae at the top and bottom of the curve) is measured to classify severity. Skeletal maturity (Risser sign) is assessed on the radiograph and predicts growth remaining. MRI is indicated if there are atypical features: pain, rapid progression, left thoracic curve (more common in underlying pathology), neurological signs, or age less than 10 years.<sup>1</sup></p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>The Cobb angle and skeletal maturity guide management. Curves less than 20 degrees in skeletally immature adolescents: observation with X-rays every 6 months during growth. Curves 20-40 degrees in skeletally immature patients: bracing has level 1 evidence and reduces progression to values requiring surgery in 25-40% of cases.<sup>2</sup> Curves greater than 45 degrees or rapidly progressive curves should be referred for surgical consultation.</p>

<p>Physiotherapy using the Schroth method is sometimes used as an adjunct to bracing but has limited evidence. Chiropractic manipulation and electrical stimulation have no evidence. Once skeletal maturity is reached (typically by age 16-18), curves tend to stabilise and observation alone is appropriate.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer adolescents with Cobb angles exceeding 20 degrees or with progressive curves to paediatric orthopaedic services. Urgent referral is warranted for curves greater than 40 degrees, rapidly progressive curves (more than 5 degrees per 6-month period), or neurological signs. Curves associated with pain, atypical age of onset, or atypical curve patterns should prompt MRI and specialist referral to exclude underlying pathology.</p>
