<p>Ankle sprain is the most common sports injury, affecting the lateral ligament complex in 85% of cases. Lateral ankle ligament injuries range from isolated ligament stretch (Grade I) to partial tear (Grade II) to complete rupture (Grade III). Early appropriate management with protected weight-bearing and physiotherapy-led proprioceptive training prevents chronic instability, which develops in 20 - 40% of those sustaining initial sprains. The Ottawa Ankle Rules guide appropriate imaging and prevent unnecessary X-rays.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Acute ankle sprain follows inversion injury, causing pain, swelling, and difficulty weight-bearing. The anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) is injured most frequently, followed by the calcaneofibular ligament (CFL). Examination elicits tenderness over the affected ligaments, swelling, and sometimes bruising. Anterior drawer test and inversion stress test assess for ligament laxity and help grade severity. Syndesmotic injuries (high ankle sprains) follow external rotation injury and are associated with deltoid or syndesmotic ligament injury - these heal more slowly and may require specialist review.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>The Ottawa Ankle Rules guide X-ray imaging: ankle series (mortise, lateral, anterior posterior) is indicated if there is bone tenderness at the posterior 6 cm of the fibula, tip of lateral malleolus, posterior 6 cm of tibia, tip of medial malleolus, OR inability to weight-bear immediately after injury and in the clinic. Most ankle sprains do not meet these criteria and do not require imaging. MRI is reserved for syndesmotic injury, suspected osteochondral lesion, or chronic instability when surgical planning is considered. Functional ankle instability (subjective giving way) may warrant MRI to assess for chronic ligamentous insufficiency.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Early protected weight-bearing is superior to immobilisation or rest. The PEACE and LOVE protocol (replaces RICE - Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) emphasises protection, early active exercise, compression, and elevation in the acute phase, followed by loading and vascularisation. NSAIDs provide short-term analgesia. Physiotherapy starting immediately (even within days of injury) is critical and includes proprioceptive training (balance board, single-leg stance), peroneal muscle strengthening, and functional ankle bracing or taping. Most acute ankle sprains heal within 2 - 6 weeks with this approach. Chronic instability (recurrent giving way, sensation of instability) requires ongoing proprioceptive training and may benefit from functional bracing; surgical reconstruction (Brostrom-Gould procedure) is reserved for those failing 3 months of optimised physiotherapy.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Ottawa-positive ankle (bone tenderness or inability to weight-bear) warrants X-ray, which may be arranged in primary care. High ankle sprain (syndesmotic involvement) should be referred to orthopedics for assessment, as healing is slower and surgical repair may be needed. Chronic instability unresponsive to 3 months of dedicated physiotherapy may warrant orthopedic consultation to discuss surgical stabilisation. ACC covers acute injury management and physiotherapy for eligible patients.</p>
