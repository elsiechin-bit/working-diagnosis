<div class="topic-body">

<p>Mechanical neck pain is the most common presentation in primary care and is usually self-limiting, resolving within weeks to months. Cervical radiculopathy - arm pain in a dermatomal distribution caused by nerve root compression - is less frequent but requires careful assessment to exclude myelopathy and determine urgency of imaging. The distinction between mechanical neck pain and radiculopathy guides investigation and referral decisions.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Mechanical neck pain presents with localised neck discomfort, often related to posture or preceding minor trauma such as whiplash. Cervical radiculopathy manifests as arm pain (often worse than neck pain) in a dermatomal pattern: C6 involvement affects the thumb and index finger with triceps weakness, C7 affects the middle finger and triceps weakness, and C8 affects the little and ring fingers. Red flags requiring urgent assessment include progressive myelopathy signs: hand clumsiness, gait disturbance, hyperreflexia, Lhermitte sign (electric shock on neck flexion), and Babinski sign. Examination should assess handwriting, gait, and upper limb power, sensation and reflexes. The Spurling test (neck extension and ipsilateral rotation causing radicular pain) has modest specificity for radiculopathy.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain cervical X-rays are not useful for uncomplicated mechanical neck pain and may identify age-related degenerative changes that do not correlate with symptoms. MRI is reserved for suspected myelopathy or radiculopathy not responding to conservative management over 6 - 8 weeks. Myelopathy with progressive neurological signs warrants urgent MRI to exclude spinal cord compression from disc herniation, spondylosis, or other compressive pathology.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Physiotherapy is first-line treatment for both mechanical neck pain and radiculopathy, focusing on soft tissue treatment, mobilisation, and neck stabilisation exercises. NSAIDs provide short-term symptom relief in acute episodes. Cervical collars lack evidence of benefit and may promote deconditioning; reassurance and early mobilisation are preferred. Corticosteroid injection can provide short-term relief of radiculopathy and facilitate engagement with physiotherapy, though repeated injections should be avoided. Most radiculopathy improves with conservative management; surgery is considered only when symptoms are disabling and neurological deficit is progressive or persistent after adequate conservative treatment.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Progressive myelopathy requires urgent neurosurgical referral for imaging and assessment. Radiculopathy with progressive weakness warrants urgent referral. Radiculopathy without neurological deficit should be managed conservatively for 6 - 8 weeks; if not improving, arrange MRI and consider orthopedic or neurology referral. Mechanical neck pain alone rarely requires specialist input unless chronicity suggests yellow flag issues or functional impairment warrants multidisciplinary management.</p>
