<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Mechanical neck pain is common and self-limiting, usually resolving within weeks to months. Cervical radiculopathy produces arm pain in a dermatomal pattern (C6: thumb/index with biceps weakness; C7: middle finger with triceps weakness; C8: little/ring fingers). The Spurling test (neck extension and ipsilateral rotation causing radicular pain) has modest specificity.<sup>1</sup></li>
    <li>Myelopathy red flags require urgent action: hand clumsiness, gait disturbance, bilateral upper limb paraesthesia, L'hermitte's sign, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars, and Hoffmann sign. Arrange same-day MRI and urgent neurosurgical consultation. See the cervical spondylosis page for full myelopathy detail.</li>
    <li>Plain cervical X-rays are not useful for uncomplicated mechanical neck pain and may cause harm by identifying age-related degenerative changes that do not correlate with symptoms. MRI is reserved for myelopathy features or radiculopathy not resolving at 6-8 weeks.</li>
    <li>Physiotherapy is first-line for both mechanical neck pain and radiculopathy. Cervical collars lack evidence of benefit and may promote deconditioning. Most radiculopathy improves with conservative management. Corticosteroid injection can provide short-term relief of radiculopathy and facilitate engagement with physiotherapy.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Mechanical neck pain presents with localised neck discomfort, often related to posture or preceding minor trauma such as whiplash. Cervical radiculopathy manifests as arm pain (often worse than neck pain) in a dermatomal pattern: C6 involvement affects the thumb and index finger with biceps weakness; C7 affects the middle finger with triceps weakness; C8 affects the little and ring fingers. Myelopathy red flags requiring urgent assessment include progressive neurological signs - hand clumsiness, gait disturbance, hyperreflexia, L'hermitte's sign (electric shock sensation on neck flexion), and upgoing plantars. Assess handwriting, gait, and upper limb power, sensation, and reflexes at every consultation with relevant symptoms. The Spurling test has modest specificity for radiculopathy.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>Plain cervical X-rays are not useful for uncomplicated mechanical neck pain. MRI is reserved for suspected myelopathy or radiculopathy not responding to conservative management over 6-8 weeks. Myelopathy with progressive neurological signs warrants urgent MRI to exclude cord compression.</p>

  <p>Physiotherapy is first-line for both mechanical neck pain and radiculopathy, focusing on soft tissue treatment, mobilisation, and neck stabilisation exercises. NSAIDs provide short-term symptom relief in acute episodes. Cervical collars lack evidence of benefit and may promote deconditioning; reassurance and early mobilisation are preferred. Corticosteroid injection can provide short-term relief of radiculopathy and facilitate physiotherapy engagement, though repeated injections should be avoided. Most radiculopathy without motor deficit resolves with conservative management over 6-12 weeks; surgery is considered only when symptoms are disabling and neurological deficit is progressive or persistent despite adequate conservative treatment.<sup>4</sup></p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Progressive myelopathy: urgent same-day neurosurgical referral and MRI. Radiculopathy with progressive motor weakness: urgent neurosurgery or orthopaedic spine referral after MRI. Radiculopathy without neurological deficit not improving at 6-8 weeks: arrange MRI and consider specialist referral if imaging shows significant compression. Mechanical neck pain alone rarely requires specialist input unless chronicity suggests yellow flag issues or functional impairment warrants multidisciplinary management.<sup>3</sup></p>
</div>
