<div class="topic-body">

<p>Trigger finger is a stenosing tenosynovitis of the flexor tendons at the A1 pulley level in the palm, affecting any finger but most commonly the thumb. The condition causes clicking or snapping with finger movement and may progress to locking in flexion requiring passive extension by the opposite hand. It is more common in women, middle-aged adults, those with diabetes, and patients with rheumatoid arthritis. Most cases respond to conservative management; surgery is reserved for refractory cases.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Patients report a clicking or snapping sensation with finger flexion and extension, often worse in the morning. Pain is typically located at the A1 pulley level in the palm. A palpable nodule or thickening may be felt on the flexor tendon. Grades range from I (pain only) through II (catching during movement), III (locking that is passively reducible), to IV (locked, not passively correctable). Risk factors include diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, and activities involving repetitive gripping. The condition tends to worsen over weeks if untreated but may spontaneously resolve in some cases.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Trigger finger is a clinical diagnosis; investigation is not routinely required. Ultrasound may show tendon thickening and A1 pulley stenosis but does not typically change management. Plain X-rays are not indicated.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Conservative management is first-line for Grades I - III. Night splinting with the metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joint in extension (allowing free motion of interphalangeal joints) reduces symptoms by preventing repeated triggering during sleep and reducing force required to overcome the stenotic pulley. Corticosteroid injection directly into the flexor tendon sheath at the A1 pulley provides symptom relief in 60 - 90% of cases for Grades I - III, though success is lower in patients with diabetes. A repeat injection may be offered if partial response occurs. Surgery (percutaneous or open A1 pulley release) is definitive and indicated after two failed injections or for Grade IV (locked finger) where non-operative measures have failed.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Hand surgery referral is appropriate after two corticosteroid injections have failed or for Grade IV (locked) trigger finger where conservative management has not provided functional improvement. Most Grade I - III cases respond to splinting and injection therapy. Early intervention prevents progression and reduces likelihood of requiring surgery.</p>
