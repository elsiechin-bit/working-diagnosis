<p>Non-specific low back pain is the most common musculoskeletal complaint in general practice globally. Approximately 80% of adults will experience at least one episode in their lifetime. The vast majority resolve within 6 weeks with conservative management and reassurance. The primary role of the GP is rigorous red flag screening, identification of yellow flags suggesting psychosocial contributors to chronicity, and avoiding unnecessary investigation and over-medicalisation.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Red flag screening is paramount. Emergency referral is needed for cauda equina syndrome (bilateral leg pain and weakness, saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, faecal incontinence). Malignancy red flags include age over 50, unintentional weight loss, constitutional symptoms, history of cancer, and unremitting night pain unrelieved by position change. Infection suggests fever, focal vertebral tenderness, and immunosuppression or IV drug use. Fracture risk is elevated with trauma, osteoporosis, prolonged corticosteroid use, and age over 70. In absence of red flags, investigation is not required and reassurance is the cornerstone of management. Yellow flags - psychosocial factors including catastrophising, fear-avoidance beliefs, passive coping strategies, and work dissatisfaction - predict chronicity and warrant early attention.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Imaging is not indicated for non-specific low back pain of less than 6 weeks' duration without red flags. MRI findings such as disc degeneration and disc bulges are ubiquitous in asymptomatic adults and do not guide management; their presence may paradoxically increase patient anxiety and lead to unnecessary treatment. Plain film X-rays are similarly unhelpful in uncomplicated mechanical low back pain. Red flag features mandate urgent investigation and specialist assessment. Persistent symptoms beyond 12 weeks warrant consideration of inflammatory arthropathy screening (ESR, CRP) and imaging review.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Reassurance that low back pain is usually self-limiting is the single most important intervention. Bed rest is harmful and should be explicitly discouraged; activity modification and staying mobile are central to recovery. Physiotherapy targeting core stability and graduated activity is effective and widely accessible in New Zealand through ACC funding for acute musculoskeletal injury. Short-course NSAIDs may provide symptom relief; topical NSAIDs minimise systemic exposure. Spinal manipulation by a trained physiotherapist has modest evidence for short-term benefit but should not replace exercise prescription. Duloxetine may be considered in chronic low back pain (duration over 12 weeks) at doses of 60 mg daily. Opioids have no role in non-specific low back pain and are associated with long-term harm; avoid their introduction. Cognitive behavioural approaches and acceptance-based therapies address yellow flags and reduce disability in chronic cases.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Red flag features warrant urgent referral for imaging and specialist assessment. Radiculopathy with objective neurological deficit persisting beyond 6 weeks requires MRI and orthopedic or neurosurgical review. Non-specific low back pain unresponsive to optimal conservative management beyond 12 weeks should be referred to a multidisciplinary pain management clinic if available, or to orthopedic or physiotherapy-led services focusing on function and return to activity rather than imaging-driven diagnosis.</p>
