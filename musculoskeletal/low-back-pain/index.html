Types of back pain â€ş

Non-specific back pain accounts for 80-90% of presentations. No single anatomical lesion explains the pain - it reflects muscle strain, ligament sprain, minor disc changes, or altered movement patterns. It is real, often disabling, but usually self-limiting. Imaging rarely changes management.

Specific back pain has an identifiable structural or systemic cause: vertebral fracture, disc prolapse compressing a nerve root, spinal stenosis, infection, or malignancy. Less common; red flags suggest this category and warrant urgent evaluation.

Referred pain originates from another organ - kidney, pelvis, or aorta - and presents as back pain. Clinical history and examination guide appropriate investigation.

Why does my back hurt? â€ş

The lumbar spine comprises vertebrae, intervertebral discs (tough outer annulus, gel-like nucleus), supporting ligaments, and surrounding musculature. Most acute pain results from soft tissue strain - muscles and ligaments inflame, causing pain and spasm that limit movement.

Disc herniation occurs when the nucleus pulposus protrudes through a tear in the annulus. If this contacts a nerve root, it causes radiculopathy - radiating leg pain, often with numbness or weakness in a dermatomal distribution. Sciatica (L4/L5/S1 nerve root compression) is the most common pattern.

Importantly, imaging findings correlate poorly with symptoms. Disc bulges and degeneration are found on MRI in a large proportion of pain-free adults. This is why imaging findings should always be interpreted in clinical context, not in isolation.

Self-management: what actually helps â€ş

The single most important thing you can do for acute back pain is to stay active. This does not mean pushing through severe pain, but rather moving gently within your tolerance and gradually returning to normal activities. Rest for more than a day or two actually worsens outcomes and prolongs pain.

Stay active and keep moving: Walking, gentle stretching, and returning to your normal routine as soon as you can manage are the cornerstones of recovery. Start with short sessions, perhaps 5 to 10 minutes of walking, and gradually increase. Movement prevents your muscles from becoming weak and stiff, maintains your confidence in your spine, and speeds recovery. Most people with acute back pain feel better within days if they move gently and regularly.

Pain relief: Over-the-counter paracetamol or ibuprofen can help manage pain while you continue moving. Ibuprofen is an NSAID and may be particularly helpful if inflammation is part of the problem, but avoid it if you have a history of ulcers, asthma, or kidney disease, or if you are dehydrated. Muscle relaxants such as benzodiazepines may help short-term but are not recommended long-term because they encourage rest and can become habit-forming.

Heat and ice: Heat feels good for muscular back pain and can ease stiffness. You might use a hot water bottle or take a warm shower. Ice is less commonly helpful for muscular back pain but some people find it soothing in the first 24 to 48 hours after an acute injury.

Posture and ergonomics: Avoid prolonged slouching or forward bending, as these place stress on the discs and ligaments. Instead, maintain a neutral spine when sitting, keep your monitor at eye level, and take regular breaks to move. Good ergonomics helps prevent relapse but will not cure acute pain by itself.

Physiotherapy: A physiotherapist can teach you exercises to strengthen your core, improve flexibility, and restore confidence in movement. They can also provide hands-on treatment such as massage or manipulation. Evidence suggests that physiotherapy is more effective than passive treatments like traction, and combined with staying active, it gives excellent outcomes2.

Sleep and stress: Poor sleep worsens pain perception, and back pain disrupts sleep, creating a vicious cycle. Prioritise sleep hygiene: keep your bedroom cool and dark, avoid screens before bed, and consider a supportive mattress. Stress management through relaxation, mindfulness, or exercise also helps reduce pain perception.

Treatments with weak or no evidence: Bed rest, traction, ultrasound, and spinal manipulation have not been shown to outperform staying active and structured exercise. While some people feel better after these treatments, the improvement is often due to the attention and reassurance rather than the treatment itself.

When imaging is needed â€ş

Most acute back pain does not require X-rays or MRI scans. In fact, early imaging often does more harm than good because it can reveal incidental findings that are not causing your pain but provoke anxiety and unnecessary further testing.

Non-specific back pain: If you have typical mechanical back pain with no red flags, imaging is not recommended in the first 4 to 6 weeks. The natural history of non-specific back pain is improvement, and imaging findings often do not change management. Many people have bulging discs, spondylosis, or other "abnormalities" visible on MRI but no pain, while others have severe pain with relatively normal imaging. This mismatch shows that imaging does not always explain pain.

When imaging is warranted: Imaging should be considered if you have red flags suggesting fracture, infection, malignancy, or cauda equina syndrome. These include sudden onset after significant trauma in someone at risk of osteoporosis, fever or unexplained weight loss, progressive neurological deficit, or saddle anaesthesia with bladder dysfunction. Your GP will decide if imaging is needed based on your clinical presentation.

What imaging shows: X-rays show bone but not discs or nerve roots well. They are useful mainly for detecting fractures or severe degenerative changes. MRI shows discs, nerves, and soft tissues in detail and is more useful if nerve root compression is suspected. However, MRI also reveals age-related changes that are common in people without back pain, which can be misleading and anxiety-provoking. A normal or abnormal MRI does not predict recovery or determine whether you need surgery.

The bottom line: In the vast majority of cases, you can recover from back pain without imaging. Reassurance about the benign nature of your pain and early mobilisation and exercise are far more important than a scan.

Treatments from your GP â€ş

Your GP can offer several approaches to managing back pain, tailored to your symptoms and how they are affecting you.

Analgesia: Paracetamol is a reasonable first-line pain reliever. NSAIDs like ibuprofen may be particularly helpful if you have inflammatory pain, but avoid them if contraindicated. Muscle relaxants such as benzodiazepines or baclofen may reduce muscle spasm and pain in the short term but have side effects and risk of dependence, so they are not recommended for prolonged use. Opioid painkillers are generally not recommended for non-specific back pain as they do not improve outcomes and carry significant risks of dependence and side effects.

Referral to physiotherapy: Your GP can refer you to a physiotherapist or physiotherapy service (including HealthPathways online physio in some regions). Structured exercise programmes and hands-on treatment have strong evidence for improving back pain and preventing relapse2.

Pain education and cognitive behavioural approaches: For chronic back pain, understanding the nature of your pain, recognising unhelpful thought patterns, and building confidence in your ability to manage pain are powerful tools. Your GP may discuss these approaches or refer you to a pain management programme.

Spinal injections: Epidural corticosteroid injections may provide short-term pain relief in sciatica but the evidence for long-term benefit is limited. They are sometimes used as a bridge to get people moving and engaged in physiotherapy, rather than as a sole treatment.

Surgery: Surgery is rarely indicated for non-specific back pain and is considered only for specific diagnoses such as severe nerve root compression not responding to conservative treatment, or cauda equina syndrome (a surgical emergency). Fusion surgery has variable outcomes and is not routinely recommended. For most people, non-surgical management is more effective and safer.

Red flags: urgent review

Certain features suggest serious pathology and require prompt assessment.

Symptom Possible concern Action
Bladder or bowel dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia Cauda equina syndrome Go to Emergency Department immediately
Fever, unintentional weight loss, night sweats Infection or malignancy See GP same day
Severe pain after significant trauma, or history of osteoporosis Vertebral fracture See GP same day, likely X-ray needed
History of cancer, progressive neurological deficit Metastatic disease or cord compression See GP urgently, imaging likely needed
Thoracic (upper back) pain, sudden onset, unremitting Aortic aneurysm, malignancy Go to Emergency Department or call 111
Progressive neurological deficit in the legs Cord or nerve root compression See GP urgently

âš  Go to ED immediately if you have:

  • Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control combined with back pain
  • Numbness in the groin, genital area, or inner thighs (saddle distribution)
  • Sudden inability to move your legs or progressive weakness

These symptoms may indicate cauda equina syndrome, a medical emergency requiring urgent surgical evaluation. Call 111 or go directly to the Emergency Department.
