<div class="topic-body">

<p>Vertebral compression fractures (VCF) are the most common fracture in osteoporosis yet frequently go unrecognized as simple "back pain." They may present acutely with sudden onset thoracic or thoracolumbar pain after trivial trauma, or be discovered incidentally on imaging for other reasons. Patients with multiple VCFs develop progressive kyphosis (Dowager's hump), leading to loss of height, functional decline, and increased falls risk. Any patient over 50 with new back pain warrants assessment for VCF, particularly those with risk factors for osteoporosis.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Acute VCF presents with sudden-onset mid-thoracic or thoracolumbar pain, often after minor trauma or even no recalled trauma. Chronic VCF may be asymptomatic and found incidentally on imaging, or presents as progressive kyphosis and loss of height. Examination shows midline thoracic or lumbar tenderness on percussion, loss of height compared to prior records, and increasing kyphosis. A height loss of more than 4 cm warrants X-ray screening for VCF. Red flags for malignant fracture include history of cancer, multiple vertebral levels involved, posterior cortex involvement, soft tissue mass, or neurological deficit.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>X-ray of the thoracic and/or lumbar spine identifies wedging, endplate depression, and reduced vertebral height. DEXA scan (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) assesses underlying bone mineral density and guides osteoporosis treatment. MRI is useful to distinguish acute (with oedema on STIR) from chronic fracture, to rule out malignant infiltration, and to evaluate for retropulsion or cord compression if neurological deficit is present. CT provides excellent bony detail if MRI is contraindicated.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Analgesia is primary: paracetamol, short-course NSAIDs (with caution in older adults with renal or cardiac disease), or weak opioids for severe acute pain. Activity modification and light activity are preferred over bed rest, which risks deconditioning and venous thromboembolism. Bracing with a thoracolumbar orthosis (TLO) reduces pain and may limit kyphosis progression. Most acute VCF pain resolves over 6 - 12 weeks. Treatment of underlying osteoporosis is essential to prevent future fractures (see osteoporosis page). Vertebroplasty or kyphoplasty (injection of cement into the fractured vertebra) are controversial; a Cochrane review does not support benefit over sham procedures for pain relief, though some centres perform these for refractory acute VCF with severe pain.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Neurological deficit (myelopathy or radiculopathy): urgent spinal surgical referral. Suspected malignant fracture: urgent imaging review and oncology referral. Routine referral to orthopaedic surgery, rheumatology, or geriatrics for osteoporosis management and fracture prevention strategy. Fracture Liaison Services (if available) should evaluate all patients with low-trauma fracture.</p>
