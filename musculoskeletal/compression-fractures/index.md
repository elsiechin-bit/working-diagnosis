<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>VCFs are the most common osteoporotic fracture and are frequently missed as "back pain." Any patient over 50 with new thoracic or thoracolumbar pain warrants X-ray. Height loss greater than 4 cm is an indication for screening.<sup>4</sup></li>
    <li>Red flags for malignant fracture: history of cancer, multiple levels involved, posterior cortex involvement, soft tissue mass, or neurological deficit. MRI distinguishes acute from chronic fracture and excludes malignant infiltration.</li>
    <li>Vertebroplasty and kyphoplasty are controversial - two randomised controlled trials show no benefit over sham for pain relief.<sup>1,2</sup> Treatment of underlying osteoporosis is essential to prevent future fractures.</li>
    <li>Neurological deficit (myelopathy or radiculopathy): urgent spinal surgical referral. Suspected malignant fracture: urgent imaging review and oncology referral.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Vertebral compression fractures present acutely with sudden-onset mid-thoracic or thoracolumbar pain after trivial or no recalled trauma, or are discovered incidentally on imaging. Chronic or multiple VCFs cause progressive kyphosis, loss of height, functional decline, and increased falls risk. Examination shows midline tenderness on percussion, increasing kyphosis, and documented loss of height. Red flags for malignant fracture include history of cancer, multiple vertebral levels, posterior cortex involvement, soft tissue mass, or neurological deficit.</p>

  <p>X-ray of the thoracic and/or lumbar spine identifies wedging, endplate depression, and reduced vertebral height. DEXA scan assesses bone mineral density and guides osteoporosis treatment. MRI distinguishes acute (STIR oedema) from chronic fracture, excludes malignant infiltration, and evaluates for cord compression if neurological signs are present. CT provides bony detail if MRI is contraindicated.</p>

  <p>Analgesia: paracetamol, short-course NSAIDs (with caution in older adults with renal or cardiac disease), or weak opioids for severe acute pain. Light activity is preferred over bed rest, which risks deconditioning and venous thromboembolism. Thoracolumbar orthosis (TLO) bracing reduces pain and may limit kyphosis progression. Most acute VCF pain resolves over 6-12 weeks. Treatment of the underlying osteoporosis is essential to prevent future fractures - see the osteoporosis page.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Neurological deficit (myelopathy or radiculopathy): urgent spinal surgical referral. Suspected malignant fracture: urgent imaging review and oncology referral. Routine referral to orthopaedic surgery, rheumatology, or geriatrics for osteoporosis management and fracture prevention. Fracture Liaison Services (where available) should evaluate all patients with low-trauma fracture.</p>
</div>
