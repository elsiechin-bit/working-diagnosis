<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Myelopathy red flags: hand clumsiness, wide-based gait disturbance, bilateral upper limb paraesthesia, L'hermitte's sign, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars, positive Hoffmann sign. Any of these warrant same-day urgent neurosurgical consultation and same-day MRI. Progressive myelopathy causes irreversible cord damage if untreated.</li>
    <li>Plain X-rays cannot visualise the spinal cord and do not reliably rule out myelopathy or radiculopathy. MRI is essential for suspected myelopathy and for radiculopathy not resolving after 6-8 weeks of conservative care.</li>
    <li>Cervical spondylosis is a radiological finding, not a clinical diagnosis. The syndromes are mechanical neck pain (most common, conservative management), radiculopathy (arm pain in a dermatomal distribution), and myelopathy (surgical emergency).</li>
    <li>Most radiculopathy without motor deficit resolves within 6-12 weeks without surgery. Motor deficit or progression despite conservative care warrants imaging and surgical consultation.<sup>2</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Cervical spondylosis (degenerative disc and facet joint disease) is ubiquitous on imaging in older adults. The clinical syndromes it can produce are mechanical neck pain (axial, activity-related, responds to conservative care), cervical radiculopathy (nerve root compression causing dermatomal arm pain, often sharp or burning, with Spurling's test positive), and cervical myelopathy (cord compression causing progressive neurological deficit). Only myelopathy constitutes a surgical emergency. Myelopathy red flags are hand clumsiness, gait disturbance, bilateral upper limb paraesthesia, L'hermitte's sign, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars, and Hoffmann sign.<sup>3</sup></p>

  <p>Plain X-rays cannot visualise the spinal cord and should not be used to rule out myelopathy or significant radiculopathy. MRI of the cervical spine is essential for any myelopathy features and for radiculopathy not resolving after 6-8 weeks. MRI defines cord compression, canal stenosis, and oedema (suggesting active compression). CT can define bony anatomy when MRI is contraindicated.</p>

  <p>Mechanical neck pain: activity modification, physiotherapy, NSAIDs, and short-term cervical collar if needed. Cervical radiculopathy: physiotherapy, NSAIDs, pregabalin or gabapentin for neuropathic pain, and cervical epidural steroid injection (fluoroscopy-guided) may accelerate recovery. Most radiculopathy without motor deficit resolves without surgery. Myelopathy is managed surgically once diagnosed - anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) or laminectomy/laminoplasty depending on pathology location. Decompression halts progression and may partially reverse deficits if performed early.<sup>1</sup></p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Suspected myelopathy: urgent same-day neurosurgical consultation and same-day MRI. Radiculopathy with motor deficit: semi-urgent referral to neurosurgery or orthopaedic spine surgery after MRI. Radiculopathy without motor deficit not improving at 6-8 weeks: MRI followed by specialist referral if imaging shows significant compression.</p>
</div>
