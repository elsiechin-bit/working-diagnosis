<p>Cervical spondylosis (degenerative cervical disc and facet joint disease) is ubiquitous on imaging in older adults but is not itself a diagnosis. Cervical spondylosis is a radiological finding; the clinical syndromes are mechanical neck pain (most common), cervical radiculopathy (nerve root compression causing arm pain and dermatomal deficit), and cervical myelopathy (cord compression causing progressive neurological deficit). Only myelopathy constitutes a surgical emergency due to risk of irreversible cord damage.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Mechanical neck pain is typically axial, worsened by activity, and responds to rest and conservative care. Cervical radiculopathy presents with arm pain (often sharp or burning) in a dermatomal distribution, sometimes with paraesthesia, weakness, or sensory loss in the same distribution. Neck rotation toward the affected side may exacerbate pain (positive Spurling's test). Myelopathy red flags are hand clumsiness (dropping objects, poor dexterity), gait disturbance, wide-based gait, bilateral upper limb paraesthesia, L'hermitte's sign (electric shock sensation down the spine on neck flexion), hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars, and a positive Hoffmann sign. Progressive myelopathy with cord compression can lead to irreversible neurological deficit; early diagnosis and decompression are critical.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain X-rays do not reliably rule in or out myelopathy or significant radiculopathy; they cannot visualise the spinal cord. MRI of the cervical spine is essential for suspected radiculopathy not resolving with conservative care (beyond 6 - 8 weeks) and for any features concerning for myelopathy. MRI defines the degree of cord compression, canal stenosis, and presence of oedema (suggesting acute or active compression). CT is less useful but can define bony anatomy if MRI is contraindicated.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Mechanical neck pain responds to activity modification, physiotherapy, NSAIDs, and sometimes cervical collars (short-term). Cervical radiculopathy often improves with conservative management over 6 - 12 weeks: physiotherapy, NSAIDs, pregabalin or gabapentin for neuropathic pain, and cervical epidural steroid injection (performed under fluoroscopy or ultrasound guidance) can accelerate recovery. Most radiculopathy without motor deficit resolves without surgery. Motor deficit (weakness) or progression despite conservative care warrants imaging and surgical consultation. Myelopathy is managed surgically once diagnosed; decompression halts progression and may partially reverse deficit if caught early. Anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) or laminectomy/laminoplasty are the main decompressive approaches depending on pathology location.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Suspected myelopathy: urgent same-day neurosurgical consultation. Arrange MRI urgently (same day if possible). Start patient on high-dose corticosteroids (methylprednisolone IV 30 mg/kg/day x 3 days if acute) pending specialist assessment. Radiculopathy with motor deficit: semi-urgent referral to neurosurgery or orthopaedic spine surgery after MRI. Radiculopathy without motor deficit not improving at 6 - 8 weeks: MRI followed by specialist referral if imaging shows significant compression.</p>
