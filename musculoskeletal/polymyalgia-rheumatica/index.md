<p>Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is a common inflammatory condition affecting older adults, predominantly in those of Northern European descent. It presents with bilateral shoulder and hip girdle stiffness and pain, worse in the morning. Profound morning stiffness lasting more than 45 minutes is the pathognomonic feature. The condition is exquisitely responsive to low-dose corticosteroids, and this response is virtually diagnostic. Always exclude giant cell arteritis (GCA) before starting steroids, as GCA requires higher steroid doses and carries risk of permanent vision loss if undertreated.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Patients (almost always age >60, female predominance 2:1) describe sudden-onset bilateral shoulder and hip girdle aching and stiffness. Typical complaints include difficulty combing hair, dressing, rising from a chair, or turning in bed. Morning stiffness persists for more than 45 minutes (often hours). Pain is aching rather than sharp. Constitutional symptoms (low-grade fever, malaise, fatigue) may accompany the articular symptoms. Examination reveals pain on active range of motion of the shoulders and hips but normal passive range of motion (distinguishing from true arthritis). Synovitis is absent on examination. Ask specifically about headache, jaw claudication (pain with chewing), scalp tenderness, and visual symptoms to screen for concurrent GCA (present in 15 - 20% of PMR patients).</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Elevated acute-phase reactants (ESR typically >40, often >80; CRP also elevated) are essential for diagnosis. FBC shows normocytic anaemia and thrombocytosis. Alkaline phosphatase may be elevated. Thyroid function (TSH) is checked to exclude hypothyroidism, which can mimic PMR. Rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP are negative. Low-titre ANA may be present but is non-specific. Do not delay diagnosis based on imaging (ultrasound or MRI may show synovitis but are not required). The clinical picture plus elevated inflammatory markers is sufficient. Patients with atypical features (sudden presentation, very high inflammatory markers, absence of morning stiffness, younger age) warrant rheumatology input.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Prednisolone 15 - 20 mg/day is typical starting dose. Patients show dramatic clinical improvement within 24 - 72 hours (failure to improve suggests misdiagnosis). This rapid response is so characteristic that it is therapeutic and diagnostic. Taper prednisolone slowly over 18 - 24 months; many patients require 2+ years of therapy. Monitor CRP and ESR monthly during taper as a guide; taper more slowly if these rise or symptoms recur. Relapse occurs in 50% of patients during taper; increase dose and taper more gradually. Bone protection is mandatory given prolonged steroid use: ensure adequate calcium (dietary + supplement if <1200 mg/day intake), vitamin D (supplement with cholecalciferol), and consider alendronate (PHARMAC-funded) if risk factors for osteoporosis are present. PHARMAC funds prednisolone, alendronate, and cholecalciferol. Rheumatology review is appropriate if atypical features, treatment failure, or recurrent relapses occur.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Most PMR is managed in primary care. Refer to rheumatology if diagnosis is uncertain, atypical features are present, or the patient is not improving as expected. Urgent referral to rheumatology and ophthalmology is required if GCA is suspected.</p>
