<p>Stress fractures and overuse injuries are increasingly common in adolescent athletes, particularly with intensifying sports participation. Training error - rapid load increase, poor technique, inadequate recovery - is the primary modifiable cause. However, underlying factors such as female athlete triad (now renamed Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport, RED-S) involving energy deficiency, menstrual dysfunction, and low bone density create a vulnerability to stress fracture that extends beyond biomechanical factors alone.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Stress fractures present with insidious-onset focal pain at a specific skeletal site that worsens with weight-bearing and repetitive activity. The pain characteristically improves with rest but returns with activity. Examination reveals exquisite point tenderness, often worse with single-leg hopping or a percussion test. Swelling is variable. Common sites include the tibial shaft (most frequent), femoral neck (high-risk, must not miss), metatarsal heads (march fracture), and pars interarticularis in young gymnasts and rowers (spondylolysis).</p>

<p>Tendinopathy (Achilles, patellar, peroneal) presents similarly with gradual-onset pain, morning stiffness, and pain exacerbated by the inciting activity. Palpable nodularity and thickening of the tendon may be present. Ecchymosis is uncommon unless there has been acute rupture.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain radiographs are the first-line investigation but may be normal in the first 2-3 weeks of a stress fracture while callus is forming. MRI is the gold standard for early stress fracture diagnosis and provides information about the fracture grade and healing stage.<sup>1</sup> For femoral neck stress fractures, MRI should not be delayed because of the risk of nonunion and avascular necrosis. Bone scintigraphy is an alternative if MRI is unavailable.</p>

<p>In female adolescent athletes, screen for RED-S using a validated tool such as the LEAF questionnaire (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport). Assess menstrual history, dietary intake, and training load. If RED-S is suspected, assess bone health with DEXA scanning and investigate with blood tests (FSH, LH, testosterone, cortisol, thyroid function).</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>The cornerstone of management is relative rest - not complete immobilisation but activity modification and load reduction. Athletes should avoid the activity that provokes pain while maintaining other aerobic fitness through low-impact activities (swimming, stationary cycling). Address training errors: gradually increase training load (no more than 10% per week), ensure adequate recovery between sessions, and optimise technique. NSAIDs may reduce pain but have no evidence for accelerating fracture healing.</p>

<p>Femoral neck stress fractures carry high risk of nonunion and require urgent orthopaedic referral for assessment of fracture geometry and consideration of surgical fixation. Other lower-risk fractures may heal with conservative management over 6-12 weeks. Tendinopathy is managed with eccentric loading programmes; avoid corticosteroid injections in young athletes due to rupture risk.</p>

<p>If RED-S is identified, address energy availability with nutrition and sports psychology input. Reduce training intensity and volume until menstrual function and bone health have improved. Return to sport should follow graduated loading protocols supervised by physiotherapy.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Femoral neck stress fracture requires urgent orthopaedic referral for imaging and consideration of surgical fixation. Pars interarticularis stress fracture warrants orthopaedic review. Other high-risk stress fracture sites (anterior cortex tibia, talar neck) should have orthopaedic consultation. Recurrent stress fractures or failure to progress with conservative management warrant imaging review and assessment for underlying RED-S or other systemic causes.</p>
