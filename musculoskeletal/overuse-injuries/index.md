<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Stress fractures present with insidious-onset focal pain at a specific skeletal site, worsening with weight-bearing and repetitive activity, with exquisite point tenderness. Common sites: tibial shaft (most frequent), metatarsal heads, and pars interarticularis in young gymnasts and rowers (spondylolysis). Training error is the primary modifiable cause.</li>
    <li>Plain X-rays may be normal for 2-3 weeks while callus forms. MRI is the gold standard for early diagnosis.<sup>1</sup> Femoral neck stress fracture must not be missed: arrange MRI without delay because of the risk of nonunion and avascular necrosis. Urgent orthopaedic referral for any femoral neck stress fracture.</li>
    <li>In female adolescent athletes, screen for RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) using the LEAF questionnaire. Assess menstrual history, dietary intake, and training load. RED-S creates bone fragility beyond biomechanical factors and requires nutritional and psychological input alongside load reduction.<sup>2</sup></li>
    <li>Management is relative rest and load modification, not complete immobilisation. Maintain aerobic fitness through low-impact activities. Training load: increase no more than 10% per week. Tendinopathy is managed with eccentric loading - avoid corticosteroid injection in young athletes due to rupture risk.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Stress fractures present with insidious-onset focal pain at a specific skeletal site that worsens with weight-bearing and repetitive activity, characteristically improving with rest but returning with activity. Examination reveals exquisite point tenderness; single-leg hopping or percussion test may be positive. Swelling is variable. Common sites include the tibial shaft, femoral neck (high-risk, must not miss), metatarsal heads (march fracture), and the pars interarticularis in gymnasts and rowers (spondylolysis). Tendinopathy (Achilles, patellar, peroneal) presents with gradual-onset pain, morning stiffness, and activity-provoked pain; palpable nodularity and thickening of the tendon may be present. Female adolescent athletes with multiple stress fractures, menstrual dysfunction, or low energy availability require RED-S screening.</p>

  <p>Plain X-rays are first-line but may be normal for the first 2-3 weeks while callus forms. MRI is the gold standard, providing fracture grade and healing stage information.<sup>1</sup> For femoral neck stress fractures, MRI should not be delayed. Screen female adolescent athletes for RED-S: LEAF questionnaire, menstrual history, dietary assessment. If RED-S suspected, assess bone health with DEXA and check FSH, LH, cortisol, and thyroid function.</p>

  <p>Relative rest with load reduction rather than complete immobilisation is the cornerstone. Athletes should avoid pain-provoking activity while maintaining aerobic fitness through swimming or stationary cycling. Address training errors: maximum 10% load increase per week, adequate recovery, optimise technique. NSAIDs for pain but have no evidence of accelerating fracture healing. Tendinopathy: eccentric loading programmes; avoid corticosteroid injection in young athletes (rupture risk). If RED-S is identified, address energy availability with nutrition and sports psychology input; graduated return to sport after menstrual function and bone health improve.<sup>2</sup></p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Femoral neck stress fracture requires urgent orthopaedic referral for imaging and consideration of surgical fixation. Pars interarticularis fracture (spondylolysis) warrants orthopaedic review. Other high-risk sites (anterior cortex tibia, talar neck) should have orthopaedic consultation. Recurrent stress fractures or failure to progress with conservative management warrant imaging review and assessment for underlying RED-S or systemic cause. ACC covers physiotherapy and surgical management for acute sports injury.</p>
</div>
