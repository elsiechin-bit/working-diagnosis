<p>Growing pains are the most common cause of lower limb pain in children, affecting 10-15% of the paediatric population at some point. Despite their name, there is no evidence that growth itself causes the pain. These are benign, self-limiting episodes of nocturnal aching in the lower limbs, characterised by complete pain-free periods during the day and normal physical examination findings. Growing pains represent a diagnosis of exclusion, and the key skill in primary care is recognising typical presentations to avoid unnecessary investigation whilst remaining alert to atypical features that warrant further workup.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>Growing pains typically present with bilateral, non-articular aching in the lower limbs, most commonly affecting the posterior thighs, calves, and the areas around the knees. The pain is predominantly nocturnal, often waking the child from sleep, though children may complain of discomfort during the day if activity levels have been high. Pain is intermittent rather than constant, with pain-free periods that may last weeks to months. The episodes are often worse after days of increased physical activity, such as sports or play.</p>

<p>The typical age of onset is between 3 and 12 years, with two peaks: one at 4-6 years and another at 8-12 years. There is no clear sex predominance. On examination, the affected child appears well, with normal gait during the day and no visible swelling, erythema, or joint effusion. Notably, children with growing pains are non-weight bearing only during the acute pain episode; they walk normally between episodes. Range of motion of the hips, knees, and ankles is full and painless, and there are no signs of systemic illness.</p>

<p>Parents frequently report that rubbing or massage of the affected areas provides relief. The natural history is typically benign: pain settles with simple analgesia and reassurance, episodes become less frequent over months, and the condition resolves entirely by the early teenage years. The variability and intermittent nature of symptoms can cause significant parental anxiety, making a clear diagnosis and explanation crucial.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>

<p>Growing pains are diagnosed clinically, and routine investigation is not indicated in typical presentations. A clear history of bilateral, nocturnal, non-articular pain in a well child with normal examination findings is sufficient to diagnose the condition. Unnecessary investigation increases parental anxiety and medicalises a benign condition.</p>

<p>However, investigation is warranted if the presentation is atypical: unilateral pain, daytime pain that is constant or affects function, morning stiffness, visible swelling, erythema, warmth, fever, or an abnormal gait during the day. Red flags that require investigation include weight loss, persistent limp, systemic symptoms, or pain localised to a single joint.</p>

<p>If atypical features are present, the following tests should be considered: full blood count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) or C-reactive protein (CRP), antinuclear antibody (ANA) if polyarticular involvement is suspected, and X-rays of the affected area if there is concern about structural pathology such as Perthes disease or bone lesions. In NZ practice, these investigations can be arranged via primary care with blood tests ordered through the usual laboratory, and imaging referrals made to district health board radiology.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>The cornerstone of management is reassurance. Parents need clear explanation that growing pains are benign, self-limiting, and compatible with normal childhood activity. This reassurance alone often significantly reduces parental anxiety and healthcare utilisation.</p>

<p>For acute pain episodes, simple analgesia is effective: paracetamol 15 mg/kg or ibuprofen 10 mg/kg, both administered orally during the pain episode. Many parents find that rubbing or massage of the affected limbs provides additional comfort. Light stretching exercises of the calf and thigh muscles may help; these can be taught to parents as a preventive strategy.</p>

<p>Parents should be counselled to avoid unnecessary activity restriction. Children should be encouraged to participate normally in play and sports, as reduced activity does not prevent pain and may fuel parental anxiety. Reassurance should address common parental concerns: that growing pains will not cause long-term damage, that they do not require special shoes or insoles, and that they are not a sign of serious illness.</p>

<p>A follow-up appointment at 4-6 weeks to review response to reassurance and simple measures is reasonable, particularly if parental anxiety remains high. If symptoms persist beyond typical patterns (i.e., daily pain, significant functional limitation) or if new features develop, revisiting the differential diagnosis is appropriate.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Routine referral is not necessary for typical growing pains. However, referral to paediatrics should be considered if: the presentation is atypical (unilateral, daytime, persistent at rest, associated with swelling or fever); investigation reveals abnormalities (elevated inflammatory markers, positive ANA, or radiological changes); symptoms persist despite reassurance and simple management over several months; parental anxiety is extreme and not allayed by explanation; or red flags for serious pathology emerge (limp, systemic symptoms, weight loss).</p>

<p>If investigation reveals findings suggesting juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA), such as elevated inflammatory markers or positive ANA, referral to paediatric rheumatology is indicated. Referral to paediatric orthopaedics should be considered if imaging suggests Perthes disease or other structural pathology.</p>
