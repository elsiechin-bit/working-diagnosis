  <div class="panic-tile dont-miss">
    <p class="panic-tile__label">Don't miss</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Inflammatory arthritis (warm, polyarticular, morning stiffness) - not OA</li>
      <li>Septic arthritis (acute hot effusion, fever) - aspirate before steroid</li>
      <li>Avascular necrosis (steroid use, alcohol, sickle cell) - MRI</li>
      <li>Spontaneous osteonecrosis of the knee in older women - acute medial pain</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="panic-tile first-line">
    <p class="panic-tile__label">First-line</p>
    <dl>
      <dt>Highest yield</dt>
      <dd>5-10% weight reduction reduces pain and improves function</dd>
      <dt>Movement</dt>
      <dd>Physio + sustained quadriceps strengthening (land or aquatic)</dd>
      <dt>Imaging</dt>
      <dd>Standing weight-bearing X-rays bilateral; add skyline if patellofemoral suspected</dd>
      <dt>Pharmacological</dt>
      <dd>Topical NSAID limited; oral NSAID with PPI cover; intra-articular triamcinolone 40 mg gives 2-4 weeks relief</dd>
    </dl>
  </div>
  <div class="panic-tile refer">
    <p class="panic-tile__label">Refer when</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Functional limitation impacting quality of life despite 3-6 months conservative care</li>
      <li>Patient understands natural history and is willing to consider surgery</li>
      <li>Diagnostic uncertainty - rheumatology if inflammatory features</li>
      <li>Refractory pain on optimised therapy</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
  <div class="panic-tile tell">
    <p class="panic-tile__label">Tell the patient</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Pain severity does not match X-ray severity - we treat you, not the picture</li>
      <li>Arthroscopic washout or meniscectomy do not help OA - do not pay for them privately</li>
      <li>Strong quads change pain more than any tablet</li>
      <li>Joint replacement works very well when the time is right</li>
    </ul>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Knee osteoarthritis is the most common indication for total knee arthroplasty in New Zealand. It predominantly affects the medial compartment (causing varus deformity), though lateral (valgus) and patellofemoral disease occur. Patients report weight-bearing pain, difficulty walking or climbing stairs, stiffness after rest (gelling phenomenon), and crepitus on movement. Examination shows crepitus, effusion, bony enlargement, and decreased range of motion. A Baker's cyst (popliteal swelling) may be present. Joint line tenderness suggests meniscal pathology but is non-specific. Older adults frequently experience sleep disruption and falls risk secondary to pain and instability.</p>

  <p>Standing weight-bearing X-rays are essential - obtain bilateral views to assess both compartments and compare laterality. Skyline (tangential) views help evaluate patellofemoral compartment involvement. Advanced imaging (MRI, CT) is not routine. Blood work (FBC, renal function) guides medication choices.</p>

  <p>Knee bracing, particularly offloader braces for unicompartmental disease, reduces pain by shifting load from the affected compartment. Topical NSAIDs have limited efficacy for the knee due to depth; oral NSAIDs (with PPI co-prescription if no contraindications) or paracetamol are more appropriate. Viscosupplementation (hyaluronic acid) is not PHARMAC-funded; evidence for benefit is modest. Intra-articular platelet-rich plasma (PRP) is also not PHARMAC-funded.</p>

  <p>Total knee arthroplasty (TKA) offers excellent pain relief and functional restoration in carefully selected patients with significant pain, functional limitation, radiographic OA, and optimised conservative management. Unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (UKA) is an option for isolated medial or lateral compartment disease in appropriate candidates, with faster recovery and superior kinematics compared to TKA, but requires appropriate patient selection. Revision rates are higher than TKA if performed outside optimal criteria.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to orthopaedic surgery when functional limitation significantly impacts quality of life and conservative management has been optimised (typically 3-6 months). The patient must understand the natural history and be willing to consider surgery. Public waiting lists vary by region; private options are available.</p>
