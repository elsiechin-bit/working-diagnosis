<p>Knee osteoarthritis is the most common indication for total knee arthroplasty in New Zealand. It predominantly affects the medial compartment (causing varus deformity), though lateral (valgus) and patellofemoral disease occur. Pain is worse with weight-bearing and activity, morning stiffness is brief, and functional limitation affects walking and stairs. Older adults frequently experience sleep disruption and falls risk secondary to pain and instability.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Patients report weight-bearing pain, difficulty walking or climbing stairs, stiffness after rest (gelling phenomenon), and crepitus on movement. Examination shows crepitus, effusion, bony enlargement, and decreased range of motion. A Baker's cyst (popliteal swelling) may be present. Joint line tenderness suggests meniscal pathology but is non-specific. Importantly, pain severity does not correlate with radiographic severity; many patients with severe imaging changes are minimally symptomatic, while others with mild radiographic changes experience significant pain.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Standing weight-bearing X-rays are essential: non-weight-bearing views significantly underestimate joint space loss. Obtain bilateral views to assess both compartments and compare laterality. Skyline (tangential) views help evaluate patellofemoral compartment involvement. Advanced imaging (MRI, CT) is not routine. Blood work (FBC, renal function) guides medication choices.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Weight loss is the highest-yield intervention. Even modest weight reduction (5 - 10% body weight) decreases pain and improves function in weight-bearing joints. Physiotherapy is first-line, with evidence supporting both land-based and aquatic exercise - the key is regular, sustained activity. Strengthening the quadriceps improves pain and function. Knee bracing, particularly offloader braces for unicompartmental disease, reduces pain by shifting load from the affected compartment. Topical NSAIDs have limited efficacy for the knee due to depth; oral NSAIDs (with PPI co-prescription if no contraindications) or paracetamol are more appropriate. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (triamcinolone 40 mg) provides 2 - 4 weeks of relief and can be repeated seasonally. Viscosupplementation (hyaluronic acid) is not PHARMAC-funded; evidence for benefit is modest. Intra-articular platelet-rich plasma (PRP) is not PHARMAC-funded. Arthroscopic washout or meniscectomy for OA alone has no benefit over sham procedures and is not recommended.</p>

<p>Total knee arthroplasty (TKA) offers excellent pain relief and functional restoration in carefully selected patients with significant pain, functional limitation, radiographic OA, and optimised conservative management. Unicompartmental knee arthroplasty (UKA) is an option for isolated medial or lateral compartment disease in appropriate candidates, with faster recovery and superior kinematics compared to TKA, but requires appropriate patient selection. Revision rates are higher than TKA if performed outside optimal criteria.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to orthopaedic surgery when functional limitation significantly impacts quality of life and conservative management has been optimised (typically 3 - 6 months). The patient must understand the natural history and be willing to consider surgery. Public waiting lists vary by region; private options are available.</p>
