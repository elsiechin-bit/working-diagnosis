<p>Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in men over 40 and is particularly prevalent in New Zealand's Māori and Pacific Islander populations due to higher genetic predisposition and socioeconomic factors. Acute gout is exquisitely painful but self-limited if treated appropriately. However, recurrent attacks and chronic tophaceous gout cause significant morbidity. Early recognition of attacks and institution of urate-lowering therapy (ULT) prevent progression and complications.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Acute gout classically presents with sudden-onset severe monoarticular inflammatory arthritis, most commonly the first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra), though ankle and knee are also common. Pain, redness, warmth, swelling, and often low-grade fever appear over hours. Triggers include alcohol, purine-rich foods (red meat, organ meats, certain seafood), dehydration, diuretics (including for hypertension), and paradoxically, initiation of urate-lowering therapy (ULT). Diagnosis is confirmed by synovial fluid aspiration showing negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals (gold standard). Serum urate may be normal or low during acute attack due to acute phase response; elevated baseline serum urate supports diagnosis but is not diagnostic. History of prior attacks, male sex, and family history of gout increase pre-test probability.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Baseline serum urate (not during acute attack), renal function (eGFR), urine albumin - creatinine ratio, fasting lipids, and glucose or HbA1c should be measured when gout is diagnosed. X-rays showing tophi and erosions indicate chronic tophaceous gout. Ultrasound may show double contour sign indicating monosodium urate deposition on cartilage. Synovial fluid analysis is the gold standard but is not routinely available in primary care; clinical diagnosis of podagra in a man with hyperuricaemia is reliable.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Acute management: NSAIDs (naproxen or diclofenac are first-line if no contraindications such as renal impairment or anticoagulation), or colchicine 500 mcg twice daily (lower dose is better tolerated than high-dose regimens). Prednisolone 20 - 30 mg daily is effective if NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection is highly effective for monoarticular attacks. Initiate ULT only after acute attack resolves (2 - 4 weeks) to avoid paradoxical flare; co-prescribe prophylactic colchicine for first 6 months of ULT initiation. Allopurinol is first-line ULT in New Zealand, PHARMAC-funded, starting at 50 - 100 mg daily and titrating to target serum urate <0.36 mmol/L (<0.30 mmol/L for tophaceous gout). Indications for ULT: ≥2 attacks per year, tophi, renal stones, or uric acid nephropathy. PHARMAC funds both allopurinol and colchicine.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Rheumatology referral is appropriate for refractory gout (frequent flares despite adequate ULT and prophylaxis), tophaceous gout, gout complicated by chronic kidney disease, or diagnostic uncertainty. For Māori and Pacific patients: acknowledge higher genetic predisposition, avoid stigmatising language ("lifestyle disease"), and emphasise that gout is treatable and that ethnic background does not reflect adherence or responsibility. Early aggressive ULT prevents disability and complications.</p>
