<div class="topic-body">

<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Do not start or stop ULT during an acute attack.</strong> Wait 2-4 weeks after full resolution; starting allopurinol during a flare can prolong or trigger a new one.</li>
    <li>Acute attack: NSAIDs first line (naproxen 500 mg BD or diclofenac 50 mg TDS) if no contraindications. Colchicine 500 mcg BD is the alternative. Prednisolone 20-30 mg/day if both contraindicated.</li>
    <li>Serum urate is often normal or low during an acute attack. A normal result does not exclude gout; check fasting urate 2-4 weeks after the attack resolves.</li>
    <li>Indications for urate-lowering therapy: two or more attacks per year, any tophi, renal calculi, or uric acid nephropathy. Target serum urate &lt;0.36 mmol/L (&lt;0.30 for tophaceous gout).</li>
    <li>Gout is significantly more prevalent in Māori and Pacific peoples. Treat early and aggressively. Avoid stigmatising framing - this is a metabolic disease with strong genetic drivers, not a lifestyle choice.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis in men over 40 and is particularly prevalent in New Zealand's Māori and Pacific Islander populations due to higher genetic predisposition to hyperuricaemia and downstream socioeconomic barriers to treatment access.<sup>4</sup> Acute attacks are among the most painful presentations in general practice but are self-limited with appropriate treatment. The greater problem is that urate-lowering therapy (ULT) is initiated late, titrated inadequately, and stopped too soon, leading to preventable recurrence, tophaceous disease, and joint destruction.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation and diagnosis</p>
  <p>
    Acute gout presents with sudden-onset, severe monoarticular inflammatory arthritis developing over hours, most commonly at the first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra). Ankle and knee are also frequent sites. Examination shows warmth, erythema, swelling, and exquisite tenderness. Low-grade fever is common. Triggers include alcohol, purine-rich foods (red meat, organ meats, shellfish, anchovies), dehydration, diuretics, and paradoxically, initiation or dose change of ULT.
  </p>
  <p>
    Clinical diagnosis of podagra in a middle-aged or older man with hyperuricaemia is reliable and does not require aspiration in primary care. Synovial fluid analysis (negatively birefringent monosodium urate crystals) is the gold standard but is not routinely needed when the clinical picture is typical. Consider aspiration when the diagnosis is uncertain or septic arthritis cannot be excluded.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Acute attack management</p>
  <p>
    Start treatment as early as possible - ideally within 12-24 hours of attack onset. First-line options:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>NSAIDs:</strong> naproxen 500 mg BD or diclofenac 50 mg TDS for 5-7 days. Avoid in renal impairment (eGFR &lt;30), anticoagulation, active peptic ulcer disease, or heart failure. Co-prescribe a PPI in patients over 65 or with GI risk.</li>
    <li><strong>Colchicine:</strong> 500 mcg BD (low-dose regimen). Better tolerated than traditional high-dose regimens with equivalent efficacy. Reduce dose in renal impairment (eGFR &lt;30: 500 mcg once daily). Avoid with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, ciclosporin).</li>
    <li><strong>Prednisolone:</strong> 20-30 mg daily for 5 days if NSAIDs and colchicine are both contraindicated. Also the preferred option in the frail elderly. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection is highly effective for monoarticular attacks accessible to aspiration.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Do not initiate ULT during the acute attack. If the patient is already established on allopurinol, continue it unchanged - stopping it is more destabilising than continuing.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Urate-lowering therapy</p>
  <p>
    Initiate ULT 2-4 weeks after the acute attack has fully resolved. Allopurinol is first-line and PHARMAC-funded in NZ. Start at 50-100 mg daily (50 mg in renal impairment) and titrate by 50-100 mg every 4 weeks until the serum urate target is reached. Check serum urate 4-6 weeks after each dose change. Maximum dose is 900 mg/day, though most patients achieve target at 300-400 mg/day.
  </p>
  <p>
    Co-prescribe prophylactic colchicine 500 mcg once or twice daily for the first 6 months of ULT to prevent mobilisation flares. Without it, initiation of ULT commonly triggers an attack and patients stop the medication.
  </p>
  <p>
    Explain to patients that ULT is a long-term commitment and that flares may paradoxically increase in the first few months as urate crystals mobilise. This is the most common reason for early discontinuation; pre-empting it significantly improves adherence.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Baseline investigations and monitoring</p>
  <p>
    At diagnosis, measure: serum urate (fasting, not during acute attack), renal function (eGFR and urine ACR), fasting lipids, and HbA1c or fasting glucose. Gout is strongly associated with metabolic syndrome, hypertension, CKD, and cardiovascular disease; the metabolic workup has management implications beyond the gout itself.
  </p>
  <p>
    Plain X-rays showing tophi or bony erosions indicate chronic tophaceous disease. Ultrasound demonstrating the double contour sign (monosodium urate deposition on cartilage surface) supports the diagnosis when it is uncertain. Neither is routinely needed in straightforward presentations.
  </p>
  <p>
    Monitor serum urate 4-6 weekly during ULT dose titration, then 6-12 monthly once stable at target. Monitor renal function and eGFR annually; allopurinol dose should be adjusted downward in progressive CKD.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Equity, referral, and refractory gout</p>
  <p>
    Māori and Pacific peoples have significantly higher rates of gout, partly due to higher genetic prevalence of ABCG2 and SLC2A9 variants affecting urate handling, and partly due to barriers to accessing consistent primary care and funded medications.<sup>4</sup> Clinically this means earlier presentation, more severe disease at diagnosis, and higher rates of tophaceous gout. Avoid attributing disease burden to lifestyle without acknowledging the genetic and systemic drivers. Emphasise that gout is highly treatable and that adherence to ULT prevents disability.
  </p>
  <p>
    Refer to rheumatology for: refractory gout (frequent flares despite adequate ULT at target dose and 6-month colchicine prophylaxis), tophaceous gout with joint erosion, diagnostic uncertainty, or gout in the context of severe CKD where allopurinol dose management is complex.
  </p>
  <p>
    Febuxostat is a xanthine oxidase inhibitor alternative to allopurinol for patients who are intolerant or in whom allopurinol is contraindicated. It is PHARMAC-funded via Special Authority in NZ. Pegloticase (recombinant uricase) is a tertiary option for severe refractory tophaceous gout and requires specialist initiation.
  </p>
</div>
