<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Three phases over 2-3 years: freezing (months 0-9, pain dominates), frozen (months 9-15, stiffness without much pain), thawing (months 15-24+, gradual spontaneous recovery). Most recover; patient understanding of the timeline is the most useful thing to convey.</li>
    <li>Diabetes is the strongest risk factor (10-20% of diabetics develop frozen shoulder). Ask about thyroid disease, Dupuytren's, and recent immobility. Bilateral disease is more common in diabetics.</li>
    <li>Loss of passive range of motion distinguishes frozen shoulder from rotator cuff pathology - in rotator cuff disease, passive ROM is initially preserved. External rotation is lost first.</li>
    <li>Intra-articular corticosteroid injection is most beneficial in the painful freezing phase. Hydrodilatation (image-guided distension arthrography) provides modest benefit in refractory cases and is available at NZ radiology centres.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Adhesive capsulitis is progressive fibrosis and contracture of the glenohumeral joint capsule, occurring predominantly in patients aged 40-60, with slight female predominance. Diabetes is the strongest risk factor; hypothyroidism, Dupuytren's disease, and prolonged immobility also increase risk.</p>

  <p>Phase 1 (freezing, months 0-9): pain predominates, sleep disturbance is marked, progressive loss of active and passive range of motion. Phase 2 (frozen, months 9-15): pain diminishes but stiffness worsens; minimal functional loss beyond restricted motion. Phase 3 (thawing, months 15-24+): gradual spontaneous recovery of movement over months. External rotation is lost first and earliest. Examination shows pain on passive external rotation and abduction. A positive empty can (Jobe's) test would suggest associated rotator cuff disease.</p>

  <p>Plain X-rays are obtained to exclude other pathology (osteoarthritis, calcific tendinopathy, fracture) but do not diagnose adhesive capsulitis. Ultrasound and MRI are not routine - diagnosis is clinical based on progressive loss of passive range of motion.</p>

  <p>Patient education about the natural history and expected timeline (2-3 years) is crucial; understanding that most recover spontaneously improves compliance with physiotherapy. NSAIDs provide modest pain relief. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection into the glenohumeral joint is most beneficial in the painful freezing phase; improvement in pain and range of motion is significant in the first few weeks but does not accelerate overall recovery. Physiotherapy with gentle range of motion exercises, pendulum exercises, and progressive stretching is appropriate - avoid aggressive mobilisation in the painful phase as this worsens pain and inflammation. As pain improves in phase 2, progressive stretching and strengthening become more appropriate. Hydrodilatation (distension arthrography) - injection of saline, corticosteroid, and local anaesthetic into the joint capsule under image guidance - provides modest benefit in refractory cases and is available in NZ radiology centres. Manipulation under anaesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release are surgical options reserved for capsular fibrosis not responding to conservative measures over 6-12 months.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to physiotherapy early for range of motion guidance. After 3-6 months of conservative care without significant improvement, consider referral to orthopaedics or radiology for hydrodilatation assessment, or to rheumatology if the diagnosis is uncertain.</p>
