<p>Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) is progressive fibrosis and contracture of the glenohumeral joint capsule, occurring predominantly in patients aged 40 - 60, with slight female predominance. Diabetes is the strongest risk factor (10 - 20% of diabetics develop frozen shoulder); hypothyroidism, Dupuytren's disease, and prolonged immobility also increase risk. The condition evolves through three phases over 2 - 3 years (sometimes longer), each with different dominant features and management implications. Patient understanding of natural history improves compliance with physiotherapy during the painful early phase.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Phase 1 (freezing, months 0 - 9): pain predominates, sleep disturbance is marked, progressive loss of active and passive range of motion. Phase 2 (frozen, months 9 - 15): pain diminishes but stiffness worsens; minimal functional loss beyond restricted motion. Phase 3 (thawing, months 15 - 24+): gradual spontaneous recovery of movement over months. The hallmark finding is loss of passive range of motion, distinguishing frozen shoulder from rotator cuff pathology (where passive ROM is initially preserved). External rotation is lost first and earliest. Examination shows pain on passive external rotation and abduction. Positive "empty can" (Jobe's) test would suggest associated rotator cuff disease.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Plain X-rays are obtained to exclude other pathology (osteoarthritis, calcific tendinopathy, fracture) but do not diagnose adhesive capsulitis. Ultrasound and MRI are not routine. If imaging is obtained, MRI may show thickened capsule and synovial fibrosis but this does not change management. Diagnosis is clinical based on progressive loss of passive range of motion.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Patient education about the natural history and expected timeline (2 - 3 years) is crucial; understanding that most recover spontaneously improves compliance with physiotherapy. NSAIDs provide modest pain relief. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection into the glenohumeral joint is most beneficial in the painful freezing phase; improvement in pain and range of motion is significant in the first few weeks but does not accelerate recovery. Physiotherapy with gentle range of motion exercises, pendulum exercises, and progressive stretching is appropriate. Avoid aggressive mobilisation in the painful phase as this worsens pain and inflammation. As pain improves (phase 2), progressive stretching and strengthening become more aggressive. Hydrodilatation (distension arthrography) - injection of saline, corticosteroid, and local anaesthetic into the joint capsule under image guidance - provides modest benefit in refractory cases; this is available in NZ radiology centres. Manipulation under anaesthesia or arthroscopic capsular release are surgical options for capsular fibrosis not responding to conservative measures over 6 - 12 months, but are reserved for refractory cases.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Refer to physiotherapy early for range of motion guidance. After 3 - 6 months of conservative care without significant improvement, consider referral to orthopaedics or radiology for hydrodilatation assessment, or to rheumatology if diagnosis is uncertain.</p>
