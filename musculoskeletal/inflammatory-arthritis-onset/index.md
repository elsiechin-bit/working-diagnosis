<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Morning stiffness lasting more than one hour that improves with activity is the key clinical feature distinguishing inflammatory from mechanical joint pain. Joint swelling (not just pain) is characteristic. JIA affects approximately 1 in 1000 children.<sup>2</sup></li>
    <li>JIA subtypes: oligoarticular (most common, 4 or fewer joints, often ANA-positive with uveitis risk), polyarticular RF-negative, polyarticular RF-positive (worst prognosis), and systemic JIA / Still's disease (quotidian fever, salmon-coloured rash, hepatosplenomegaly).</li>
    <li>Monoarticular arthritis with fever must be assumed to be septic arthritis until proven otherwise - joint aspiration with culture is essential before starting antibiotics.</li>
    <li>DMARD therapy (typically methotrexate) must be initiated by a paediatric rheumatologist as early as possible - delays beyond 2-3 months of symptom onset are associated with worse joint outcomes and permanent damage.<sup>2</sup> Refer urgently; do not wait for complete investigation results.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Inflammatory arthritis in adolescents is often missed or delayed in diagnosis. Juvenile idiopathic arthritis encompasses several subtypes with distinct prognoses and management approaches. The key distinguishing feature from mechanical joint pain is morning stiffness exceeding one hour that improves with activity. Joint swelling - not just pain - is characteristic. Polyarticular involvement is common and typically symmetrical. Systemic features may include low-grade fever, malaise, weight loss, or rash.</p>

  <p>Oligoarticular JIA (4 or fewer joints) is the most common subtype, often ANA-positive with significant risk of uveitis. Polyarticular RF-positive disease has the worst prognosis, more closely resembling adult rheumatoid arthritis with early aggressive disease. Systemic JIA (Still's disease) presents with quotidian fever, a salmon-coloured rash, and may have hepatosplenomegaly and serositis. Distinguish from reactive arthritis (post-infection, typically oligoarticular, self-limited), viral arthritis, and septic arthritis.</p>

  <p>Initial investigations: FBC (anaemia and thrombocytosis suggest inflammation), CRP and ESR, rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP antibodies, ANA, and HLA-B27 if spondyloarthropathy features are present. For monoarticular effusion, joint aspiration with culture is essential to exclude septic arthritis.<sup>1</sup> Continue investigations while arranging urgent rheumatology referral - do not delay referral waiting for complete results.</p>

  <p>NSAIDs provide initial symptom control but are never sufficient as monotherapy for inflammatory arthritis. DMARD therapy, typically methotrexate, must be initiated by a paediatric rheumatologist as early as possible.<sup>2</sup> All JIA patients should be referred for ophthalmology screening for uveitis, particularly those who are ANA-positive with oligoarticular disease. Biologic agents (TNF inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors) are used if conventional DMARDs fail to achieve remission. Physiotherapy is an important adjunct for maintaining joint mobility and function.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Any child or adolescent presenting with suspected new-onset inflammatory arthritis should be referred urgently to a paediatric rheumatology service. Delays in DMARD initiation are associated with worse joint damage and long-term outcomes. Monoarticular arthritis with fever or systemic features should also be referred urgently to exclude septic arthritis.</p>
</div>
