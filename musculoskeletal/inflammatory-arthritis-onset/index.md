<p>Inflammatory arthritis in adolescents is often missed or delayed in diagnosis. Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) affects approximately 1 in 1000 children and encompasses several subtypes with distinct prognoses and management approaches. Early recognition and initiation of disease-modifying antirheumatic therapy (DMARD) within weeks of onset significantly improves outcomes and prevents irreversible joint damage.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>The key clinical feature distinguishing inflammatory arthritis from mechanical joint pain is morning stiffness lasting more than one hour that improves with activity. Joint swelling (not just pain) is characteristic. Polyarticular involvement is common and typically symmetrical. Systemic features may include low-grade fever, malaise, weight loss, or rash.</p>

<p>JIA subtypes include: oligoarticular (most common, 4 or fewer joints, often ANA-positive, significant risk of uveitis), polyarticular RF-negative, polyarticular RF-positive (worst prognosis, more similar to adult RA with early aggressive disease), and systemic JIA (Still's disease - quotidian fever, salmon-coloured rash, hepatosplenomegaly, serositis). Distinguish from reactive arthritis (post-infection, typically oligoarticular, self-limited), viral arthritis, and septic arthritis.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>Initial investigations should include full blood count (anaemia, thrombocytosis suggest inflammation), C-reactive protein and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (elevated in inflammation), rheumatoid factor, anti-CCP antibodies, ANA, and HLA-B27 if spondyloarthropathy features are present. If there is a monoarticular effusion, joint aspiration with culture is essential to exclude septic arthritis.<sup>1</sup> Continue investigations while arranging urgent rheumatology referral - do not delay referral waiting for complete results.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>NSAIDs may provide initial symptom control but are never sufficient as monotherapy for inflammatory arthritis. DMARD therapy (typically methotrexate) must be initiated by a paediatric rheumatologist and started as early as possible - delays beyond 2-3 months of symptom onset are associated with worse outcomes and permanent joint damage.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>Biologic agents (TNF inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors) are increasingly used if conventional DMARDs fail to achieve remission. All JIA patients should be referred for ophthalmology screening for uveitis, particularly those who are ANA-positive and oligoarticular. Physiotherapy is an important adjunct to pharmacotherapy for maintaining joint mobility and function. ACC may fund physiotherapy for acute inflammatory flares but does not fund DMARD drugs or biologics.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Any adolescent presenting with suspected new-onset inflammatory arthritis should be referred urgently to a paediatric rheumatology service. The "2-hour rule" advocated in some international guidelines emphasises the importance of early referral - delays in DMARD initiation are associated with worse joint damage and long-term outcomes. Monoarticular arthritis with fever or systemic features should also be referred urgently to exclude septic arthritis or systemic disease.</p>
