<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Lateral epicondylitis involves the extensor carpi radialis brevis (ECRB) tendon origin, causing lateral elbow pain worse with wrist extension and gripping. Cozen's test (resisted wrist extension with elbow extended) reproduces the pain. Medial epicondylitis affects the flexor pronator mass, causing medial elbow pain worse with wrist flexion and pronation; ulnar nerve irritation must be excluded by assessing for little finger paraesthesia. Both conditions may have a palpable tendon nodule and pain on passive stretching of the affected tendon. Despite their names, the majority of cases occur in non-athletes due to occupational or repetitive activities.</p>

  <p>These are clinical diagnoses. Investigation is usually unnecessary. Plain X-rays are not indicated unless bony pathology is suspected (fracture, osteochondritis, loose body). Ultrasound can confirm tendinopathy and guide injection but does not routinely change management. MRI may identify partial tears but is reserved for refractory cases or surgical planning.</p>

  <p>Activity modification and load management are paramount. Physiotherapy including eccentric strengthening exercises (contracted muscle lengthening against resistance) and soft tissue techniques improves outcomes.<sup>4</sup> A counterforce brace worn just below the epicondyle has modest evidence for pain relief. NSAIDs provide short-term analgesia. Corticosteroid injection provides good short-term relief but is associated with worse outcomes at 12 months compared to wait-and-see or physiotherapy<sup>1,2</sup> - use judiciously and reserve for those unable to engage in physiotherapy. Most cases resolve within 12-18 months regardless of treatment. ACC covers acute workplace or sports-related injury.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Initial referral should be to physiotherapy for assessment and exercise prescription. Orthopaedic referral is appropriate only after symptoms persist beyond 12 months despite optimised conservative management including physiotherapy, activity modification, and consideration of injection therapy. Surgery (epicondyle release) is curative but reserved for the small proportion of cases with genuine functional impairment unresponsive to prolonged conservative management.</p>
</div>
