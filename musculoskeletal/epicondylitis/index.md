<p>Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) and medial epicondylitis (golfer's elbow) are common overuse conditions affecting the common extensor and flexor origins at the elbow. Despite their names, the majority of cases occur in non-athletes due to occupational or repetitive activities. Most cases resolve within 12 - 18 months regardless of treatment, though early conservative management optimises outcomes and hastens return to function.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>
<p>Lateral epicondylitis involves the extensor carpi radialis brevis tendon, causing lateral elbow pain worse with wrist extension and gripping activities. Cozen's test (resistance to wrist extension with elbow extended) reproduces the pain. Medial epicondylitis affects the flexor pronator mass, presenting with medial elbow pain worse on wrist flexion and pronation; ulnar nerve irritation must be excluded by assessing for little finger paraesthesia. Both conditions may be associated with a palpable tendon nodule and pain on passive stretching of the affected tendon. Age, onset (acute versus gradual), and functional demands inform management approach.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>
<p>These are clinical diagnoses and investigation is usually unnecessary. Plain X-rays are not indicated unless bony pathology is suspected (fracture, osteochondritis, loose body). Ultrasound can confirm tendinopathy and guide injection technique but does not routinely change management. MRI may identify partial tears but is reserved for refractory cases or surgical planning.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>
<p>Activity modification and load management are paramount. Physiotherapy including eccentric strengthening exercises (contracted muscle lengthening against resistance) and soft tissue techniques improves outcomes. A counterforce brace worn just below the epicondyle has modest evidence for pain relief. NSAIDs provide short-term analgesia. Corticosteroid injection provides good short-term pain relief (weeks to months) but is associated with worse outcomes at 12 months compared to wait-and-see or physiotherapy; use judiciously and reserve for those unable to engage in physiotherapy. Platelet-rich plasma has emerging evidence but is not routinely funded. Most cases resolve within 12 - 18 months. ACC covers acute workplace or sports-related injury.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>
<p>Initial referral should be to physiotherapy for assessment and exercise prescription. Orthopedic referral is appropriate only after symptoms persist beyond 12 months despite optimised conservative management including physiotherapy, activity modification, and consideration of injection therapy. Surgery (epicondyle release) is curative but reserved for the small proportion of cases with genuine functional impairment unresponsive to prolonged conservative management.</p>
