The Shoulder Problem

Working Diagnosis  ·  GP Perspective

On why shoulder pain is harder than it looks, what happens when we get the diagnosis wrong, and what actually helps at the end of the day.

← Patient handout version

Shoulder pain is the third most common musculoskeletal complaint presenting to general practice, after back and knee pain. Unlike back pain, it carries less of the "just keep moving" reassurance that has become our mantra for most spinal conditions. Shoulders, in my experience, demand a diagnosis. Manage the wrong condition and you spend months getting nowhere. Get it right and the path forward is usually clear.

The problem is that getting it right requires a level of clinical specificity that is easy to underestimate on a busy day. The shoulder is a famously complex joint, and the region between the neck, scapula, and upper arm is a tangle of overlapping structures. Pain referral from the cervical spine mimics shoulder pathology. Subacromial impingement coexists with AC joint OA in many older patients. And the condition with the most striking natural history, frozen shoulder, presents early on looking very similar to rotator cuff tendinopathy before the global restriction of movement declares itself.

I have started thinking about shoulder pain in middle-aged and older patients as a triage problem before it is a treatment problem. The first question is not "what should I do?" but "what is this actually?"

The diagnostic divide

Four conditions account for the great majority of shoulder pain in patients over 50: rotator cuff disease (tendinopathy or tear), frozen shoulder (adhesive capsulitis), glenohumeral osteoarthritis, and acromioclavicular joint disease. These often coexist, but they have fundamentally different natural histories and respond to different interventions. The clinical examination, done properly, usually points to the right diagnosis. Imaging confirms and quantifies.

The key distinguishing feature is the pattern of movement restriction. Rotator cuff disease characteristically produces a painful arc, with pain peaking on elevation between 60 and 120 degrees, and external rotation is typically preserved or only mildly reduced. Frozen shoulder, by contrast, restricts movement globally, with loss of external rotation being the most characteristic and often the earliest sign. Glenohumeral OA restricts movement in multiple planes with crepitus and end-range pain. AC joint pathology is painful at the very top of the shoulder and is reproduced specifically by cross-body adduction.

The clinical examination, done properly, usually points to the right diagnosis. The mistake is treating the symptom without settling on a diagnosis first.

Where I see management go wrong most often is when a patient is labelled with "rotator cuff/impingement" because they have shoulder pain and the supraspinatus is tender, without noticing that external rotation is also globally reduced. That patient has frozen shoulder. They will not benefit from six weeks of rotator cuff strengthening exercises. They will get more frustrated, their physiotherapist will get more frustrated, and by the time the penny drops, they have lost months during which a corticosteroid injection in the freezing phase would have made a real difference to their quality of life.

This is not a criticism of physiotherapists. Good physiotherapists identify frozen shoulder early. The issue arises when patients are referred with a diagnosis that has not been properly examined, or when the GP's role stops at the referral letter.

Frozen shoulder: the long game

Of all the shoulder conditions, frozen shoulder tests a GP's ability to counsel more than to treat. The natural history, untreated, is 1 to 3 years of progressive pain and then stiffness before spontaneous recovery. That is a long time. Most people presenting in the freezing phase cannot sleep on the affected side, struggle to reach behind their back, and have significant pain at rest and at night. Telling them it will "probably get better on its own" without a plan for the next several months is not adequate management.

The freezing phase is when intervention matters most, and specifically when corticosteroid injection has its strongest evidence base. A short course of oral prednisolone can provide faster short-term pain relief than injection alone,1 though the effect is temporary and the risks of systemic steroids in older patients (blood glucose, bone, sleep) are worth weighing. An intra-articular corticosteroid injection is a more targeted intervention with a better risk profile, and it can substantially improve pain and function for 6 to 12 weeks, which is exactly the window needed to get people sleeping again and engaging with physiotherapy.

Hydrodilatation, where the shoulder capsule is distended with a mixture of local anaesthetic, steroid, and saline under fluoroscopic guidance, is an option worth discussing with patients who are struggling in the frozen phase. The evidence is not as robust as one might hope given the procedural effort involved, but clinical experience and the available data suggest it can accelerate the thawing phase. In New Zealand it is available through radiology departments, and for the right patient at the right stage, it is worth the referral.

The question is not whether to intervene, but what you are intervening for and when. Most procedures in frozen shoulder are about quality of life during the wait, not about changing the final outcome.

The group I think about most are patients with diabetes. People with type 2 diabetes are several times more likely to develop frozen shoulder than the general population,2 they tend to have a more protracted and bilateral course, and they respond less reliably to injections, partly because their blood glucose often spikes after corticosteroid injection. Forewarning a diabetic patient that their shoulder will need more careful glucose monitoring after an injection, and that their course may be longer, is part of informed consent that is easy to overlook in a standard consultation.

The rotator cuff: imaging and the incidental finding

Rotator cuff disease is the condition where imaging most consistently misleads. The problem is straightforward: full-thickness rotator cuff tears become increasingly prevalent with age and are often asymptomatic. Roughly one in five people in their 60s and one in three in their 70s have a full-thickness tear visible on ultrasound or MRI, many of whom have never had shoulder pain.3 This means that when a 72-year-old presents with shoulder pain and an ultrasound finds a supraspinatus tear, that tear may or may not be responsible for their symptoms.

It is a version of the same problem we see with lumbar disc findings, but perhaps more consequential in the shoulder because tears suggest surgery more readily than bulging discs do. The question I try to ask myself is whether the clinical picture fits a cuff tear as the pain generator: is there a painful arc, weakness on resisted external rotation or abduction, a history of overhead loading, acute onset that could represent an acute-on-chronic tear? Or does the clinical picture fit something else, with a tear along for the ride?

Clinical note Age-related rotator cuff tears are normal variants in many older adults. The presence of a tear on imaging must be interpreted alongside the clinical picture. A tear that is small, located in a preserved location, and inconsistent with the symptom pattern is likely incidental. Treat the patient, not the scan.

The evidence on rotator cuff surgery has become considerably more nuanced over the past decade. Several randomised controlled trials comparing arthroscopic repair to supervised physiotherapy for full-thickness tears in adults over 50 have found equivalent outcomes at 1 and 2 years, with physiotherapy patients achieving comparable pain relief and function without surgical risk or prolonged rehabilitation.4 This does not mean surgery is never indicated. For a younger patient with an acute traumatic tear, a high-demand shoulder, and a physiologically complete rupture, repair makes sense. But for a 65-year-old with a degenerative tear and a reasonable function at baseline, a serious trial of physiotherapy first is well justified.

The practical implication for general practice is that a referral to orthopaedics for a rotator cuff tear in an older patient should not be reflexive. A referral to physiotherapy with a clear diagnosis and a structured exercise programme is almost always the right first step, and it is also what the orthopaedic surgeon will recommend at the initial outpatient appointment.

The corticosteroid injection: what we know vs what we do

Corticosteroid injections are among the most commonly performed procedures in musculoskeletal general practice. For the shoulder, the evidence is most convincing for frozen shoulder in the freezing phase, reasonably good for subacromial pain (rotator cuff tendinopathy) as a short-term analgesic adjunct, and decent for AC joint arthritis as a diagnostic-therapeutic intervention. What is also clear from the literature is that the benefit is typically short-term, with most studies showing the treatment effect diminishing significantly by 3 months.5

The tension in practice is between the strong evidence for short-term benefit and the natural tendency to repeat injections when someone feels better and then declines again. Repeated corticosteroid injections into the same area raise legitimate concerns about tendon integrity. Animal studies and some clinical evidence suggest that high-dose or repeated injections can weaken tendon tissue, potentially increasing tear risk over time.5 Most guidelines now suggest limiting subacromial injections to two or three courses in any single tendon. The clinical implication is that an injection should function as a pain bridge to enable active rehabilitation, not as an endpoint in itself.

What I try to communicate to patients is that the injection is doing two things: it is reducing inflammation acutely, and it is buying you a window during which exercise and physiotherapy are more comfortable and therefore more effective. If you use that window for the physiotherapy, the injection has earned its place. If you feel better and stop the exercises, you are likely to be back in 3 months in the same position.

An injection is a pain bridge, not a cure. The physiotherapy on the other side of that bridge is what changes the long-term trajectory.

Shoulder arthritis: the joint no one mentions

Glenohumeral OA tends to occupy less of our clinical attention than hip or knee arthritis, perhaps because the shoulder bears no weight and people adapt around it more readily. But it is not uncommon, and in some patients, particularly those with a history of heavy manual work, old shoulder injuries, or large rotator cuff tears leading to cuff arthropathy, it can be severely disabling.

The management pathway mirrors that of large joint OA: optimise analgesia, physiotherapy to maintain strength and function, activity modification, and corticosteroid injection for acute flares. Total shoulder replacement has come a long way and produces reliably good outcomes in the right patient. The reverse total shoulder replacement, developed specifically for rotator cuff arthropathy where the conventional biomechanics no longer apply, has been a genuine advance in shoulder surgery for the elderly.

In New Zealand, access to shoulder arthroplasty through the public system requires a meaningful CPAC score and involves real waiting time. For patients with end-stage disease who are poor private insurance candidates, this means a difficult period of expectant management. Being honest about this, and supporting patients through it with adequate analgesia and a realistic conversation about what surgery can and cannot restore, is part of the job.

AC joint disease often gets lumped into "shoulder pain" without being specifically identified and treated. In my experience, a targeted AC joint injection, particularly as a diagnostic-therapeutic test, is underused. If a patient's superior shoulder pain resolves dramatically after an accurate AC joint injection, that is useful information about what is driving the problem and potentially avoids months of subacromial treatment aimed at the wrong structure.

When to refer: navigating the NZ orthopaedic pathway

Referral to orthopaedics is warranted in a fairly defined set of circumstances: significant rotator cuff tear in a younger or high-demand patient after a genuine trial of physiotherapy, frozen shoulder not improving after 12 to 18 months of conservative management (including hydrodilatation), glenohumeral OA causing severe functional impairment, and any concern about an acute structural injury requiring surgical assessment.

The reality of the New Zealand public orthopaedic system is that wait times for elective shoulder surgery are significant in most DHB regions, and the threshold for acceptance onto a waiting list means that many patients will be declined or deferred at first referral. Knowing this ahead of time allows us to set appropriate expectations and to have the documentation in order: a clear diagnosis, the modality and duration of conservative management tried, imaging with a formal report, and a functional impact summary. Vague referrals to "shoulder pain" that have not gone through a physiotherapy trial are routinely declined and help no one.

Private orthopaedic care is faster but financially significant. For patients with Southern Cross or other health insurance, early specialist review is often accessible. It is worth asking about insurance at the time of referral, as some patients are unaware of what their policy covers.

The older patient: adjusting the frame

One adjustment I find myself making more often as patients get older is shifting the conversation from "fixing the shoulder" to "what does this person actually need the shoulder to do?" A 78-year-old with glenohumeral OA and a sedentary lifestyle has different goals than a 55-year-old who wants to keep playing tennis. For the former, comfort, independence in dressing, and the ability to sleep are the metrics. For the latter, functional restoration matters more.

This sounds obvious but it changes the treatment plan meaningfully. An aggressive surgical pathway that requires 6 months of rehabilitation is not appropriate for someone who is otherwise frail and whose primary concern is pain at rest. Optimising analgesia, providing an injection, and giving a home exercise programme with realistic expectations may be the more humane and effective approach. Conversely, declining to refer a 62-year-old for surgical consideration because they are "elderly" is a different kind of error.

I also think about medication more carefully in older patients. NSAIDs, while useful for acute inflammatory shoulder pain, carry real risks in the over-70s: renal impairment, peptic ulcer disease, cardiovascular events, and fluid retention. Topical NSAIDs are a reasonable compromise, offering local anti-inflammatory effect with much lower systemic absorption. Paracetamol at regular doses is safe and underestimated in its effectiveness for background pain control. Opioids are rarely appropriate for chronic shoulder pain and should almost never be the primary strategy.

Getting the treatment right means knowing who you are treating. A 78-year-old and a 55-year-old with the same shoulder finding are different clinical problems.

A few things I have learned

Ask about sleep early. Shoulder pain that disturbs sleep is a different clinical severity than shoulder pain that doesn't, and people often do not volunteer it unless asked. Night pain is one of the strongest predictors of frozen shoulder and also the symptom most responsive to early corticosteroid injection. It is also the symptom most likely to drive someone to request something stronger, and being ahead of it with a sensible management plan avoids the "something must be done" consultation six weeks later.

Check cervical rotation. A meaningful proportion of what presents as shoulder pain originates from the cervical spine, particularly C5 radiculopathy, which refers pain to the outer shoulder in a pattern almost identical to rotator cuff tendinopathy. If cervical rotation reproduces or changes the shoulder pain, the neck needs to be part of the workup. Shoulder treatment will not help a cervicogenic presentation.

Be specific in physiotherapy referrals. A referral that says "physiotherapy for shoulder pain" is less useful than one that says "rotator cuff tendinopathy, right shoulder, moderate severity, not yet had formal physiotherapy, nil surgical intent at this stage." The latter lets the physiotherapist start in the right place and aligns their expectations with yours and the patient's.

Warn patients about the injection glucose spike. For diabetic patients receiving a corticosteroid injection, blood glucose may rise significantly for 2 to 5 days. Insulin-dependent diabetics in particular need a plan for this. A quick conversation at the time of injection, and a note to check glucose more frequently over the following week, is the difference between a predictable expected response and a frightening hyperosmolar presentation.

The shoulder that is not getting better deserves a second look. If someone has been through a reasonable course of treatment without improvement, the question is whether the diagnosis is correct, not just whether the treatment has been adequate. A frozen shoulder misdiagnosed as rotator cuff tendinopathy will not respond to rotator cuff exercises. A Pancoast tumour at the lung apex presents as shoulder and arm pain before it declares itself on CXR. The non-improving shoulder warrants a fresh diagnostic look, not just more of the same.
