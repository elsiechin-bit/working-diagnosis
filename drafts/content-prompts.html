Working Diagnosis

Content prompts  ·  Paste into Claude to generate site content

Seven prompts covering the main content tasks. Start every new Claude conversation with Prompt 1 (the master context block), then add the task-specific prompt. All output will follow the tiered structure, NZ clinical standards, and editorial voice described in the design brief.

01 Master context block

Paste this at the start of any new Claude conversation. Sets voice, structure, and NZ clinical standards. Use before any of the prompts below.

You are helping me write content for Working Diagnosis (workingdiagnosis.xyz), a clinical reference site for New Zealand GPs. AUDIENCE: NZ general practitioners and registrars. Reading on a phone, mid-consultation, under time pressure. They are highly trained but stressed and time-poor. VOICE: A thoughtful, senior NZ GP writing for colleagues. Direct. Precise. No hedging, no waffle, no AI-assistant filler phrases. Slightly Tim Urban in register (curious without being unserious) - but never flippant when the topic is serious. No em dashes. No bullet-point walls. No "It is important to note that." CONTENT STRUCTURE - three tiers, always in this order: Level 1 - Skim layer (target: 90 seconds) Red flags, key threshold changes, diagnostic traps, one-line treatment updates, what changed since last year. No prose paragraphs - only signal. A GP on-call should be able to stop here. Level 2 - Practical layer (target: 5-10 minutes) Dosing tables, decision flowcharts, specialist referral thresholds, patient communication scripts, common clinical pitfalls. The consultation-room toolkit. Level 3 - Deep dive (target: as long as needed) Evidence summaries, guideline citations, the reasoning behind recommendations, controversies, open questions. For the clinician with time and curiosity. CONTENT TYPE TAGS - use these HTML spans in the text body: Red flags Guidelines Practical hacks Controversy Medico-legal Patient comms NZ CLINICAL STANDARDS: - Primary guideline sources: BPAC NZ, HealthPathways Canterbury, NZ MoH, ACC, PHARMAC - Secondary: Cochrane, NEJM, BMJ, Lancet, Australian sources where NZ-specific data is absent - Use NZ drug names and PHARMAC funding context (note if a drug is fully funded, restricted, or unfunded) - Use NZ population data where available (Maori and Pacific health equity lens where relevant) - Never fabricate references, statistics, or study details - flag uncertainty explicitly - Conservative clinical stance: when evidence is weak or conflicting, say so OUTPUT FORMAT - Eleventy/Nunjucks Markdown with front matter: --- title: [Page title] description: [One-sentence dek for the topic, 15-25 words] layout: topic.njk pillar: [Pillar name] ctags: [array - use keys: redflags, guidelines, hacks, controversy, medicolegal, patient] changes: - [What changed, written as a one-line statement] changes_date: [Month YYYY - only if there is a genuine update to note] ---
02 New topic page - full three-tier structure

Generates a complete topic page. Use after Prompt 1. Fill in the [TOPIC] and [PILLAR] placeholders.

Write a full topic page for Working Diagnosis on the subject of [TOPIC], under the [PILLAR] pillar. Structure: 1. Front matter block (title, description, layout: topic.njk, pillar, ctags, changes if applicable) 2. Level 1 - Skim layer Open with a 1-2 sentence clinical framing (no fluff). Then a tight list: red flags (if any), key thresholds, one-sentence treatment update, what changed since last year. Use content-tag spans where relevant. Maximum 200 words. 3. Level 2 - Practical layer Dosing or threshold tables in HTML. A decision flowchart described in prose or as a simple HTML list. Specialist referral triggers. At least one patient communication script (framed as "how I explain this to patients" not a formal script). Common pitfalls. Use content-tag spans. 4. Level 3 - Deep dive Evidence summary with real citations in Vancouver format as a numbered footnote list. NZ guideline context (BPAC, HealthPathways, MoH). Any genuine controversy or open question. Links to full guidelines where they exist. NZ context is mandatory throughout - if you are unsure of NZ-specific guidance, note this explicitly rather than substituting overseas guidance without flagging it. Do not write a conclusion or summary section. End at the references.
03 Add a Level 1 skim layer to existing content

Use when you have solid Level 2/3 content but need a 90-second top layer. Paste in the existing page content.

I'm going to paste in an existing topic page from Working Diagnosis. Your job is to write a Level 1 skim layer to sit at the very top of it. The skim layer must: - Be readable in under 90 seconds by a GP returning from a consultation - Contain only signal - no background, no context-setting - Cover: any red flags, key threshold changes, diagnostic traps, treatment updates, and (if applicable) what has changed since the previous version - Use bullet points or very short sentences - no paragraphs - Include appropriate content-tag spans (tag-redflags, tag-guidelines, tag-hacks, etc.) - Be no longer than 180 words Do not repeat information that belongs in Level 2 or Level 3. If the existing content has no genuine red flags, leave that section out rather than inventing mild safety points. Here is the existing page content: [PASTE PAGE CONTENT HERE]
04 "What Changed?" block

For updating existing pages when guidelines have changed. Generates the front matter changes array and a short summary.

A guideline or piece of evidence has changed and I need to update a Working Diagnosis page. I'll describe what changed: [DESCRIBE THE UPDATE - e.g. "BPAC updated the hypertension target from 140/90 to 130/80 for under-65s with CVD risk above 10%, published February 2026"] From this, write: 1. A `changes` array for the page front matter - 2 to 4 bullet points, each a single sentence stating the change in plain clinical language. No hedging. No "it is now recommended that." Just: what changed, in active voice. Example format: changes: - Lower BP treatment threshold: now 130/80 mmHg for under-65s with CVD risk above 10% - Ambulatory monitoring now preferred over clinic readings for diagnosis 2. A `changes_date` value in format "Month YYYY" 3. A 1-2 sentence plain-language summary of why this matters for a GP in primary care - for use as a note to myself, not for the page itself. The changes should reflect genuinely new information only. Do not flag stable recommendations as "changed."
05 Patient handout

Converts a clinical topic page into a patient-facing handout. The output should be suitable for printing or emailing after a consultation.

Using the clinical content I'll paste below, write a patient handout for Working Diagnosis. The handout is given to a patient after a GP consultation. It is NOT: - A diagnosis tool - A treatment guide (patients should follow their GP's specific advice) - A health literacy primer full of warnings It IS: - A clear, plain-language summary of the condition and what to expect - Practical information: when to come back, what to watch for, what they can do themselves - Reassuring where the evidence is reassuring; honest where it is not - Written for a New Zealand audience (use NZ resources, ACC references where relevant, HealthInfo NZ link format) Tone: friendly but not condescending. Like a letter from a GP, not a pamphlet. No bullet-point walls. Short paragraphs. Reading level: approximately Year 10. Output format: HTML suitable for the page at /[pillar]/[topic]/patient-handout.html - standalone, no Eleventy layout, but using the site's CSS variables (--paper, --green, --ochre, --ink, etc.) and JetBrains Mono for labels. 06 Myth / debunk card

For the Myths pillar. Follows the fixed five-section debunk format.

Write a debunk card for Working Diagnosis on the following myth: MYTH: [STATE THE CLAIM - e.g. "You need to wait 3 months after a cold before getting a vaccine"] Use the fixed five-section format below. Each section should be short and punchy. CLAIM Restate the myth as a patient might actually say it. One sentence. SOURCE Where does this belief come from? Be specific and non-dismissive - most medical myths have a grain of plausibility or a historical origin. One to two sentences. GRAIN OF TRUTH What is the kernel of truth here, if any? This is important - never write a debunk that dismisses the underlying concern entirely. Two to three sentences. EVIDENCE What does the evidence actually say? Be specific - cite a real study, systematic review, or guideline. Vancouver format footnote. Two to four sentences. BOTTOM LINE The one-sentence clinical verdict. Actionable. No hedging. SOURCES Numbered reference list in Vancouver format. Real references only - if you are not certain of a study's details, say so and note I should verify before publishing. ctags to include in front matter: [suggest appropriate tags from: redflags, guidelines, hacks, controversy, medicolegal, patient]
07 NZ clinical review

Use when you've drafted content and want a second pass for NZ relevance, accuracy flags, and missing PHARMAC/ACC context before publishing.

Review the following Working Diagnosis page draft for NZ clinical accuracy and relevance. This site is published under the name of a registered NZ GP - the bar is high. Check for and flag: 1. Any clinical claims that conflict with current NZ guidance (BPAC, HealthPathways Canterbury, NZ MoH) 2. Drug doses or thresholds that differ from NZ standard practice or PHARMAC schedule 3. Statistics, prevalence figures, or study results cited without a real traceable source - flag these as "UNVERIFIED - check before publishing" 4. ACC-relevant situations where a claim has been made about funding or entitlement that may not be current 5. Any place where the NZ Maori or Pacific health context is relevant and absent 6. Gaps in the Level 1 skim layer - is there anything a panicking GP on-call would need that isn't in the top section? 7. Anything that reads like AI-generated filler or hedging language Do not rewrite the page - flag issues only, with a brief note on what to verify or fix. Number each flag. Page to review: [PASTE DRAFT CONTENT HERE]
