<p>The returned traveller with fever is not a rare presentation in New Zealand general practice &#8212; it is a regular one, particularly after summer holidays and around periods of high outbound travel to Southeast Asia, India, and the Pacific. The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> covers incubation periods and the major diagnoses to consider. This page provides the operational detail: what to order, how to interpret it, what cannot wait, and when to admit.</p>

<h2>The core principle: malaria first</h2>

<p>Any febrile returned traveller from a malaria-endemic area has malaria until proven otherwise. This applies regardless of whether the patient took prophylaxis (prophylaxis is not 100% protective), regardless of the fever pattern (classical periodicity is often absent in early or partially treated disease), and regardless of what else seems to explain the fever. Ordering a blood film is a simple, inexpensive test; missing falciparum malaria is a life-threatening error.</p>

<p>The malaria-endemic areas relevant to NZ travellers include sub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea and the Pacific Islands (Vanuatu, Solomon Islands, some of Melanesia), South and Southeast Asia, and parts of Central and South America. New Zealand and Australia are not endemic. Patients returning from Bali&#8217;s main tourist areas, Thailand&#8217;s cities, or the Pacific&#8217;s non-endemic island groups (Fiji, Samoa, Tonga, Cook Islands) are not at risk of malaria &#8212; but Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, and Vanuatu are.</p>

<h2>Malaria workup protocol</h2>

<p>Order simultaneously:</p>
<ol style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li><strong>Thick and thin blood film</strong> &#8212; thick film has higher sensitivity for parasite detection; thin film allows species identification. The sensitivity of a single film for <em>P. falciparum</em> is approximately 75&#8211;90%; for <em>P. vivax</em> it may be lower.<sup>1</sup> If clinical suspicion is high and the first film is negative, repeat at 12&#8211;24 hour intervals for up to three films before excluding malaria.</li>
  <li><strong>Malaria rapid diagnostic test (RDT)</strong> &#8212; detects malaria antigens (HRP2 for <em>P. falciparum</em>; pLDH or aldolase for non-falciparum species). Use in parallel with blood film, not as a replacement. False negatives occur with low parasitaemia; false positives can persist for weeks after treatment due to antigen persistence.</li>
</ol>

<p>Do not delay antimalarial treatment while waiting for blood film results if the patient is critically unwell with suspected falciparum malaria. Call the on-call ID registrar and initiate empirical treatment (IV artesunate is the treatment of choice for severe falciparum malaria in NZ) while awaiting confirmatory results. Severe falciparum malaria is defined by the WHO criteria and includes: cerebral malaria (altered consciousness), hyperparasitaemia (above 5% parasitaemia), severe anaemia (haemoglobin below 70 g/L), acute kidney injury, respiratory failure, shock, or spontaneous bleeding.</p>

<h2>Notifiable diseases in New Zealand</h2>

<p>Several travel-acquired infections are notifiable to the Medical Officer of Health. The notification obligations fall on the attending clinician (GP or hospital doctor), not the laboratory. Key notifiable travel-related diseases include malaria, typhoid and paratyphoid fever, hepatitis A, leptospirosis, dengue fever, chikungunya, Zika virus infection, and any suspected viral haemorrhagic fever. For the complete current list, see the Ministry of Health notifiable disease page. Notification is via the online EpiSurv system or by phone to the local Public Health Unit &#8212; do not wait for laboratory confirmation for diseases where clinical suspicion alone triggers notification (e.g. suspected VHF).</p>

<h2>Criteria for urgent hospital admission</h2>

<p>The following features in a returned traveller with fever warrant urgent hospital assessment (call ID on-call for advice before referring if uncertain):</p>

<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Confirmed or strongly suspected falciparum malaria (all cases require admission for IV treatment in NZ)</li>
  <li>Any altered consciousness or neurological signs</li>
  <li>Haemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia out of proportion to fever)</li>
  <li>Severe thrombocytopenia (platelet count below 50 &#215; 10&#8309;/L) or active bleeding</li>
  <li>Significant renal impairment (creatinine acutely elevated)</li>
  <li>Jaundice with liver failure features</li>
  <li>Respiratory compromise</li>
  <li>Inability to tolerate oral fluids or medications</li>
  <li>Suspicion of typhoid with systemic toxicity</li>
  <li>Any suspicion of viral haemorrhagic fever (contact ID immediately, implement infection control before further assessment)</li>
</ul>

<h2>The asymptomatic returned traveller</h2>

<p>Post-travel screening for asymptomatic infection is not routinely indicated for all travellers. It is warranted for those with specific risk exposures:</p>

<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li><strong>Freshwater exposure in sub-Saharan Africa:</strong> schistosomiasis serology at 6&#8211;8 weeks after last exposure (serology is negative in the first 6 weeks while the worm load is developing).</li>
  <li><strong>Extended travel in Southeast Asia or tropical regions:</strong> Strongyloides serology. Stool microscopy misses Strongyloides in the majority of cases; serology is required. Strongyloides can persist for decades without symptoms but causes life-threatening hyperinfection syndrome in immunocompromised patients &#8212; this matters if the patient is ever given corticosteroids or immunosuppression.</li>
  <li><strong>HIV and STI screening</strong> after potential exposure during travel; standard NZ testing pathways apply.</li>
  <li><strong>Hepatitis B and C serology</strong> if there was a significant exposure risk (medical procedures, tattoos, sexual contact in high-prevalence areas).</li>
</ul>
