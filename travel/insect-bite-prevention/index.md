<p>Chemoprophylaxis and vaccines address specific pathogens, but insect bite prevention is the upstream intervention that reduces exposure to multiple vector-borne diseases simultaneously. It is often covered briefly at the end of the pre-travel consultation and then forgotten. This page provides the detail needed to give specific, actionable advice: which repellents work, at what concentrations, for how long, and how to layer them with other physical measures.</p>

<h2>Why vector timing matters</h2>

<p>Different mosquito species bite at different times of day, and knowing this shapes the advice you give. The two clinically most important genera for NZ travellers are:</p>

<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li><strong><em>Anopheles</em> mosquitoes</strong> (malaria vectors): primarily bite from dusk to dawn, with peak activity in the two hours after sunset and before sunrise. Outdoor exposure in the evening and sleeping without protection are the highest-risk periods. DEET applied in the evening, bed nets, and indoor sleeping with windows closed or screened are the key measures.</li>
  <li><strong><em>Aedes</em> mosquitoes</strong> (dengue, Zika, chikungunya, yellow fever vectors): primarily bite during the day, particularly in the hours around dawn and dusk. <em>Aedes aegypti</em>, the principal dengue vector, is a peri-urban species that breeds in standing water around human habitation &#8212; flower vases, tyres, containers. Air-conditioned urban hotel rooms provide little protection against it because the mosquito enters during daylight. DEET must be applied during daytime hours, not only in the evening.</li>
</ul>

<p>A traveller using only a bed net and evening DEET for an itinerary including Bangkok or Bali has good protection against malaria (if there is any risk at all) but minimal protection against dengue. This is a common gap worth addressing explicitly.</p>

<h2>DEET (N,N-diethyl-meta-toluamide)</h2>

<p>DEET is the most extensively studied and widely available insect repellent. It works by interfering with the olfactory receptors mosquitoes use to detect human scent. It is effective against mosquitoes, ticks, sand flies, and other biting arthropods.</p>

<p><strong>Concentration and duration of protection:</strong> Higher concentrations provide longer duration of protection, not greater intensity. A 10% DEET formulation protects for approximately 2 hours; 30% for approximately 6 hours; 50% for up to 12 hours. For most destinations in SE Asia or the Pacific, 30&#8211;40% provides practical all-day coverage with a single application. 50% DEET is appropriate for very high-risk areas (rural sub-Saharan Africa, rainforest travel in endemic regions).</p>

<p><strong>Safety in children:</strong> DEET is safe for use in children aged 2 months and older. In children under 12, concentrations of 10&#8211;30% are recommended; apply to clothing and exposed skin, avoiding the hands (which go into the mouth), eyes, and irritated skin. Wash off with soap and water when indoors or after risk period ends. DEET is not recommended under 2 months of age.<sup>1</sup></p>

<p><strong>Safety in pregnancy:</strong> DEET is safe for use during pregnancy at standard concentrations. The risk of the vector-borne diseases DEET prevents (malaria, dengue, Zika) outweighs any theoretical risk from the repellent itself. WHO and CDC both recommend DEET use in pregnancy for travellers to endemic areas.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p><strong>Application technique:</strong> Apply to exposed skin and clothing. Do not apply under clothing or to covered skin (absorbed without benefit). Apply sunscreen first if both are being used, then DEET on top &#8212; the order matters because DEET reduces sunscreen SPF by approximately one-third, so a higher SPF sunscreen should be used. Wash hands after application. Do not spray directly onto the face; apply to hands and then wipe onto face, avoiding the eyes and mouth.</p>

<h2>Picaridin (icaridin)</h2>

<p>Picaridin (known as icaridin in some markets) is a synthetic compound with comparable efficacy to DEET at equivalent concentrations, a better cosmetic profile (no plastic damage, no greasy texture), and an equivalent safety record. It is registered in New Zealand and available in some pharmacies and outdoor gear shops.</p>

<p>Standard picaridin formulations available in NZ and Australia are 9.5&#8211;20%. A 20% picaridin formulation provides approximately 8&#8211;12 hours of mosquito protection. It is safe from 2 months of age. For patients who find DEET&#8217;s texture objectionable, or who need to apply it to synthetic fabrics (DEET dissolves some plastics and synthetic textiles), picaridin is a practical alternative.</p>

<h2>Permethrin for clothing and nets</h2>

<p>Permethrin is a synthetic pyrethroid insecticide used to treat clothing, bed nets, and gear. Unlike DEET and picaridin, it is not applied to skin &#8212; it is a contact insecticide that kills or repels insects that land on treated fabric.</p>

<p>Permethrin-treated clothing provides a significant additional layer of protection beyond repellents on skin alone. Studies in military and field populations show approximately 30&#8211;70% additional reduction in biting compared with repellent alone.<sup>3</sup> In high-risk settings (jungle, rural Africa, high malaria transmission areas), the combination of permethrin-treated clothing and DEET on exposed skin is substantially more protective than either alone.</p>

<p>Permethrin spray can be purchased and applied to clothing at home before departure. Treatment lasts approximately 6 washes or 6 weeks of field use. Alternatively, pre-treated clothing (ExOfficio Bugs Away, Craghoppers NosiLife, and similar brands) is available from outdoor retailers. Permethrin is safe when used as directed on clothing; it degrades on skin and should not be applied directly.</p>

<p>Insecticidal bed nets (long-lasting insecticidal nets, LLIN) use permethrin or other pyrethroids embedded in the net fibres. They remain effective for 3&#8211;5 years of use without re-treatment. For travel to rural sub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea, or other high malaria transmission areas where indoor residual spraying is not in place, LLINs are an important preventive measure. Advise patients to:</p>
<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Use a net over any bed or sleeping mat, not just in accommodation that lacks screens</li>
  <li>Tuck the net under the mattress all around (Anopheles can find a 1 cm gap)</li>
  <li>Apply DEET before getting under the net (skin contact with the net is a route for mosquitoes to bite through if net is too small)</li>
  <li>Check for holes before use</li>
</ul>

<h2>Other physical measures</h2>

<p>Clothing choice reduces exposure substantially and is often overlooked in the advice given. Specific recommendations:</p>
<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Long-sleeved shirts and long trousers in the evening for malaria risk; during the day for dengue risk</li>
  <li>Light-coloured clothing (some evidence that dark colours attract mosquitoes)</li>
  <li>Socks and closed shoes when walking on grass or soil in tick-endemic areas (Europe, North America, Central Asia)</li>
  <li>Air-conditioned accommodation with closed windows where available, for high-transmission zones</li>
  <li>Electric mosquito-repellent vaporisers (pyrethroid-based plug-ins) for indoor use at night &#8212; useful for rooms without screens</li>
</ul>

<p>For tick-borne diseases, advise travellers to check for ticks after any outdoor activity in endemic areas &#8212; particularly checking the scalp, behind the ears, groin, and axillae. Remove attached ticks by grasping as close to the skin surface as possible with fine-pointed tweezers and pulling steadily outward; do not twist. Crushing, burning, or applying petroleum jelly increases the risk of disease transmission by inducing regurgitation.</p>
