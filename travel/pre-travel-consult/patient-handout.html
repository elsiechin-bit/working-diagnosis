Preparing for safe travel abroad

International travel poses specific health risks that are often preventable. The key is planning early, understanding what vaccines and precautions you need, and recognising warning signs after you return home.

Before you go ›

Ideally, see your GP or a travel medicine clinic 6-8 weeks before departure. This allows time for vaccine courses (some require multiple doses spaced weeks apart). Bring a list of your current medications, any medical conditions, and your vaccination history.

Key questions your GP will ask: destination(s), duration, type of travel (backpacker versus resort), rural versus urban, activities planned (swimming, animal contact, high altitude), pregnancy status, immune status.

Travel insurance with medical evacuation cover is strongly recommended for any international travel. Medical evacuation from Asia or the Pacific can cost well over $100,000.

Vaccines for travel ›

Some vaccines are required for entry to certain countries (yellow fever certificate). Most travel vaccines are recommended based on your destination and activities.

Common travel vaccines:

  • Hepatitis A — food and water-borne, highly effective vaccine
  • Typhoid — food and water-borne, especially South Asia
  • Hepatitis B — blood-borne, sexual contact; recommended if unvaccinated
  • Rabies — pre-exposure for animal contact in endemic countries
  • Japanese encephalitis — rural Asia
  • Meningococcal — sub-Saharan Africa, Hajj pilgrims

Malaria is not vaccine-preventable but antimalarial medications are highly effective when taken correctly. Some vaccines (hepatitis A, MMR) may be partially subsidised through NZ's schedule.

Food, water and hygiene ›

Traveller's diarrhea affects up to 60% of travellers to high-risk regions (South and Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America).1 The mantra: boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it.

Safe options: Bottled water from sealed bottles, hot drinks, cooked food served hot, fruits you peel yourself.

Risky options: Tap water (including ice), salads and raw vegetables washed in tap water, shellfish, buffet food that has been sitting out.

Hand hygiene is critical. Bring oral rehydration sachets. Antibiotics (usually azithromycin or ciprofloxacin) can shorten severe diarrhea but should be reserved for severe cases. Bismuth subsalicylate has some preventive evidence but is not standard in NZ practice.

During travel: sun, insects, altitude ›

Sun: UV is higher at altitude and near the equator. Use SPF 30+ and cover up. Heat exhaustion is a real risk in tropical climates.

Insects: Dengue has no prophylaxis available; avoid mosquito bites especially at dawn and dusk. Malaria prophylaxis is essential in endemic areas; take as prescribed and continue after return. Zika poses risk in pregnancy.

Altitude: Ascend gradually above 3000 metres. Recognise acute mountain sickness (headache, nausea, fatigue); if severe (ataxia, confusion, chest symptoms), descend immediately. Acetazolamide can be prescribed for prophylaxis.

After you return ›

Many travel-acquired infections have incubation periods that mean symptoms appear after return home. Fever after travel from a malaria-endemic region is a medical emergency until proven otherwise.

See your GP promptly for: fever, persistent diarrhea (more than 5-7 days), jaundice, unusual skin lesions, respiratory symptoms. Always mention your travel history to any health professional you see.

Travel health by region

Regional variation in risk guides your vaccination and prophylaxis strategy.

Region Key risks Key precautions
Southeast Asia Dengue, rabies, hepatitis A/B, typhoid, malaria (some areas) Insect repellent, hepatitis A/typhoid vaccines, malaria prophylaxis if rural
South Asia Typhoid, hepatitis A/B, malaria, air quality Vaccines, bottled water, malaria prophylaxis
Sub-Saharan Africa Malaria, yellow fever, typhoid, hepatitis A Yellow fever certificate, malaria prophylaxis, vaccines
Pacific Islands Dengue, leptospirosis, hepatitis A Insect repellent, avoid freshwater exposure
Central and South America Yellow fever (Amazon), typhoid, hepatitis A, altitude Vaccines, altitude awareness, water safety
Eastern Europe, Middle East Hepatitis A, rabies Hepatitis A vaccine, avoid animal contact
See a doctor urgently if you have
  • Fever (especially within 3 months of returning from a malaria-endemic area)
  • Persistent diarrhea or blood in stools
  • Jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes)
  • Unexplained rash or skin lesion
  • Shortness of breath
