<p>The pre-travel consultation is one of the few occasions in general practice where a single appointment can meaningfully prevent serious illness. Done well, it covers risk assessment, vaccination, chemoprophylaxis, and safety counselling in a structured way. Done poorly, it misses the patient who returns with typhoid because nobody asked whether they were visiting family in a village rather than staying in a hotel.</p>

<h2>Timing</h2>

<p>The ideal lead time is 6&#8211;8 weeks before departure. This allows time to complete multi-dose vaccine courses (hepatitis B requires three doses over two months; rabies pre-exposure requires three doses over 28 days), to begin chemoprophylaxis where a trial period is indicated (mefloquine should be started three weeks before travel to detect neuropsychiatric intolerance before departure), and to allow seroconversion after live vaccines.</p>

<p>Many patients present far later. A patient walking in the week before departure is not an opportunity to refuse the consultation &#8212; it is an opportunity to maximise what can still be done. Single-dose vaccines (hepatitis A, typhoid Vi, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis dose one) can all be given at this stage. Atovaquone-proguanil chemoprophylaxis starts 1&#8211;2 days before entry into an endemic area and requires no trial period. Document the timing constraint clearly in the notes.</p>

<h2>Complexity triage: who needs specialist input</h2>

<p>Not every pre-travel consultation belongs in general practice. Consider referring to a travel medicine clinic for:</p>

<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Immunocompromised travellers (solid organ transplant, haematopoietic stem cell transplant, active malignancy, biologic immunosuppression, HIV with CD4 below 200)</li>
  <li>Pregnant travellers considering malaria-endemic destinations, especially with chloroquine-resistant areas</li>
  <li>Travellers with complex multi-country itineraries including both yellow fever-endemic and non-endemic countries (certificate timing and validity requirements are intricate)</li>
  <li>Travellers to areas with active outbreaks of VHF or unusual endemic disease</li>
  <li>Children under 2 years travelling to high-risk regions</li>
  <li>Travellers with a prior severe reaction to a travel vaccine or antimalarial</li>
</ul>

<p>For uncomplicated itineraries, general practice is the right setting. The travel medicine clinics in New Zealand&#8217;s main centres (often attached to ID departments or specialist travel health services) see complex cases and issue Yellow Fever certificates; they welcome telephone advice for GPs with borderline cases.</p>

<h2>Yellow Fever certificates</h2>

<p>Yellow fever vaccination must be administered by a registered Yellow Fever Vaccination Centre, and the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis (ICVP &#8212; the &#8220;yellow card&#8221;) must be issued by an authorised centre. Not all general practices are registered. Check the IMAC website for the current NZ list of authorised centres.</p>

<p>A single dose of yellow fever vaccine confers lifelong immunity in immunocompetent adults, and the certificate is now valid for life (updated by WHO in 2016).<sup>1</sup> For immunocompromised travellers, a booster may be recommended &#8212; specialist input required. The vaccine is contraindicated under 9 months of age and in immunocompromised patients; a medical exemption letter may be issued by an authorised centre where the vaccine is genuinely contraindicated, but this does not protect the traveller biologically in endemic areas.</p>

<p>Travel-specific vaccines (hepatitis A, hepatitis B, typhoid, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, rabies pre-exposure, meningococcal) are all unfunded in New Zealand. Patients pay privately for both the vaccine and administration. Frame cost expectations before the consult; combined hepatitis A and B courses and multi-dose rabies pre-exposure series are expensive. For some itineraries, it is worth prioritising which vaccines have the highest risk-benefit ratio if budget is a genuine constraint.</p>

<h2>The travel health kit</h2>

<p>Counsel all travellers on what to carry. The contents depend on the destination and itinerary, but a sensible baseline for travel to high-risk regions:</p>

<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Oral rehydration sachets (Gastrolyte or equivalent) &#8212; minimum 6</li>
  <li>Standby antibiotic (azithromycin or ciprofloxacin as appropriate to region &#8212; see <a href="/travel/travellers-diarrhoea/">Traveller&#8217;s diarrhoea</a>)</li>
  <li>Loperamide for adults (for symptomatic control of non-bloody, non-febrile diarrhoea)</li>
  <li>Insect repellent with DEET (30&#8211;50% for high-risk areas) or picaridin (see <a href="/travel/insect-bite-prevention/">Insect bite prevention</a>)</li>
  <li>Permethrin spray for treating clothing and bed nets</li>
  <li>Broad-spectrum sunscreen</li>
  <li>Wound care basics (antiseptic, adhesive dressings, blister treatment)</li>
  <li>Thermometer</li>
  <li>Sufficient supply of all regular prescription medications, in original labelled containers with a letter from the prescribing GP</li>
  <li>Copy of vaccination certificate and key medical documents</li>
</ul>

<p>For trekkers to altitude: acetazolamide (if prescribed) and instructions for its use; a written altitude illness action plan is useful for multi-week treks. See <a href="/travel/altitude-illness/">Altitude illness</a> for detail.</p>

<h2>Documentation</h2>

<p>Record the consultation clearly. Note the destination, dates of travel, vaccines given and lot numbers, chemoprophylaxis prescribed, and any specific counselling provided (safe food and water, insect precautions, sun protection, sexual health, road safety). If a patient declines a recommended vaccine or prophylaxis, document this with the reason. For medically complex patients, a travel-specific clinical summary is useful if the patient needs medical attention abroad.</p>

<p>Travel insurance is not medical advice, but it is worth asking whether the patient has coverage that includes medical evacuation. Patients underestimate evacuation costs from remote destinations: medical evacuation from rural SE Asia or the Pacific can cost NZ$50,000&#8211;100,000 or more. Existing credit card travel insurance frequently excludes pre-existing conditions, has low emergency medical limits, or has been cancelled in recent product changes &#8212; encourage patients to read the policy.</p>
