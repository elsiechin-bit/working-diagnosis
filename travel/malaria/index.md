<p>The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> covers the four main prophylactic agents and their key contraindications. This page goes deeper on regional risk stratification, paediatric dosing, clinically significant drug interactions, and the two scenarios that require extra thought: the remote traveller who needs standby emergency treatment, and the returned traveller with <em>Plasmodium vivax</em> or <em>P. ovale</em> who needs radical cure.</p>

<h2>Regional risk stratification</h2>

<p>Not all malaria risk is equal. The species present, the intensity of transmission, and local drug resistance patterns vary considerably by region, and the choice of prophylaxis flows directly from that assessment.</p>

<h2>Paediatric dosing</h2>

<p>Children develop more severe malaria than adults, particularly under 5 years of age. Prophylaxis is appropriate for children travelling to endemic regions.</p>

<h2>Radical cure for <em>P. vivax</em> and <em>P. ovale</em></h2>

<p><em>P. vivax</em> and <em>P. ovale</em> form dormant liver stages (hypnozoites) that are not eliminated by the standard prophylactic agents or by the artemisinin-based combination therapies used to treat falciparum malaria. These hypnozoites can reactivate months to years after travel, causing relapsing malaria long after the patient has returned to New Zealand.</p>

<p>Radical cure with primaquine eliminates the hypnozoite reservoir. It is considered for travellers who have had significant exposure to vivax or ovale endemic areas (particularly Southeast Asia, South Asia, Papua New Guinea, and the Pacific) and who want to reduce the risk of future relapse.</p>

<p><strong>G6PD screening is mandatory before prescribing primaquine.</strong> Primaquine causes acute haemolysis in patients with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency, a common enzyme deficiency in populations from malaria-endemic regions. Quantitative G6PD testing (enzyme activity level) is preferred over qualitative testing as it detects the heterozygous state in females, who may have intermediate activity and still be at risk. Order G6PD quantitative level before prescribing.</p>

<p>Standard dosing for radical cure: primaquine 30 mg base (two 15 mg tablets) daily for 14 days, given with food. In G6PD-deficient patients, weekly low-dose regimens have been used under specialist supervision but carry significant complexity &#8212; refer to infectious diseases or a travel medicine clinic for this scenario. Primaquine is contraindicated in pregnancy (risk of haemolysis in a G6PD-deficient foetus). This is a specialist discussion for most NZ GPs; ID or travel medicine clinic input is warranted.</p>

<h2>Standby emergency treatment (SBET)</h2>

<p>For most NZ travellers, prophylaxis plus early medical review on fever development is the standard approach. However, some travellers are genuinely remote &#8212; on extended overland journeys, expedition travel to areas without medical facilities, or visiting very remote islands &#8212; and may be unable to access medical care for 24 hours or more after developing fever.</p>

<p>In this situation, standby emergency treatment (SBET) is appropriate. The traveller carries a full treatment course of an appropriate antimalarial, with written instructions to self-treat if they develop fever and cannot access a medical facility within 24 hours. This is a pragmatic harm-reduction strategy, not a replacement for seeking medical care &#8212; the patient should still reach a medical facility as soon as possible for blood film confirmation and to ensure treatment has worked.</p>

<p>The preferred SBET regimen for most NZ travellers is artemether-lumefantrine (Riamet&#174;, Coartem&#174;): six doses over 60 hours (4 tablets at 0, 8, 24, 36, 48, and 60 hours), taken with food or milk (high-fat meal improves absorption). It is effective against multidrug-resistant <em>P. falciparum</em>. Atovaquone-proguanil (adult dose: 4 adult tablets once daily for 3 days) is a reasonable alternative if artemether-lumefantrine is not available. SBET is not appropriate if the traveller is on the same drug for prophylaxis.</p>

<p>Document SBET prescribing carefully and counsel the patient that a negative self-treatment attempt does not rule out malaria &#8212; they must still seek medical review on return or on reaching facilities.</p>
