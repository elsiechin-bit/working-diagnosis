  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>SpO2 at rest: above 95% is generally safe for short-haul without formal testing; 92-95% warrants formal hypoxia inhalation test (HIT) consideration; below 92% - supplemental in-flight oxygen is likely required. FEV1 below 50% predicted also warrants HIT referral.</li>
    <li>Pneumothorax not fully resolved on chest X-ray is an absolute contraindication to flying. After radiological resolution: minimum 7 days (traumatic) to 6 weeks (spontaneous, given recurrence risk).</li>
    <li>Plaster casts applied within 24-48 hours of flight must be bivalved (split and held with crepe bandage) to allow for swelling at altitude. Document this in the notes.</li>
    <li>MEDIF form required for in-flight supplemental oxygen, recent hospitalisation, and other conditions - submit to the airline's medical department 48-72 hours before departure; oxygen arrangements may need 2-4 weeks' notice.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>Fitness-to-fly assessment is a routine but underappreciated consultation. The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> lists minimum intervals for common conditions and covers in-flight VTE risk. This page goes deeper on the two tools that underpin the assessment - the hypoxia inhalation test and the MEDIF form - and provides specific guidance for cardiac and respiratory conditions beyond the standard waiting period table.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Physiology, HIT, and MEDIF</p>
  <p><strong>The physiology:</strong> commercial aircraft cabins are pressurised to an equivalent altitude of approximately 1,800-2,400 m, producing a partial pressure of oxygen of around 15.1 kPa compared with 21.3 kPa at sea level. As a working rule: SpO2 above 95% at sea level - unlikely to desaturate below 85% in-flight; SpO2 92-95% at rest or FEV1 below 50% predicted - consider formal HIT; SpO2 below 92% at rest - supplemental oxygen likely required.</p>
  <p><strong>Hypoxia inhalation test (HIT):</strong> simulates in-flight conditions by having the patient breathe 15% oxygen in nitrogen for 20 minutes while SpO2 and symptoms are monitored. Performed in secondary care (respiratory medicine) in New Zealand. Refer for HIT: SpO2 at or below 95% at sea level; FEV1 below 50% predicted; significant interstitial lung disease or pulmonary fibrosis; children with cyanotic congenital heart disease; severe pulmonary hypertension; patients who have required oxygen on a previous flight. If HIT shows in-flight SpO2 below 85%, supplemental oxygen is required at the rate indicated (usually 2-4 L/min). SpO2 above 85% - no supplemental oxygen needed.</p>
  <p><strong>MEDIF form:</strong> the standardised form used by most international airlines for passengers with medical conditions requiring special assistance or equipment. Must be completed by the treating physician and submitted in advance (typically 48-72 hours; oxygen arrangements may require 2-4 weeks). Required when the passenger: needs in-flight supplemental oxygen; requires stretcher carriage; travels with a medical device (portable concentrator, CPAP, defibrillator, infusion pump); has a condition that might deteriorate during the flight; has been recently hospitalised or had surgery; or has a communicable disease with in-flight transmission risk. Airlines provide in-flight oxygen at 2-4 L/min from the aircraft's onboard system (arranged via MEDIF) - patients cannot carry their own cylinders. Battery-powered portable oxygen concentrators (POC) are permitted on most airlines if they meet aviation requirements; arrange approval with each airline independently and document the POC model in the MEDIF.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Specific conditions and DVT risk</p>
  <p><strong>Coronary artery disease and recent PCI:</strong> uncomplicated PCI for stable angina - generally 5 days before short-haul, 2 weeks for long-haul. PCI for ACS - 4-6 weeks before long-haul travel. Dual antiplatelet therapy should continue throughout; risk of stent thrombosis from interruption outweighs VTE risk.</p>
  <p><strong>Heart failure:</strong> compensated heart failure is not an absolute contraindication. Decompensated heart failure is. Patient should be euvolaemic and clinically stable; supplemental oxygen may be required if SpO2 below 95%. Do not adjust diuretics immediately before long-haul travel without specialist advice.</p>
  <p><strong>Cardiac implantable devices:</strong> pacemakers, ICDs, and loop recorders are not affected by airport security screening or in-flight electromagnetic environments. Metal detector wands should not be held directly over the device for extended periods; full-body scanners are safe.</p>
  <p><strong>Pneumothorax:</strong> a pneumothorax not fully resolved on chest X-ray is an absolute contraindication to flying (gas expands at altitude). After radiological resolution: minimum 7 days for simple traumatic pneumothorax; some guidance extends to 6 weeks for spontaneous pneumothorax due to recurrence risk. Frank discussion of recurrence risk is needed for young otherwise-well patients who are motivated to travel.</p>
  <p><strong>Middle ear and sinus disease:</strong> active otitis media, sinusitis, and recent ear surgery are relative contraindications due to barotrauma risk on descent. Prescribe a nasal decongestant and counsel on the Valsalva manoeuvre. After mastoidectomy or stapedectomy, defer flying until ENT clearance (typically 2-4 weeks minimum).</p>
  <p><strong>Scuba diving and flying:</strong> minimum before flying - 12-24 hours after single dives not requiring decompression stops; 24 hours after repetitive or multiple days of diving; at least 24 hours (some guidance 48 hours) after dives requiring decompression stops.<sup>1</sup></p>
  <p><strong>Plaster casts:</strong> casts applied within 24-48 hours of flight must be bivalved (split along one or both sides and held with a crepe bandage) to allow for swelling. Document the bivalving; ensure the patient understands they should not walk on a bivalved lower limb cast.</p>

  <p>For patients on therapeutic anticoagulation (warfarin, DOAC), flying is generally safe; existing anticoagulation provides VTE protection. Ensure INR is in range before travel for warfarin-treated patients, and counsel on what to do if a dose is missed mid-trip.</p>
