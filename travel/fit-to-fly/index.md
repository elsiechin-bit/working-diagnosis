<p>Fitness-to-fly assessment is a routine but underappreciated consultation. The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> lists minimum intervals for common conditions and covers in-flight VTE risk. This page goes deeper on the two tools that underpin the assessment &#8212; the hypoxia inhalation test and the MEDIF form &#8212; and provides specific guidance for cardiac and respiratory conditions beyond the standard waiting period table.</p>

<h2>The physiology that matters</h2>

<p>Commercial aircraft cabins are pressurised to an equivalent altitude of approximately 1,800&#8211;2,400 m, producing a partial pressure of oxygen of around 15.1 kPa compared with 21.3 kPa at sea level. A healthy passenger maintains adequate oxygenation easily. The relevant clinical question is: which patients will desaturate to a clinically significant degree at this inspired oxygen level?</p>

<p>As a working rule: if a patient&#8217;s resting SpO&#8322; is above 95% at sea level, they are unlikely to desaturate below 85% in-flight and generally do not need formal hypoxia testing before short-haul travel. If SpO&#8322; is 92&#8211;95% at rest, or FEV&#8322; is below 50% predicted, formal assessment should be considered. If SpO&#8322; is below 92% at rest, supplemental oxygen during flight is likely to be required.</p>

<h2>Hypoxia inhalation test (HIT)</h2>

<p>The hypoxia inhalation test (also called the &#8220;fit to fly test&#8221;) simulates in-flight conditions by having the patient breathe a hypoxic gas mixture (15% oxygen in nitrogen) for 20 minutes while SpO&#8322; and symptoms are monitored. It is the formal assessment used to determine whether supplemental in-flight oxygen is required.</p>

<p><strong>Who should be referred for HIT:</strong></p>
<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Resting SpO&#8322; at or below 95% (at sea level)</li>
  <li>FEV&#8322; below 50% predicted</li>
  <li>Patients with significant interstitial lung disease or pulmonary fibrosis</li>
  <li>Children with cyanotic congenital heart disease</li>
  <li>Severe pulmonary hypertension (resting SpO&#8322; may be adequate but exercise reserve is not)</li>
  <li>Patients who have required oxygen during a previous flight</li>
</ul>

<p>HIT is performed in secondary care (respiratory or ID medicine) in New Zealand; referral is via the patient&#8217;s respiratory physician or as an outpatient assessment. If HIT shows in-flight SpO&#8322; falling below 85%, supplemental oxygen is required at the rate indicated by the test (usually 2&#8211;4 L/min). If SpO&#8322; remains above 85%, no supplemental oxygen is needed.</p>

<h2>MEDIF forms and in-flight oxygen</h2>

<p>The Medical Information Form (MEDIF) is the standardised form used by most international airlines to assess passengers with medical conditions who may require special assistance or medical equipment during the flight. It must be completed by the treating physician and submitted to the airline&#8217;s medical department in advance (typically 48&#8211;72 hours before departure, though some conditions require 2&#8211;4 weeks&#8217; notice for oxygen arrangements).</p>

<p>A MEDIF is required when the passenger:</p>
<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Requires in-flight supplemental oxygen</li>
  <li>Requires stretcher carriage</li>
  <li>Is travelling with a medical device (portable concentrator, infusion pump, CPAP, defibrillator)</li>
  <li>Has a condition that might deteriorate or become life-threatening during the flight</li>
  <li>Has recently been hospitalised or had surgery</li>
  <li>Has a communicable disease with a risk of in-flight transmission</li>
</ul>

<p>Airlines provide in-flight oxygen at approximately 2&#8211;4 L/min, either from the aircraft&#8217;s onboard system (for passengers who have arranged this via MEDIF) or from the emergency oxygen supply (for in-flight emergencies). Patients cannot carry their own oxygen cylinders. Portable oxygen concentrators (POC) are permitted on most airlines if they are battery-powered and meet aviation requirements &#8212; the patient must arrange approval with each airline independently, as requirements vary. Document the POC model in the MEDIF.</p>

<h2>Specific conditions beyond the interval table</h2>

<p>The minimum interval table in the <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> covers the most common situations. Several conditions require more nuanced assessment:</p>

<p><strong>Coronary artery disease and recent PCI:</strong> The minimum interval after uncomplicated PCI for stable angina is generally 5 days (some guidance 2&#8211;3 days) for short-haul, and 2 weeks for long-haul. After PCI for ACS, 4&#8211;6 weeks is generally advised before long-haul travel. Dual antiplatelet therapy should be continued throughout travel; the risk of stent thrombosis from trip interruption outweighs the VTE risk from the flight.</p>

<p><strong>Heart failure:</strong> Compensated heart failure is not an absolute contraindication to air travel. Decompensated heart failure is. Patients should be euvolaemic and clinically stable before travel; supplemental oxygen may be required if resting SpO&#8322; is below 95%. Diuretics should not be adjusted immediately before long-haul travel without specialist advice.</p>

<p><strong>Cardiac implantable devices:</strong> Pacemakers, ICDs, and loop recorders are not affected by airport security screening or in-flight electromagnetic environments. Metal detector wands should not be held directly over the device for extended periods; full-body scanners are safe. An ICD may fire in-flight if a sustained ventricular arrhythmia occurs &#8212; the clinical question is whether the underlying rhythm disorder is adequately controlled before travel.</p>

<p><strong>Pneumothorax:</strong> Gas within a closed body cavity expands at altitude. A pneumothorax that has not fully resolved on chest X-ray is an absolute contraindication to flying. After radiological resolution: minimum 7 days for a simple traumatic pneumothorax; some guidance extends this to 6 weeks for spontaneous pneumothorax due to recurrence risk. Primary spontaneous pneumothorax patients are often young, otherwise well, and strongly motivated to travel &#8212; frank discussion of recurrence risk is needed.</p>

<p><strong>Middle ear and sinus disease:</strong> Active otitis media, sinusitis, and recent ear surgery are relative contraindications due to the risk of barotrauma during pressure changes (particularly on descent). Prescribe a nasal decongestant and counsel on the Valsalva manoeuvre. After mastoidectomy or stapedectomy, flying should be deferred until cleared by ENT &#8212; typically 2&#8211;4 weeks minimum.</p>

<p><strong>Scuba diving and flying:</strong> Dissolved nitrogen from recent dives can cause decompression sickness at altitude. Recommended minimum intervals before flying: 12&#8211;24 hours after single dives not requiring decompression stops; 24 hours after repetitive dives or multiple days of diving; at least 24 hours (some guidance 48 hours) after dives requiring decompression stops.<sup>1</sup></p>

<p><strong>Plaster casts:</strong> Gas in soft tissue and fractured bone expands at altitude, causing cast tightness and vascular compromise. Plaster casts applied within 24&#8211;48 hours of flight should be bivalved (split along one or both sides and held with a crepe bandage) to allow swelling. Alternatively, the cast can be replaced with a synthetic split cast before travel. Document the bivalving in the notes and ensure the patient understands they should not walk on a bivalved lower limb cast.</p>

<h2>DVT risk stratification and prevention</h2>

<p>In-flight DVT risk is elevated in all passengers on flights above 4 hours, but the absolute risk in healthy low-risk individuals remains low (approximately 1 per 4,600 flights).<sup>2</sup> Risk increases with flight duration and with additional individual risk factors.</p>

<p>For patients on therapeutic anticoagulation (warfarin, DOAC), flying is generally safe; the existing anticoagulation provides VTE protection. Ensure INR is in range before travel for warfarin-treated patients, and counsel on what to do if a dose is missed mid-trip. Warfarin interactions with altitude-associated dehydration and dietary changes abroad are worth mentioning.</p>
