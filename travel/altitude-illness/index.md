<p>The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> covers the three altitude illness syndromes (AMS, HACE, HAPE) and the basics of acetazolamide prophylaxis and emergency treatment. This page provides the diagnostic criteria in usable form, practical acclimatisation schedules for the treks NZ patients commonly attempt, the evidence for ibuprofen as an alternative prophylactic agent, and field management protocols for HACE and HAPE when descent is delayed.</p>

<h2>Lake Louise Score for AMS diagnosis</h2>

<p>Acute mountain sickness (AMS) is defined as headache at altitude plus one or more additional symptoms, scored using the Lake Louise criteria. The score is widely used in clinical trials and field settings because it can be self-administered.</p>

<p><strong>Interpretation:</strong> AMS is diagnosed when headache is present AND total score is 3 or above, having arrived at altitude in the preceding 6&#8211;12 hours. A score of 3&#8211;5 indicates mild AMS; 6 or above indicates severe AMS. The score should not be used if ataxia or altered consciousness is present &#8212; those features indicate HACE, which is a clinical emergency regardless of score.</p>

<p><strong>Testing for ataxia:</strong> Tandem gait test &#8212; ask the person to walk heel-to-toe in a straight line for 10 steps. Inability to maintain the line without stumbling, or loss of balance while stationary with feet together and eyes closed (Romberg test), indicates ataxia in the altitude context. These tests take 30 seconds and should be done routinely on any trekker who is symptomatic at altitude.</p>

<h2>Acclimatisation schedules for common itineraries</h2>

<p>The general rule above 3,000 m is: do not increase sleeping altitude by more than 300&#8211;500 m per night, and take a rest day for every 1,000 m gained. This is often aspirational on commercial treks with fixed itineraries. The practical question is whether the patient&#8217;s planned itinerary is reasonable, and what to do if it is not.</p>

<h2>Ibuprofen versus acetazolamide for AMS prevention</h2>

<p>Acetazolamide is the standard agent for pharmacological AMS prophylaxis, but ibuprofen has emerged as a reasonable alternative with good evidence and a different side-effect profile.</p>

<p>A 2012 randomised controlled trial found that ibuprofen 600 mg three times daily (starting 6 hours before ascent and continuing for 24 hours after reaching altitude) significantly reduced AMS incidence compared with placebo in climbers ascending to altitude.<sup>1</sup> A 2014 meta-analysis supported its efficacy for prevention of AMS but found it less effective than acetazolamide for more severe altitude illness.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>Ibuprofen may be preferred in patients who:</p>
<ul style="padding-left: 1.25rem; color: var(--ink-soft); line-height: 2.0;">
  <li>Are sulfonamide-allergic (acetazolamide is a sulfonamide derivative)</li>
  <li>Cannot tolerate acetazolamide&#8217;s predictable side effects (paraesthesia, polyuria, altered taste of carbonated drinks)</li>
  <li>Have no contraindications to NSAIDs (renal impairment, peptic ulcer disease, aspirin-sensitive asthma)</li>
</ul>

<p>Ibuprofen is not appropriate where the NSAID side-effect profile is a concern, particularly in older patients, those with renal impairment, or those who will be exercising vigorously at altitude where NSAID-related renal risk is higher. For most patients with a straightforward altitude itinerary, acetazolamide remains first choice.</p>

<h2>Who should not ascend above 3,500 m</h2>

<p>Absolute contraindications to high-altitude travel are uncommon but include: prior HACE or HAPE (high recurrence risk at similar altitudes), sickle cell disease (vaso-occlusive crisis risk), severe pulmonary hypertension, and severe uncontrolled heart failure. Most patients with well-controlled cardiac or respiratory disease can ascend with appropriate precautions and a conservative itinerary, but specialist clearance and careful pre-travel counselling are warranted. Pacemakers and implantable cardiac devices function normally at altitude. Children with congenital heart disease, particularly those with cyanotic lesions or pulmonary hypertension, need specialist assessment before altitude travel.</p>

<p>A previous episode of AMS is predictive of recurrence at similar altitudes, but does not preclude future altitude travel &#8212; it indicates that a slower ascent profile and/or acetazolamide prophylaxis is particularly warranted.</p>
