Oral contraceptives remain among the most prescribed and reliable reversible contraceptive methods in New Zealand. This guide covers all commonly available formulations, explains mechanisms and ingredients, clarifies PHARMAC funding, and provides practical troubleshooting for common side effects.

## Understanding Oral Contraceptive Mechanisms

Oral contraceptives prevent pregnancy through multiple overlapping mechanisms, all of which contribute to their high efficacy (>99% perfect use, ~91% typical use).

### Estrogen component

Ethinyl estradiol (EE) is the standard estrogen in New Zealand oral contraceptives, ranging from 20–30 μg per pill. Estrogen dose does not improve contraceptive efficacy above 20 μg; higher doses increase side effects. Lower-dose pills may allow slightly earlier ovulation suppression after pill stoppage, though clinical differences are minimal.

**Mechanism:** Estrogen suppresses follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), preventing follicle maturation and estrogen rise. It also alters cervical mucus, making it thicker and less penetrable to sperm.

### Progestogen component

Progestogens inhibit luteinising hormone (LH), preventing ovulation even if estrogen suppression is incomplete. Different progestogens have subtly different metabolic and androgenic profiles.

**Second-generation progestogens** (levonorgestrel, norgestimate): Well-established use, predictable metabolism, low VTE risk. Levonorgestrel remains the benchmark for safety comparisons.

**Third-generation progestogens** (gestodene, desogestrel): Developed to reduce androgenicity. VTE risk is comparable to levonorgestrel based on current evidence, contrary to earlier concerns. More expensive.

**Fourth-generation progestogens** (drospirenone): Novel antimineralocorticoid activity with clinical benefits for fluid retention and acne. No increased VTE risk. Higher cost.

### Combined effect

Progestogen provides backup ovulation suppression if estrogen dose is inadequate. This redundancy means:

- Missing one pill is usually tolerated (LH suppression continues)
- VTE risk correlates more with estrogen dose than progestogen type (for second- and third-generation formulations)
- Consistency matters more than which specific pill is chosen

## Troubleshooting Common Side Effects

Most side effects resolve by 3 months as the body adapts. Before switching pills, counsel users to persist if tolerable, and use targeted strategies to manage specific symptoms.

### Breakthrough bleeding (BTB)

**Definition:** Unscheduled vaginal bleeding whilst taking active pills.

**Mechanism:** Often results from insufficient endometrial proliferation (due to low estrogen dose) or inadequate progestogen suppression of FSH, allowing breakthrough follicular activity.

**Management:**
- Reassure: BTB is not a sign of failed contraception. Pregnancy rates are unaffected.
- First-line: Continue pill without change. Most BTB resolves by cycle 3.
- Ensure perfect adherence: Missed pills or late pills are the most common cause.
- Consider doxycycline: If BTB persists, low-dose doxycycline (50 mg daily) for 10–14 days can accelerate endometrial stabilisation by suppressing proinflammatory cytokines.
- Switch to triphasic formulation: If BTB persists after 3 cycles on monophasic pill, try Cilest or Gracial. Varying progestogen doses may stabilise endometrium.
- Increase estrogen: Switch from 20 μg to 30 μg EE formulation if triphasic fails and BTB is troublesome.
- Ibuprofen trial: NSAIDs may improve endometrial stability via COX inhibition; try 400 mg three times daily during affected cycles.

### Nausea

**Mechanism:** Estrogen-induced, dose-dependent. More common in first 1–2 cycles.

**Management:**
- Reassure: Usually mild and transient.
- Take pill with food: Reduces gastric irritation.
- Take pill in evening: Nausea occurs whilst user is asleep.
- Continue for 3 months: Nausea resolves in >60% of users by cycle 3.
- Ginger or metoclopramide: Consider prochlorperazine 5 mg daily for 1–2 weeks if severe.
- Switch to lower estrogen: If persistent, try 20 μg EE formulation (Yaz, Mercilon).

### Mood changes

**Mechanism:** Estrogen modulates serotonin and GABA signalling. Individual sensitivity varies widely. Role of progestogen is debated.

**Red flags:** Suicidal ideation or severe depressive episodes warrant immediate pill cessation and mental health referral.

**Management:**
- Screen baseline mood: Ask about depression, anxiety, or dysphoria at initiation.
- Continue for 3 months: Mood disturbances often resolve as brain adapts.
- Consider lifestyle: Stress, sleep, and exercise significantly influence mood on OCP.
- Trial drospirenone-containing pill: Limited evidence, but some users report mood improvement with Yasmin or Yaz (possibly placebo or due to reduced fluid retention affecting mood).
- Switch to progestogen-only pill: If worsening mood persists, consider switching to progestogen-only pill or non-hormonal method.
- Psychiatric support: Concurrent antidepressants (SSRIs) are safe with OCP.

### Headaches and migraine

**Mechanism:** Estrogen withdrawal during hormone-free interval can trigger migraines. Continuous progestogen can help. Aura-type migraine carries VTE risk with estrogen; non-aura migraine does not.

**Red flags:** New onset migraine with aura is an absolute contraindication to estrogen-containing pills.

**Management:**
- Migraine without aura: All OCPs are safe.
- Migraine with aura: Use progestogen-only pill or non-hormonal method.
- Withdrawal headache: Reduce or eliminate hormone-free interval using Yaz (24/4 cycle), or use extended-cycle regimen (skip placebo weeks).
- Timed analgesia: If headache occurs during hormone-free interval, take ibuprofen or paracetamol daily during those 4–7 days.
- Magnesium supplement: 400–500 mg daily may reduce migraine frequency (evidence weak but safe).

### Weight change

**Mechanism:** Estrogen and progestogen affect appetite, fluid retention, and fat distribution. Most weight changes are modest (<2 kg).

**Management:**
- Baseline assessment: Weigh user before starting pill.
- Reassure: Weight gain >2 kg is uncommon. Most users gain 0–2 kg or remain stable.
- Check timing: Weight changes are often unrelated to pill; distinguish appetite changes from actual fat gain.
- Fluid retention: Drospirenone (Yasmin, Yaz) is antimineralocorticoid; may help if bloating is prominent.
- Lower estrogen: If weight gain persists and is bothersome, try lower-dose formulation (20 μg EE).
- Lifestyle optimisation: Encourage consistent exercise and balanced diet; OCP should not prevent weight loss if calories are managed.

### Breast tenderness

**Mechanism:** Estrogen-induced fluid retention and breast tissue proliferation.

**Management:**
- Reassure: Usually mild and transient.
- Supportive bra: May reduce discomfort.
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 400 mg as needed for pain.
- Continue for 3 months: Resolves in most users.
- Drospirenone trial: Yasmin or Yaz may reduce fluid retention.
- Lower estrogen: If persistent, switch to 20 μg EE formulation.

### Acne

**Mechanism:** Progestogens vary in androgenicity. Some users experience improved acne; others worsen.

**Management:**
- Assess baseline: Ask about acne at initiation; may improve or worsen unpredictably.
- Drospirenone-containing pills: Yasmin and Yaz have anti-androgenic properties; consider for users with acne or polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS).
- Topical treatments: Benzoyl peroxide or tretinoin can be used safely alongside any OCP.
- Antibiotics: Doxycycline (50 mg daily) for acne is safe with OCP.
- Persistent severe acne: If acne worsens despite pill optimisation, consider referral to dermatology or gynaecology for isotretinoin (requires two contraceptive methods).

### Decreased libido

**Mechanism:** Estrogen may reduce testosterone slightly. Psychosocial factors are significant.

**Management:**
- Reassure: No direct link between pill use and libido loss. Relationship stress, mood, and partner communication are usually more relevant.
- Continue for 3 months: Libido often normalises.
- Hormonal optimisation: If persistent, switching pill type is unlikely to help. Consider progestogen-only pill or non-hormonal method if partner communication and relationship factors are ruled out.
- Sexual health support: Refer to sexual health specialist if concerned.

## Prescriber Management Strategies

### First-time users

- **Timing:** Start on first day of period (immediate protection) or first Sunday after period (Sunday start) for convenience.
- **Backup contraception:** Recommend condoms for 7 days if starting mid-cycle (to allow progestogen to suppress LH).
- **Counsel:**
  - Pill efficacy depends on adherence; missing pills increases pregnancy risk.
  - Common side effects often resolve by 3 months.
  - Breakthrough bleeding is not contraceptive failure.
  - When to stop pill and seek advice: severe headache with aura, unilateral leg pain/swelling (DVT), chest pain, severe abdominal pain.

- **Choice of formulation:** Start with PHARMAC-funded monophasic 30 μg EE pill (Levora or Microgynon) unless contraindication or specific indication exists (e.g., acne → Yasmin; dysmenorrhoea → Yaz).

### Switching from one pill to another

**Within the same cycle:** Finish current packet, then start new pill. No extra contraceptive protection needed.

**Different timing in cycle:** No additional contraceptive cover is needed if switching pills within the same cycle, as progestogen ovulation suppression continues.

**Switching back to a previously tried pill:** Reassure user that previous tolerability doesn't predict current tolerability; re-trial is reasonable if dose/formulation has changed.

### Managing contraceptive failure or non-adherence

- **Missed pills:**
  - 1 pill missed: Take immediately when remembered, continue as normal. No backup needed.
  - 2+ pills missed: Take most recent missed pill immediately, continue daily pills. Use condoms for 7 days. Consider emergency contraception (levonorgestrel within 72 hours, copper IUD within 5 days).
  - If missed pills include the first or last pill of packet: Greater risk of ovulation. Advise longer backup period.

- **Diarrhoea/vomiting:** If occurs within 3–4 hours of pill, absorption may be impaired. Treat as missed pill. Antimotility agents (loperamide) can be used alongside pill.

- **Drug interactions:** Antibiotics (except metronidazole) do not reduce OCP efficacy. Enzyme inducers (rifampicin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, St John's Wort) reduce OCP levels; recommend long-acting reversible contraception or increased pill dose with enzyme inducers.

### Monitoring and follow-up

- **Timing:** Review at 3 months to assess tolerability and side effects.
- **Parameters:**
  - Side effect profile: Are breakthrough bleeding, nausea, or mood changes settling?
  - Adherence: Is user taking pills consistently?
  - Cycle control: Are breakthrough bleeding or breakthrough ovulation occurring?
  - Sexual function: Any change in libido or satisfaction?

- **Blood pressure:** Check at initiation and annually. If >140/90 mmHg, consider switching to progestogen-only method.

- **Weight:** Document at initiation and annually. Most users remain stable; exclude diet/activity changes before attributing weight change to pill.

### When to consider switching pills

- Persistent breakthrough bleeding (>3 cycles) despite adherence → try triphasic formulation or increase estrogen dose.
- Unmanageable nausea (after 3 cycles) → switch to 20 μg EE formulation or lower dose.
- Mood disturbance (after 3 months) → consider progestogen-only pill; trial of drospirenone-containing pill is optional.
- Headaches with aura → contraindicated; switch to progestogen-only pill or non-hormonal method.
- Acne exacerbation → trial of drospirenone-containing pill (Yasmin, Yaz) for anti-androgenic effect.

### When to refer or cease OCP

- Unilateral leg pain, swelling, or calf tenderness: Assess for DVT; cease pill pending imaging.
- Severe headache or migraine with aura: Cease pill; refer to neurology if new onset.
- Chest pain: Cease pill; assess for pulmonary embolism.
- Severe mood disturbance or suicidal ideation: Cease pill; refer to mental health.
- Visual changes (scotomata, peripheral visual loss): Cease pill; refer to ophthalmology.
- Uncontrolled hypertension (>160/100 mmHg): Cease pill; refer to hypertension specialist.
- Age >40 years with smoking: Cessation of OCP recommended due to increased cardiovascular risk.

## Safety Considerations

### Absolute contraindications to estrogen-containing pills

- Current or history of VTE (DVT or PE)
- Migraine with aura
- Age ≥35 years and smoking (any amount)
- Major surgery with prolonged immobilisation
- Known thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, Prothrombin G20210A, Antithrombin III deficiency, Protein C/S deficiency)
- History of stroke or myocardial infarction
- Uncontrolled hypertension (>160/100 mmHg)
- Superficial thrombophlebitis
- Current or history of breast cancer
- Hepatic cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma
- Current or recent combination HRT use (within 3 months)

### Relative contraindications (use progestogen-only pill instead)

- Age ≥35 years and smoking (switch to progestogen-only pill rather than cease contraception entirely)
- Well-controlled hypertension (140–159/90–99 mmHg)
- History of gestational diabetes
- Systemic lupus erythematosus with positive antiphospholipid antibodies
- Valvular heart disease without thrombosis

### Drug interactions

- Enzyme inducers (rifampicin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, St John's Wort): Reduce OCP efficacy. Recommend long-acting reversible contraception or additional barrier contraception.
- Certain antibiotics: Metronidazole may interact; others (amoxicillin, erythromycin) do not reduce OCP efficacy in current evidence.
- Antidepressants: SSRIs are safe; no dose adjustment needed.
