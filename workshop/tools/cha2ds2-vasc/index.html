The CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc score was derived from large European cohorts (16,622 patients with AF across 29 studies) and validates the original CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚ scheme by stratifying intermediate-risk patients. ARISTOTLE (apixaban in AF)

Landmark trial (18,201 patients) showing apixaban superior to warfarin for stroke prevention in AF, with lower major bleeding and all-cause mortality. Baseline median CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc was 3.5; anticoagulation reduced stroke by 21% relative risk.

Sex as a biological risk factor in AF

Female sex independently increases stroke risk in AF beyond traditional factors, partly due to higher inflammatory markers and prothrombotic tendencies. The CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc includes female sex to better identify intermediate-risk women who would otherwise be classified as "low-risk" on CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚ alone.

Andersson T, et al. Lancet. 2013;381(9879):1668-1676.

Very low-risk AF and anticoagulation

In patients with CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc 0 or 1, absolute stroke risk is <1% per year without anticoagulation. Current international guidelines do not recommend routine anticoagulation in these patients, though individual factors (type of AF, reversibility of risk factors) may change practice.

Kirchhof P, et al. Eur Heart J. 2019;40(32):2680-2769.
NZ-specific context: HealthPathways & Cardiac Society guidance

HealthPathways Canterbury recommends anticoagulation for CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc â‰Ą2 in men and â‰Ą3 in women, aligning with ESC 2019 guidance. The NZ Cardiac Society position supports this for paroxysmal, persistent, and permanent AF. Consider shared decision-making for borderline risk (score 1-2).

DOAC vs. warfarin in primary care

DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are first-line in primary care AF. They avoid INR monitoring, have fewer drug interactions, and are superior to warfarin for reducing intracranial haemorrhage. Warfarin remains relevant only if: mechanical valve, severe CKD (eGFR <15), patient preference, or cost.

When CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc = 1: be thoughtful

A score of 1 is the "grey zone." Most guidelines suggest anticoagulation for â‰Ą2, but a single point (e.g., age 65-74 alone, or female sex alone) may not warrant it. Document your reasoning and consider whether that single factor is truly active (e.g., recent hypertension diagnosis vs. well-controlled).

Reversible risk factors matter

If the only reason for a CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc â‰Ą2 is hypertension that was just diagnosed and is being aggressively treated, consider delaying anticoagulation for 3-6 months while optimising BP, then recalculate. Similarly, if someone has AF triggered by acute coronary syndrome, reassess once acute phase is over.

CKD modifies DOAC choice

In moderate-severe CKD (eGFR 15-60), all DOACs are usable but require dose adjustment. Dabigatran is contraindicated in eGFR <30. If eGFR <15, consider warfarin or specialist review. Always check current renal function before prescribing and annually thereafter.

Hybrid approaches: DOAC dosing in borderline cases

For patients with CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc â‰Ą2 but bleeding risk concerns, evidence supports using lower-dose DOACs in specific scenarios (age â‰Ą60, weight <60 kg, creatinine >1.5). Document the rationale; this is not "underdosing" but risk-stratified dosing.

Paroxysmal AF carries the same stroke risk

Many GPs assume paroxysmal AF is "lower risk" than persistent. This is incorrect-stroke risk is determined by CHAâ‚‚DSâ‚‚-VASc score, not AF type. However, some patients with AF triggered by treatable causes (alcohol, overweight, sleep apnoea) may have lower recurrence if triggers are addressed.

Aspirin is no longer first-line in AF

Aspirin is inferior to anticoagulation for stroke prevention in AF and is no longer recommended as monotherapy in moderate-high-risk patients. It may still be used for secondary prevention if anticoagulation is truly contraindicated, but this is rare with modern DOACs.
