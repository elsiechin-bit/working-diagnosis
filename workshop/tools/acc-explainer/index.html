A plain-language guide

Understanding ACC

New Zealand's no-fault accident compensation scheme — what it is, how it works, and how to access support.

What ACC actually is

The Accident Compensation Corporation is a Crown entity that provides universal, no-fault personal injury cover for everyone in New Zealand — citizens, residents, and visitors alike. Set up in 1974 under the Accident Compensation Act, it replaced the right to sue for personal injury with a single nationwide scheme.

If you're hurt in NZ — at work, on the road, playing sport, slipping in the shower — ACC covers your treatment and recovery, regardless of who caused it.

Quick numbers: ACC accepts roughly 2 million new claims a year, manages $50B+ in investment assets, and covers everyone in NZ at any moment — about 5 million people plus visitors.

Browse the official site: acc.co.nz

The "social contract" — what you give up, what you get

The trade-off: In exchange for guaranteed cover, you generally cannot sue anyone for personal injury in NZ. This is unique globally and is the foundation of the scheme.

Recommended by the 1967 Woodhouse Report, the scheme rests on five principles:

  • Community responsibility — society as a whole bears the cost of injury.
  • Comprehensive entitlement — cover for all, regardless of cause or fault.
  • Complete rehabilitation — restore the injured person, not just compensate.
  • Real compensation — meaningful income replacement, not a token payment.
  • Administrative efficiency — one scheme, no court battles.

The 5 levy accounts

ACC isn't funded by general tax. It runs on five separate accounts, each with its own levy payers and a ring-fenced pool of money.

🛠️

Work Account

Injuries at work or caused by work.

Funded by: employer + self-employed levies
👥

Earners' Account

Non-work injuries to wage/salary earners (e.g. weekend sports).

Funded by: PAYE deduction from earners
🚗

Motor Vehicle Account

Injuries from motor-vehicle crashes on public roads.

Funded by: vehicle licensing (rego) + petrol levy
🏡

Non-Earners' Account

Non-work injuries to people who don't earn — children, retirees, beneficiaries.

Funded by: government (general taxation)
🏥

Treatment Injury Account

Injuries caused by medical treatment itself.

Funded by: Earners' & Non-Earners' Accounts

How a claim flows

  1. Get treatmentVisit a GP, physio, hospital, or other registered provider. The provider usually lodges the claim form (ACC45) electronically — you don't need to file paperwork yourself.
  2. ACC decides coverACC reviews whether the injury meets the legal definition of a "personal injury caused by accident." Most claims are accepted within days.
  3. Treatment costs coveredSubsidised or fully-covered visits to GPs, physios, specialists, surgery, imaging, prescriptions, and aids/equipment.
  4. Weekly compensation kicks inIf you can't work for more than a week, ACC pays 80% of your pre-injury earnings (up to a cap). Your employer pays the first week.
  5. Rehabilitation planA case manager works with you on returning to work, retraining, home help, transport, or whatever is needed to recover.
  6. Long-term outcomesIf permanent impairment remains, you may receive a lump sum. If a partner or child dies, survivors receive ongoing support.

What ACC pays for — and how to access it

Click any item to expand. Each entry explains what's covered, who's eligible, how to apply, and links to the official ACC page.

🩺
Treatment costs GP, physio, surgery, dental, imaging, prescriptions
▾
What's covered

Visits to GPs, physios, osteos, chiros, podiatrists, specialists, hospital and surgical care, dental for accident-related dental injury, x-rays/MRI/CT scans, and prescription medicines related to your injury.

Eligibility

You must have an accepted ACC claim for the injury. Treatment must be necessary and appropriate for that injury.

How to access
  • Just visit a registered NZ provider — they lodge the ACC45 claim form on your behalf.
  • Show photo ID or your NHI number.
  • ACC pays the provider directly (you may pay a small co-payment depending on the clinic).
Cost to you

Most ACC visits have a part-charge ($30–$60 for a GP is typical). Hospital and ED visits for accidents are fully funded.

Weekly compensation 80% of pre-injury earnings while unable to work
▾
What's covered

If your injury stops you working, ACC pays 80% of your pre-injury weekly earnings, up to a maximum cap (adjusted yearly). Self-employed people are paid based on their declared earnings.

Eligibility
  • You must be an "earner" at the time of injury (employed, self-employed, or shareholder-employee).
  • A medical certificate from your GP must say you're unfit for work.
  • You must be off work more than the first 7 days (employer pays week one at 80%).
How to access
  • Get a medical certificate (ACC18) from your GP at your first visit.
  • ACC sends you an earnings information form — return it with payslips/IR records.
  • Payments are made fortnightly, taxed as income (PAYE).
  • Renew the medical certificate every few weeks until you're cleared to return.

Rehabilitation Return-to-work, retraining, vocational and social rehab
▾
What's covered

Two streams: vocational rehab (getting back to work — graduated return, retraining, modified duties, workplace assessments) and social rehab (everyday-life support — counselling, attendant care, household help).

Eligibility

Anyone with an accepted claim whose injury is creating barriers to work or normal life.

How to access
  • For longer claims, ACC assigns you a recovery team member (case manager).
  • You and the case manager build an Individual Recovery Plan together.
  • The plan lists agreed services, timelines, and goals — you can review and challenge it.
  • Vocational assessments may be arranged with an occupational therapist or specialist.

Help at home Cleaning, cooking, laundry, attendant care
▾
What's covered

Home-help services like vacuuming, washing, ironing, basic cooking, lawn-mowing, and personal care (showering, dressing) if your injury prevents you doing them.

Eligibility
  • You must be unable to do these tasks because of your injury.
  • ACC considers what you did before the injury — they won't pay for jobs someone else in your household always did.
  • An ACC needs assessment determines hours per week.
How to access
  • Tell your GP, physio, or case manager that you need help at home.
  • ACC arranges an assessment by an occupational therapist.
  • Approved hours are delivered by an ACC-contracted provider, or you can claim reimbursement if a friend/family member helps.

Childcare payments If your injury stops you caring for your kids
▾
What's covered

A weekly amount towards childcare costs for children under 14 (or under 18 if disabled), for parents who can't provide care because of their injury.

Eligibility
  • You're the primary caregiver before the injury.
  • Your medical certificate states you can't care for the kids.
How to access
  • Ask your case manager to add childcare to your recovery plan.
  • ACC pays a set weekly rate per child (rates published on the ACC site).
  • You can use a registered carer or a relative — receipts may be required.

Travel & transport Mileage, taxis, public transport to appointments
▾
What's covered

Travel to and from injury-related appointments — by car (mileage), public transport (fares), taxi (if you can't drive or use buses), or air travel for specialist trips.

Eligibility

Travel must be necessary, reasonable, and to a treatment or rehab provider for your covered injury. There's a minimum distance threshold for mileage claims.

How to access
  • Keep records: dates, addresses, distances, receipts.
  • Submit through MyACC online or via your case manager.
  • Mileage rate is the standard ACC rate (updated yearly).
  • Pre-approve taxi or air travel before booking.

Aids, equipment & home modifications Crutches, wheelchairs, ramps, bathroom aids, prosthetics
▾
What's covered

Short-term equipment (crutches, moon boots, slings), long-term aids (wheelchairs, prosthetics, hearing aids if injury-related), home modifications (ramps, grab rails, bathroom alterations), and vehicle modifications (hand controls, hoists).

Eligibility

Equipment must be needed because of your covered injury. Major modifications require an OT assessment and ACC approval before work begins.

How to access
  • Short-term gear: pharmacies, physios, and hospitals can dispense on the spot.
  • Long-term: case manager arranges an occupational therapist assessment.
  • OT writes a recommendation; ACC approves and arranges installation through contracted suppliers.
  • Don't pay out-of-pocket for major items — get pre-approval.

Lump sum for permanent impairment One-off payment if you have lasting impairment of 10% or more
▾
What's covered

A tax-free lump sum if your injury leaves you with permanent whole-person impairment of 10% or more. The amount scales with the impairment percentage, up to a maximum (around $170k+ at the top end).

Eligibility
  • Your injury occurred on or after 1 April 2002 (older injuries get the Independence Allowance instead — a fortnightly payment).
  • You're medically stable — usually at least 2 years after the injury.
  • An accredited assessor measures impairment using AMA guidelines.
How to access
  • Ask your case manager about a Whole Person Impairment (WPI) assessment once your condition has stabilised.
  • ACC arranges and pays for the assessment.
  • If you score 10%+, ACC calculates and pays the lump sum.
  • You can have a reassessment later if your condition worsens.

Survivors' support (fatal injuries) Funeral grant, survivor's grant, weekly payments to spouse and children
▾
What's covered
  • Funeral grant — a contribution toward funeral costs (around $7,000+, indexed yearly).
  • Survivor's grant — tax-free lump sum to spouse, children, and other dependants.
  • Weekly compensation — ongoing payments to the surviving spouse (60% of the deceased's weekly comp, for up to 5 years) and children (20% each, until 18 or older if studying).
  • Childcare payments — for surviving children.
Eligibility

Death must result from a covered personal injury (including treatment injury). Available to spouses, civil-union/de-facto partners, dependent children, and other dependants.

How to access
  • Contact ACC directly: 0800 101 996.
  • ACC assigns a dedicated case manager for fatal claims.
  • You'll need the death certificate, proof of relationship, and (for weekly comp) the deceased's earnings records.
📎 acc.co.nz — Help after a fatal injury
🧠
Sensitive claims & mental injury Counselling and support for mental injury caused by sexual abuse or trauma
▾
What's covered

Mental injury caused by sexual abuse, certain criminal acts, or as a consequence of a covered physical injury or qualifying work-related traumatic event. Includes counselling, therapy, social workers, and weekly compensation if unable to work.

Eligibility

You don't need to have reported the abuse to police. The path to ACC is through an approved mental-health provider who lodges the claim.

How to access
  • Find a sensitive-claims-registered counsellor or social worker — directories are on the ACC site or via Find Support / 0800 735 566.
  • Initial sessions are funded while ACC assesses cover.
  • You can self-refer; no GP referral is needed.

Injury prevention programmes Public campaigns, workplace safety, community grants
▾
What's covered

Not a personal entitlement — these are ACC-funded programmes that aim to reduce injuries before they happen: workplace safety partnerships, road safety campaigns, sports injury prevention (e.g. RugbySmart, SportSmart), home and community programmes, and the Live Stronger for Longer falls-prevention initiative.

How to access (for individuals)
  • Free resources, courses, and toolkits available on the ACC website.
  • Community organisations can apply for partnership funding.

How ACC is structured

ACC is a Crown entity — owned by the government but run at arm's length. It is governed by the Accident Compensation Act 2001 and accountable to Parliament through the Minister for ACC.

If you disagree with a decision

You can ask for a review (handled by an independent reviewer through FairWay or similar). If still unhappy, you can appeal to the District Court, then the High Court, and ultimately the Court of Appeal.

Common questions

Does ACC cover illness?

Generally no. ACC covers injury, not illness. Cancer, heart attacks, mental illness on its own — these are health-system, not ACC. Exceptions exist for work-related illness (e.g. asbestos exposure) and mental injury caused by a covered physical injury or certain traumatic events.

Am I covered as a tourist?

Yes — anyone in NZ is covered for personal injury by accident, including overseas visitors. The same trade-off applies: you cannot sue.

What's the weekly compensation cap?

ACC pays 80% of your pre-injury weekly earnings, up to a maximum income threshold adjusted yearly. High earners may not be fully compensated above that cap.

Who pays the first week off work?

Your employer pays the first week of weekly compensation (at 80%). ACC takes over from week two.

Can I see any doctor I want?

Yes — any registered GP, physio, or specialist in NZ can lodge ACC claims. ACC subsidises the visit; some providers charge a small co-payment on top.

How do I check the status of my claim?

Sign in to MyACC with RealMe to see your claim, payments, and contact your case manager.

Where does ACC's investment money come from?

Levies are collected ahead of expected lifetime claim costs. The surplus is invested by ACC's investment team to fund long-tail claims (someone injured today may need cover for 50+ years). ACC manages tens of billions of dollars in assets.
