# Living with Chronic Pain

Chronic pain - pain that persists for more than three months - affects roughly one in five New Zealand adults. If you're living with it, you're not alone. This guide is here to help you understand what's happening, what you can do about it, and when to see your GP.

## What is chronic pain?

When you injure yourself, pain is useful. It warns you something is wrong and stops you from making it worse. Usually, pain fades as you heal.

Chronic pain is different. Your nervous system keeps sending pain signals long after the initial injury has healed, or sometimes in the absence of any clear injury at all. It's real pain - not in your head - but it doesn't work like acute injury pain anymore.

Common types of chronic pain include lower back pain, osteoarthritis, fibromyalgia, headaches and migraines, neuropathic pain (like diabetic nerve pain), and chronic pain after injury or surgery.

## Why does pain persist?

Think of chronic pain as your nervous system learning a habit. The nerves in your spine and brain become sensitised - they turn up their alarm volume and keep it turned up, even when there's no danger.

This happens partly because of physical changes in your tissues and nerves, but also because of how your nervous system has learned to respond to certain movements, situations, or emotions. Your brain is essentially trying to protect you, but it's overprotecting.

Several things keep chronic pain going:
- **Movement avoidance**: avoiding activity because it hurts, which weakens muscles and makes pain worse
- **Stress and worry**: anxiety about pain, fear of re-injury, and stress all amplify pain signals
- **Sleep disruption**: poor sleep makes pain feel worse and harder to cope with
- **Negative thinking**: focusing on pain, catastrophising, and believing pain means damage
- **Deconditioning**: being less fit and strong means your body handles pain poorly

Understanding this is actually good news: because pain isn't just about tissue damage, it can improve even when scans show wear and tear.

## The biopsychosocial model

Your pain isn't just physical. It's also shaped by how you think about it, how stressed you are, how well you sleep, your relationships, your work, and your general wellbeing.

This doesn't mean it's "all in your head." It means the whole picture matters.

A GP or specialist might recommend that you work on multiple areas at once:
- Physical: movement, strength, flexibility
- Psychological: pain management skills, addressing worry or low mood
- Social: staying connected, keeping activities meaningful

The best outcomes come from addressing all three.

## Self-management strategies

### Movement and exercise

This might sound counterintuitive, but moving is usually better than resting. The goal isn't to be pain-free during exercise; it's to gradually rebuild strength and confidence.

- Start gently: a 10-minute walk, stretching, or water-based exercise
- Aim for activities you find tolerable or enjoyable, not just what you think you "should" do
- Build gradually: add a little more movement each week
- Consistency matters more than intensity: three times a week is better than one hard session
- Don't avoid movement because of fear; fear of pain often becomes the bigger problem
- Strength work helps: gentle resistance training or physio-guided exercises rebuild muscle that supports your painful area

### Pacing

Rather than boom-bust (doing lots on good days, crashing on bad days), aim for consistent activity. This means:
- Do less on good days than you're capable of
- Don't overdo it because you feel okay
- Plan ahead: list activities you need to do, spread them across the week

### Sleep

Pain is worse when you're tired. Good sleep hygiene matters:
- Aim for 7-9 hours
- Keep bedtimes consistent
- Avoid screens an hour before bed
- Make your bedroom cool, dark, and quiet
- Avoid caffeine after 2 pm

If pain is keeping you awake, talk to your GP about short-term support while you work on other strategies.

### Stress and mood

Stress amplifies pain. Things that help:
- Mindfulness or meditation (even 5 minutes daily helps)
- Deep breathing when you notice tension
- Time in nature or green spaces
- Hobbies and activities that feel meaningful
- Staying socially connected
- Limiting catastrophic thinking ("my pain will never get better" vs "today is hard, but it does improve with time")

If you're struggling with low mood or anxiety, tell your GP. These are common with chronic pain and treatable.

### Heat and ice

Both can help temporarily:
- Heat (warm bath, heat pack) relaxes muscles
- Ice reduces inflammation in the first 48 hours after injury
- Neither fixes the underlying problem, but they may reduce symptoms enough to help you move better

### Medications

Depending on your pain type, medications might include:
- Paracetamol and NSAIDs (ibuprofen): most effective for inflammatory pain
- Paracetamol alone often works better than stronger opioids for chronic pain
- Neuropathic pain medications like gabapentin or pregabalin
- Muscle relaxants for muscle tightness
- Antidepressants (low-dose) help some people, especially with sleep

Medications work best alongside self-management, not instead of it. Talk to your GP about what fits your situation.

### Other approaches

- **Physio**: tailored exercise programs with professional guidance
- **Acupuncture**: evidence is mixed but some people find it helpful
- **Massage**: can help muscle tension, though effects are usually temporary
- **Psychological therapies**: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) have strong evidence for chronic pain
- **Pain management programs**: group or individual programs that teach pain skills

## When to see your GP

You should see your GP if you have:
- Pain lasting more than three months
- Pain that's getting worse or affecting your life
- Uncertainty about what's causing the pain
- Pain with new symptoms: fever, unexplained weight loss, neurological changes, severe swelling
- Pain affecting sleep, mood, or ability to work
- Difficulty managing pain on your own

Red flags that need urgent assessment (same day):
- Severe pain with fever or signs of infection
- Sudden loss of bladder/bowel control
- Weakness or numbness progressing rapidly
- Pain after trauma or fall

## What to expect at your GP appointment

- Your doctor will ask about pain location, severity, what makes it better or worse, and how it affects your life
- They may examine you and might suggest scans or blood tests
- Together you'll discuss what might be causing it and what options might help
- You might be referred to a specialist or physiotherapist

Keep a brief pain diary before your appointment: when pain is worst, what makes it better, how it affects your activities. This helps your GP understand what's happening.

## Key things to remember

1. **Chronic pain is real**, even if scans show nothing or if symptoms don't match a single diagnosis
2. **Pain doesn't mean damage**: pain can improve even when degenerative changes are visible
3. **You have more control than it feels like**: movement, sleep, stress, and mood all affect pain
4. **Recovery is possible**: many people with chronic pain manage it well and return to activities that matter to them
5. **It takes time**: changes usually take weeks to months, not days
6. **Support helps**: talking to your GP, a physio, or a psychologist makes a real difference
