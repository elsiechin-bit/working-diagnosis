<div class="topic-body">

<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Psoriasis affects approximately 2-3% of the NZ population and is lifelong. It is more than a skin condition: screen for psoriatic arthritis at every visit (affects up to 30% of patients), and monitor cardiovascular and metabolic risk.</li>
    <li>Mild disease (under 3% BSA, DLQI ≤10): topical corticosteroids plus calcipotriol. Daivobet (calcipotriol/betamethasone dipropionate combination) is PHARMAC-funded and highly effective for body and scalp plaques.</li>
    <li>Moderate-to-severe (BSA ≥10% or DLQI >10, or difficult sites): refer for phototherapy or biologics. Several IL-17, IL-23, and TNF inhibitors are PHARMAC-funded with criteria.</li>
    <li>Quality-of-life impact matters as much as body surface area. A small plaque on the hands, face, or genitalia may warrant referral even if the BSA is technically "mild".</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Psoriasis affects approximately 2-3% of the New Zealand population.<sup>1</sup> It is a lifelong condition with a relapsing-remitting course, driven by dysregulated T-cell activity leading to accelerated keratinocyte proliferation. Beyond skin involvement, it carries significant comorbidity burden: psoriatic arthritis, cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, depression, and inflammatory bowel disease all occur at higher rates than in the general population.<sup>2</sup>
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Severity assessment</p>
  <p>
    Two parameters matter: body surface area (BSA) and quality-of-life impact (Dermatology Life Quality Index, DLQI).
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Mild:</strong> BSA under 3% and DLQI ≤10. Manage in primary care with optimised topicals.</li>
    <li><strong>Moderate-to-severe:</strong> BSA ≥10%, or DLQI >10, or involvement of difficult sites (face, hands, feet, genitalia, nails, scalp). Refer for phototherapy or systemic/biologic therapy.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The DLQI is worth using. A patient with 5% BSA involvement but severely affected sleep, social life, and work capacity has moderate-to-severe disease by functional classification. Quality of life drives the referral decision as much as extent.
  </p>
  <p>
    Difficult sites warrant earlier consideration of referral regardless of BSA: palmoplantar psoriasis (hands and feet) is functionally disabling; facial and scalp psoriasis is visible and psychologically burdensome; nail psoriasis (pitting, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis) is often treatment-resistant. Genital psoriasis is underreported and underdiagnosed - it warrants specific enquiry and appropriate management.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Topical management</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Topical corticosteroids</strong> are first-line for mild-to-moderate plaque psoriasis. Potent or very potent formulations are needed for body plaques - the thickened scale of psoriasis requires higher potency than, for example, eczema. Moderate potency for face and flexures. Intermittent use (weekend therapy, pulse dosing) reduces the risk of tachyphylaxis.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Vitamin D analogues</strong> (calcipotriol) are often combined with topical corticosteroids. The fixed-dose combination calcipotriol/betamethasone dipropionate (Daivobet gel or ointment) is PHARMAC-funded and highly effective for body plaques and scalp psoriasis. The combination allows once-daily application and reduces the total corticosteroid exposure compared with TCS alone.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Emollients</strong> reduce scale and itch and improve the efficacy of active treatments. They are an adjunct, not a standalone treatment.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Scalp psoriasis:</strong> Daivobet scalp gel is effective. Coal tar shampoos help with scale. For thick adherent scale, overnight application of salicylic acid 2-3% ointment under a shower cap softens scale before topical treatment.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Psoriatic arthritis: screen at every visit</p>
  <p>
    Psoriatic arthritis develops in up to 30% of people with psoriasis and can occur before, with, or after the onset of skin disease. It causes irreversible joint damage and requires DMARD therapy - missed diagnosis delays treatment in the same way as rheumatoid arthritis. Screen at every psoriasis review:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>Inflammatory joint symptoms: morning stiffness lasting over 30 minutes, joints that improve with activity</li>
    <li>Dactylitis: diffuse swelling of an entire digit ("sausage finger" or "sausage toe")</li>
    <li>Enthesitis: tenderness at tendon/ligament insertion sites, particularly the Achilles tendon and plantar fascia</li>
    <li>Spinal involvement: inflammatory back pain (worse at rest, better with movement, nocturnal)</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    If psoriatic arthritis is suspected: refer urgently to rheumatology. Investigate with RF (usually negative), anti-CCP (usually negative), CRP/ESR, and X-rays of affected joints.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Systemic therapy and comorbidity management</p>
  <p>
    Moderate-to-severe psoriasis not responding to optimised topicals warrants referral for phototherapy, conventional systemic therapy, or biologics. Narrowband UVB phototherapy is effective and available through dermatology. Conventional systemic agents include methotrexate and cyclosporin - both require monitoring and are initiated and managed by specialists.
  </p>
  <p>
    Biologic therapies have transformed outcomes for moderate-to-severe psoriasis. Several agents are PHARMAC-funded under Special Authority criteria, including TNF inhibitors (etanercept, adalimumab) and newer IL-17 inhibitors (secukinumab, ixekizumab) and IL-23 inhibitors (guselkumab, risankizumab). Response rates in clinical trials exceed 90% clearance for the newer agents. Criteria require documented failure of at least two conventional therapies.
  </p>
  <p>
    Comorbidity management is an active part of psoriasis care in primary care. Cardiovascular risk should be assessed and managed - the inflammation burden in psoriasis confers an independent cardiovascular risk equivalent to moderate cardiovascular disease in severe cases. Screen for metabolic syndrome (obesity, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, insulin resistance). Address alcohol use - it worsens psoriasis directly and interacts with methotrexate if the patient is referred for systemic therapy. Depression and anxiety are prevalent; screen with PHQ-9 and GAD-7 and manage proactively.
  </p>
</div>
