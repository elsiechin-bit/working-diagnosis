Skin is the most common reason patients book a GP appointment after general malaise and respiratory symptoms. Most of it is straightforward. The hard part is recognising the few presentations that are not. New Zealand has one of the highest melanoma incidence rates in the world, which shapes how we approach every pigmented lesion and every "I just want this checked" consultation.[^1] This section maps conditions by clinical category and links to deeper pages on each.

---

## Pigmented lesions and skin cancer

The can-not-miss category. Australian and NZ data suggest one in 14 New Zealanders will be diagnosed with melanoma in their lifetime, and more than 80% of those melanomas are first picked up by the patient or a primary-care clinician.[^1] [^2] A high index of suspicion, dermoscopy where you can, and a low threshold for excision biopsy are the three things that change outcomes.

### Common presentations

- **Melanoma**  -  asymmetry, irregular border, multiple colours, diameter > 6mm, or change over time (ABCDE). The "ugly duckling" lesion is the one that looks unlike the others on that patient. Nodular melanoma can lack ABCDE features and is identified by EFG: Elevated, Firm, Growing.
- **Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)**  -  pearly translucent papule with telangiectasia, often on sun-exposed sites; central ulceration in larger lesions. Indolent locally; metastasis is rare.
- **Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)**  -  scaly, tender, hyperkeratotic, often on the dorsum of hands, scalp, lower lip, ears. Faster growing than BCC; metastasis possible especially from lip/ear/immunosuppressed sites.
- **Actinic (solar) keratoses**  -  rough, scaly patches on chronically sun-exposed skin; precursor to SCC in a small fraction. Field treatment with cryotherapy, 5-fluorouracil, or imiquimod where indicated.
- **Seborrhoeic keratoses**  -  benign, "stuck-on" warty plaques. Common after age 40. Diagnosis usually clinical; reassurance unless atypical.
- **Dysplastic naevi**  -  atypical moles in patients with the dysplastic naevus syndrome carry an elevated personal melanoma risk. Photographic surveillance and dermatology referral if multiple or suspicious.

### Red flags to exclude

- New, changing, or symptomatic pigmented lesion - especially after age 40
- Non-healing ulcer or "spot that bleeds when I knock it" on sun-exposed skin
- Rapidly growing pink or red nodule (consider amelanotic melanoma, Merkel cell carcinoma)
- Lesion in immunosuppressed patient (transplant, biologics) - lower threshold for biopsy
- Family history of melanoma in two or more first-degree relatives - dermatology surveillance

## Inflammatory dermatoses

The bulk of the dermatology consult: eczema, psoriasis, and rosacea between them account for a large share of repeat visits. They share a final common pathway of cutaneous inflammation but the patterns, triggers, and treatment ladders are distinct.

### Common presentations

- **Atopic eczema**  -  itchy, ill-defined erythematous patches in flexures (cubital and popliteal fossae) in older children and adults; cheeks and extensors in infants. Personal or family history of atopy. Dry, lichenified, sometimes weeping or crusted in flares. Bacterial and viral superinfection are common (eczema herpeticum is a referral).[^3]
- **Contact dermatitis (irritant or allergic)**  -  geography matches the trigger (rings, watch straps, perfume, hair dye, latex). Patch testing in unclear cases.
- **Seborrhoeic dermatitis**  -  greasy yellow scale on scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial folds, mid-chest. Malassezia-driven. Adult and infantile (cradle cap) forms.
- **Psoriasis**  -  well-demarcated salmon-pink plaques with silvery scale, classically on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, and sacrum. Nail pitting, oil-drop sign. Auspitz sign on plaque scratch. Comorbidity matters: psoriatic arthritis, metabolic syndrome, IBD, mood disorders.[^4]
- **Rosacea**  -  centrofacial erythema, telangiectasia, papules and pustules without comedones, ocular involvement in around 20%. Triggers: heat, alcohol, sun, spicy food, hot drinks. Phymatous changes (rhinophyma) in chronic disease.
- **Lichen planus**  -  violaceous, polygonal, pruritic papules; Wickham striae on the surface; oral and genital mucosa often involved.
- **Urticaria**  -  transient wheals, individual lesions resolve within 24 hours. Acute (< 6 weeks) usually self-limited; chronic spontaneous urticaria needs second-generation antihistamines and dermatology if refractory.

### Red flags to exclude

- Erythroderma (> 90% body surface erythema) - admission for fluid loss, temperature dysregulation
- Stevens-Johnson syndrome / TEN - mucosal involvement, painful skin, drug history. Urgent transfer.
- Eczema herpeticum - monomorphic vesicles or punched-out erosions on a background of eczema; aciclovir and dermatology
- Pustular psoriasis with systemic features (fever, malaise) - admission
- Suspected drug eruption with eosinophilia, fever, lymphadenopathy (DRESS) - admission

## Infections of the skin

Bacterial, fungal, and viral skin infections are bread-and-butter primary care work. The pitfalls are missing necrotising fasciitis, missing scabies in an institutional setting, and overdiagnosing fungal where the answer is dermatitis.

### Common presentations

- **Tinea (corporis, pedis, cruris, capitis, unguium)**  -  annular, scaly, advancing edge with central clearing on the body; interdigital maceration on feet; alopecic patches with broken hairs in tinea capitis. Confirm with KOH or fungal culture before oral therapy. Tinea unguium needs lab confirmation before terbinafine.[^5]
- **Bacterial cellulitis**  -  warmth, erythema, tenderness, often on lower limb; portal of entry from tinea or eczema. Streptococcal more often than staphylococcal; ACC indicated if injury-related.
- **Impetigo**  -  honey-coloured crusts on face and limbs, often in children; bullous form (staph). Topical fusidic acid for limited disease; oral flucloxacillin for spreading.
- **Folliculitis and furunculosis**  -  pustules at hair follicles; recurrent disease think nasal carriage of S. aureus, decolonisation may help.
- **Viral warts and molluscum contagiosum**  -  benign, often self-resolving; treat for cosmesis or symptoms.
- **Herpes simplex**  -  recurrent grouped vesicles at a fixed site; oral or genital. Antiviral early in prodrome.
- **Herpes zoster (shingles)**  -  dermatomal painful vesicular rash. Antivirals within 72 hours. Vaccinate eligible adults (Shingrix in NZ; partly funded for 65-year-olds).
- **Scabies**  -  intensely pruritic, worse at night, finger webs, wrist flexures, periumbilical, genitalia. Treat the patient and all close contacts simultaneously with permethrin 5%.

### Red flags to exclude

- Necrotising fasciitis - pain out of proportion, rapidly advancing erythema, systemic toxicity, bullae or skin necrosis. Surgical emergency.
- Periorbital or orbital cellulitis - urgent imaging and IV antibiotics
- Crusted (Norwegian) scabies in an immunosuppressed or institutionalised patient - high transmission risk
- Recurrent impetigo or boils - consider PVL-positive Staph aureus, screen and decolonise

## Acne and follicular conditions

Acne sits at the intersection of dermatology and adolescent mental health. NZ work confirms what international data show: psychological burden is high and underestimated, and effective treatment changes outcomes far beyond the skin.[^6]

### Common presentations

- **Acne vulgaris**  -  comedones (open and closed), inflammatory papules and pustules, nodules and cysts in severe disease. Face, back, chest. Pathogenesis: follicular hyperkeratinisation, sebum, Cutibacterium acnes, inflammation, hormonal contribution.
- **Adult female acne**  -  jawline distribution, premenstrual flare, often with hirsutism or menstrual irregularity (consider PCOS).
- **Hidradenitis suppurativa**  -  recurrent painful nodules and abscesses in apocrine-bearing areas (axillae, groin, infra-mammary). Underdiagnosed; refer early. Smoking and obesity are risk factors.
- **Folliculitis**  -  see infections.
- **Pseudofolliculitis barbae**  -  ingrown hairs in the beard area; common in patients with curly hair.

### Red flags to exclude

- Acne fulminans - sudden severe nodulocystic acne with systemic upset (fever, arthralgia)
- Hirsutism + irregular periods + acne - investigate PCOS, less commonly androgen-secreting tumour
- Refractory disease on adequate treatment by 3 to 6 months - dermatology referral for isotretinoin (specialist-only in NZ)

## Pigmentary disorders

A category where the cosmetic implications drive the consultation as much as the pathology. Sun protection is the through-line.

### Common presentations

- **Melasma**  -  symmetrical brown patches on cheeks, forehead, upper lip, chin. Pregnancy, oral contraception, and UV exposure are triggers. Slow to resolve; often chronic.
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation**  -  brown discolouration after eczema, acne, or trauma. Resolves over months; sun protection accelerates.
- **Vitiligo**  -  well-demarcated depigmented patches, often symmetrical, with classical sites (periorificial, hands, feet). Autoimmune association with thyroid disease, B12 deficiency, type 1 diabetes.
- **Cafe-au-lait macules**  -  benign in small numbers; six or more may suggest neurofibromatosis type 1 in children.
- **Solar lentigines**  -  small flat brown macules on chronically sun-exposed skin. Benign; reassurance after distinguishing from lentigo maligna.

### Red flags to exclude

- Lentigo maligna mistaken for solar lentigo - dermatoscopy or referral if any asymmetry, irregularity, or growth
- New-onset extensive vitiligo - check thyroid function, B12, and screen for autoimmune comorbidities
- Cafe-au-lait macules with axillary freckling, neurofibromas, Lisch nodules, or family history of NF1 - paediatric referral

## Paediatric rashes

Children's skin is its own specialty. Most rashes are viral or atopic. The rarities matter because they can be missed.

### Common presentations

- **Atopic eczema (paediatric pattern)**  -  see inflammatory dermatoses; cheeks and extensors in infants, flexures from toddlerhood.
- **Viral exanthems**  -  morbilliform rashes with fever; measles, rubella, parvovirus B19 (slapped cheek), roseola, hand-foot-and-mouth. Vaccination history matters in NZ given recent measles outbreaks.
- **Cradle cap (infantile seborrhoeic dermatitis)**  -  yellow greasy scale on scalp; benign, settles with bland emollient.
- **Nappy dermatitis**  -  irritant (convex surfaces spared in skin folds), candidal (involves skin folds with satellite pustules), or both.
- **Molluscum contagiosum**  -  pearly umbilicated papules; self-limited but can persist 12 to 24 months. Reassure.
- **Pityriasis rosea**  -  herald patch followed by Christmas-tree distribution on trunk; self-limiting over 6 to 8 weeks.
- **Urticaria**  -  see inflammatory dermatoses.

### Red flags to exclude

- Non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash in an unwell child - meningococcal sepsis, urgent
- Strawberry tongue with rash, conjunctival injection, fever > 5 days - Kawasaki disease
- Targetoid lesions with mucosal involvement - erythema multiforme major / SJS
- Failure to thrive with eczema - immunodeficiency syndromes (rare but consider)
- Honey-coloured crusts in epidemic clusters at school - manage as impetigo and notify if scale warrants

## Common benign lesions

These are most of what patients want "checked". Recognising the benign confidently is as important as recognising the malignant.

### Common presentations

- **Skin tags (acrochordons)**  -  pedunculated soft papules, neck, axillae, groin. Snip excision or cryotherapy if symptomatic.
- **Cherry angiomas**  -  bright-red papules on trunk after age 30; benign.
- **Sebaceous (epidermoid) cysts**  -  central punctum, can become inflamed or infected. Excise quiescent if symptomatic.
- **Lipomas**  -  soft, mobile, subcutaneous; typically painless. Excise for symptoms or diagnostic uncertainty (atypical features warrant imaging).
- **Dermatofibromas**  -  firm, pigmented papule, often on lower legs; "dimple sign" on lateral compression.
- **Pyogenic granulomas**  -  rapidly growing friable vascular nodule; bleeds easily; excise or curette.
- **Sebaceous hyperplasia**  -  yellowish umbilicated papules on the face after middle age; confused with BCC. Dermatoscopy clarifies.

### Red flags to exclude

- Any "benign-looking" lesion that has changed, ulcerated, or bled - low threshold for excision biopsy
- Subcutaneous mass > 5 cm or rapidly growing - imaging to exclude soft tissue sarcoma
- Lesion at sites of prior radiotherapy or chronic ulceration - Marjolin ulcer (SCC) consideration

## Cross-cutting themes

### Investigation approach

- **Pigmented lesion uncertainty**  -  dermoscopy if trained; otherwise photograph and review at 4 to 8 weeks, OR refer if features concerning. Excision biopsy (not shave) for any lesion you would not be happy not to see again.
- **Suspected fungal**  -  scrape for KOH or send for fungal culture before starting oral antifungals; ensure compliance for nail infections (3 months for fingernails, 6 months for toenails on terbinafine).
- **Suspected infection**  -  swab if discharge, blood cultures if systemically unwell; mark erythema with a pen and review in 24 to 48 hours.
- **Atopic eczema with frequent infection**  -  consider barrier dysfunction, contact triggers, and patch testing if persistent.

### Management principles in NZ primary care

- **Topical corticosteroids**  -  most underused class for eczema: appropriate potency for site (face/groin: hydrocortisone or mild; body: betamethasone valerate; palms/soles: clobetasol short course). Steroid phobia drives undertreatment; address explicitly.
- **Emollient and moisturiser quantity**  -  500g per week for whole-body eczema in an adult is normal; under-application is the most common cause of "treatment failure".
- **PHARMAC-funded options**  -  hydrocortisone, clobetasone, betamethasone valerate and dipropionate, mometasone, calcipotriol/betamethasone (Daivobet) for psoriasis, methotrexate, ciclosporin for severe disease. Biologics (adalimumab, secukinumab, ustekinumab) are funded with Special Authority for severe psoriasis. Always check current schedule.
- **Sun protection messaging**  -  the single most important intervention in NZ. SunSmart guidance: shade, clothing, hat, sunscreen, sunglasses, between September and April, especially 10am to 4pm. SPF 30+ broad-spectrum, applied generously (2mg/cm^2), reapplied every 2 hours and after swimming.
- **Patient education and adherence**  -  written action plans for chronic eczema and psoriasis improve outcomes. Most "treatment failures" are adherence-related, not pharmacology-related.
- **When to refer**  -  diagnostic uncertainty, severe or refractory disease, paediatric eczema with growth failure or recurrent infection, suspected skin cancer needing excision beyond your scope, suspected isotretinoin candidate, biologic candidate.
