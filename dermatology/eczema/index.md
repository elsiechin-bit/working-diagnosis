<div class="topic-header">
  <p class="pillar-label">Dermatology</p>
  <h1>Eczema / atopic dermatitis</h1>
  <p class="topic-dek">The most common inflammatory skin condition in primary care. The diagnosis is usually clinical; the management is mostly about emollients done properly and steroids used correctly.</p>
</div>

<div class="topic-with-aside">
<div class="topic-body">

<p>Atopic dermatitis affects around 15–20% of children and 2–5% of adults in New Zealand.<sup>1</sup> It is part of the atopic triad alongside asthma and allergic rhinitis, and family history is strongly predictive. The skin barrier dysfunction model — rather than a purely immunological one — has reshaped how we think about prevention and early treatment.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Clinical. Chronic or relapsing pruritic dermatitis with typical distribution (flexural in older children and adults; facial and extensor in infants) plus at least three of: personal or family history of atopy, dry skin, onset before age 2, visible flexural involvement.<sup>2</sup></p>

<h2>Stepwise management</h2>
<ul class="derm-feature-list">
  <li><strong>Step 1 — Emollients, always.</strong> Applied at least twice daily to all affected and unaffected skin. This is the foundation. Quantities matter — a child with moderate eczema may need 250 g per week.</li>
  <li><strong>Step 2 — Mild topical corticosteroid for flares.</strong> 1% hydrocortisone for face and flexures. Moderate potency (e.g. betamethasone valerate 0.02%) for body flares.</li>
  <li><strong>Step 3 — Moderate/potent TCS short courses.</strong> For persistent or severe flares, with clear instructions on duration and tapering.</li>
  <li><strong>Step 4 — Topical calcineurin inhibitors.</strong> Tacrolimus or pimecrolimus for facial or sensitive-site eczema where steroid side-effects are a concern.</li>
  <li><strong>Step 5 — Refer.</strong> Dupilumab (PHARMAC-funded with criteria) for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis not controlled by optimised topical therapy.<sup>3</sup></li>
</ul>

<h2>What to tell the patient</h2>
<p>Steroid phobia is a real barrier to treatment. Patients often under-use topical steroids from fear of skin thinning — reassure clearly. Emollient-first, steroid-second is the frame that helps.</p>
