<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Stepwise management: emollients always (Step 1), mild TCS for flares (Step 2), moderate/potent TCS short courses (Step 3), topical calcineurin inhibitors for face and flexures (Step 4), dupilumab referral for uncontrolled moderate-to-severe disease (Step 5).</li>
    <li>Emollient quantities matter. A child with moderate eczema needs around 250 g per week. Under-prescribing emollients is the most common reason treatment fails at Step 1.</li>
    <li>Steroid phobia is a real barrier. Patients frequently under-use topical corticosteroids from fear of skin thinning. Address it directly and early - the risks of under-treated eczema (infection, sleeplessness, school/work impact) outweigh the risks of appropriate TCS use.</li>
    <li>Dupilumab is PHARMAC-funded in NZ for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis with inadequate response to optimised topical therapy. Know the criteria before referring.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Atopic dermatitis affects around 15-20% of children and 2-5% of adults in New Zealand.<sup>1</sup> It is part of the atopic triad alongside asthma and allergic rhinitis, and family history is strongly predictive. The skin barrier dysfunction model - rather than a purely immunological one - has reshaped how we think about prevention and early treatment.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Diagnosis</p>
  <p>
    Clinical diagnosis. Chronic or relapsing pruritic dermatitis with typical distribution (flexural in older children and adults; facial and extensor in infants) plus at least three of: personal or family history of atopy, dry skin, onset before age 2, visible flexural involvement.<sup>2</sup>
  </p>
  <p>
    The distribution shifts with age: infants present with facial, scalp, and extensor surface involvement; from toddlerhood the classic flexural pattern (antecubital and popliteal fossae, wrists, ankles) predominates. In adults, the pattern is more variable and hand eczema is common. Seborrhoeic dermatitis, psoriasis, and contact dermatitis are the main differentials in adults.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Stepwise management</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Step 1 - Emollients, always.</strong> Applied at least twice daily to all affected and unaffected skin. This is the foundation of eczema management, not an adjunct. Quantities matter - a child with moderate eczema may need 250 g per week. Prescribe by weight, not tube size. Greaser preparations (ointments, thick creams) are more effective than lotions; the greasier the better for dry skin, though patient preference and season affect adherence.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Step 2 - Mild topical corticosteroid for flares.</strong> 1% hydrocortisone for face and flexures. Moderate potency (betamethasone valerate 0.02%) for body flares. Use the finger-tip unit (FTU) guide for dosing - one FTU covers approximately two adult palms.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Step 3 - Moderate or potent TCS short courses.</strong> For persistent or severe flares, with clear instructions on duration and tapering. Document the potency and site - strong TCS (e.g. betamethasone valerate 0.1%) should not be used on the face or groin. "Weekend therapy" - TCS two days per week on previously affected skin - reduces relapse frequency in patients with frequent flares.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Step 4 - Topical calcineurin inhibitors.</strong> Tacrolimus 0.03% (children) or 0.1% (adults) and pimecrolimus are steroid-sparing alternatives for facial or sensitive-site eczema where TCS side-effects are a concern. They are not associated with skin atrophy and are safe for periorbital use. Initial stinging on application usually settles after the first few days.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Step 5 - Refer for dupilumab assessment.</strong> Dupilumab (Dupixent) is a biologic targeting IL-4 and IL-13 signalling and is PHARMAC-funded in NZ for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis not adequately controlled by optimised topical therapy.<sup>3</sup> Refer to dermatology. Before referring, document that emollients have been consistently prescribed and used, and that TCS and/or calcineurin inhibitors have been trialled adequately.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Addressing steroid phobia</p>
  <p>
    Steroid phobia is a real barrier to effective treatment. Patients and parents frequently under-use topical corticosteroids from fear of skin thinning, systemic absorption, or addiction. The evidence base does not support these fears at the potencies and quantities used for eczema management - topical steroid atrophy occurs with prolonged use of potent steroids on thin skin, not with appropriate-potency short courses. Reassure directly: use the right strength, in the right place, for the right duration.
  </p>
  <p>
    The "emollient first, steroid second" framing helps. Emollient as the daily foundation; TCS as a targeted tool for flares. Avoid language that reinforces fear ("use this sparingly" without context, or "be careful with this") - it undermines the treatment plan without clinical justification.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Flare triggers, infection, and itch-scratch cycle</p>
  <p>
    Common triggers include soap and detergent contact (switch to soap substitutes for all washing, use non-biological washing powder), overheating and sweating, environmental allergens (house dust mite, pet dander), and emotional stress. Allergy testing is rarely useful in childhood eczema unless there is a clear history of food-triggered flares - routine IgE testing leads to over-diagnosis of food allergy and unnecessary dietary restriction.
  </p>
  <p>
    Secondary infection is common in eczema. Staphylococcus aureus colonises virtually all eczema skin; frank infection (weeping, crusting, rapidly worsening) warrants treatment. A short course of a topical antibiotic-steroid combination (e.g. betamethasone/fusidic acid) is appropriate for localised infection. Widespread infection or failure to respond requires systemic antibiotics - flucloxacillin or cefalexin. Recurrent infection may warrant MRSA swab.
  </p>
  <p>
    The itch-scratch cycle is central to eczema pathophysiology and chronicity. Scratching worsens barrier disruption, increases inflammation, and perpetuates the cycle. Antihistamines are not effective for itch in eczema - the itch is not histamine-mediated - but sedating antihistamines (e.g. promethazine) may help sleep in acutely flaring patients. Nail trimming, cotton mitts in infants, and keeping skin cool reduce nocturnal scratching.
  </p>
</div>
