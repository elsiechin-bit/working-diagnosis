<div class="topic-body">

<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>New Zealand has one of the highest melanoma rates in the world. Around 4,000 new cases are diagnosed annually; incidence is highest in fair-skinned New Zealanders of European descent over 50, but acral lentiginous melanoma (palms, soles, subungual) is disproportionately represented in Māori and Pacific peoples and is not UV-related.<sup>1</sup></li>
    <li><strong>If you cannot confidently exclude melanoma: excise it.</strong> Take a 2 mm margin for diagnostic excision and send for histology. Do not watch and wait.</li>
    <li>Urgent referral (within 2 weeks) to dermatology or plastic surgery for any lesion suspicious for melanoma that you are not excising yourself, any confirmed melanoma requiring wide local excision planning, or any lesion you are not confident to manage in primary care.</li>
    <li>ABCDE plus the ugly duckling sign are useful screens, but neither replaces dermoscopy or clinical gestalt in high-risk patients.</li>
    <li>Annual full-skin checks for patients with a personal or first-degree family history of melanoma, multiple atypical naevi, or significant cumulative UV exposure history.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Melanoma is New Zealand's most dangerous skin cancer and the country has one of the highest per-capita incidence rates in the world, driven by high ambient UV, outdoor culture, and a predominantly fair-skinned population. Non-melanoma skin cancer (basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma) is far more common but less likely to be fatal; both warrant prompt recognition and appropriate management in primary care.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Recognising melanoma: ABCDE and beyond</p>
  <p>The ABCDE criteria provide a structured approach to pigmented lesions:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Asymmetry:</strong> one half does not mirror the other</li>
    <li><strong>Border:</strong> irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges</li>
    <li><strong>Colour:</strong> variation within the lesion - shades of brown, black, red, white, or blue</li>
    <li><strong>Diameter:</strong> greater than 6 mm, though early melanomas can be smaller</li>
    <li><strong>Evolving:</strong> any change in size, shape, or colour, or a new symptom (bleeding, itching) over weeks to months</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The ugly duckling sign - a lesion that simply looks different from a patient's other naevi - has comparable sensitivity to ABCDE and is particularly useful in patients with many moles, where individual lesion characteristics may be less distinctive.<sup>2</sup>
  </p>
  <p>
    Dermoscopy significantly improves diagnostic accuracy and reduces unnecessary excisions in experienced hands. If you use it, specific features associated with melanoma include atypical pigment network, regression structures, blue-white veil, and atypical vascular patterns. If you do not use dermoscopy, a lower threshold for excision is appropriate.
  </p>
  <p>
    Subungual melanoma (dark streak under a nail) and acral lentiginous melanoma (irregular pigmentation on palms or soles) are less common but disproportionately affect Māori and Pacific peoples. They are often diagnosed later because UV-related aetiology framing means they are not considered in lower-risk skin phototypes. Any new or changing periungual or acral pigmented lesion warrants prompt assessment regardless of ethnicity.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Non-melanoma skin cancer: BCC and SCC</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)</strong> is the most common skin cancer. It rarely metastasises but causes significant local destruction if untreated. Classic presentation: pearly, rolled-edge nodule on sun-exposed skin, often with telangiectasia or central ulceration. Superficial BCCs may appear as a pink, scaly plaque. Low-risk BCCs (small, primary, nodular, well-defined on trunk or limbs) can be excised in primary care with 3-4 mm margins. High-risk BCCs (large, recurrent, morphoeic, periocular, perineural, or on face/scalp) should be referred to dermatology or plastic surgery.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)</strong> carries a small but real metastatic risk, particularly on the ear, lip, and in immunosuppressed patients. Presentation: firm, hyperkeratotic nodule or ulcer on sun-exposed skin, often arising on a background of actinic keratosis. All SCCs should be excised with adequate margins (minimum 4-6 mm for low-risk lesions); any SCC on high-risk sites or in immunosuppressed patients warrants specialist referral.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Actinic keratoses (AK)</strong> are UV-induced precursor lesions. Options for management: topical 5-fluorouracil (Efudix), imiquimod, ingenol mebutate, photodynamic therapy, or cryotherapy. Choice depends on lesion count, distribution, and patient preference. A single thickened or indurated AK should be biopsied or excised rather than treated topically, as it may represent SCC in situ or early invasive SCC.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Management decision: excise, refer, or monitor</p>
  <p>
    The default for any lesion you cannot confidently exclude as melanoma is excision with a 2 mm clinical margin for diagnostic histology. Do not perform incisional biopsy of a pigmented lesion suspected to be melanoma; excise the whole lesion. Do not shave off a pigmented lesion; thickness (Breslow depth) cannot be assessed on a shave biopsy and this significantly impairs staging and management planning.
  </p>
  <p>
    If histology confirms melanoma: wide local excision margins are determined by Breslow thickness. This planning should happen at specialist level. Refer urgently following any confirmed melanoma diagnosis.
  </p>
  <p>
    For low-risk benign-appearing naevi in a patient without risk factors: photograph and review in 3-6 months, or use dermoscopy to confirm benign features. Any uncertainty tips the decision toward excision.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Prevention and surveillance</p>
  <p>
    Sun protection counselling should be part of routine primary care for at-risk patients, not reserved for dermatology visits. Key messages: broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen applied generously and reapplied every 2 hours in sun; UV-protective clothing and wide-brimmed hat; avoid deliberate tanning including solarium use; seek shade between 10 am and 4 pm in summer.
  </p>
  <p>
    Annual full-skin examination is recommended for patients with: personal history of melanoma; first-degree family history of melanoma; five or more atypical naevi; prior BCC or SCC; history of significant UV exposure (outdoor occupation, solarium use, blistering sunburns in childhood). Document lesions photographically where possible; a baseline photograph is often the most useful comparator at follow-up.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral, staging, and specialist management</p>
  <p>
    Refer urgently (within 2 weeks) to dermatology or plastic surgery for: any lesion suspicious for melanoma that you are not excising in primary care; any confirmed melanoma for wide local excision planning and sentinel lymph node biopsy discussion; high-risk BCC or SCC (see above); and any pigmented lesion in a patient with familial atypical mole and melanoma (FAM-M) syndrome or a strong family history.
  </p>
  <p>
    Following confirmed melanoma diagnosis, staging is determined by Breslow thickness, ulceration, mitotic rate, and lymph node involvement. Wide local excision margins are: &lt;1 mm Breslow depth - 1 cm margin; 1-2 mm - 1-2 cm; &gt;2 mm - 2 cm. Sentinel lymph node biopsy is offered for melanomas &gt;1 mm or with adverse features. Adjuvant immunotherapy (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) and targeted therapy (BRAF/MEK inhibitors for BRAF-mutant disease) are available in NZ for high-risk resected and metastatic disease; these decisions are made at multidisciplinary level.
  </p>
  <p>
    Patients with confirmed melanoma should be enrolled in a structured surveillance programme. The NZ Cancer Society and regional melanoma follow-up guidelines specify frequency of skin checks and imaging by stage. The GP role in ongoing surveillance is to perform annual skin checks, monitor for locoregional recurrence, and coordinate with the specialist team.
  </p>
</div>
