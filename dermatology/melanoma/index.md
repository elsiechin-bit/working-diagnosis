<p>Melanoma accounts for approximately 4,000 new diagnoses per year in New Zealand, with incidence highest in fair-skinned New Zealanders of European descent and in those over 50.<sup>1</sup> Non-melanoma skin cancer (basal cell carcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma) is far more common but rarely reported in cancer registries, making true incidence estimates difficult.</p>

<h2>The ABCDE rule</h2>
<p>A useful screen for concerning pigmented lesions  -  but not a substitute for dermoscopy or clinical gestalt in high-risk patients.</p>

<ul class="derm-feature-list">
  <li><strong>A  -  Asymmetry.</strong> One half doesn't mirror the other.</li>
  <li><strong>B  -  Border.</strong> Irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges.</li>
  <li><strong>C  -  Colour.</strong> Variation  -  shades of brown, black, red, white, or blue within the same lesion.</li>
  <li><strong>D  -  Diameter.</strong> Larger than 6 mm (roughly the diameter of a pencil eraser), though early melanomas can be smaller.</li>
  <li><strong>E  -  Evolving.</strong> Any change in size, shape, colour, or new symptom (bleeding, itching) over weeks to months.</li>
</ul>

<p>The "ugly duckling" sign  -  a lesion that simply looks different from a patient's other naevi  -  has comparable sensitivity to ABCDE and is worth keeping in mind for patients with many moles.<sup>2</sup></p>

<h2>When to refer</h2>
<p>Urgent (within 2 weeks) referral to dermatology or plastic surgery is appropriate for any lesion you cannot confidently exclude as melanoma. If in doubt, excise with a 2 mm margin for histology rather than watch and wait.</p>

<h2>What to tell the patient</h2>
<p>Annual full-skin checks for anyone with a personal or first-degree family history of melanoma. Broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen, UV-protective clothing, and avoiding tanning  -  not just burn avoidance.</p>
