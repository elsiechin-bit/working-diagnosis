## Topic body

### Definition

Severe asthma is defined by one of two criteria:

- **Difficult-to-treat asthma**: asthma requiring high-dose ICS/LABA therapy to maintain control or remaining uncontrolled despite such therapy
- **Oral corticosteroid-dependent asthma**: asthma requiring maintenance oral corticosteroid therapy (≥4 mg prednisolone daily or equivalent) to prevent exacerbations

Severe asthma encompasses both rare eosinophilic or immune-driven asthma (potentially responsive to biologics) and treatment-resistant disease driven by poor adherence, incorrect technique, comorbidities, or persistent trigger exposure.

### Prevalence and phenotypes

Severe asthma accounts for 5–10% of the asthma population but drives approximately 50% of asthma resource use. Phenotypes include:

- **Eosinophilic asthma**: elevated sputum/blood eosinophils (≥300 cells/μL or ≥150 cells/μL in some phenotypes), responsive to ICS and biologics
- **Allergic asthma**: IgE-mediated, triggered by specific allergens, potentially responsive to anti-IgE monoclonal antibody
- **Neutrophilic asthma**: elevated sputum neutrophils, often corticosteroid-resistant, less specific biologic targets
- **Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD)**: acute exacerbations after NSAIDs or aspirin, often with chronic rhinosinusitis and nasal polyps
- **Asthma with obesity**: mechanical airflow limitation, low-grade airway inflammation, metabolic factors

### Diagnosis and initial assessment

**Step 1: Confirm asthma diagnosis**
- Spirometry showing reversibility (FEV₁ improvement ≥12% and ≥200 mL post-bronchodilator) or positive bronchial hyperresponsiveness challenge
- Exclude alternative diagnoses (VCD, COPD overlap, ILD, cardiac causes)

**Step 2: Assess control and current therapy**

ACQ-7 score (Asthma Control Questionnaire) ≥1.5 indicates uncontrolled disease. Assess:
- Daytime symptoms (daily use of SABA suggests inadequate control)
- Night-time awakening frequency
- Activity limitation
- Exacerbation frequency (≥2/year in past 12 months)
- Oral corticosteroid requirement

**Step 3: Adherence and inhaler technique assessment**

Poor control often reflects poor adherence (30–40% of "severe" asthma) or incorrect inhaler technique. Assess:
- Prescription refill data (pharmacy claims) to estimate adherence
- Observed inhaler technique with patient demonstrating use
- Device appropriateness (pMDI + spacer vs DPI for patient's coordination and strength)
- Barriers to adherence (cost, complexity, belief in need, forgetfulness)

Correct technique can transform apparent "severe" asthma into controlled disease.

**Step 4: Investigate reversible contributing factors**

Before escalating to specialist care, exclude or treat:
- **Active smoking or vaping**: major driver of poor response; strongly advise cessation
- **Rhinosinusitis or nasal polyps**: can perpetuate airway inflammation; treat with nasal ICS
- **Gastro-oesophageal reflux**: acid aspiration worsens asthma; trial of PPI
- **Obesity**: weight loss improves asthma control in many
- **Allergen exposure**: dust mite, pet dander, occupational triggers; environmental controls
- **Inadequate ICS dose or formulation**: consider changing ICS type or device
- **Comorbid anxiety or depression**: psychological support may improve adherence and perception of symptoms

**Step 5: Phenotyping for biologic therapy**

If asthma remains uncontrolled despite high-dose ICS/LABA and adherence is documented as good, arrange:
- **Blood eosinophil count** (≥300 cells/μL or ≥150 cells/μL in some phenotypes suggests responsiveness to anti-IL-5 biologics)
- **Serum IgE** (elevated total or specific IgE to allergen suggests anti-IgE therapy candidacy)
- **Sputum or exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO)** (if available; FeNO ≥25 ppb suggests eosinophilic inflammation)
- **Allergy testing** (skin prick test or specific IgE) to guide environmental avoidance or immunotherapy
- **Consideration of AERD phenotype**: history of rhinosinusitis, nasal polyps, reactions to NSAIDs

### Current therapy escalation: before biologic referral

**High-dose ICS/LABA combination**
- Fluticasone/salmeterol (500/50 μg BD) or equivalent
- Budesonide/formoterol (320/9 μg BD or 160/4.5 μg BD) with faster onset of action

**Triple therapy (add one of)**
- **LAMA** (long-acting muscarinic antagonist, e.g. tiotropium 18 μg daily): anticholinergic, reduces exacerbations particularly in smokers or COPD overlap
- **LTRA** (leukotriene receptor antagonist, e.g. montelukast 10 mg nocte): useful in allergic asthma, AERD, or aspirin sensitivity
- **Slow-release theophylline** (rarely used now due to narrow therapeutic window; consider only if biologic unavailable)

Ensure high-dose ICS/LABA prescribed BD (not once-daily), with documented adherence via pharmacy refill records and direct patient observation of technique over 2–4 weeks before deeming therapy "failed".

### Severe asthma phenotypes and biologic therapies

Referral to respiratory specialist is indicated for asthma uncontrolled on high-dose ICS/LABA ± LAMA/LTRA. Specialist may offer:

**Anti-IL-5 monoclonal antibody (mepolizumab)**
- Indication: blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL, eosinophilic asthma phenotype, or eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA)
- Effect: reduces exacerbations and oral corticosteroid requirement by 50–90%
- Dosing: subcutaneous injection monthly or IV quarterly

**Anti-IL-5 receptor antibody (reslizumab)**
- Similar to mepolizumab; alternative if mepolizumab ineffective or poorly tolerated
- IV infusion 3-monthly

**Anti-IgE monoclonal antibody (omalizumab)**
- Indication: allergic asthma with total IgE 30–700 IU/mL (dose depends on baseline IgE)
- Effect: binds circulating IgE, reduces mast cell degranulation, exacerbation reduction 25–50%
- Dosing: subcutaneous injection 2–4 weekly

**Anti-IL-4Rα (dupilumab)**
- Indication: moderate-to-severe allergic or eosinophilic asthma
- Effect: blocks IL-4 receptor, reduces Th2 inflammation, improves lung function
- Dosing: subcutaneous injection 2–4 weekly
- Benefit: also improves atopic dermatitis and allergic rhinitis if coexistent

**Anti-TSLP (tezepelumab)**
- Indication: moderate-to-severe asthma uncontrolled on high-dose ICS/LABA; benefits both allergic and non-allergic phenotypes
- Effect: upstream Th2 pathway inhibition; exacerbation reduction ~60%
- Dosing: subcutaneous injection 4-weekly

**JAK inhibitor (abrocitinib or baricitinib)**
- Emerging role in severe asthma; blocks Janus kinase signalling
- Indication: eosinophilic or non-eosinophilic severe asthma
- Effect: oral therapy, potential for improved adherence vs biologics

Specialist will determine biologic choice based on phenotype, comorbidities (atopy, eczema, rhinitis), and local funding criteria.

### Acute exacerbations in severe asthma

**Recognition of life-threatening attack**
- Inability to complete sentences, silent chest, confusion, altered mental state, SpO₂ <92%, peak flow <33% predicted, bradycardia
- **Action**: immediate hospital admission; do not manage in primary care

**Management of non-life-threatening exacerbation (can be managed at home if early, mild-to-moderate)**
- **Reliever**: salbutamol 2 puffs via spacer every 20 minutes × 3, then every 1–4 hours as needed
- **Oral corticosteroid**: prednisolone 40–50 mg as single dose; continue 40 mg daily for 5–7 days (no taper needed)
- **Ipratropium**: 40 μg (1 unit dose) BD via spacer if poor initial response

**Red flags for hospital referral during home management**
- No improvement in peak flow or symptoms within 1–2 hours
- SpO₂ persistently <94% on room air
- Unable to maintain hydration or take medications
- Carer unable to monitor closely
- Patient anxiety or inability to self-manage

**After exacerbation review**
- Determine trigger (poor adherence, infection, allergen exposure, medication change)
- Review and reinforce inhaler technique
- Consider stepping up baseline therapy (increase ICS dose, add third agent, or plan biologic referral)
- Provide self-management plan and urgent access to healthcare

### Oral corticosteroid-dependent asthma

Some patients with severe asthma require maintenance oral corticosteroid therapy (≥4 mg prednisolone daily or equivalent) to prevent exacerbations. Management:

- **Lowest effective dose**: titrate down monthly in 1–2 mg decrements while monitoring for exacerbations
- **Bone protection**: offer calcium 1000 mg daily + vitamin D 800 IU daily; consider DEXA scan if ≥3 months on ≥7.5 mg daily
- **Infection prevention**: annual influenza vaccine, 23-valent pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine (PPSV23) followed by 13-valent pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (PCV13) if not previously given
- **Gastric protection**: PPI (omeprazole 20 mg daily) if additional risk factors (age >65, NSAID use, prior peptic ulcer disease)
- **Specialist biologic therapy**: urgent priority given corticosteroid burden; most biologics reduce or eliminate oral corticosteroid need

### Special populations and comorbidities

**Asthma-COPD overlap (ACO)**
- Features of both COPD (fixed airflow obstruction, smoking history) and asthma (history of asthma, atopy)
- Management: high-dose ICS/LABA essential; LAMA added for triple therapy
- Survival improved with ICS in ACO; avoid LABA or LAMA monotherapy

**Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD)**
- Triad of asthma, chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps, reactions to NSAIDs/aspirin
- Management: avoid NSAIDs; use paracetamol or paracetamol + codeine instead
- Trial of aspirin desensitisation (specialist procedure) can allow NSAID use in selected patients
- LTRA (montelukast) may offer modest benefit; leukotriene pathway often overactive in AERD

**Occupational asthma**
- New onset or worsening asthma temporally related to workplace exposure
- Diagnosis: workplace-related symptom diary, peak flow monitoring, immunological or challenge testing
- Management: eliminate exposure if possible; if not, arrange respiratory specialist assessment
- Occupational rehabilitation specialist input if exposure cannot be avoided

**Pregnancy and severe asthma**
- Asthma severity unpredictable in pregnancy (improve, worsen, or remain stable)
- **Continue high-dose ICS**: risk of asthma exacerbation and poor fetal oxygenation far exceeds ICS risk
- **Biologics**: limited pregnancy data for most; mepolizumab and omalizumab have more safety data; defer new biologic initiation until third trimester if possible
- **LTRA**: montelukast is safe and can be continued
- **Exacerbations**: treat as non-pregnant asthma; oral corticosteroids safe

### Monitoring and follow-up

**After biologic initiation or escalation**
- Assess control at 4 weeks (early improvement expected)
- Full response evaluation at 12 weeks; if inadequate response, consider switch to alternative biologic or re-assess diagnosis
- Then 3–6 monthly follow-up; can extend to 6–12 monthly once stable

**Objective measures of control**
- Exacerbation frequency (target: zero exacerbations/year on treatment)
- Oral corticosteroid requirement (target: discontinuation or minimisation)
- Lung function (FEV₁, target: ≥80% predicted if asthma control achieved)
- ACQ-7 score <1.5 indicates adequate control

**Annual review**
- Spirometry to document baseline and monitor for decline
- Blood eosinophil count (if on anti-IL-5 biologic, confirm target eosinophil reduction)
- Inhaler technique reassessment
- Medication adherence check (pharmacy refill data)
- Vaccination status (annual flu, pneumococcal boosters if on oral corticosteroids)

### Red flags and urgent referral

- **Life-threatening exacerbation** (altered mental state, silent chest, peak flow <33% predicted): emergency department
- **Uncontrolled asthma despite high-dose therapy and confirmed good adherence**: urgent respiratory specialist
- **Oral corticosteroid requirement developing**: urgent specialist referral for biologic assessment and bone protection
- **Sudden deterioration in previously well-controlled asthma**: review for acute trigger (infection, allergen, medication change, anaphylaxis); consider alternative diagnosis (PE, pneumothorax, VCD)
