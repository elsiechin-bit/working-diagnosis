## Topic body

### Definition and pathophysiology

Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) is characterised by repetitive collapse of the upper airway during sleep, causing partial (hypopnoea) or complete (apnoea) cessation of airflow. Airway collapse is driven by:

- **Loss of muscle tone**: decreased pharyngeal dilator muscle activity during sleep
- **Anatomical obstruction**: narrow pharynx, large soft palate, large tonsils, retrognathia (small lower jaw), obesity (reduced oropharyngeal space due to fat deposition)
- **Sleep stage**: most events occur in REM sleep when muscle atonia is greatest

Each event lasts 10–120 seconds, terminating with arousal (lightening of sleep) and often gasping for breath. Repeated arousals fragment sleep architecture, reducing restorative deep sleep and REM sleep, causing daytime somnolence.

### Epidemiology and risk factors

**Prevalence**
- Affects 4–10% of adults; higher in men and with advancing age
- Mild OSA (AHI 5–15) very common; only 5–10% with AHI ≥15 report symptoms

**Risk factors**
- **Age**: increases with advancing age (peaks age 50–60, then plateaus)
- **Sex**: male predominance 2:1 (androgens increase pharyngeal collapsibility); gender difference narrows post-menopause
- **Obesity**: BMI >30 kg/m² increases risk 10-fold; each 10% weight gain increases AHI ~25–30%
- **Anatomical factors**: retrognathia, micrognathia, macroglossia, large soft palate, large tonsils, high arched palate
- **Nasal obstruction**: chronic rhinitis, septal deviation, nasal polyps
- **Medications**: sedatives, opioids, muscle relaxants (worsen airway collapse by reducing pharyngeal tone)
- **Alcohol and smoking**: acute alcohol use worsens events; smoking increases airway inflammation and oedema
- **Hypothyroidism and acromegaly**: metabolic/endocrine conditions with airway narrowing
- **Family history**: genetic predisposition (familial clustering common)

### Clinical presentation

**Classic triad**
- **Witnessed apnoeas**: partner reports breathing pauses, gasping, or snorting
- **Loud snoring**: present in ~85% of OSA; may be absent in mild or sleeping alone
- **Daytime somnolence**: sleepiness despite long sleep duration; difficulty maintaining alertness, inadvertent napping

**Other symptoms**
- **Sleep maintenance insomnia**: frequent night-time awakenings, restless sleep
- **Morning symptoms**: dry mouth, sore throat, headache (post-hypercapnic, present in ~20%)
- **Nocturia**: arousals trigger urine production; 2–3× nightly micturition common
- **Mood and cognition**: irritability, depression, poor concentration, memory impairment, decreased libido
- **Hypertension**: resistant to medication (present in 50–80% OSA)
- **Nocturnal angina or dyspnoea**: chest pain or breathlessness during apnoeas (indicates severe OSA with hypoxaemia)

**Severity classifications**
- **Mild**: AHI 5–15; often asymptomatic or minimal symptoms
- **Moderate**: AHI 15–30; variable symptoms (sleepiness not always present)
- **Severe**: AHI >30; usually symptomatic daytime somnolence, significant hypoxaemia (SpO₂ dips ≥4% per event)

### Diagnosis: clinical assessment and sleep study

**Clinical suspicion**
- Ask all patients with hypertension, diabetes, obesity, or cardiac disease about snoring, witnessed apnoeas, daytime sleepiness
- Use **STOP-BANG questionnaire** as screening tool (positive if ≥3/8 items predicts OSA):
  - **S**noring (loud)
  - **T**ired during day
  - **O**bserved apnoeas
  - **B**lood pressure (elevated)
  - **B**MI >35 kg/m²
  - **A**ge >50 years
  - **N**eck circumference >40 cm (men) or >35 cm (women)
  - **G**ender (male)

**Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS)**
- Patient-rated daytime somnolence; score 0–24
- ≥11 indicates excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS)
- Not diagnostic alone; some OSA patients have low ESS; some sleepiness from other causes (depression, narcolepsy, insufficient sleep)

**Physical examination**
- **Oropharynx**: Mallampati score (soft palate visibility; grade III–IV suggests narrowing), tonsillar enlargement, posterior pharyngeal wall redundancy
- **Nasal cavity**: septal deviation, polyps, turbinate hypertrophy (may contribute to obstruction)
- **Neck**: circumference, goitre (thyroid enlargement)
- **Chin/jaw**: micrognathia, retrognathia (profile assessment)
- **BMI**: weight and height; abdominal obesity (waist circumference)
- **Vital signs**: blood pressure elevation common

**Sleep study**
- **Essential for diagnosis**: OSA diagnosis cannot be made clinically; testing required to confirm AHI and severity
- **Home sleep apnoea test (polygraphy)**: 
  - Measures airflow, respiratory effort, SpO₂, heart rate
  - Doesn't record EEG (sleep staging); approximates AHI by counting events over total sleep time
  - Acceptable for moderate-to-high suspicion; cannot rule out OSA if negative
  - Advantages: convenience, cost (~NZ$400–600), faster turnaround
  - Limitations: cannot detect arousals accurately; may miss central apnoeas
- **Laboratory polysomnography (PSG)**:
  - Gold standard; includes EEG, EMG (muscle tone), eye movement, airflow, respiratory effort, SpO₂, heart rate
  - Accurately classifies apnoea type (obstructive vs central), sleep stages, arousals
  - Indicated: central apnoea suspected, positive home test but symptoms persist, complex sleep-related hypoventilation, post-treatment titration failure
  - Disadvantages: cost, time to appointment, not needed for straightforward OSA

**Test interpretation**
- **Apnoea**: complete breathing cessation ≥10 seconds
- **Hypopnoea**: partial airflow reduction ≥30% + oxygen desaturation ≥3% OR arousal
- **Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI)**: number of events per hour of sleep
- **Oxygen desaturation index (ODI)**: oxygen drops ≥3% per hour; reflects hypoxaemic burden
- **Central apnoeas**: respiratory effort absent (contrast obstructive where effort increases despite airway closure)

### Differential diagnosis and mimics

**Sleep-related hypoventilation**
- Shallow breathing during sleep without discrete apnoeas
- Associated with obesity-hypoventilation syndrome (OHS), COPD, neuromuscular disease
- Requires PSG with CO₂ monitoring to diagnose

**Central sleep apnoea (CSA)**
- Apnoeas with absent respiratory effort (no arousal of breathing muscles)
- Associated with heart failure, opioids, altitude, neurological disease
- Treatment differs (address underlying cause; avoid CPAP alone; may need supplemental oxygen or ASV if idiopathic)

**Sleep-related hypoventilation/hypoxaemia in COPD**
- COPD patients may have nocturnal desaturation without apnoeas
- Treat with supplemental oxygen; CPAP not indicated if no airway obstruction

**Narcolepsy**
- Excessive daytime somnolence + cataplexy (sudden muscle tone loss), sleep paralysis, hypnagogic hallucinations
- Sleep study shows short sleep latency to REM, REM episodes within 15 min of sleep onset
- OSA can coexist with narcolepsy; both need treatment

**Periodic breathing (Cheyne-Stokes respiration)**
- Crescendo-decrescendo pattern; common in heart failure and high altitude
- Treated differently from OSA (address heart failure, avoid CPAP if purely CSA)

### Management of obstructive sleep apnoea

**Lifestyle modifications**
- **Weight loss**: each 10% weight loss decreases AHI ~25–30%; aim for BMI <30 kg/m²
- **Sleep position**: lateral sleeping reduces AHI by ~50% in positional OSA (events only supine); positional devices, tennis ball in back of shirt, or mattress vibration alarm
- **Avoid sedatives and alcohol**: sedating drugs (benzodiazepines, opioids, antihistamines) relax airway muscles; alcohol ~4 hours before bed worsens apnoeas
- **Nasal hygiene**: treat nasal obstruction (rhinitis, polyps, septal deviation) to improve CPAP tolerance
- **Sleep hygiene**: regular sleep schedule, 7–9 hours nightly, avoid sleep deprivation (worsens airway collapse)

**CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)**
- **Mechanism**: positive pressure splints pharynx open, preventing collapse
- **First-line treatment**: effective for all OSA severity; improves daytime somnolence within 1–2 weeks
- **Efficacy**: resolves apnoeas in >95% if tolerated; improves blood pressure (~5–10 mmHg reduction), cardiovascular outcomes
- **Indications**:
  - AHI ≥15 (regardless of symptoms) OR
  - AHI ≥5 with symptoms (daytime somnolence, fatigue, insomnia, witnessed apnoeas)
- **Pressure titration**: started at lower pressure (4–5 cmH₂O), increased gradually to optimal pressure (usually 7–14 cmH₂O); titration by technician or auto-CPAP
- **Auto-CPAP**: machine adjusts pressure breath-by-breath to maintain airway patency; reduces side effects vs fixed CPAP
- **Adherence**: 50–80% of patients use CPAP regularly; most improve symptoms dramatically within 1–2 weeks (positive reinforcement); poor initial experience (discomfort, nasal irritation) main reason for dropout
- **Pressure-related side effects**:
  - **Nasal congestion, dryness, epistaxis**: use heated humidifier, saline irrigation, decongestants
  - **Claustrophobia**: gradual acclimatisation, mask selection (oronasal vs nasal), telemonitoring support
  - **Mask-related skin irritation**: mask fit assessment, different mask styles, hydrocolloid cushions

**BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure)**
- Lower pressure during exhalation; more comfortable for some; used if CPAP intolerant
- Efficacy equivalent to CPAP if adequate pressure achieved
- Slower onset of airway collapse prevention; not ideal for REM sleep dominant apnoea

**Oral appliances (mandibular advancement devices)**
- **Mechanism**: forward mandibular displacement increases oropharyngeal space
- **Efficacy**: reduces AHI by ~50% on average; less effective than CPAP but superior to no treatment
- **Indications**: mild-moderate OSA or CPAP intolerance/refusal
- **Advantages**: portable, smaller, quieter than CPAP; better travel compliance
- **Side effects**: jaw and tooth discomfort, dental caries, TMJ dysfunction; requires ongoing dental monitoring
- **Prescription**: fitted by dentist experienced in sleep medicine; multiple visits for titration; follow-up dental checks annually

**Positional therapy**
- **Indicated**: purely positional OSA (AHI supine ≥2× AHI non-supine)
- **Methods**: positional devices (vibration alarm, zoning mattress), positional ball/vest (tennis ball sewn into back pocket), sleeping on side
- **Efficacy**: reduces AHI 50–90% in positional disease; does not eliminate if non-supine events present
- **Limitations**: difficult long-term compliance; single night on back can restore AHI

**Surgical options** (specialist referral)
- **Indications**: OSA with specific anatomical obstruction AND failure or intolerance of CPAP/oral appliance
- **Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UVPP)**: removes excess soft tissue; success rate ~50%; risk of postoperative nasopharyngeal stenosis
- **Maxillomandibular advancement (MMA)**: surgical jaw advancement; highly effective (~75%) for moderate-severe OSA; major surgery (requires orthodontia, orthognathic surgery input)
- **Tongue-based procedures**: genioglossus advancement, hyoid myotomy; newer, less invasive; success rate variable
- **Nasal surgery**: septoplasty, turbinate reduction for coexisting nasal obstruction
- **Bariatric surgery**: considered in severe obesity (BMI >40) with multiple comorbidities; weight loss from bariatric surgery often improves OSA dramatically

**Pharmacological options** (limited evidence)
- **Modafinil** or **armodafinil**: wakefulness-promoting agents; improve daytime somnolence but do NOT treat underlying apnoea; used as adjunct only if CPAP-treated OSA still symptomatic
- **No medication treats OSA directly** (unlike CPAP/oral appliance); caution with sedating drugs (worsen apnoeas)

### Management of specific complications

**Resistant hypertension (BP target unmet despite 3+ agents)**
- Screen for OSA; treat with CPAP
- Blood pressure improvement occurs within weeks of CPAP use in ~50% with OSA hypertension

**Atrial fibrillation (AF)**
- OSA increases AF risk 4-fold; intermittent hypoxaemia triggers arrhythmias
- Treat OSA with CPAP; improves AF control and reduces AF recurrence post-ablation

**Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF)**
- Both OSA and central apnoea common; CSA associated with poor prognosis
- If OSA: CPAP improves ejection fraction
- If CSA: avoid CPAP alone; address heart failure (ACE inhibitor, beta-blocker); may need adaptive servo-ventilation (ASV) if inadequate response

**Pulmonary hypertension**
- Severe OSA (AHI >50) with significant nocturnal hypoxaemia can cause pulmonary hypertension
- CPAP reduces PA pressure if PH is OSA-related
- Screen with echocardiography if symptoms suggest PH

**Driving safety**
- **Legal**: in NZ, driving with untreated OSA is unsafe; notify NZTA if diagnosed
- **Daytime somnolence substantially increases accident risk**: 2–7× increased risk
- **CPAP treatment reduces accident risk** back to baseline within 2–4 weeks of effective use
- **Advice**: patients with daytime somnolence should not drive until treated; inform of road safety obligation

### Special populations

**OSA in pregnancy**
- Pregnancy increases OSA risk (upper airway oedema, weight gain, progesterone effects on central drive)
- Treatment: positional therapy, oxygen if hypoxaemia, CPAP if indicated (safe; no fetal risk)
- Avoid sedating drugs; monitor fetal growth if recurrent hypoxaemia

**OSA with COPD overlap**
- COPD patients with coexisting OSA have worse outcomes; management complex
- CPAP may worsen CO₂ retention in some; close monitoring needed; may require bilevel ventilation or supplemental oxygen
- Refer to respiratory/sleep specialist for coordination

**Residual sleepiness on CPAP**
- Despite good AHI control (AHI <5 on CPAP); occurs in ~10% on CPAP
- Causes: inadequate CPAP pressure, sleep fragmentation from other causes (periodic breathing, arousals from other sources), coexisting narcolepsy/idiopathic hypersomnia
- Manage: repeat sleep study on CPAP, increase pressure if needed, trial modafinil if CPAP optimised

### Monitoring and follow-up

**After diagnosis and treatment initiation**
- **1 month**: assess symptom improvement, CPAP tolerance, mask fit, pressure adequacy
- **3 months**: repeat symptoms (ESS score should improve), assess adherence via machine download (>4 hours nightly = effective), check BP
- **Annual**: objective AHI confirmation (repeat home study or PSG if clinical change), adherence monitoring, comorbidity management

**CPAP adherence metrics**
- **Adequate use**: ≥4 hours nightly, ≥5 nights/week
- **Residual AHI**: should be <5/hour on CPAP
- **Leak rate**: excessive leaks reduce efficacy; mask adjustment needed

**Reassessment indications**
- Recurrence of daytime somnolence despite CPAP use: check adherence, pressure adequacy, new obstruction (septal deviation worsening, nasal polyp development)
- Weight gain >10 kg: reassess severity; may need CPAP pressure increase
- Change in symptoms or cardiac events: repeat testing
