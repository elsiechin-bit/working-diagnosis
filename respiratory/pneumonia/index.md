<div class="panic-tile dont-miss">
<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>Pneumonia presents with cough, fever, and focal lung signs. Confirm with chest X-ray (consolidation) or clinical diagnosis if CXR unavailable. Use CURB-65 score to assess severity and admit decision. Community-acquired pneumonia in immunocompetent patients: beta-lactam ± macrolide. Severe or atypical: broad-spectrum coverage. Hypoxia (SpO₂ <90%), respiratory distress, or sepsis: admit urgently.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Take respiratory history (cough duration, sputum production, fever, dyspnoea, pleurisy). Examine for focal crackles, consolidation, or pleural rub. Measure SpO₂, RR, HR, BP. Calculate CURB-65 (confusion, urea >7, RR ≥30, SBP <90, age ≥65). Mild (0–1): oral amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily or azithromycin 500 mg daily for 7 days. Moderate (2): consider admission or close follow-up. Severe (≥3): admit for IV therapy.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>CURB-65 score ≥2 (admit for IV therapy); SpO₂ <90% or respiratory distress; confusion or altered consciousness; signs of sepsis (fever, tachycardia, hypotension); severe comorbidities (heart failure, severe COPD, immunosuppression); failure to improve after 48 hours of oral antibiotics; or suspected complications (empyema, abscess).</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile tell-patient">
<h3>Tell the patient</h3>
<p>"You have an infection in your lungs causing inflammation. Antibiotics will help your body fight the infection. Most people recover at home with treatment, but we'll monitor you closely. Return immediately if you develop difficulty breathing or chest pain."</p>
</div>

## Diagnosis

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is acute infection of the lung parenchyma presenting with respiratory symptoms and clinical or radiological evidence of consolidation. Diagnosis is clinical; chest X-ray confirms but should not delay treatment if clinical suspicion is high.

**Typical presentation:**
- Cough (productive or dry)
- Fever or hypothermia (in older patients)
- Dyspnoea or tachypnoea
- Pleuritic chest pain
- Focal crackles or consolidation on auscultation

**Atypical presentations:**
- Older or immunocompromised patients may present with few respiratory symptoms; confusion, falls, or functional decline may predominate
- Atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia) may cause more gradual onset with systemic symptoms

## Risk factors for severe disease

- Age >65 years
- Chronic lung disease (COPD, asthma, ILD)
- Chronic heart disease
- Diabetes
- Immunosuppression (HIV, chemotherapy, long-term steroids)
- Smoking
- Alcohol dependence
- Aspiration risk (swallowing disorders, unconsciousness)

## Assessment

**History:** duration of cough, sputum colour and volume, fever, dyspnoea, pleurisy, recent antibiotics, vaccination status (pneumococcal, influenza).

**Examination:** respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, pulse, blood pressure, temperature. Auscultate for crackles, wheezes, or focal signs.

**Investigations:**
- Chest X-ray (consolidation, infiltrates, pleural effusion). Not essential if clinical diagnosis clear and low severity, but confirm diagnosis if possible.
- Full blood count: may show leukocytosis
- Urea and electrolytes: urea >7 mmol/L is part of CURB-65
- Blood cultures: optional in primary care but helpful if hospitalisation likely

## CURB-65 severity score

Calculate on first assessment (one point each):
- **C**: new Confusion (AMTS ≤8)
- **U**: Urea >7 mmol/L
- **R**: Respiratory rate ≥30
- **B**: systolic Blood pressure <90 or diastolic <60
- **Age**: ≥65 years

**Interpret:**
- **0–1 points (low risk):** mortality <1%; likely safe for home management
- **2 points (intermediate risk):** mortality 2–3%; consider hospitalisation or very close follow-up with safe discharge criteria
- **≥3 points (high risk):** mortality >10%; admit for inpatient care

## Microbiology and antibiotic choice

**Typical organisms:** Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis, viral (influenza, RSV).

**Atypical organisms:** Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella.

**Mild-to-moderate CAP (CURB-65 0–1):**
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg twice daily for 7 days, OR
- Azithromycin 500 mg daily (if penicillin allergy or atypicals suspected)
- Dual therapy (beta-lactam + macrolide) if atypical features or severe comorbidities

**Severe CAP (CURB-65 ≥2) or hospital-acquired pneumonia:**
- IV ceftriaxone 1 g twice daily + IV azithromycin 500 mg daily, or
- IV amoxicillin-clavulanate 1.2 g three times daily + azithromycin

**Atypical pneumonia (suspicion of Mycoplasma or Chlamydia):**
- Azithromycin or doxycycline (avoid in pregnancy and children <12 years)

## Management

**Home management (low severity, reliable patient, safe discharge):**
- Oral antibiotics as above
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and malaise
- Encourage fluids and rest
- Advise on symptoms requiring urgent review (worsening dyspnoea, chest pain, confusion, falling SpO₂)

**Admission (moderate to high severity or complications):**
- Oxygen to maintain SpO₂ >90%
- IV antibiotics
- IV fluids
- Investigations as above plus blood cultures, sputum culture if productive
- Repeat CXR after 6 weeks if pneumonia was severe (to rule out underlying malignancy or other pathology)

## Complications

- Empyema (fever and pleurisy despite antibiotics; requires drainage)
- Lung abscess (cavitation on imaging; treat with prolonged antibiotics ± drainage)
- Sepsis (hypotension, altered mental status, organ dysfunction)
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)

## Response to treatment

Most patients improve within 48–72 hours of appropriate antibiotics. If no improvement or deterioration:
- Reassess diagnosis (consider alternative diagnoses, atypical organisms, complications)
- Check antibiotic levels and compliance
- Repeat imaging if clinically indicated
- Refer for specialist assessment

## Special populations

**Older patients:** often present atypically with minimal fever; confusion, falls, or delirium may be initial sign. Lower threshold for admission.

**COPD:** increased risk of Haemophilus and resistant organisms; consider broader-spectrum antibiotics.

**Immunocompromised (HIV, chemotherapy, long-term steroids):** increased risk of opportunistic infections (PCP, CMV, fungi); lower threshold for admission and specialist input.

## Prevention

- Pneumococcal vaccination: 23-valent PPV23 for age ≥65 or chronic disease; discuss 13-valent PCV13 for certain risk groups (HIV, asplenia, chronic liver disease)
- Influenza vaccination: annually for all age ≥65 and those with chronic disease
- Smoking cessation
- Alcohol moderation

## Follow-up

Review at 48 hours if managed at home. Arrange follow-up CXR after 6 weeks for severe pneumonia (to document resolution and exclude underlying malignancy). Counsel on smoking cessation and vaccination if not up to date.

## Red flags

- Hypoxia (SpO₂ <90%)
- Respiratory distress (RR >30, accessory muscle use)
- Hypotension or signs of sepsis
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Pleurisy or suspected empyema
- Failure to respond to antibiotics within 48–72 hours
