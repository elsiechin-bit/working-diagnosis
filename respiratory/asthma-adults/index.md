<p>Take symptom history (triggers, frequency, night-time wakening, activity limitation). Examine for wheeze, peak flow, SpO₂. Perform spirometry if asthma suspected (FEV1 before and after beta-agonist). Diagnose asthma if symptoms + reversible airflow obstruction on spirometry. ## Diagnosis

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterised by symptoms (wheezing, dyspnoea, chest tightness, cough) and reversible airflow obstruction. Diagnosis requires clinical suspicion plus objective evidence of variable airflow obstruction.

**Typical presentation:**
- Wheezing or dyspnoea triggered by allergens, cold air, exercise, viral infection
- Cough, especially nocturnal or with laughing/crying
- Shortness of breath with activity
- Chest tightness
- Symptoms worse at night or early morning

**Atypical presentations:**
- Cough without wheeze (cough-variant asthma)
- Exercise-induced symptoms only (exercise-induced bronchoconstriction)
- Dyspnoea misattributed to anxiety or deconditioning

## Investigations for diagnosis

**Spirometry (essential):**
- FEV1/FVC <70% indicates airflow obstruction
- FEV1 <80% predicted suggests obstructive pattern
- Perform FEV1 pre- and post-beta-agonist (salbutamol 200 mcg via spacer)
- Increase in FEV1 ≥12% and ≥200 mL confirms reversibility and asthma diagnosis

**Alternative tests if spirometry unavailable:**
- Peak expiratory flow (PEF) diary: variability ≥20% between morning and evening suggests asthma
- Exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO >35 ppb in adults suggests eosinophilic inflammation)
- Bronchial challenge testing (histamine or methacholine) if spirometry normal but suspicion remains

**Chest X-ray:** not routinely needed unless suspected alternative diagnosis (pneumonia, pneumothorax).

## Classification of asthma severity and control

**Severity at presentation (intermittent vs persistent):**
- Intermittent: ≤2 days per week symptoms, ≤2 nights per month awakenings
- Persistent (mild/moderate/severe): daily symptoms or frequent night-time awakenings

**Asthma control (after starting treatment):**
- Well controlled: ≤2 days per week symptoms, ≤2 nights per month awakenings, normal lung function, no activity limitation
- Partially controlled: 3+ days per week symptoms or 3+ nights per month awakenings
- Uncontrolled: four+ symptoms/signs of control problems

## Step-wise treatment approach

**Step 1 (intermittent symptoms):** as-needed short-acting beta-agonist (SABA) only. NOT recommended as monotherapy.

**Step 2 (mild persistent):** low-dose ICS daily (e.g., beclomethasone 100 mcg once daily). SABA as reliever.

**Step 3 (moderate persistent):** increase to medium-dose ICS (beclomethasone 200–400 mcg daily) OR add LABA to low-dose ICS (ICS/LABA combination). SABA as reliever.

**Step 4 (moderate-to-severe persistent):** medium-to-high-dose ICS/LABA (e.g., beclomethasone/formoterol 200/6 mcg twice daily). SABA as reliever. Consider leukotriene antagonist or theophylline.

**Step 5 (severe persistent):** high-dose ICS/LABA ± additional agent (leukotriene antagonist, tiotropium, theophylline). Consider biologic therapy if eosinophils ≥300 (mepolizumab, reslizumab) or allergic phenotype (omalizumab).

## Inhaler choice and technique

**Device types:** metered-dose inhaler (MDI) with spacer preferred; dry powder inhalers (DPI) for >6 years if able to generate inspiratory flow; nebulisers for acute exacerbations or unable to use other devices.

**Inhaler technique:** critical for efficacy. Demonstrate, observe, and reassess regularly. Common errors: not actuating before inhaling, not holding breath, poor coordination.

**ICS options:** beclomethasone, budesonide, fluticasone propionate, ciclesonide (all equally effective at equivalent doses).

**LABA options (always with ICS):** formoterol, salmeterol, vilanterol.

## Management of asthma exacerbations

**Mild exacerbation (at home):**
- Increase SABA use (salbutamol 200 mcg every 4–6 hours)
- Oral corticosteroids (prednisolone 40–50 mg once daily for 5–7 days)
- Increase ICS dose temporarily
- Contact GP within 24 hours

**Severe exacerbation (requires hospital):**
- Oxygen to maintain SpO₂ >90%
- Salbutamol 5 mg nebulised continuously or 200 mcg MDI every 15 minutes
- IV or IM corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 500 mg)
- IV magnesium sulphate 2 g if poor response
- Consider IV salbutamol (20 mcg/min) if life-threatening
- Discharge with oral corticosteroids and ICS prescription

## Asthma action plan

Provide all asthma patients with a written plan:
- Green zone (doing well): no symptoms, peak flow ≥80% predicted, use preventer daily
- Yellow zone (caution): mild symptoms or peak flow 50–80% predicted, increase reliever use, contact GP
- Red zone (danger): severe dyspnoea, peak flow <50% predicted, unable to speak, call 999

## Long-term management

**Medication adherence:** emphasise daily preventer use even when symptom-free. SABA use >2 days per week suggests poor control.

**Triggers:** identify and eliminate (allergens, occupational exposure, pets, dust). Avoid smoking and secondhand smoke.

**Monitoring:** assess control at each visit; consider peak flow diary or asthma control questionnaire (ACQ).

**Vaccination:** annual influenza and once-lifetime pneumococcal vaccination.

**Referral to specialist:** if diagnosis uncertain, asthma uncontrolled on treatment, need for biologic therapy, or occupational asthma suspected.

## Red flags for severe asthma

- Frequent exacerbations requiring oral corticosteroids (≥2 per year)
- High oral corticosteroid requirement
- Persistent airflow obstruction despite treatment
- Psychological distress or medical complexity

## Follow-up

Review at 1–2 weeks after initiating or changing treatment. Once stable, review 3–6 monthly. Reinforce inhaler technique, assess control, adjust therapy as needed. Encourage engagement with asthma action plan and smoking cessation.
