<div class="panic-tile dont-miss">
<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>PE presents with acute dyspnoea, chest pain, or syncope. Assess clinical probability using Wells score. Massive PE (haemodynamic instability or RV dysfunction): urgent hospital referral for thrombolysis or IVC filter. Non-massive PE (stable vitals): manage with anticoagulation (DOAC or LMWH/warfarin). Avoid unnecessary imaging; use clinical gestalt plus D-dimer or CTPA.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Take history of dyspnoea onset, chest pain (pleuritic?), leg swelling, calf pain. Examine for tachycardia, hypoxia, leg signs (asymmetric swelling, warmth, calf tenderness). Measure Wells score (PE ≥2 points likely; <2 unlikely). If PE likely or Wells ≥2: refer for CTPA or D-dimer if low clinical suspicion. If massive PE (shock, RV dysfunction): admit immediately for thrombolysis consideration. Start anticoagulation (LMWH or fondaparinux) while awaiting imaging if high suspicion.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>Haemodynamic instability (SBP <90, HR >120, shock); signs of massive PE (RV dysfunction on ECG, elevated troponin, hypoxia); Wells score ≥2 or high clinical suspicion; syncope with dyspnoea; or ongoing anticoagulation failure.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile tell-patient">
<h3>Tell the patient</h3>
<p>"A blood clot has travelled to your lungs, blocking blood flow. We need urgent imaging to confirm this and start blood thinner medication immediately. Most patients do well with treatment, but you need to go to hospital now."</p>
</div>

## Diagnosis

Pulmonary embolism is a blood clot (thrombus) lodged in the pulmonary arterial tree, usually originating from deep veins of the legs (DVT). Presentation ranges from incidental finding to sudden death.

**Acute presentation:**
- Dyspnoea (sudden onset, exertional)
- Pleuritic chest pain
- Haemoptysis (rare; suggests infarction)
- Syncope (indicates massive PE)
- Tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypoxia

**Atypical presentations:**
- Older patients: subtle dyspnoea, functional decline
- Massive PE: shock, hypotension, cardiovascular collapse
- Incidental: found on imaging done for other reasons

## Risk factors

**Virchow's triad:**
- Venous stasis: immobility (bed rest, long flight), atrial fibrillation, heart failure, post-operatively
- Endothelial injury: trauma, surgery, central line, thrombophlebitis
- Hypercoagulability: malignancy, thrombophilia, recent major surgery, hormone therapy, pregnancy/postpartum

## Assessment

**History:** onset of dyspnoea, chest pain characteristics, leg swelling or calf pain, recent immobility (flights, bed rest), surgery, malignancy, hormone therapy, previous VTE.

**Examination:** respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, pulse, blood pressure. Look for signs of DVT (unilateral leg swelling, calf tenderness, warmth). Listen for pleural rub or signs of heart failure.

**Investigations:**
- ECG: may show sinus tachycardia, right axis deviation, S1Q3T3 pattern (rare; indicates massive PE). Normal ECG does not exclude PE.
- Chest X-ray: often normal. May show wedge-shaped infarct (Hampton's hump) or pleural effusion.
- Arterial blood gas: hypoxia and hypocapnia (due to hyperventilation). Normal ABG does not exclude PE.
- Troponin and BNP: elevated in massive PE; indicate RV strain
- D-dimer: negative (<500) excludes PE if low clinical probability
- CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA): gold standard for confirmation. Used if Wells ≥2 or D-dimer positive.

## Wells score for PE probability

Assign points:
- Clinical signs of DVT (leg swelling, calf swelling): 3
- PE is most likely diagnosis: 3
- Heart rate >100: 1.5
- Immobilisation ≥3 days or surgery <4 weeks: 1.5
- Previous VTE: 1.5
- Haemoptysis: 1
- Signs of right heart failure (JVP, peripheral oedema): 1.5

**Interpret:**
- <2 points: PE unlikely (mortality 0.4%)
- ≥2 points: PE likely (mortality 10–15%)

## Management of non-massive PE

**Anticoagulation:** start immediately if clinical suspicion high, while awaiting imaging confirmation.

**First-line DOAC:** apixaban 10 mg twice daily for 7 days, then 5 mg twice daily for minimum 3 months. Alternatively: dabigatran (following 5–10 days LMWH/UFH loading), edoxaban, or rivaroxaban.

**Alternative: LMWH + warfarin:** enoxaparin 1 mg/kg IV 12-hourly, with warfarin target INR 2–3. Continue LMWH for minimum 5 days and until INR ≥2 for ≥24 hours.

**Duration:** minimum 3 months for first VTE (provoked or unprovoked); indefinite if recurrent or malignancy-associated.

## Management of massive PE

**Medical emergency.** Refer immediately to hospital for:
- Oxygen and IV access
- Urgent CTPA or bedside echo to confirm
- Thrombolysis (alteplase IV or tenecteplase) if high-risk features (shock, RV dysfunction, cardiac biomarker elevation)
- Anticoagulation as above
- Consider IVC filter if anticoagulation contraindicated

## Prevention of recurrent PE

- Anticoagulation as above
- Early mobilisation
- Compression stockings (limited evidence for symptom relief in post-thrombotic syndrome)
- IVC filter only if anticoagulation contraindicated

## Special populations

**Pregnancy:** LMWH is preferred (warfarin teratogenic first trimester). Continue throughout pregnancy and 6 weeks postpartum.

**Cancer-associated VTE:** LMWH or DOAC preferred over warfarin. Longer anticoagulation period (often indefinite until cancer remission).

**Antiphospholipid syndrome:** warfarin with higher target INR (2.5–3.5) or LMWH.

## Complications

- Massive PE (sudden deterioration)
- Recurrent PE
- Post-thrombotic syndrome (leg oedema, pain, skin changes after DVT)
- Pulmonary hypertension (chronic thromboembolism)

## Follow-up

Once discharged on anticoagulation, review at 1–2 weeks to ensure tolerance and compliance. Reassess bleeding and clotting risk at 3 months to decide on duration of anticoagulation. Document indication and duration in medical record. Counsel on signs of recurrence (new dyspnoea, chest pain) and bleeding (haemoptysis, haematuria).

## Red flags

- Hypotension or shock
- Syncope
- Severe hypoxia (SpO₂ <85%)
- Haemodynamic instability
- Signs of right heart failure
