## Topic body

### Definition and pathophysiology

COPD is a preventable and treatable disease characterised by persistent airflow limitation due to abnormal inflammatory response to noxious particles or gases, predominantly from tobacco smoke. Key pathological features:

- **Emphysema**: destruction of alveolar walls, loss of elastic recoil
- **Small airway disease**: inflammation, narrowing, goblet cell hyperplasia
- **Mucus hypersecretion**: chronic productive cough
- **Systemic inflammation**: elevated circulating IL-6, TNF-α, contributing to weight loss, muscle wasting, cardiovascular disease

COPD is not asthma; the airflow obstruction does not reverse substantially with bronchodilators (unlike asthma FEV₁ reversibility ≥12% post-salbutamol).

### Epidemiology and risk factors

**Major risk factors**
- **Smoking**: dose-dependent relationship; smoking >10 pack-years increases COPD risk significantly; ex-smokers remain at risk
- **Occupational exposure**: chronic dust, fumes, chemicals (mining, welding, agriculture, textiles)
- **Air pollution**: long-term exposure to high levels of ambient or indoor smoke
- **Genetic**: alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency (rare but important to screen in early-onset COPD <45 years)

**Protective factors**
- **Smoking cessation**: decline in lung function slows to age-expected rate (~30 mL/year FEV₁ loss) once quit
- **Avoiding occupational exposure**: wear appropriate respiratory protection if exposure unavoidable

### Clinical presentation

**Typical symptoms**
- **Dyspnoea**: slowly progressive, exertional initially (notice climbing stairs), progressing to dyspnoea at rest in severe disease
- **Persistent cough**: often productive, worse in morning; productive cough >3 months defines chronic bronchitis
- **Sputum production**: white or clear (rarely purulent unless acute infection)
- **Wheezing or chest tightness**: may occur, more prominent during exacerbations

**Variable presentations**
- **Pink puffer**: predominantly emphysema-predominant, lean, dyspnoeic, hyperventilating (attempting to maintain oxygenation)
- **Blue bloater**: predominantly small airway disease, obese, cyanotic, cor pulmonale (elevated JVP, peripheral oedema); indicates more severe gas exchange impairment

### Diagnosis: spirometry essentials

**Indications for spirometry**
- Smoking history (current or ex-smoker with ≥10 pack-years) AND any of:
  - Dyspnoea on exertion
  - Persistent cough (>3 months)
  - Frequent respiratory infections (>3/year)
  - Occupational exposure history
- Age >40 with symptoms in high-risk countries (NZ population prevalence ~3–4% in adults >40)

**GOLD diagnostic criterion**
- **Airflow obstruction**: FEV₁/FVC ratio <70% after adequate bronchodilator (salbutamol 400 μg, wait 15 minutes)
- **Obstruction is persistent** (not fully reversible, unlike asthma where FEV₁ typically normalises)
- **Post-bronchodilator FEV₁ <80% predicted** confirms airflow obstruction (if FEV₁/FVC <70% but FEV₁ ≥80% predicted, diagnosis may still apply but is less certain)

**Pre-bronchodilator assessment**
- Measure FEV₁, FVC, and FEV₁/FVC before bronchodilator
- If post-bronchodilator FEV₁ improves >12% and >200 mL, reversibility is significant and asthma/ACO (asthma-COPD overlap) should be considered
- In ACO, asthma features (allergy history, atopic status, eosinophilia) coexist with COPD (fixed obstruction, smoking history); ICS/LABA is essential

**Interpretation**
- **FEV₁ 80–100% predicted**: COPD diagnosis certain if FEV₁/FVC <70%, but mild disease
- **FEV₁ 50–80% predicted**: moderate airflow obstruction
- **FEV₁ 30–50% predicted**: severe airflow obstruction
- **FEV₁ <30% predicted**: very severe airflow obstruction; heightened risk of hospitalisation, death

### COPD severity classification (GOLD 2024)

COPD is classified by **FEV₁ and symptom burden**, not spirometry alone:

**GOLD Group A**: FEV₁ ≥50% predicted AND symptom burden low (CAT <10 or mMRC 0–1)
- Exacerbation risk low
- Management: bronchodilator monotherapy (LAMA or LABA)

**GOLD Group B**: FEV₁ ≥50% predicted AND symptom burden high (CAT ≥10 or mMRC ≥2)
- Exacerbation risk low but symptomatic
- Management: LAMA preferred over LABA (superior dyspnoea relief); add LABA if insufficient response

**GOLD Group C**: FEV₁ <50% predicted AND exacerbation history low (0–1/year)
- Moderate airflow obstruction, lower exacerbation risk
- Management: LAMA (preferred) or LABA; add second agent if inadequate symptom control

**GOLD Group D**: FEV₁ <50% predicted AND exacerbation history high (≥2/year or ≥1 with hospitalisation)
- Severe airflow obstruction, high exacerbation risk
- Management: combination therapy essential; LAMA + LABA (dual therapy) minimum; triple therapy (LAMA + LABA + ICS) if eosinophils elevated or asthma-COPD overlap

**Symptom assessment tools**
- **mMRC (modified Medical Research Council) dyspnoea scale** (0–4): simple, patient-rated
- **CAT (COPD Assessment Test)** (0–40): comprehensive symptom and impact score; ≥10 indicates high symptom burden

### Spirometry technique considerations

**Before spirometry**
- Patient should not use SABA ≥4 hours prior (short-acting beta-agonist causes bronchodilation)
- Avoid LABA/LAMA use if possible; if on regular bronchodilators, document and perform post-bronchodilator testing after salbutamol challenge
- Sit upright, comfortable, rested

**Acceptability criteria**
- At least 3 acceptable manoeuvres (FEV₁, FVC within 150 mL of each other)
- No coughing, glottis closure, or early termination during expiration
- Exhale for ≥6 seconds (allows FVC measurement in obstructed airways)

**Interpretation pitfalls**
- **FEV₁/FVC borderline <70%**: true COPD if FEV₁ <80% predicted; if FEV₁ ≥80%, consider restrictive component or measurement error
- **Incomplete reversibility post-bronchodilator**: does not exclude asthma diagnosis; some asthmatics have persistent obstruction
- **Age-related decline**: FEV₁ declines ~30 mL/year in non-smokers; smokers lose ~60–100 mL/year (accelerated decline)

### Differential diagnosis

**Asthma**
- Reversible airflow obstruction (FEV₁ improves ≥12% post-bronchodilator)
- Atopy history, allergic rhinitis, eczema
- Onset before age 40
- Absence of fixed smoking history
- **Note**: ACO (asthma-COPD overlap) has features of both; ICS/LABA + LAMA often required

**Asthma-COPD overlap (ACO)**
- Fixed airflow obstruction (post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <70%) with:
  - Asthma diagnosis history OR
  - Allergy/atopy markers OR
  - Significant reversibility post-bronchodilator (≥12%) OR
  - Blood eosinophilia (≥100 cells/μL)
- Management: high-dose ICS/LABA essential; add LAMA for symptoms
- Prognosis: worse exacerbations, higher mortality than COPD alone

**Restrictive lung disease (ILD, chest wall deformity)**
- Reduced FVC and FEV₁ proportionally; FEV₁/FVC typically ≥70%
- Imaging (HRCT) shows reticular pattern, honeycomb, traction bronchiectasis
- Restrictive pattern on spirometry

**Bronchiectasis**
- Chronic suppurative airway disease with recurrent infections
- Productive cough with copious sputum
- HRCT shows bronchial dilatation (bronchus-to-artery ratio >1)
- May coexist with COPD (especially COPD with AAT deficiency)

**Emphysema vs chronic bronchitis distinction** (clinical phenotypes, not diagnostic):
- **Emphysema**: morphological destruction of alveoli; typically dyspnoeic, weight loss ("pink puffer")
- **Chronic bronchitis**: productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 years; may have better gas exchange initially ("blue bloater")

### Investigations beyond spirometry

**Chest imaging (CXR or HRCT)**
- **Not diagnostic for COPD** but useful for excluding alternative diagnoses
- CXR findings suggestive of COPD: hyperinflation, flattened diaphragms, increased retrosternal space
- HRCT indicates emphysema pattern if present; also detects bronchiectasis (may influence exacerbation management), ILD, bronchial abnormalities

**Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) screening**
- **Indicated**: COPD diagnosis <45 years, non-smoking history, or emphysema in basilar distribution (unusual)
- Test: serum AAT level; if <57 μmol/L (200 mg/dL), perform phenotyping (PI typing) and genetic sequencing
- AAT deficiency increases lung disease risk and lung cancer risk; family screening warranted; augmentation therapy considered if FEV₁ 35–65% and emphysema on imaging

**Arterial blood gas (ABG) or venous blood gas**
- **Indicated**: clinical signs of hypoxaemia (cyanosis, confusion), cor pulmonale (JVP, oedema), or severe airflow obstruction (FEV₁ <30% predicted)
- **Interpretation**: PaCO₂ elevation (>45 mmHg) suggests CO₂ retention (type II respiratory failure); low HCO₃⁻ (overexertion) vs high HCO₃⁻ (chronic compensation)
- **Target SpO₂ in COPD**: 88–92% (higher oxygen targets increase CO₂ retention in some patients)

**Full blood count (FBC)**
- Raised haemoglobin/haematocrit: secondary polycythaemia from chronic hypoxaemia
- White cell elevation: acute infection or systemic inflammation

**Functional assessment**
- **6-minute walk distance (6MWD)**: objective measure of exercise capacity; <250 m suggests poor prognosis
- **Shuttle walk test**: incremental, reproducible, less operator-dependent

### Special populations

**Young age at presentation (<45 years)**
- Screen for AAT deficiency
- Consider occupational exposure, genetic factors
- Smoking history less likely but must be explored
- Higher suspicion for bronchiectasis, ILD overlap

**Non-smokers with COPD**
- Occupational or environmental exposure (indoor biomass smoke, outdoor air pollution) more likely
- AAT deficiency more probable; screen
- Prognosis may differ (less pronounced bronchitis component)

**COPD in women**
- Increasing prevalence; tobacco smoke exposure, occupational exposures (textiles, agriculture)
- May present differently: more dyspnoea, less chronic cough
- Lung cancer risk: higher per cigarette compared to men

### Red flags for urgent investigation

- **Haemoptysis**: suggests bronchiectasis, lung cancer, or aspergilloma; refer for bronchoscopy/imaging
- **Rapid decline in FEV₁** (>100 mL/year loss): unusual in stable COPD; suspect acute/subacute process (infection, malignancy, ILD, PE)
- **Unexplained anaemia or polycythaemia**: investigate alternative causes
- **Asymmetric findings or focal crackles**: suggests pneumonia, aspiration, or alternative diagnosis
- **Cor pulmonale on presentation**: indicates advanced disease; refer urgently for oxygen assessment and specialist input

### Monitoring and follow-up

**Initial post-diagnosis follow-up**
- 1–2 weeks: review smoking cessation progress, assess medication tolerance
- 4 weeks: confirm diagnosis and severity with patient; discuss exacerbation risk, action plan
- 3 months: review symptoms, inhaler technique, medication adherence; consider repeat spirometry if significant symptom change

**Annual review**
- Repeat spirometry to document FEV₁ decline trajectory
- mMRC dyspnoea and CAT symptom scores
- Exacerbation frequency in past year
- Smoking status and cessation progress
- Vaccination status (annual flu, pneumococcal, COVID-19)
- Inhaler technique reassessment
- Consider pulmonary rehabilitation if moderate-severe disease

**Escalation criteria**
- FEV₁ decline >100 mL/year: accelerated decline; review exposure, disease modifiers
- Exacerbations ≥2/year: escalate therapy (add second bronchodilator, consider ICS if eosinophils elevated)
