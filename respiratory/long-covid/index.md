## Definition

Long COVID (post-COVID-19 syndrome) is the presence of symptoms ≥4 weeks after acute COVID-19 infection that were not present before infection. Symptoms persist or fluctuate and often affect functional capacity and quality of life.

**Key features:**
- Symptom onset after acute COVID (though may worsen from initial infection)
- Multisystem involvement common
- Significant functional impact
- Highly variable trajectory (improving, stable, or progressive)

## Prevalence and risk factors

Approximately 10–30% of people who have had COVID-19 experience persistent symptoms. Risk factors for more severe long COVID include:
- Severity of acute COVID infection
- Female sex
- Older age
- Pre-existing conditions
- Psychological distress during acute illness

## Common symptoms

**Fatigue:** overwhelming, disproportionate to activity; not improved by rest alone; often accompanied by post-exertional malaise (symptom worsening after exertion).

**Respiratory:** dyspnoea, reduced exercise tolerance, persistent cough, chest tightness.

**Cardiovascular:** palpitations, syncope, orthostatic intolerance, chest pain.

**Cognitive:** "brain fog," difficulty concentrating, memory impairment, slowed processing.

**Other:** headache, joint/muscle pain, temperature dysregulation, sleep disturbance, mood changes.

## Assessment

**History:** detailed timeline of symptom onset and trajectory; which symptoms are worst; triggers (activity, stress, menstrual cycle); prior function versus current; impact on work, self-care, social activities; psychological symptoms; medications tried.

**Examination:** vital signs (check for postural tachycardia), cardiac and respiratory examination, neurological assessment if cognitive symptoms prominent.

**Investigations to exclude other causes:**
- Full blood count (anaemia)
- Urea and electrolytes (renal function, hyponatraemia)
- Thyroid function (TSH)
- Liver function tests
- Fasting glucose or HbA1c (diabetes)
- C-reactive protein (persistent inflammation, though usually normal in long COVID)
- Troponin and BNP if cardiac symptoms
- ECG if palpitations or chest pain
- Chest X-ray if respiratory symptoms (usually normal)

**Specialist investigations:** echocardiography, Holter monitoring, or exercise testing only if symptoms suggest specific cardiac or pulmonary pathology.

## Differential diagnosis

Distinguish from:
- Cardiac disease (myocarditis, arrhythmia, ischaemia)
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Anaemia
- Chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS/ME) — may overlap with long COVID
- Depression or anxiety (though these often coexist with long COVID)
- Deconditioning from inactivity

## Management principles

**No disease-modifying cure exists.** Management focuses on symptom relief, restoration of function, and preventing deterioration.

**Pacing and energy management:**
- Activity envelope approach: identify current sustainable activity level
- Avoid "boom-bust" cycles (overactivity followed by crash)
- Structured rest and activity balancing
- Energy budgeting: prioritise essential activities

**Symptom-specific management:**
- Fatigue: sleep hygiene, structured pacing, low-intensity movement (tai chi, gentle yoga; avoid high-intensity exercise if post-exertional malaise present)
- Cognitive: use of aids (reminders, lists), cognitive therapy, reduced multitasking
- Sleep: sleep hygiene, short naps discouraged; melatonin or low-dose amitriptyline if insomnia persists
- Pain: paracetamol or NSAIDs; physiotherapy; consider gabapentin if neuropathic features
- Mood: CBT, counselling; antidepressants (SSRIs) if depression/anxiety

**Rehabilitation:**
- Refer to post-COVID rehabilitation services if available
- Graded exercise (only if post-exertional malaise absent; stop if symptoms worsen)
- Psychological support and mindfulness
- Social reintegration planning

**Medications:**
- Antihistamines (H1 and H2 blockers) in select cases with suspected mast cell activation
- Low-dose naltrexone: emerging evidence, not yet standard care
- Avoid unnecessary polypharmacy

## When exercise is appropriate

Exercise is beneficial if post-exertional malaise is absent. If present, exercise may worsen symptoms and should be avoided or minimised. Work with patient to identify sustainable activity level; gentle movement (walking, stretching) is preferable to structured exercise programmes initially.

## Psychological support

Long COVID has significant psychological impact: anxiety about persistent symptoms, loss of identity and function, grief. Early psychological intervention (CBT, counselling) improves outcomes. Screen for depression and anxiety; refer if moderate-to-severe.

## Work and function

Many patients struggle with return to work. Discuss flexible work arrangements, phased return, and workplace accommodations. Occupational health referral may help with work capacity planning. Benefit eligibility (if work incapacity persists).

## Follow-up

Review at 4–6 weeks after diagnosis to assess response to pacing and support. Most improve gradually over months; however, some remain significantly impaired after 6–12 months. Regular check-in provides opportunity to address emerging concerns, adjust management, and provide psychological support.

## Red flags

- Progressive worsening of cardiac or respiratory symptoms
- Syncope or significant postural symptoms
- New focal neurological signs
- Severe mood disturbance or suicidal ideation
