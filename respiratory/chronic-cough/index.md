## Topic body

### Definition and epidemiology

Chronic cough is defined as cough lasting >8 weeks (some guidelines use 3 weeks if preceded by URI). It is one of the most common reasons for primary care consultation, accounting for ~5–10% of presentations.

**Epidemiology**
- Prevalence: ~5% of general population report chronic cough
- Age: increases with advancing age; peaks in patients >60 years
- Sex: female predominance (1.5–2:1)
- Smoking: current smokers have high prevalence; risk persists after quitting

**Impact**
- Affects quality of life (sleep disruption, embarrassment, social withdrawal)
- Can cause syncope, urinary incontinence, rib fractures, laryngeal injury
- Workdays lost, healthcare utilisation

### Pathophysiology and cough reflex

The cough reflex involves:

1. **Afferent stimulus**: irritation of airway mucosa (mechanical, chemical, thermal, infectious)
2. **Sensory innervation**: 
   - **Vagus nerve** (CN X): primary; TRP channel activation (TRPV1, TRPA1)
   - **Trigeminal nerve** (CN V): supplementary (nasal, laryngeal innervation)
   - **Glossopharyngeal nerve** (CN IX): pharyngeal involvement
3. **Central processing**: cough centre in medulla; modulation by higher brainstem and cortex
4. **Efferent response**: coordinated muscular action (deep inspiration, glottis closure, expulsion)

**Why some patients cough more**
- **Heightened cough reflex sensitivity**: viral illness, smoking, environmental irritants, reflux
- **Opioids suppress cough** by CNS suppression; ACE inhibitors increase cough (increase bradykinin, a potent cough stimulant)
- **Persistent hypersensitivity**: post-viral cough can persist weeks after infection resolution (persistent airway inflammation and heightened reflex)

### Classification by duration and character

**Duration**
- **Acute cough**: <3 weeks (usually viral URI, acute infection)
- **Subacute cough**: 3–8 weeks (post-viral, pertussis, early COPD)
- **Chronic cough**: >8 weeks (focus of this topic)

**Character**
- **Dry cough**: minimal sputum; suggests upper airway origin (PND, GORD, asthma, ACE inhibitor), parenchymal inflammation (early ILD), or irritant exposure
- **Productive cough**: sputum present; suggests lower airway or parenchymal disease (pneumonia, bronchiectasis, COPD, ILD with airway involvement)
- **Sputum character**:
  - White/clear: viral, asthma, PND, GORD
  - Yellow/green: suggests bacterial infection (though colour alone not diagnostic of bacterial infection)
  - Purulent/copious: bronchiectasis, lung abscess
  - Haemoptysis: malignancy, TB, bronchiectasis, PE, vasculitis

### Clinical assessment

**History**
- **Onset and duration**: acute-on-chronic (recent worsening), gradual progression
- **Character**: dry vs productive, sputum colour and volume
- **Timing**: nocturnal (PND, GORD, asthma), daytime (occupational exposure, postural), episodic, constant
- **Triggers**: cold air, exercise (asthma), lying flat (PND, GORD, heart failure), eating/talking, laughing
- **Associated symptoms**:
  - Throat clearing, nasal congestion, post-nasal drip (PND)
  - Heartburn, regurgitation, nocturnal awakening with bitter taste (GORD)
  - Dyspnoea, wheezing, chest tightness (asthma, COPD)
  - Fever, dyspnoea, pleuritic pain (infection, pneumonia)
  - Weight loss, night sweats, haemoptysis (malignancy, TB, vasculitis)
- **Smoking history**: current vs ex-smoker, pack-years
- **Medication history**: 
  - **ACE inhibitor**: check timing of cough onset relative to initiation (can occur weeks after starting)
  - **Beta-blockers**: less likely but possible
  - **NSAIDs**: can precipitate cough in asthmatics
- **Occupational/environmental exposure**: asbestos, silica, bird/animal exposure
- **Pets**: birds (psittacosis), cats (Toxoplasma, allergic), dogs
- **Travel history**: TB risk regions, coccidioidomycosis (southwestern USA)
- **Immunocompromise**: HIV, immunosuppressive therapy (increases infection risk: PCP, fungal)
- **Psychiatric**: chronic cough can be psychogenic (habit cough, tic); diagnosis of exclusion

**Physical examination**
- **Vital signs**: fever (infection), tachypnoea (parenchymal disease)
- **Upper airway**: inspection of pharynx (post-nasal discharge, erythema), nasal examination (rhinitis, polyps, septal deviation)
- **Chest auscultation**: 
  - Normal (suggests upper airway cause, asthma, early ILD, or PND)
  - Crackles (bronchiectasis, ILD, pneumonia, pulmonary oedema)
  - Wheezes (asthma, COPD, bronchiectasis)
  - Absent breath sounds (pleural effusion, pneumothorax)
- **Laryngeal findings**: vocal cord movement (rarely assessed in primary care but possible)

### Investigation

**Chest X-ray (mandatory)**
- **Indications**: all patients with chronic cough (even if clinical examination normal)
- **Abnormalities guiding diagnosis**:
  - **Normal CXR**: suggests upper airway cause (PND, GORD, asthma, ACE inhibitor, psychogenic), or early asthma/ILD
  - **Infiltrate/consolidation**: pneumonia, TB, aspiration, malignancy, vasculitis
  - **Hilar lymphadenopathy**: TB, sarcoidosis, lymphoma, malignancy
  - **Pleural effusion**: malignancy, heart failure, pneumonia with parapneumonic effusion, PE, vasculitis
  - **Hyperinflation/flattened diaphragms**: COPD, asthma
  - **Reticular/nodular pattern**: ILD, TB, fungal infection, sarcoidosis
  - **Cavitation**: TB, fungal infection (aspergilloma), anaerobic infection, malignancy with necrosis
  - **Bronchial wall thickening, tram-track sign**: bronchiectasis

**High-resolution CT thorax (HRCT)**
- **Indications**: abnormal CXR, persistent cough despite initial workup, suspicion of ILD or bronchiectasis
- **Advantages**: superior sensitivity for bronchiectasis, ILD, small nodules, atelectasis
- **Disadvantages**: radiation exposure, cost, may detect incidental abnormalities

**Spirometry**
- **Indications**: cough with dyspnoea, smoking history, risk for asthma or COPD
- **Interpretation**:
  - **Obstructive pattern** (FEV₁/FVC <70%): asthma or COPD
  - **Restrictive pattern** (low FVC, normal or high FEV₁/FVC): ILD or chest wall restriction
  - **Bronchial hyperresponsiveness** (positive methacholine challenge): suggests asthma

**Allergic/immunological investigations**
- **Skin prick test or specific IgE**: confirm allergic rhinitis/allergic asthma if suspected
- **Total IgE, specific IgE to Aspergillus**: if recurrent pneumonia or bronchiectasis suspected

**Microbiological investigations**
- **Sputum culture**: if productive cough with purulent sputum (bronchiectasis, CF, recurrent infection)
- **Sputum smear and culture for AFB**: if TB suspected (fever, night sweats, weight loss, high-risk groups)
- **Respiratory viral PCR**: recent URI if symptoms suggest post-viral cough

**Specialist investigations** (referral indicated)
- **Bronchoscopy**: if CXR abnormality, haemoptysis, immunocompromised, suspected foreign body
- **Oesophageal pH monitoring or impedance monitoring**: if GORD diagnosis uncertain despite PPI trial
- **Sleep study**: if OSA suspected (daytime somnolence, witnessed apnoeas)
- **ACE level, calcium, chest imaging**: if sarcoidosis suspected

### Diagnosis: common causes in order of frequency

**In non-smokers with normal CXR**, three causes account for >90%:

**1. Post-nasal drip (PND) — 25–30% of chronic cough cases**
- **Mechanism**: upper airway inflammation (allergic or non-allergic rhinitis) causing drainage of mucus posteriorly and cough reflex trigger
- **Features**:
  - Sensation of drainage from nasopharynx
  - Throat clearing, constant urge to swallow
  - Worse when lying flat or upon waking
  - May be productive (whitish sputum)
  - Nasal symptoms (congestion, rhinorrhoea) may coexist or be absent
- **Causes**:
  - **Allergic rhinitis**: seasonal or perennial; associated with sneezing, pruritus
  - **Non-allergic rhinitis**: vasomotor (triggered by cold, humidity, irritants), occupational, hormonal
  - **Acute/chronic rhinosinusitis**: nasal congestion, facial pain/pressure
- **Diagnosis**: clinical; nasal endoscopy if needed
- **Treatment**:
  - First-line: nasal corticosteroid spray (fluticasone propionate 50 μg BD) ± antihistamine (cetirizine 10 mg daily)
  - If allergic: allergen avoidance, leukotriene receptor antagonist (montelukast 10 mg nocte)
  - Nasal saline irrigation (sodium chloride 0.9%, 240 mL twice daily) to mechanically clear secretions
  - Decongestants (xylometazoline, oxymetazoline) short-term only (<10 days due to rebound congestion)
  - Response: typically within 2–4 weeks of nasal spray initiation

**2. Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) — 20–25% of chronic cough cases**
- **Mechanism**: acid reflux triggering cough reflex; inflammation of larynx and lower oesophageal mucosa; aspiration of gastric contents
- **Features**:
  - Dry cough; may be productively clear/foamy
  - Worse after meals, lying flat, bending forward
  - Heartburn or regurgitation may be absent (cough can be sole GORD symptom in 50%)
  - Nocturnal cough awakening from sleep
  - Hoarseness, throat clearing, sensation of lump in throat (laryngeal irritation)
- **Diagnosis**: clinical; empirical PPI trial diagnostic and therapeutic
- **Treatment**:
  - Lifestyle modification: weight loss (if BMI >25), avoid large meals, no food 2–3 hours before bed, elevate head of bed, avoid NSAIDs
  - **PPI therapy**: omeprazole 20 mg BD or lansoprazole 30 mg BD (requires 2–4 weeks for symptom resolution as inflammation must heal)
  - **H2-receptor antagonist** (ranitidine): less effective for cough; use if PPI intolerant
  - Antacids: symptomatic only, short-acting
  - Response rate: ~60–80% cough improvement within 3 months; full resolution may take up to 8 weeks
  - If inadequate response: consider dual PPI + H2 antagonist, increase PPI dose, or referral for pH monitoring

**3. Asthma (including cough-variant asthma) — 20–25% of chronic cough cases**
- **Mechanism**: airway inflammation and bronchoconstriction triggering cough reflex; reactive airway disease
- **Features**:
  - Dry or minimally productive cough
  - Triggered by cold air, exercise, allergens, URI, stress
  - Worse at night or early morning
  - May have associated dyspnoea, wheezing, or chest tightness, OR cough can be the ONLY symptom (cough-variant asthma, CVA)
  - Responsiveness to beta-2 agonist inhaler (symptom relief after 2–3 puffs salbutamol is clue)
  - Atopy history (eczema, allergic rhinitis, food allergy)
- **Diagnosis**:
  - **Spirometry**: usually normal in CVA or mild asthma (FEV₁/FVC may be preserved); valuable if abnormal
  - **Methacholine challenge test**: positive (PC20 <16 mg/mL) suggests airway hyperresponsiveness; gold standard but not widely available
  - **Empirical trial of ICS**: if suspicion high, start inhaled corticosteroid (fluticasone 100 μg daily) and assess response after 4–8 weeks
  - **Fractional exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO)**: elevated (≥25 ppb) supports eosinophilic airway inflammation; <20 ppb makes asthma less likely
- **Treatment**:
  - **ICS inhaler**: fluticasone 100–200 μg daily (low-to-moderate dose) or equivalent
  - **SABA as needed**: salbutamol 2 puffs PRN
  - **LTRA**: montelukast 10 mg nocte (particularly helpful if allergic asthma component)
  - Response: cough should improve within 4 weeks; expect resolution by 8 weeks on ICS

**ACE inhibitor-induced cough — 5–10% of chronic cough cases**
- **Mechanism**: ACE inhibitors block angiotensin-converting enzyme, reducing breakdown of bradykinin; elevated bradykinin increases cough sensitivity
- **Features**:
  - Dry cough, often irritating
  - Onset within days to months of ACE inhibitor initiation
  - Resolves within 4 weeks of ACE inhibitor discontinuation
  - Affects 10–20% of ACE inhibitor users
- **Diagnosis**: temporal relationship with ACE inhibitor; trial of discontinuation (rechallenge if recurs with reinitiation confirms diagnosis)
- **Management**:
  - Discontinue ACE inhibitor and switch to alternative antihypertensive (ARB does not cause cough in most; occasional cough reported)
  - Cough resolves within 4 weeks post-discontinuation

**Smoking-related cough**
- **Mechanism**: chronic irritation, airway inflammation, mucus hypersecretion
- **Features**:
  - Productive cough in chronic smokers (>10 pack-years)
  - White/clear sputum; increases upon waking
  - Improves minimally if at all without smoking cessation
- **Management**: smoking cessation (cough persists for weeks/months in ex-smokers as ciliary function recovers and mucociliary clearance improves)

**Other causes** (5–10% of remaining cough cases)

- **COPD**: smoker with productive cough, dyspnoea; FEV₁/FVC <70% on spirometry
- **Bronchiectasis**: copious sputum (>30 mL daily), recurrent infections, CXR/HRCT abnormality
- **ILD** (idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis): dyspnoea, restrictive pattern on spirometry, HRCT changes
- **Tuberculosis**: fever, night sweats, weight loss, high-risk groups; confirm with sputum AFB
- **Lung cancer**: smoking history >20 pack-years, weight loss, haemoptysis, age >40; CXR abnormality
- **Aspiration**: swallowing dysfunction, neurological disease, GORD; recurrent lower lobe pneumonia
- **Psychogenic/tic cough**: after exclusion of organic disease; habit cough, worsens with stress/anxiety, absent during sleep
- **Occupational/environmental exposure**: asbestos, silica, bird proteins; latency between exposure and onset

### Management approach: systematic algorithm

**Step 1: History and CXR** (all patients)
- Take detailed history and examine
- Obtain CXR
- If CXR abnormal → refer to respiratory specialist (likely alternate diagnosis: malignancy, TB, ILD, pneumonia)

**Step 2: Identify likely cause(s)** (normal CXR)
- PND: nasal congestion, post-nasal drainage sensation, allergy history
- GORD: heartburn/regurgitation history, worse lying flat, nocturnal, OR cough as isolated symptom
- Asthma: exercise/cold trigger, atopy history, dyspnoea/wheezing (or cough-only presentation)
- ACE inhibitor: recent initiation correlating with cough
- Smoking: active or recent smoker (cough may persist for months post-cessation)

**Step 3: Targeted initial treatment** (based on likely cause)

*If suspected PND*:
- Nasal corticosteroid spray (fluticasone 50 μg BD) and/or saline rinse
- Review in 2–4 weeks; if >50% improvement, continue; if no improvement, reassess

*If suspected GORD*:
- PPI (omeprazole 20 mg BD) for 4–8 weeks; lifestyle modifications
- Review in 4 weeks; if improvement, continue; if no improvement, reassess

*If suspected asthma*:
- Spirometry (or methacholine if normal spirometry and high suspicion)
- Start ICS (fluticasone 100 μg daily) and SABA PRN
- Review in 4–8 weeks; if >50% improvement, continue ICS; if no improvement, reassess

*If suspected ACE inhibitor*:
- Consider switch to ARB or alternative antihypertensive
- Cough should resolve within 4 weeks

*If smoking-related*:
- Strong smoking cessation counselling and pharmacotherapy
- Referral to cessation service
- Cough may worsen initially as mucociliary clearance improves; reassure patient

**Step 4: Re-evaluation at 4–8 weeks**
- If substantial improvement (>50% cough reduction): continue current therapy, plan tapering after 3–6 months
- If partial improvement: consider combination therapy (e.g. PPI + ICS if mixed GORD and asthma features)
- If no improvement: 
  - Consider that diagnosis was incorrect (reassess history)
  - Arrange spirometry (if not done)
  - Consider HRCT if upper lobe involvement or recurrent infections
  - Refer to respiratory specialist if diagnosis unclear or cough persistent after 3 months

**Step 5: Specialist referral** (if diagnosis unclear or cough persists >3 months)
- Cough clinic or respiratory specialist
- Further imaging (HRCT)
- Bronchoscopy (if high-risk features: haemoptysis, weight loss, immunocompromised)
- Specialist investigations (pH monitoring, sleep study, etc.)

### Refractory chronic cough

**Definition**: persistent cough despite appropriate treatment of identified cause(s) or absence of identifiable cause after standard investigation

**Characteristics**
- Often severe, affecting quality of life
- Female predominance
- Frequent hospitalisation for diagnostic workup

**Management**
- **Neuromodulators**: gabapentin (300–3600 mg daily in 3 divided doses), pregabalin, amitriptyline (low-dose, 10–25 mg nocte)
  - Work by reducing cough reflex sensitivity centrally
  - Efficacy modest (~30–40% symptom improvement)
  - Trial 4–12 weeks; monitor for side effects (dizziness, sedation)
- **Opioids**: tramadol or codeine; reserved for refractory cases due to addiction risk
- **Speech-language pathology**: cough suppression techniques, desensitisation
- **Cough centre referral**: specialist multidisciplinary assessment if available

### Red flags and urgent referral

- **Haemoptysis**: malignancy, TB, bronchiectasis, vasculitis; refer for bronchoscopy/imaging
- **Weight loss**: malignancy, TB, immunocompromise
- **Night sweats**: TB, malignancy, lymphoma
- **Persistent fever**: pneumonia, TB, abscess
- **Acute dyspnoea**: pneumonia, PE, pneumothorax; assess urgency based on severity
- **Immunocompromise** (HIV, chemotherapy, transplant): increased infection risk (PCP, fungal); low threshold for referral
- **CXR abnormality**: always refer for specialist evaluation
- **Cough unresponsive to initial therapy**: reassess and escalate

### Follow-up and monitoring

**During treatment**
- Review at 4 weeks: assess symptom improvement, treatment adherence, side effects
- If improvement >50%: continue current therapy; plan reassessment at 8 weeks
- If minimal improvement: reassess diagnosis, consider adding second agent, or refer

**After cough resolution**
- Continue ICS 3–6 months before taper (prevent recurrence)
- Discontinue PPI after 8 weeks if GORD-treated; note many require continuation (chronic reflux)
- Taper nasal corticosteroid over 2–4 weeks if PND-treated
- Review smoking cessation progress
