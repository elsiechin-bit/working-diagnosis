<h3>Don't miss</h3>
<p>Asthma in children <6 years is often viral-triggered; diagnosis is clinical (recurrent wheezing, cough, dyspnoea). Spirometry difficult before age 5; use symptom patterns and response to bronchodilators to guide diagnosis. All persistent asthma: ICS inhaler daily via spacer with face mask (ages <6) or mouthpiece (ages ≥6). SABA for relief. Severe asthma attack: immediate oxygen, SABA nebulised, oral corticosteroids. Hypoxia, silent chest, exhaustion: admit urgently.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile first-line">
<h3>First-line</h3>
<p>Take detailed history of cough, wheeze, dyspnoea (onset, triggers: viral, exercise, allergens, cold), night-time wakening, activity limitation. Examine for wheeze, accessory muscle use, peak flow if age ≥6. Diagnose asthma if recurrent episodes of wheeze or cough with response to beta-agonist. For intermittent asthma: SABA only. For persistent asthma: low-dose ICS daily (beclomethasone 50–100 mcg via spacer/mask). Teach carers correct inhaler technique and when to seek help.</p>
</div>

<div class="panic-tile refer-when">
<h3>Refer when</h3>
<p>Recurrent severe exacerbations (≥2 per year); uncontrolled asthma despite ICS; need for oral corticosteroids; life-threatening asthma attack; hypoxia (SpO₂ <90%); signs of severe obstruction (inability to feed, silent chest); suspected alternative diagnosis (foreign body, CF, cardiac disease).</p>
</div>

## Diagnosis in children

Asthma in children is characterised by recurrent symptoms (wheeze, cough, dyspnoea, chest tightness) that are reversible with bronchodilators or time. Diagnosis is clinical; young children (<6 years) cannot perform spirometry reliably.

**In children <6 years:**
- Recurrent wheezing (>3 episodes in a year)
- Persistent cough (especially at night, with play, or crying)
- Response to bronchodilators (improvement in symptoms with SABA)
- Risk factors: family history of asthma, eczema, food allergies, or seasonal patterns

**In children ≥6 years:**
- Similar symptoms plus spirometry if available (FEV1/FVC <85%, reversibility ≥12%)
- Peak expiratory flow (PEF) variability ≥20%

**Viral-triggered wheezing in <6 years:**
- Wheezing triggered by colds; other triggers less clear
- May not warrant asthma diagnosis if triggers are purely viral and infrequent
- Trial of ICS + SABA on-demand may help distinguish asthma from viral wheeze

## Differential diagnosis

Distinguish from:
- Viral-induced wheezing (isolated episodes with upper respiratory infection; no symptoms between)
- Cough-variant asthma (cough without wheeze)
- Reflux-induced cough
- Recurrent infections (bronchitis, pneumonia)
- Cystic fibrosis or other chronic lung disease
- Cardiac disease (murmur, cyanosis, poor feeding)
- Foreign body aspiration
- Tracheomalacia

## Step-wise treatment approach

**Intermittent symptoms (≤2 days per week, ≤2 nights per month):**
- SABA inhaler (salbutamol 200 mcg via spacer) as needed
- No regular medication if symptoms infrequent and mild

**Mild persistent (>2 days per week or ≥3 nights per month):**
- Low-dose ICS daily (beclomethasone 50 mcg or budesonide 100 mcg once daily) via spacer and face mask (<2 years) or mouthpiece (≥2 years)
- SABA as reliever

**Moderate persistent:**
- Increase ICS to medium-dose (beclomethasone 100 mcg twice daily or budesonide 200 mcg daily) OR add LABA to low-dose ICS
- SABA as reliever
- Consider trial for 4–6 weeks before escalating

**Severe persistent:**
- High-dose ICS (beclomethasone 200–400 mcg twice daily) plus LABA
- Leukotriene antagonist (montelukast) if eosinophilic features
- Consider specialist referral for biologic or systemic therapy

## Device selection by age

**<2 years:** pressurised metered-dose inhaler (pMDI) with spacer and face mask. Ensure good seal. Spacer volume should be 110–160 mL; avoid large spacers.

**2–6 years:** pMDI with spacer and mouthpiece (child should form seal around spacer). Face mask if unable to form seal.

**≥6 years:** pMDI with spacer and mouthpiece, or dry powder inhaler if able to generate sufficient inspiratory flow.

**Nebulisers:** use if child unable to cooperate with inhaler; takes longer (10–15 minutes) but not more effective than spacer + pMDI in stable asthma.

## Asthma action plan for carers

Provide written plan:
- **Green (doing well):** no symptoms, using preventer daily
- **Yellow (caution):** mild symptoms, increased reliever use, call GP if not improving in 24 hours
- **Red (danger):** severe dyspnoea, difficulty feeding or speaking, drowsiness, cyanosis, reliever not helping; call 999

## Acute asthma exacerbation

**Mild-to-moderate (at home):**
- Salbutamol 200 mcg via spacer every 15 minutes for first hour (up to 4 doses)
- Oral prednisolone 1–2 mg/kg (max 40 mg) once daily for 3–5 days
- Contact GP same day; arrange review

**Severe exacerbation (hospital admission):**
- Oxygen to maintain SpO₂ >90%
- Salbutamol 5 mg nebulised or 200 mcg via spacer every 15–20 minutes
- Ipratropium 250 mcg nebulised (enhances bronchodilation with SABA)
- IV or IM corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg)
- IV magnesium sulphate 25 mg/kg if poor response
- Discharge with oral corticosteroids and increased ICS dose

## Special considerations

**Viral-induced wheeze:** some children wheeze only with viral infections. If triggers are purely viral and episodes are infrequent, asthma diagnosis may not be needed; trial of SABA or ICS on-demand may suffice.

**Exercise-induced asthma:** SABA inhaler 5–15 minutes before sports; ICS improves exercise tolerance. Encourage participation in sports.

**Allergen avoidance:** identify triggers (pollen, dust, pets); environmental control (HEPA filters, mattress covers) has limited evidence but reasonable to try.

**School and sports:** ensure school has asthma action plan and SABA inhaler available. Encourage PE and sports participation.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**At diagnosis:** demonstrate inhaler technique, provide asthma action plan, discuss triggers, arrange GP review at 2–4 weeks.

**Ongoing:** review 1–2 months after initiation or change of treatment. Once stable, review 6–12 monthly. Reinforce inhaler technique at every visit. Assess asthma control and adjust therapy. Screen for comorbidities (eczema, food allergies, rhinitis).

**Transition to adulthood:** discuss long-term management, driving restrictions if poorly controlled, occupation choices (avoid dust, fumes), contraception (oral corticosteroids may reduce efficacy).

## Prognosis

Most children with asthma have excellent control with low-dose ICS. Many children with intermittent asthma improve or resolve by adulthood, though some develop persistent asthma. Early ICS use reduces risk of persistent disease.
