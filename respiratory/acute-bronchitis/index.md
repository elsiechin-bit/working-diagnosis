## Diagnosis

Acute bronchitis is inflammation of the lower airways (trachea, bronchi) causing cough, usually following an upper respiratory viral infection. It is self-limiting (2–3 weeks) and almost always viral; bacterial superinfection is rare.

**Typical presentation:**
- Cough for 1–3 weeks (initially dry, may become productive with clear or white sputum)
- Low-grade fever or none
- Dyspnoea may occur but mild
- No focal signs on auscultation
- Normal oxygen saturation and respiratory rate

**Risk factors for worse course:**
- Smoking
- Underlying chronic lung disease (COPD, asthma)
- Age >65 years
- Comorbidities (heart failure, immunosuppression)

## Differential diagnosis

Distinguish from:
- **Pneumonia**: focal crackles, consolidation on X-ray, high fever, dyspnoea, hypoxia
- **Asthma or reactive airway disease**: wheeze, reversible airflow obstruction on spirometry
- **COPD exacerbation**: cough with change in baseline sputum volume or purulence; pre-existing diagnosis
- **Upper respiratory infection**: cough but mainly sore throat, nasal symptoms
- **Post-viral cough**: persists beyond acute illness (>3 weeks); consider TB, whooping cough, asthma

## Assessment

Take history of cough onset (gradual vs sudden), character (dry vs productive, blood-tinged?), associated symptoms (fever, dyspnoea, malaise), smoking status, and any chronic lung disease.

Examine chest: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, auscultation (crackles, wheeze, or clear?), and look for signs of consolidation or respiratory distress.

**Investigations:** not routinely needed if clinical diagnosis of uncomplicated viral bronchitis is clear. Chest X-ray only if pneumonia is suspected (focal findings, high fever, hypoxia, dyspnoea, or immunocompromised).

## Management

**No antibiotics:** meta-analyses consistently show antibiotics do not shorten symptom duration, reduce complications, or prevent pneumonia in uncomplicated acute bronchitis. Exception: if bacterial superinfection is suspected (worsening fever, purulent sputum after initial improvement, focal consolidation), consider antibiotics, but this is uncommon.

**Symptomatic relief:**
- Cough: honey (for adults) or simple linctus; avoid routine cough suppressants (cough helps clear secretions)
- Fever and malaise: paracetamol or ibuprofen
- Rest and fluids
- Humidifier if air is dry

**Smoking cessation:** strongly encourage.

**Reassurance:** explain viral course, expected duration, and that cough does not mean pneumonia or need for antibiotics.

## When to investigate or treat

**Chest X-ray:** if focal crackles, consolidation suspected, high fever, hypoxia, dyspnoea, immunocompromised, or any atypical features.

**Antibiotics:** only if bacterial pneumonia is confirmed or strongly suspected (consolidation on imaging or clinical deterioration).

**Spirometry or peak flow:** if cough is accompanied by wheeze or dyspnoea; may reveal asthma or reactive airway disease.

## Follow-up

Most cases resolve within 2–3 weeks. If cough persists beyond 3 weeks, reassess:
- Smoking or environmental exposure?
- Asthma or COPD?
- Pertussis (whooping cough)?
- TB or other chronic infection?
- ACE inhibitor-induced cough?
- Acid reflux?

Consider spirometry, chest X-ray, or pertussis serology as appropriate.

## Red flags

- Haemoptysis (consider TB, malignancy, infection)
- Dyspnoea at rest or hypoxia (pneumonia or underlying lung disease)
- High fever or systemic toxicity (pneumonia)
- Immunocompromised patient with respiratory symptoms (opportunistic infection)
- Cough persisting >3 weeks (rule out alternative diagnosis)
