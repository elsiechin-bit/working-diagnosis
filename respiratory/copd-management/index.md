## Topic body

### Principles of COPD management

Management aims to:
- **Reduce symptoms**: dyspnoea, cough, activity limitation
- **Reduce exacerbation frequency and severity**: prevent hospitalisations
- **Slow lung function decline**: smoking cessation is the only intervention that reduces FEV₁ loss to age-expected rate
- **Optimise exercise capacity and quality of life**: pulmonary rehabilitation, oxygen if indicated
- **Identify and manage comorbidities**: cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, anxiety/depression
- **Prepare for end-of-life care**: advance care planning, palliative care access for advanced disease

### Smoking cessation

**Rationale**
- Smoking cessation is the only intervention that slows FEV₁ decline from ~60–100 mL/year (smoker) to ~30 mL/year (non-smoker)
- Current smokers with COPD have worse symptoms, more exacerbations, poorer prognosis

**Approach**
- **Ask and advise**: all visits; document smoking status
- **Assess readiness**: brief intervention for pre-contemplative smokers; intensive support for those ready to quit
- **Pharmacotherapy**:
  - **Varenicline** (Champix): partial nicotine receptor agonist; highest quit rate (~35% at 6 months); start 0.5 mg daily × 3 days, escalate to 1 mg BD; 12-week course; avoid if psychiatric history (black box warning for suicidality)
  - **Bupropion** (Zyban): antidepressant; ~25% quit rate; 150 mg daily then BD × 7–9 weeks; contraindicated if seizure history
  - **Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT)**: transdermal patch ± lozenge/gum for breakthrough; quit rate ~15–20%; safe to combine NRT with varenicline or bupropion
- **Behavioural support**: refer to smoking cessation service (NZ: free phone line 0800 778 778, web quit coach, group programmes)
- **Monitor**: ask at every visit; relapse is common; reoffer support after failed quit attempt

### Pharmacological management

**GOLD Group A** (FEV₁ ≥50%, CAT <10 or mMRC 0–1)
- **Monotherapy**: LAMA (tiotropium 18 μg daily) preferred over LABA
- **Rationale**: LAMA superior for dyspnoea; exacerbation risk low, so LABA (exacerbation-sparing benefit) less critical
- **Options**: tiotropium, glycopyrronium, umeclidinium (all once-daily); avoid LABA monotherapy

**GOLD Group B** (FEV₁ ≥50%, CAT ≥10 or mMRC ≥2)
- **Dual therapy**: LAMA + LABA (preferred) or LABA monotherapy if LAMA intolerated
- **Rationale**: high symptom burden requires combination for dyspnoea relief; LABA adds exacerbation benefit
- **Options**: 
  - LAMA + LABA fixed-dose combination (e.g. tiotropium/olodaterol, glycopyrronium/formoterol, umeclidinium/vilanterol)
  - Separate inhalers if combination unavailable
- **Escalation**: if inadequate control, add ICS if eosinophils ≥100 cells/μL (reduces exacerbations) or move to GOLD Group C/D triple therapy

**GOLD Group C** (FEV₁ <50%, <2 exacerbations/year)
- **Dual therapy minimum**: LAMA ± LABA
- **Rationale**: severe airflow obstruction warrants intensive therapy; low exacerbation history suggests eosinophil-low phenotype (ICS less critical)
- **If inadequate control**: add ICS if exacerbation history develops OR eosinophils ≥100 cells/μL; consider triple therapy

**GOLD Group D** (FEV₁ <50%, ≥2 exacerbations/year or ≥1 with hospitalisation)
- **Triple therapy minimum**: LAMA + LABA + ICS
- **Rationale**: high exacerbation risk; ICS reduces exacerbations in this group by ~25%; prevents acute exacerbation-driven mortality
- **Options**:
  - Fixed-dose triple (fluticasone/umeclidinium/vilanterol, beclomethasone/glycopyrronium/formoterol)
  - Or separate inhalers (dual LAMA/LABA + ICS or LABA/ICS)
- **ICS choice**: beclomethasone or fluticasone propionate; same ICS dose as asthma (not higher)
- **Escalation**: if eosinophils ≥100 cells/μL and exacerbations persistent despite triple therapy, consider:
  - **Roflumilast**: phosphodiesterase-4 (PDE-4) inhibitor; oral, reduces exacerbations ~20% in patients with chronic bronchitis phenotype and eosinophils; GI side effects (diarrhoea) common; consider if eosinophils <100 and chronic bronchitis features
  - **Macrolide antibiotics** (azithromycin 250 mg 3× weekly): reduces exacerbations in patients with frequent infections; risk of resistance and long QT; use only after specialist assessment

### Inhaler technique and device choice

**Assess at diagnosis and annually**
- Ask patient to demonstrate technique on their device
- Common errors: failure to seal lips, no breath-hold, poor hand-breath coordination, failure to actuate
- Incorrect technique can reduce drug delivery by 50–80%

**Device selection by patient capability**
- **pMDI + spacer**: preferred for most; easier coordination than dry powder
- **DPI (Ellipta, Turbuhaler)**: requires adequate inspiratory flow (≥60 L/min); not suitable if dyspnoeic
- **Soft mist inhaler (Respimat)**: slower aerosol release; may suit poor coordinators; more expensive

**Adherence support**
- Prescribe one inhaler type per patient to simplify routine
- Use same device class (DPI or pMDI) across all drugs if possible
- Regular pharmacy review (every 6 months) to monitor refills and identify non-adherence
- Combination inhalers (LAMA/LABA, LABA/ICS, or triple) improve adherence vs separate devices

### Acute exacerbation management

**Recognition of exacerbation**
- Increase in dyspnoea, cough frequency, or sputum production/purulence over days
- Associated symptoms: fever, fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance
- **Severe exacerbation indicators**: breathlessness at rest, inability to speak full sentences, altered consciousness, SpO₂ <88%, peak flow decline, new cor pulmonale signs

**Home management (for mild-moderate exacerbations)**
- Criteria for home management: FEV₁ >50%, no hypoxaemia, no signs of severe illness, reliable carer, able to take medications
- **Salbutamol**: 2 puffs via spacer every 4–6 hours (can increase to every 2–4 hours if breathless)
- **Ipratropium**: 40 μg (1 unit) via spacer BD, added if poor initial salbutamol response
- **Oral corticosteroid**: prednisolone 30–40 mg once daily × 5–7 days (no taper needed); effective for exacerbations with increased sputum
- **Antibiotics**: only if evidence of bacterial infection (purulent sputum, fever, WBC elevated); first-line: amoxicillin-clavulanate or azithromycin × 5–7 days; if penicillin allergy, use macrolide (azithromycin) or fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin)
- **Review after 48 hours**: if no improvement, consider hospital referral
- **Escalation to hospital if**: SpO₂ <88%, breathless at rest, confusion, cyanosis, new arrhythmia, or no improvement within 48 hours

**Hospital management (moderate-severe exacerbations)**
- Criteria: FEV₁ <50%, SpO₂ <88% on room air, cyanosis, confusion, haemodynamic instability, or failure to improve at home
- **Oxygen**: target SpO₂ 88–92% (higher targets worsen CO₂ retention in some); monitor ABG if PaCO₂ initially elevated
- **Nebulised bronchodilators**: salbutamol 5 mg BD–QID, ipratropium 500 μg BD
- **Oral corticosteroid**: prednisolone 40 mg daily × 5–7 days
- **Antibiotics**: as above, or add fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin 500 mg daily) if severe or high risk of resistant organisms
- **Monitoring**: ABG if respiratory distress, PaCO₂ >50 mmHg or pH <7.35; ECG (arrhythmia risk); CXR to exclude alternative diagnosis (pneumonia, pneumothorax, PE)
- **Non-invasive ventilation (NIV)**: if PaCO₂ >50 mmHg and pH <7.35 despite optimised medical therapy; CPAP or BiPAP via mask; reduces intubation risk and mortality

**Prevention of recurrent exacerbations**
- Optimise baseline therapy (escalate to higher GOLD group if ≥2 exacerbations/year)
- Smoking cessation
- Vaccinations: annual influenza, pneumococcal, COVID-19
- Early treatment of respiratory infections
- Action plan for patient recognition and early intervention

### Oxygen therapy

**Indications for long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT)**
- **Resting hypoxaemia**: PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg or SpO₂ ≤88% on two readings ≥3 weeks apart, at sea level, on room air
- **Resting PaO₂ 56–59 mmHg or SpO₂ 89% plus cor pulmonale** (RV dysfunction, elevated JVP, ankle oedema) or secondary polycythaemia (Hb >160 g/L)
- **Nocturnal hypoxaemia alone** not an indication; consider if daytime borderline
- **Exertional desaturation** (SpO₂ <88% on exertion): oxygen for exercise if symptomatic benefit confirmed

**LTOT dosing and target**
- Titrate to maintain SpO₂ 88–92% (higher targets increase CO₂ retention)
- **Concentrator at home**: standard; patient compliance superior to cylinder
- **Cylinder/portable supply**: for mobility (travel, outings)
- **Duration**: typically ≥16 hours daily; minimum 15 hours for mortality benefit
- **Compliance monitoring**: ask patient about adherence; poor compliance predicts poor outcome

**Nocturnal oxygen**
- Consider if SpO₂ <88% during sleep despite acceptable daytime SpO₂
- Improves sleep quality and may slow pulmonary hypertension progression

**Exertional oxygen**
- Prescribe if SpO₂ drops to <88% during exertion and oxygen improves symptoms and walk distance
- Reduces dyspnoea sensation; enables activity participation

### Pulmonary rehabilitation

**Indications**
- **Moderate-severe COPD** (FEV₁ <60% predicted): significantly improves exercise capacity, dyspnoea, and quality of life
- **Any symptomatic COPD** if activity limited despite optimal pharmacotherapy
- **Particularly effective** post-exacerbation hospitalisation

**Components**
- **Exercise training**: supervised aerobic (cycling, walking) and resistance (leg, arm weights) 2–3 sessions weekly
- **Education**: COPD pathophysiology, self-management, recognising exacerbations, medication use, airway clearance techniques
- **Psychological support**: anxiety, depression, social isolation (common comorbidities)
- **Nutritional assessment**: weight loss, muscle wasting risk (associated with poor prognosis)
- **Duration**: 8–12 weeks minimum; maintenance programme following completion

**Outcomes**
- Improves 6-minute walk distance, quadriceps strength, dyspnoea sensation
- Reduces anxiety/depression, healthcare utilisation
- Benefits plateau at ~12 weeks; maintenance programme keeps improvements

### Management of comorbidities

**Cardiovascular disease**
- COPD patients have ≥2× cardiovascular mortality risk (smoking, systemic inflammation)
- Manage hypertension (target <140/90 mmHg), dyslipidaemia (statin if CVD risk >5%), and antiplatelet therapy per CVD guidelines
- Beta-blockers safe in COPD if cardioselective (e.g. bisoprolol); avoid non-selective
- Monitor for COPD-induced pulmonary hypertension if cor pulmonale develops

**Osteoporosis**
- ICS therapy increases fracture risk (even inhaled)
- Screen with DEXA if on ICS >3 months or postmenopausal women
- Manage with calcium 1000 mg daily + vitamin D 800 IU daily; bisphosphonate if T-score <-2.5

**Anxiety and depression**
- Present in ~40% COPD; worsen dyspnoea perception, reduce exercise participation
- Screen with PHQ-9 or GAD-7
- Treat with SSRI (sertraline, citalopram); psychological therapy (CBT) effective

**Sleep apnoea (OSA)**
- COPD-OSA overlap increases mortality; assess for daytime somnolence, witnessed apnoeas
- CPAP improves survival; coordinate with sleep specialist if overlap suspected

### Special populations

**COPD with emphysema and bullae**
- Large bullae (>1 cm) can compress functioning lung; if >30% hemithorax involvement and FEV₁ <45% predicted, lung volume reduction surgery may be considered (specialist referral)

**COPD with bronchiectasis**
- Increased airway infection risk; manage with airway clearance techniques, mucolytics (carbocisteine), prophylactic antibiotics if recurrent infections
- Some COPD (especially AAT-deficient) develops bronchiectasis; imaging (HRCT) may reveal traction bronchiectasis

**COPD with lung cancer**
- Smoking history increases lung cancer risk; screening CT for high-risk patients (current or ex-smoker ≥30 pack-years) age 50–75 improves outcomes
- Co-existing lung cancer worsens COPD prognosis; manage multidisciplinary

**COPD in pregnancy**
- Rare (COPD typically affects older population)
- Continue LABA/LAMA; avoid ICS if not already on (minimal evidence); salbutamol for exacerbations
- Smoking cessation essential

### End-of-life and palliative care

**When to introduce palliative care discussions**
- FEV₁ <25% predicted or frequent exacerbations (≥3/year) requiring hospitalisation
- Cor pulmonale, recurrent respiratory failure, or palliative oxygen dependence
- Patient preference for comfort over aggressive treatment

**Goals of care conversations**
- Discuss life expectancy (median survival FEV₁ <25% is 4–5 years, but highly variable)
- Clarify preferences: intubation status, escalation limits, symptom management vs life prolongation
- Introduce advance care planning, discuss DNR if appropriate

**Symptom palliation in advanced COPD**
- **Dyspnoea**: oxygen (if not already prescribed), opioids (morphine 5–10 mg 4-hourly for dyspnoea refractory to other measures), benzodiazepines (lorazepam if anxiety component)
- **Cough**: suppressed with low-dose morphine, glyceryl trinitrate spray (if COPD induced pulmonary oedema), nebulised saline
- **Anxiety**: benzodiazepine, psychological support
- **Fatigue**: address underlying anaemia, depression, nutritional status

### Monitoring and follow-up

**Stable COPD review** (3–6 monthly)
- Symptom control (mMRC, CAT score)
- Exacerbation frequency in past months
- Inhaler technique and adherence
- Smoking status
- SpO₂ or ABG if O₂ therapy

**Annual spirometry**
- Document FEV₁ trajectory; >100 mL/year decline suggests accelerated loss (review exposure, smoking status)
- Baseline for future comparison

**Vaccinations**
- Annual influenza vaccine (inactivated; live LAIV not recommended in COPD)
- One-off pneumococcal: PPSV23 (23-valent) or newer PCV20 (13-valent conjugate + 23-valent polysaccharide in two doses)
- COVID-19 vaccine (annual booster)
