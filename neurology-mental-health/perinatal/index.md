<p>Perinatal mental health problems - spanning pregnancy through the first postnatal year - affect up to 20% of women and around 10% of partners.<sup>1</sup> They are the leading cause of maternal mortality in high-income countries when indirect causes are included.<sup>2</sup> They are also the most commonly missed.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Screening and assessment</p>
  <p>The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the validated screening tool for the perinatal period. A score of at least 13 warrants clinical assessment. The EPDS is recommended at the booking visit and 6-week postnatal check in NZ, though implementation is inconsistent.<sup>3</sup> A score in the 10-12 range warrants a follow-up conversation even if it does not meet threshold - this is often where people are carrying significant distress that they have not disclosed.</p>
  <p>Partners matter. Paternal perinatal depression is underscreened and undertreated; offer an EPDS or PHQ-9 to partners at the same contact if they are present. Also screen at each contact rather than only at scheduled timepoints - onset across the perinatal period is not confined to the first postnatal weeks.</p>
  <p>Ask about suicidal ideation directly. Maternal suicide is a real risk in the perinatal period and is systematically underestimated by clinicians - do not let the context of new motherhood lower your threshold for direct enquiry.</p>
</div>
