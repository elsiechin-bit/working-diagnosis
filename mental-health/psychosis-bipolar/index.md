<p>Psychosis and bipolar spectrum disorders are less common but carry disproportionate morbidity. Early psychosis - the period surrounding a first episode - is a critical window: duration of untreated psychosis predicts long-term outcome, and DUP under 3 months is associated with significantly better functional recovery.<sup>1</sup></p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Early psychosis and bipolar screening</p>
  <p>Red flags for early psychosis in general practice: social withdrawal over weeks to months, perplexity or odd reasoning, decline in work or study function, new-onset sleep reversal, or a family member reporting the person "isn't themselves." These warrant urgent mental health team referral. In NZ, Early Intervention in Psychosis (EIP) services exist in most DHB localities - referral criteria and access vary; document clearly that you are referring for suspected first-episode psychosis, as this affects triage priority.</p>
  <p>For bipolar disorder: patients present depressed far more often than manic, and hypomania is often experienced as productive and not volunteered. Screen before starting any antidepressant in a patient with recurrent depression. A useful prompt: "Have you ever had a period, lasting at least a few days, where you felt unusually energetic, needed much less sleep, and were significantly more productive or impulsive than usual?" A positive response warrants fuller exploration with a structured tool (MDQ or HCL-32).</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ The GP's role in ongoing care</p>
  <p>In NZ, secondary mental health services hold ongoing care for most patients with psychosis and bipolar I. The GP role is monitoring metabolic effects of antipsychotics (weight, glucose, lipids, prolactin where indicated), medication adherence, and physical health. This is a genuinely important function that is often under-resourced within secondary services, and the GP is frequently the only clinician seeing the patient for physical health concerns.</p>
