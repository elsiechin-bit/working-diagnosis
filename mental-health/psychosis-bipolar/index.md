<p>Psychosis and bipolar spectrum disorders are less common but carry disproportionate morbidity. Early psychosis -- the period surrounding a first episode -- is a critical window: duration of untreated psychosis (DUP) predicts long-term outcome, and DUP under 3 months is associated with significantly better functional recovery.<sup>1</sup></p>

<h2>Early psychosis: what to watch for</h2>

<p>Red flags in general practice: social withdrawal over weeks to months, perplexity or odd reasoning, decline in work or study function, new-onset sleep reversal, or a family member reporting the person "isn't themselves." These warrant urgent mental health team referral, not watchful waiting.</p>

<p>In NZ, Early Intervention in Psychosis (EIP) services exist in most DHB localities -- referral criteria and access vary. Document clearly when you are referring for suspected first-episode psychosis, as this affects triage priority.</p>

<h2>Bipolar disorder: the missed diagnosis</h2>

<p>Bipolar I and II are frequently misdiagnosed as unipolar depression -- partly because patients present depressed more often than manic, and partly because hypomania is often experienced as productive and not volunteered. Always screen for past hypomanic or manic episodes before starting an antidepressant in a patient with recurrent depression. Antidepressant monotherapy in unrecognised bipolar disorder can precipitate mixed states or rapid cycling.<sup>2</sup></p>

<p>A useful screening prompt: "Have you ever had a period, lasting at least a few days, where you felt unusually energetic, needed much less sleep, and were significantly more productive or impulsive than usual?" A positive response warrants fuller exploration with a structured tool (MDQ or HCL-32).</p>

<h2>The GP's role in ongoing care</h2>

<p>In NZ, secondary mental health services hold ongoing care for most patients with psychosis and bipolar I. The GP role is monitoring metabolic effects of antipsychotics (weight, glucose, lipids, prolactin where indicated), medication adherence, and physical health -- a genuinely important function that is often under-resourced. Clozapine monitoring in particular carries specific requirements; ensure the patient is enrolled in the appropriate registry.</p>
