Psychosis and bipolar disorder — what to know if you or someone close to you is affected

These are serious conditions, but they are treatable. Getting help early makes a real difference. This page is for people who have been told they may have one of these conditions, and for the whanau and family around them.

Psychosis is not a diagnosis on its own — it is a collection of experiences that can arise in several different conditions. The core features are:

  • Hallucinations — hearing, seeing, or sensing things that others do not perceive. Hearing voices is the most common.
  • Delusions — strongly held beliefs that are not based in reality and that persist despite evidence to the contrary.
  • Disorganised thinking — difficulty following a train of thought, speech that is hard to follow, making connections that others cannot.

People experiencing psychosis are usually not aware that their perceptions are not real. It is a medical condition, not a moral failing or a sign of violence. Most people with psychosis recover well, especially with early treatment.

Bipolar disorder involves episodes of depression and episodes of elevated or irritable mood (mania or hypomania) that are more extreme than ordinary mood variation.

Bipolar I involves full manic episodes — very high energy, reduced need for sleep, grandiosity, impulsive decisions, sometimes psychosis.

Bipolar II involves hypomanic episodes (less severe than full mania) alongside depressive episodes.

The depressive episodes of bipolar disorder often look exactly like ordinary depression, which is why bipolar is frequently missed or misdiagnosed. People are often not aware that their high periods are a problem — they may feel productive and energised, and only recognise them as problematic in retrospect.

For psychosis:

  • Gradual social withdrawal
  • Saying or believing things that seem odd or out of character
  • Becoming suspicious or guarded
  • Difficulty concentrating or following conversations
  • Declining function at work or study
  • Not sleeping for extended periods

For a manic episode:

  • Sleeping much less than usual without feeling tired
  • Talking faster than usual
  • Spending money or making decisions impulsively
  • Feeling unusually powerful, special, or certain
  • Becoming irritable or aggressive when others raise concern

Recognising these patterns early — and having a plan — makes a significant difference to outcomes.

If you or someone you know is showing these signs, contact your GP as soon as possible. GPs can initiate referral to mental health services, which in most NZ regions include Early Intervention in Psychosis (EIP) teams for first episodes. The time between symptoms starting and effective treatment beginning matters: shorter is better. Do not wait to see if it settles on its own.

Medications for psychosis (antipsychotics) and bipolar disorder (mood stabilisers such as lithium or valproate, plus antipsychotics for acute mania) are effective and available in NZ. Most people find a regimen that works with support from their mental health team.

Supporting someone with psychosis or bipolar disorder is genuinely hard work. Some things that help:

  • Stay calm, do not argue with delusions, and focus on safety and connection rather than trying to convince the person of what is real.
  • Express concern in terms of behaviour you have observed ("I've noticed you haven't been sleeping") rather than diagnosis.
  • Know when to escalate: if the person is in immediate danger to themselves or others, call 111.
  • Learn about the condition — the Mental Health Foundation and Bipolar New Zealand have accessible resources.
  • Look after your own wellbeing; carer burnout is real and support is available.
This is an emergency

Call 111 or go to your nearest emergency department immediately if:

  • the person is in immediate danger of harming themselves or others
  • behaviour is severely disorganised and the person cannot care for themselves
  • there is a first episode of psychosis that appears to be escalating rapidly

Do not wait to see if it improves. Time matters.
