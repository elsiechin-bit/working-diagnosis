<p>One in five New Zealanders will experience a mental health problem in any given year.<sup>1</sup> The majority will first present -- or never present at all -- to a GP. These pages are a working reference for those consultations: what to look for, what the evidence actually supports, and what to do next in a fifteen-minute slot.</p>

<p>A note on framing: <em>diagnosis names are shorthand, not verdicts.</em> They guide treatment decisions; they shouldn't foreclose curiosity about why this person, at this time, is struggling.</p>

<h2>Across all presentations</h2>

<p><em>Ask about sleep, exercise, and connection</em> -- not as lifestyle platitudes, but as data. Sleep disturbance is both a symptom and a perpetuating factor across virtually every mental health condition. Exercise has antidepressant effect sizes of 0.43--0.66 in meta-analyses, comparable to pharmacotherapy in mild-to-moderate illness.<sup>2</sup> Social isolation amplifies almost everything.</p>

<p>And ask about whanau. NZ general practice sits inside a bicultural context that mainstream clinical frameworks mostly ignore. Maori mental health is shaped by determinants -- colonisation, land loss, institutional racism, socioeconomic disadvantage -- that are not captured in a PHQ-9 score. Kaupapa Maori services (like Maori Mental Health teams and Whanau Ora) offer pathways that are more culturally safe for many patients. Knowing what's available locally matters.</p>
