<p>Perinatal mental health problems - spanning pregnancy through the first postnatal year - affect up to 20% of women and around 10% of partners.<sup>1</sup> They are the leading cause of maternal mortality in high-income countries when indirect causes are included.<sup>2</sup> They are also the most commonly missed.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Screening and assessment</p>
  <p>The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the validated screening tool for the perinatal period. A score of at least 13 warrants clinical assessment. The EPDS is recommended at the booking visit and 6-week postnatal check in NZ, though implementation is inconsistent.<sup>3</sup> A score in the 10-12 range warrants a follow-up conversation even if it does not meet threshold - this is often where people are carrying significant distress that they have not disclosed.</p>
  <p>Partners matter. Paternal perinatal depression is underscreened and undertreated; offer an EPDS or PHQ-9 to partners at the same contact if they are present. Also screen at each contact rather than only at scheduled timepoints - onset across the perinatal period is not confined to the first postnatal weeks.</p>
  <p>Ask about suicidal ideation directly. Maternal suicide is a real risk in the perinatal period and is systematically underestimated by clinicians - do not let the context of new motherhood lower your threshold for direct enquiry.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Treatment and prescribing safety</p>
  <p>Psychological therapies (CBT, IPT) are first-line for mild-to-moderate perinatal depression and anxiety. For moderate-to-severe illness, or where the woman has a pre-existing psychiatric condition, pharmacotherapy is often appropriate and necessary. The benefit-risk calculation is frequently misframed: untreated severe depression in pregnancy carries its own fetal and maternal risks - including preterm birth, low birthweight, poor antenatal care attendance, and impaired mother-infant attachment.</p>
  <p>Sertraline and paroxetine have the most perinatal safety data of the SSRIs.<sup>4</sup> Discuss PPHN risk (small but real with SSRI use late in pregnancy) and neonatal adaptation syndrome (jitteriness, poor feeding, transient hypotonia in the first days of life - self-limiting, not a reason to avoid treatment in moderate-severe illness). The risk of untreated depression to the infant through impaired bonding and development is generally greater than the risk of SSRI exposure.</p>
  <p>Breastfeeding: sertraline and paroxetine have the best safety data for breastfeeding. Infant serum levels are generally low. Assess each case individually; the benefits of breastfeeding should be weighed against treatment risks.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Postpartum psychosis and high-risk management</p>
  <p>Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency with onset typically within days to 2 weeks of delivery. Features: rapid onset confusion, emotional lability, perceptual disturbance (hallucinations, delusions), and behaviour that is out of character. It requires same-day secondary care referral without exception. Arrange urgent assessment and do not leave the person alone with the infant until assessed. Maternal suicide and infanticide are both real risks.</p>
  <p>Women with a personal history of bipolar disorder or a previous episode of postpartum psychosis have a 25-50% risk of recurrence after a subsequent delivery. These patients require a perinatal mental health plan before delivery in collaboration with secondary care. In NZ, perinatal psychiatry services are available in major centres - early referral in pregnancy for women with significant psychiatric history is best practice.</p>
  <p>For women with pre-existing psychiatric conditions on regular medication: do not discontinue medication without specialist input. Lithium requires specific monitoring around delivery (levels fluctuate significantly with fluid shifts). Valproate must not be used in pregnancy due to teratogenicity. Women of reproductive age on valproate require a PHARMAC-mandated risk acknowledgement form and highly effective contraception.</p>
</div>
