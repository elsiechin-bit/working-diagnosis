<p>Perinatal mental health problems -- spanning pregnancy through the first postnatal year -- affect up to 20% of women and around 10% of partners.<sup>1</sup> They are the leading cause of maternal mortality in high-income countries when indirect causes are included.<sup>2</sup> They are also the most commonly missed.</p>

<h2>Screening</h2>

<p>The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the validated screening tool for the perinatal period; a score ≥13 warrants clinical assessment. The EPDS is recommended at the booking visit and 6-week postnatal check in NZ, though implementation is inconsistent.<sup>3</sup> A score in the 10--12 range warrants a follow-up conversation even if it doesn't meet threshold -- this is often where people are hiding significant distress.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Psychological therapies are first-line for mild-to-moderate perinatal depression and anxiety. For moderate-to-severe illness, or where the woman has a pre-existing psychiatric condition, pharmacotherapy is often appropriate. The benefit-risk calculation here is frequently misframed: untreated severe depression in pregnancy carries its own fetal and maternal risks. Sertraline and paroxetine have the most perinatal safety data; discuss PPHN risk (small but real for SSRIs late in pregnancy) and neonatal adaptation syndrome.<sup>4</sup></p>

<p>Partners matter. Paternal perinatal depression is underscreened and undertreated; offer an EPDS (or PHQ-9) to partners at the same contact if they are present.</p>

<h2>Postpartum psychosis</h2>

<p>Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency: rapid onset within days of delivery, confusion, emotional lability, perceptual disturbance, and behaviour that is out of character. It requires same-day secondary care referral without exception. Maternal suicide is a real risk in the perinatal period and is underestimated by clinicians -- do not let the context of new motherhood lower your threshold for direct enquiry.</p>
