<p>Major depressive disorder affects around 6% of New Zealanders at any one time.<sup>1</sup> The PHQ-9 remains the most validated brief screening tool in primary care, with a score ≥10 having sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 85% for major depression.<sup>2</sup> But the score is a prompt, not a diagnosis -- always ask about duration, functional impact, and suicidality directly.</p>

<h2>What the evidence supports</h2>

<p>For mild-to-moderate depression, structured exercise, behavioural activation, and brief psychological therapies (particularly CBT) have effect sizes comparable to antidepressants with fewer adverse effects.<sup>3</sup> For moderate-to-severe depression, SSRIs remain first-line pharmacotherapy. Sertraline has the best evidence base for combined efficacy and tolerability across meta-analyses.<sup>4</sup> Advise patients that onset of effect takes 2--4 weeks and that discontinuing abruptly after several months carries a real discontinuation syndrome risk.</p>

<p>In NZ primary care: BPAC's depression management pathway and HealthPathways Canterbury are the most pragmatic local references. Access and Choice funding (2022 onwards) has expanded funded brief-therapy access substantially -- check your local Primary Mental Health and Addictions (PMHA) service for current referral criteria.</p>

<h2>Practical notes</h2>

<p>Recurrent depression (two or more episodes) warrants a longer maintenance phase -- at least 2 years of treatment after remission reduces relapse risk significantly. Review the diagnosis at each episode: persistent low mood with atypical features, seasonality, or a history suggesting hypomanic periods warrants bipolar screening before adding or increasing antidepressants.</p>

<p>Treatment-resistant depression (failure of two adequate antidepressant trials) should prompt specialist liaison. In NZ, psychiatry waitlists are long -- document clearly when you have reached this threshold so the referral can be appropriately triaged.</p>
