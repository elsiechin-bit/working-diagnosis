<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>PHQ-9 ≥10 has sensitivity 88%, specificity 85% for major depression, but the score is a prompt not a diagnosis. Always ask about duration, functional impact, and suicidality directly.<sup>2</sup></li>
    <li>Mild-to-moderate depression: structured exercise, behavioural activation, and brief CBT have effect sizes comparable to antidepressants with fewer adverse effects.<sup>3</sup></li>
    <li>Moderate-to-severe depression: SSRIs first line. Sertraline has the best evidence for combined efficacy and tolerability across meta-analyses.<sup>4</sup></li>
    <li>Access and Choice funding (PMHA services) has expanded funded brief-therapy access substantially. Check your local PMHA service for current referral criteria before prescribing by default.</li>
    <li>Two or more episodes warrants at least 2 years of maintenance treatment after remission to reduce relapse risk.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Major depressive disorder affects around 6% of New Zealanders at any one time.<sup>1</sup> The gap between presentation and adequate treatment remains wide in primary care, partly because mild-to-moderate depression is often undertreated with non-pharmacological options that have strong evidence and fewer side effects than antidepressants.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Assessment and PHQ-9 use</p>
  <p>
    The PHQ-9 is the most validated brief screening tool for primary care and is a useful baseline and monitoring instrument. Score ≥10 suggests probable major depression; ≥15 is moderate-to-severe. However, the score does not replace clinical assessment. At every presentation: ask about duration of symptoms (minimum 2 weeks for MDD diagnosis), functional impairment in work and relationships, and suicidal ideation directly. The PHQ-9 item 9 prompts this but a direct conversation is more sensitive.
  </p>
  <p>
    Consider comorbid anxiety (GAD-7 is a useful paired screen), substance use, and significant psychosocial stressors. Document the PHQ-9 score as a baseline for monitoring treatment response.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Treatment: mild to moderate depression</p>
  <p>
    For PHQ-9 10-14 (mild-to-moderate), offer non-pharmacological treatment as first line. Structured exercise (150 min/week moderate intensity) has a meaningful effect size in meta-analyses and should be actively recommended, not mentioned in passing. Behavioural activation and brief CBT have comparable efficacy to SSRIs in this range with a more favourable side effect profile.<sup>3</sup>
  </p>
  <p>
    Access and Choice funding has substantially expanded funded psychological therapy access via Primary Mental Health and Addictions (PMHA) services since 2022. Contact your local PMHA service for current referral criteria and wait times. In Canterbury, HealthPathways has up-to-date local referral pathways.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Treatment: moderate to severe depression</p>
  <p>
    For PHQ-9 ≥15, or where non-pharmacological treatment has failed or is not feasible, SSRIs are first-line pharmacotherapy. Sertraline (50-200 mg daily) is the preferred agent based on the largest network meta-analysis to date: best balance of efficacy and tolerability across 21 antidepressants.<sup>4</sup> Fluoxetine is a reasonable alternative, particularly where cost is a consideration.
  </p>
  <p>
    Advise patients clearly: therapeutic onset takes 2-4 weeks; side effects (nausea, initial anxiety) typically peak in the first 1-2 weeks and then resolve. If stopping after several months of treatment, taper over at least 4 weeks to reduce discontinuation syndrome risk. Review at 2 weeks after starting, then 4-6 weekly until stable.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Recurrent depression and treatment resistance</p>
  <p>
    Recurrent depression (two or more episodes) warrants a longer maintenance phase. Current evidence supports at least 2 years of continued treatment after remission to reduce relapse risk; some patients with three or more episodes benefit from indefinite maintenance. Have this conversation explicitly rather than relying on default repeat prescribing.
  </p>
  <p>
    At each new episode, review the diagnosis. Persistent low mood with atypical features (hypersomnia, mood reactivity, leaden paralysis), marked seasonality, or any history suggesting hypomanic periods warrants bipolar screening before adding or increasing antidepressants. Starting antidepressants in unrecognised bipolar II disorder can precipitate mixed states.
  </p>
  <p>
    Treatment-resistant depression (TRD) is generally defined as failure of two adequate antidepressant trials (adequate dose, adequate duration of at least 6 weeks). At this point, document clearly that you have reached the TRD threshold and refer to psychiatry. NZ psychiatry waitlists are long; a well-documented referral with a clear clinical formulation is more likely to be triaged appropriately. In Canterbury, consider an advice and liaison call to the secondary mental health service before referring formally.
  </p>
</div>
