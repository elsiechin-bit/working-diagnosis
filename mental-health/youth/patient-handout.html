Youth mental health — getting help when things feel hard

A lot of young people in New Zealand are struggling right now. If that is you, or if it is someone you care about, this page explains what is going on and where to find support. You do not have to manage alone.

Mental health difficulties in young people do not always look the way you might expect. Young people are less likely to say "I feel depressed" and more likely to show:

  • Irritability and anger rather than sadness
  • Stomach aches, headaches, or other physical symptoms with no clear cause
  • Avoiding school, social situations, or things they used to enjoy
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Spending much more time alone
  • Dropping grades or losing interest in activities

Anxiety in young people often looks like extreme worry about school performance, friendships, or social situations. It can also show up as physical symptoms before stressful events.

These are not attention-seeking behaviours or phases that can simply be pushed through. They are signs that the nervous system is struggling and that support is needed.

Self-harm means hurting your own body as a way of coping with emotional pain. It is more common in young people than most adults realise. It does not mean someone wants to die. It is usually a way of dealing with feelings that feel unbearable, or of feeling something physical when emotions feel numb.

If you are self-harming, or if someone you know is: you are not alone, and it does not make you broken or dangerous. The first step is telling someone you trust. That might be a friend, a parent, a school counsellor, or your GP. If you are not ready to tell someone in person, you can text 1737.

Recovery is possible with the right support. If you are self-harming and feel unsafe right now, please call 1737, call Youthline on 0800 376 633, or go to your nearest emergency department.

Eating disorders, including anorexia nervosa, bulimia, and binge eating disorder, are serious mental health conditions that can have significant physical consequences. Warning signs include:

  • Extreme concern about weight or shape
  • Restricting food intake well below what is needed
  • Eating in secret or disappearing to the bathroom after meals
  • Using exercise in a driven, excessive way
  • Noticeable weight loss or weight fluctuation

Eating disorders have the highest mortality of any psychiatric condition. Early help dramatically improves outcomes. If you or someone you know shows these signs, raise it with a GP as soon as possible. Do not wait until the person is "sick enough."

Talking to someone who is struggling can feel very hard. Some suggestions:

  • Choose a private, low-pressure moment rather than a formal sit-down conversation.
  • Lead with care: "I've noticed you seem really stressed lately and I wanted to check in."
  • Listen without immediately trying to fix things.
  • Do not promise to keep secrets if they share something that puts them at risk. It is okay to say: "I care about you too much to keep this to myself."
  • If you are seriously worried about someone's safety, tell an adult or call for help.

Being a good friend does not mean carrying this alone.

Your GP is a good starting point. They will not judge you, they are bound by confidentiality rules (with some safety exceptions), and they can refer you to the right support. You can ask to be seen without a parent or caregiver present if you are old enough to consent to care for yourself.

School counsellors are another low-barrier option. The following services are available 24/7:

If you need help right now
  • 1737 - Free call or text, 24/7, trained counsellors
  • Youthline - 0800 376 633, youth-focused, free, available evenings and nights
  • Need to Talk? - Text or call 1737, anonymous and free
  • In immediate danger: Call 111 or go to your nearest emergency department
