Alcohol and drug problems - getting support in New Zealand

Problem alcohol or drug use is common, and it is treatable. Asking for help is the hardest step. Your GP is a good place to start - there is no judgment, and there are real options available.

It is not simply about how much you use. Substance use becomes a problem when it is affecting areas of your life that matter to you: your health, your relationships, your work, your finances, or your sense of control.

Signs that use may have crossed a line include:

  • Using more than you intended
  • Finding it hard to cut back even when you want to
  • Spending a lot of time using, recovering, or thinking about it
  • Giving up things you used to enjoy
  • Continuing to use despite negative consequences
  • Needing more to get the same effect
  • Feeling unwell when you stop (withdrawal)

None of this means you are a bad person or that recovery is not possible. It means your brain has adapted to a substance in a way that requires support to address.

Alcohol is the most common substance that causes harm in New Zealand. Drinking at high levels over time damages the liver, heart, brain, and immune system. Alcohol is also a depressant: it may feel like it helps with anxiety or sleep in the short term, but it worsens both over time.

If you are drinking heavily and want to cut back:

If you drink large amounts daily, do not stop abruptly without medical advice. Sudden alcohol withdrawal can cause serious symptoms, including seizures, in people who are physically dependent. Your GP can help you detoxify safely.

For hazardous drinking that has not reached dependence, a brief conversation with your GP about your patterns and goals (called a brief intervention) has good evidence for reducing drinking.

Cannabis use disorder affects around 1 in 10 people who use cannabis regularly. It is characterised by difficulty stopping despite wanting to, and continued use that interferes with function. Treatment is through counselling (motivational interviewing and CBT). There is no approved medication.

Methamphetamine (P) is a significant issue in New Zealand. It causes severe psychological dependence and can trigger psychosis. Harm reduction, motivational interviewing, and connection to addiction services are the main tools.

Opioid use disorder (from prescribed or illicit opioids) can be treated with buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone), which is funded in NZ. This medication markedly reduces overdose risk and supports recovery. Your GP may be able to prescribe this, or can refer you to a service that does.

Starting a conversation with your GP is a genuine first step. You do not need to have hit "rock bottom." Your GP can:

  • Screen for the level of use and related harm
  • Offer brief advice and motivational support
  • Prescribe medication where appropriate (buprenorphine for opioids, diazepam for supervised alcohol withdrawal)
  • Refer you to specialist addiction services
  • Connect you with peer support

Addiction counselling and residential programmes are also available. The approach that works best varies by person and substance. Most people who seek help do reduce or stop their use - often with more than one attempt.

If you are worried about someone else's alcohol or drug use, it is important to know: you cannot force someone to change who is not ready.

What you can do:

  • Express concern from a place of care rather than blame
  • Set clear limits about what you will and will not accept
  • Look after your own wellbeing (Al-Anon and Nar-Anon are peer support groups for family members)
  • Encourage them to speak with their GP or an addiction service

If someone is in immediate danger due to intoxication or overdose, call 111.

Alcohol withdrawal - do not stop suddenly

If you or someone you know drinks heavily every day, stopping alcohol suddenly can cause dangerous withdrawal: sweating, tremor, confusion, and in serious cases, seizures. This is a medical situation. Talk to your GP before stopping. Safe supported withdrawal (detox) is available.
