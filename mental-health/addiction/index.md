<p>Substance use disorders are common, under-screened, and underestimated in clinical complexity. Alcohol alone contributes to around 800 deaths and 18,000 hospitalisations annually in NZ.<sup>1</sup> Cannabis use disorder affects an estimated 9% of those who ever use, rising to 17% in daily users.<sup>2</sup></p>

<h2>Brief intervention for alcohol</h2>

<p>For hazardous alcohol use (AUDIT-C ≥5, or AUDIT ≥8), a structured 5--15 minute brief motivational intervention reduces consumption at 12 months with a number needed to treat of around 8.<sup>3</sup> This is one of the better NNTs in primary care and is deliverable within a standard consultation. The key components are: feedback on risk, clear advice, exploration of ambivalence, and a plan. Don't save it for a dedicated appointment -- seize the consultation you have.</p>

<h2>Opioid use disorder</h2>

<p>Buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone) is funded in NZ and is the evidence-based standard of care for opioid use disorder. Methadone remains available via specialist programmes. GPs with appropriate training can prescribe buprenorphine -- the NZAM and BPAC provide local guidance. Withholding or delaying opioid agonist therapy substantially increases overdose mortality risk.<sup>4</sup> If you are not yet an OAT prescriber, warm referral to a service that is makes a material difference to outcomes.</p>

<h2>Alcohol withdrawal</h2>

<p>For alcohol use disorder with physical dependence, ambulatory detoxification with a diazepam reducing regimen (CIWA-Ar guided) is appropriate for low-to-moderate severity withdrawal. High-severity presentations -- prior seizures, high AUDIT scores, significant comorbidity, or poor social support -- warrant inpatient assessment. Document clearly when ambulatory withdrawal is chosen and ensure daily review capacity.</p>

<h2>Cannabis and other substances</h2>

<p>Cannabis use disorder responds to motivational enhancement therapy and CBT; there is no approved pharmacotherapy. Methamphetamine use is a significant issue in NZ; presentations are often via crisis or via concern from family. Harm reduction, motivational interviewing, and connection to addiction services (CADS in Auckland; local DHB addiction services elsewhere) are the practical tools available.</p>
