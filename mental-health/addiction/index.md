<p>Substance use disorders are common, under-screened, and underestimated in clinical complexity. Alcohol alone contributes to around 800 deaths and 18,000 hospitalisations annually in NZ.<sup>1</sup> Cannabis use disorder affects an estimated 9% of those who ever use, rising to 17% in daily users.<sup>2</sup></p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Brief intervention for alcohol and opioid use disorder</p>
  <p>For hazardous alcohol use (AUDIT-C ≥5, or AUDIT ≥8), a structured 5-15 minute brief motivational intervention reduces consumption at 12 months with a number needed to treat of around 8.<sup>3</sup> This is one of the better NNTs in primary care and is deliverable within a standard consultation. The key components are: feedback on risk, clear advice, exploration of ambivalence, and a plan. Don't save it for a dedicated appointment - seize the consultation you have.</p>
  <p>Buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone) is funded in NZ and is the evidence-based standard of care for opioid use disorder. Methadone remains available via specialist programmes. GPs with appropriate training can prescribe buprenorphine - the NZAM and BPAC provide local guidance. Withholding or delaying opioid agonist therapy substantially increases overdose mortality risk.<sup>4</sup> If you are not yet an OAT prescriber, a warm referral to a service that is makes a material difference to outcomes.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Alcohol withdrawal and other substances</p>
  <p>For alcohol use disorder with physical dependence, ambulatory detoxification with a diazepam reducing regimen (CIWA-Ar guided) is appropriate for low-to-moderate severity withdrawal. High-severity presentations - prior seizures, high AUDIT scores, significant comorbidity, or poor social support - warrant inpatient assessment. Document clearly when ambulatory withdrawal is chosen and ensure daily review capacity.</p>
  <p>Cannabis use disorder responds to motivational enhancement therapy and CBT; there is no approved pharmacotherapy. Methamphetamine use is a significant issue in NZ; presentations are often via crisis or via concern from family. Harm reduction, motivational interviewing, and connection to addiction services (CADS in Auckland; local DHB addiction services elsewhere) are the practical tools available.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ NZ access and referral</p>
  <p>CADS (Community Alcohol and Drug Service) in Auckland provides assessment, brief intervention, and opioid agonist therapy. Equivalent DHB addiction services operate in other regions - check your local HealthPathways for current pathways, referral thresholds, and wait times. For alcohol withdrawal in the community, ensure a clear safety net: daily contact or review during the first 72 hours, written instructions for the patient and a support person, and explicit criteria for when to go to the ED. Seizure risk peaks at 24-48 hours; Wernicke's encephalopathy risk is highest in malnourished patients and requires thiamine replacement before any carbohydrate load.</p>
  <p>For patients with co-occurring mental health and substance use disorders, the two should ideally be treated concurrently rather than sequentially. Alcohol worsens depression and anxiety acutely; the common clinical mistake is treating the mental health presentation without addressing the underlying substance use, or deferring mental health treatment until after a detox that never happens.</p>
</div>
