<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>GAD-7 ≥10 is the threshold for probable generalised anxiety disorder. Use it as a baseline and monitoring tool, not a diagnosis in isolation.</li>
    <li>CBT has the strongest evidence base across all anxiety disorders and effects that outlast pharmacotherapy at follow-up. Access and Choice (PMHA) funding has expanded funded access substantially - check local criteria before defaulting to prescribing.<sup>3</sup></li>
    <li>SSRIs and SNRIs are effective adjuncts or alternatives where therapy is unavailable or declined. Start low, titrate slowly - anxiety disorders are more sensitive to initial activating side effects than depression.</li>
    <li><strong>Do not prescribe benzodiazepines as first-line treatment.</strong> Short-term relief, worse long-term outcomes, significant dependence risk, and particular harm in older adults and those with concurrent substance use.<sup>4</sup></li>
    <li>Panic disorder: explaining the panic cycle (hyperventilation → hypocapnia → paraesthesia → more fear) takes ten minutes and is often all that mild cases need. This is underused.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health conditions in New Zealand and globally.<sup>1</sup> GAD, panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and health anxiety each present differently and respond to different approaches. Conflating them leads to under-treatment. The consultation task is accurate diagnosis and directing treatment at the right target.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Assessment and diagnosis</p>
  <p>
    The GAD-7 performs well for GAD and as a general anxiety severity screen across disorders (threshold ≥10 for probable GAD).<sup>2</sup> Pair it with the PHQ-9 in most presentations since depression and anxiety co-occur frequently and each can mask the other. Document baseline scores for monitoring treatment response.
  </p>
  <p>
    Distinguish between disorder subtypes at assessment, as treatment differs:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>GAD:</strong> persistent, generalised worry across multiple domains, difficult to control, present more days than not for at least 6 months</li>
    <li><strong>Panic disorder:</strong> recurrent unexpected panic attacks plus persistent concern about future attacks or behavioural change because of them</li>
    <li><strong>Social anxiety disorder:</strong> fear of scrutiny or humiliation in social situations, often misread as introversion or low confidence</li>
    <li><strong>Health anxiety:</strong> excessive worry about having or developing a serious illness, disproportionate to symptoms or medical findings</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Rule out medical causes in new presentations: hyperthyroidism, arrhythmia, stimulant or caffeine use, medication side effects (salbutamol, corticosteroids), and alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Treatment: psychological and pharmacological</p>
  <p>
    CBT is the gold standard psychological treatment and should be offered first. Refer to a PMHA-funded brief therapy service before prescribing in mild-to-moderate presentations. In Canterbury, check HealthPathways for current PMHA referral criteria and wait times.
  </p>
  <p>
    When pharmacotherapy is used: SSRIs and SNRIs are first-line. Sertraline, escitalopram, and venlafaxine have good evidence across multiple anxiety disorder subtypes. Start at half the usual antidepressant dose and titrate slowly over 2-4 weeks - anxiety disorders show heightened sensitivity to the initial activating effects of SSRIs and starting too high drives non-adherence. Advise patients that therapeutic benefit takes 4-6 weeks.
  </p>
  <p>
    Pregabalin has evidence for GAD and may be used when SSRIs are not tolerated. It carries dependence risk and should not be co-prescribed with opioids without careful review.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Panic disorder and health anxiety: specific approaches</p>
  <p>
    <strong>Panic disorder:</strong> psychoeducation alone is effective in mild cases and takes one structured ten-minute explanation. The panic cycle: a perceived threat triggers sympathetic arousal → hyperventilation → hypocapnia → cerebral vasoconstriction and peripheral paraesthesia → catastrophic interpretation ("I'm dying/having a heart attack") → more arousal. Naming this loop, normalising the physical sensations as harmless, and explaining that avoidance maintains the disorder is often enough to interrupt it. Breathing retraining (slow, diaphragmatic) addresses the hyperventilation component.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Health anxiety:</strong> reassurance temporarily reduces anxiety and then raises the threshold required for the next reassurance - this is the reassurance trap. Repeated investigation reinforces the belief that symptoms require investigation. Brief CBT approaches (explaining the model, reducing reassurance-seeking behaviour, and gradually increasing tolerance of uncertainty) are more effective than either repeated investigation or dismissal. Validate the distress while redirecting from symptom monitoring.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Benzodiazepines: when and how to deprescribe</p>
  <p>
    Benzodiazepines provide rapid short-term symptom relief and remain appropriate in specific acute contexts (procedural anxiety, acute panic attack in the emergency setting). They should not be initiated for ongoing anxiety management in general practice. In patients already established on benzodiazepines: assess dependence, explore readiness to taper, and plan a slow reduction - typically 10% of the current dose every 2-4 weeks, with slower reductions at lower doses where withdrawal symptoms are more pronounced.
  </p>
  <p>
    The Ashton Manual provides a widely-used tapering framework. Switching from a short-acting agent (lorazepam, oxazepam) to an equivalent dose of diazepam before tapering improves tolerability. Psychological support during taper significantly improves completion rates.
  </p>
</div>
