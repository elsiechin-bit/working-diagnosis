<p>Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health conditions globally and in NZ.<sup>1</sup> Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and health anxiety each present differently -- and conflating them leads to under-treatment. The GAD-7 (threshold ≥10) performs well for GAD and moderate-to-severe anxiety across disorders.<sup>2</sup></p>

<h2>What the evidence supports</h2>

<p>CBT has the strongest evidence base across anxiety disorders, with effects that outlast those of pharmacotherapy at follow-up.<sup>3</sup> SSRIs and SNRIs are effective adjuncts or alternatives where therapy is unavailable or declined. Benzodiazepines should not be first-line: they provide short-term symptom relief but worsen long-term outcomes, drive tolerance, and carry significant dependence risk -- particularly in older adults and those with concurrent substance use.<sup>4</sup></p>

<h2>Panic disorder: a practical note</h2>

<p>Panic disorder responds particularly well to psychoeducation alone in mild cases. Explaining the physiology of the panic cycle -- hyperventilation → hypocapnia → peripheral vasoconstriction → paraesthesia → more fear -- demystifies the experience and interrupts catastrophic interpretation. This takes ten minutes and is underused.</p>

<h2>Health anxiety</h2>

<p>Health anxiety (illness anxiety disorder, formerly hypochondriasis) is a high-consultation, high-investigation condition that is frequently managed by reassurance -- which paradoxically reinforces the cycle. Brief CBT-based approaches, including an explanation of the reassurance trap, are more effective than repeated investigation or dismissal. Validate the distress while redirecting attention from symptom monitoring.</p>
