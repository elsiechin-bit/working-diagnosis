<p>
  Polymyalgia rheumatica is a common inflammatory condition of older adults, predominantly in those of Northern European descent. It presents with bilateral shoulder and hip girdle stiffness and pain, worse in the morning. It is exquisitely responsive to low-dose corticosteroids - this response is so characteristic that it is both therapeutic and diagnostic. Always exclude giant cell arteritis before starting steroids, as GCA requires higher doses and carries a risk of permanent vision loss if undertreated.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation and screening for GCA</p>
  <p>
    Patients - almost always over 60, with a female predominance of 2:1 - describe sudden-onset bilateral shoulder and hip girdle aching and stiffness. Typical complaints include difficulty combing hair, dressing, rising from a chair, or turning in bed. Morning stiffness persists for more than 45 minutes, often hours. Pain is aching rather than sharp. Constitutional symptoms (low-grade fever, malaise, fatigue, unintentional weight loss) are common.
  </p>
  <p>
    Examination reveals pain on active range of motion of the shoulders and hips but normal passive range of motion - this distinguishes PMR from true arthritis. Synovitis is absent on examination.
  </p>
  <p>
    Before starting steroids, screen specifically for GCA. Ask about: headache (new-onset, temporal, or occipital), jaw claudication (pain with chewing that improves with rest), scalp tenderness (pain on combing or resting head on pillow), and visual symptoms (blurring, diplopia, amaurosis fugax). Palpate the temporal artery for tenderness, nodularity, or absent pulsation. If GCA is suspected, refer urgently to rheumatology and ophthalmology - do not delay; start prednisolone 40-60 mg/day immediately if vision is at risk.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Investigation</p>
  <p>
    Elevated acute-phase reactants are essential: ESR typically above 40, often above 80; CRP also elevated. Both should be checked - they can be discordant, and both are useful for monitoring treatment response.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>FBC: normocytic anaemia and thrombocytosis are common</li>
    <li>Alkaline phosphatase: may be elevated</li>
    <li>TSH: exclude hypothyroidism, which can cause proximal myopathy and fatigue</li>
    <li>RF and anti-CCP: negative in PMR; positive values suggest rheumatoid arthritis or psoriatic arthritis</li>
    <li>ANA: may be mildly positive but non-specific</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The clinical picture plus elevated inflammatory markers is sufficient for diagnosis. Imaging (ultrasound or MRI) is not required; shoulder and hip synovitis may be present but adds little to the clinical diagnosis. Patients with atypical features - very young for the diagnosis, asymmetric involvement, very high inflammatory markers out of proportion to symptoms, or absent stiffness - warrant rheumatology input before treatment.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Management: steroids, taper, and monitoring</p>
  <p>
    Prednisolone 15-20 mg/day is the typical starting dose for PMR without GCA. Patients should experience dramatic clinical improvement within 24-72 hours. This rapid response is pathognomonic - failure to improve substantially within a week suggests the diagnosis should be reconsidered.
