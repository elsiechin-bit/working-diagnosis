<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Bilateral nocturnal lower limb pain in children aged 3-12, typically affecting the posterior thighs, calves, and peri-knee areas. Pain-free periods during the day with normal examination are the hallmarks.<sup>1</sup> The condition is benign and self-limiting, resolving by the early teenage years.</li>
    <li>Diagnosis of exclusion - typical presentations do not require investigation. Routine investigations increase parental anxiety without clinical benefit. The key skill is pattern recognition, not workup.</li>
    <li>Red flags warranting investigation: unilateral pain, daytime pain affecting function, morning stiffness, visible joint swelling or erythema, fever, persistent limp, weight loss, or pain localised to a single joint.</li>
    <li>Management: reassurance plus paracetamol 15 mg/kg or ibuprofen 10 mg/kg during episodes. No activity restriction. Massage or rubbing of the limbs often provides comfort.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Growing pains are the most common cause of lower limb pain in children, affecting 10-15% of the paediatric population at some point.<sup>1</sup> Despite their name, there is no evidence that growth itself causes the pain. These are benign, self-limiting episodes of nocturnal aching in the lower limbs characterised by complete pain-free periods during the day and normal physical examination findings.</p>

  <p>Pain is predominantly nocturnal, often waking the child from sleep, and intermittent rather than constant - episodes are often worse after days of high physical activity. The typical age of onset is 3-12 years, with peaks at 4-6 and 8-12 years. On examination, the child appears well with normal gait during the day, no visible swelling or erythema, no joint effusion, and full painless range of motion. Children with growing pains are non-weight-bearing only during the acute pain episode; they walk normally between episodes. Parents frequently report that rubbing or massage provides relief.</p>

  <p>Growing pains are diagnosed clinically - routine investigation is not indicated for typical presentations. Investigation is warranted for atypical features: unilateral pain, daytime pain that is constant or affects function, morning stiffness, visible swelling, erythema, warmth, fever, or abnormal gait. Red flags requiring investigation include weight loss, persistent limp, systemic symptoms, or pain localised to a single joint. If atypical features are present, consider FBC, ESR or CRP, ANA (if polyarticular involvement is suspected), and X-rays if structural pathology such as Perthes disease is a concern.</p>

  <p>The cornerstone of management is reassurance: growing pains are benign, self-limiting, and compatible with normal childhood activity. For acute episodes, paracetamol 15 mg/kg or ibuprofen 10 mg/kg orally. Light calf and thigh stretching may help. Encourage normal participation in play and sports - activity restriction does not prevent pain and fuels parental anxiety. A follow-up at 4-6 weeks is reasonable if parental concern remains high.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Routine referral is not necessary for typical growing pains. Consider referral to paediatrics if the presentation is atypical (unilateral, daytime, persistent at rest, associated with swelling or fever), investigation reveals abnormalities (elevated inflammatory markers, positive ANA, or radiological changes), symptoms persist despite reassurance over several months, or red flags for serious pathology emerge. If elevated inflammatory markers or positive ANA suggest juvenile idiopathic arthritis, refer to paediatric rheumatology. Refer to paediatric orthopaedics if imaging suggests Perthes disease or other structural pathology.</p>
</div>
