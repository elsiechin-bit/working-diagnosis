<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Trigger finger is a stenosing tenosynovitis of the flexor tendons at the A1 pulley level in the palm, affecting any finger but most commonly the thumb. Patients report a clicking or snapping sensation with finger flexion and extension, typically worse in the morning. Pain is located at the A1 pulley level in the palm; a palpable nodule or tendon thickening may be felt. The condition can progress from pain only (Grade I), through catching (Grade II) and passively reducible locking (Grade III), to locked finger not passively correctable (Grade IV). Risk factors include diabetes (lower injection response rates), rheumatoid arthritis, and repetitive gripping activities. The condition may worsen over weeks if untreated but can spontaneously resolve in some cases.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>Trigger finger is a clinical diagnosis. Ultrasound can confirm tendon thickening and A1 pulley stenosis but does not typically change management. Plain X-rays are not indicated.</p>

  <p>Conservative management is first-line for Grades I-III. Night splinting with the metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joint in extension (interphalangeal joints free) reduces symptoms by preventing repeated triggering during sleep. Corticosteroid injection into the flexor tendon sheath at the A1 pulley achieves symptom relief in 60-90% of Grades I-III cases; success is lower in patients with diabetes.<sup>4</sup> A second injection may be offered if there is a partial response. Surgery (percutaneous or open A1 pulley release) is definitive and indicated after two failed injections or for Grade IV.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to hand surgery after two corticosteroid injections have failed, or for Grade IV (locked) trigger finger where conservative management has not provided functional improvement. Most Grade I-III cases respond to splinting and injection therapy without surgical referral. Early intervention reduces the likelihood of progression to the locked grade requiring surgery.</p>
</div>
