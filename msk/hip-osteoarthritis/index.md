<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Hip OA presents insidiously with groin or anterior thigh pain, often radiating to the medial knee. The hip joint is deep and pain is frequently felt in the groin, buttock, or referred to the knee - a pattern that may mislead patients into seeking knee evaluation. Functional limitation includes difficulty putting on shoes, rising from sitting, and walking. Older adults with hip OA have reduced mobility and increased falls risk. On examination, loss of internal rotation is the earliest and most sensitive sign, accompanied by hip flexion contracture, Trendelenburg gait, and anterolateral hip pain. The FADIR test (flexion-adduction-internal rotation) indicates intra-articular pathology. Key differentials are trochanteric pain syndrome (lateral hip pain, worse lying on the affected side, greater trochanter tenderness), meralgia paraesthetica (purely neuropathic anterolateral thigh numbness), and referred lumbar spine pain.</p>

  <p>Standing anteroposterior pelvis plus lateral hip X-rays are the initial investigation. Classic OA changes include superior joint space narrowing, osteophytes, and subarticular sclerosis. MRI is not routine unless avascular necrosis, labral pathology, or occult fracture is suspected. Blood work (FBC, renal function, inflammatory markers) guides pharmacotherapy decisions.</p>

  <p>Weight loss and load reduction are foundational. Physiotherapy emphasising hip abductor strengthening, hip flexor stretching, and activity modification improves pain and function; water-based exercise is particularly beneficial. Topical NSAIDs have limited efficacy for hip pain due to depth; oral NSAIDs or paracetamol are more appropriate, used cautiously in older adults. Intra-articular corticosteroid injection (20-40 mg triamcinolone) under ultrasound guidance provides short-term relief and can be repeated 3-4 times yearly. Trochanteric pain syndrome, often coexisting with hip OA, responds to physiotherapy, load modification, and peritrochanteric corticosteroid injection.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to orthopaedic surgery when conservative management has been optimised (3-6 months) and the patient has significant functional limitation and wishes to consider surgery. Referral urgency is routine; waiting times vary by region and sector. Total hip arthroplasty is highly effective and should not be withheld on the basis of age alone - medical fitness and motivation are the relevant considerations. Consider early physiotherapy referral at any stage of the disease course.</p>
