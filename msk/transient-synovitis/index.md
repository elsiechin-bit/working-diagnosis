<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Transient synovitis is the most common cause of acute hip pain and limp in children aged 3-10, often following a viral URTI. The child holds the hip in flexion, abduction, and external rotation (position of comfort). Key distinguishing feature from septic arthritis: well-appearing child with mild or no fever. High fever (above 38.5°C) and systemic toxicity suggest septic arthritis until proven otherwise.</li>
    <li>Use the Kocher criteria to quantify septic arthritis risk: fever above 38.5°C, non-weight-bearing status, ESR above 40 mm/hr, and WBC above 12 x 10<sup>9</sup>/L.<sup>1</sup> Risk: 0 criteria = less than 0.2%; 1 criterion = 3%; 2 criteria = 40%; 3 criteria = 93%; 4 criteria = 99%. Two or more criteria require urgent orthopaedic assessment and hip imaging.</li>
    <li>For 0-1 Kocher criteria: manage conservatively in the community - rest, paracetamol or ibuprofen (10 mg/kg), and weight-bearing as tolerated. Review within 1 week. Most cases resolve within 1-4 weeks without specific treatment.</li>
    <li>Follow up all cases at 4-6 weeks to confirm complete resolution and screen for Perthes disease, which occurs in 1-2% of children following transient synovitis. Warn parents: persistent limp or pain at follow-up requires re-referral for X-ray with frog-leg views.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>Transient synovitis presents with acute or subacute onset of hip, groin, or referred thigh pain in a child aged 3-10, causing an antalgic gait. Many children recall a viral illness in the preceding 1-2 weeks. On examination, the hip is held in flexion, external rotation, and abduction. Internal rotation and abduction are painful and restricted. The child typically appears well between pain episodes - the absence of systemic toxicity is the key differentiator from septic arthritis. High fever (above 38.5°C), marked lethargy, poor feeding, or systemic toxicity should raise immediate concern for septic arthritis.<sup>3</sup></p>

  <p>The Kocher criteria quantify septic arthritis risk using four variables: fever above 38.5°C, non-weight-bearing status, ESR above 40 mm/hr, and WBC above 12 x 10<sup>9</sup>/L. Risk with 0 criteria is under 0.2%; 1 criterion = 3%; 2 criteria = 40%; 3 criteria = 93%; 4 criteria = 99%.<sup>1</sup> In primary care, first-line investigation: FBC (WBC), ESR and/or CRP, and fever assessment. Hip X-ray may show mild joint space widening from effusion but is mainly useful to exclude fracture or structural pathology. Hip ultrasound is more sensitive for detecting effusion (present in 95% of cases but also in septic arthritis). For 2 or more Kocher criteria with a substantial ultrasound effusion (above 10 mm), urgent orthopaedic referral is needed for possible joint aspiration to exclude septic arthritis.</p>

  <p>For 0-1 Kocher criteria (low risk): conservative management at home - rest, analgesia (paracetamol or ibuprofen 10 mg/kg), and weight-bearing as tolerated. Review within 1 week. For 2 or more Kocher criteria: urgent orthopaedic assessment, typically via the emergency department. Management remains supportive for confirmed transient synovitis, but joint aspiration may be needed. Most cases resolve within 1-4 weeks. Follow up at 4-6 weeks to confirm complete resolution and screen for Perthes disease (1-2% incidence after transient synovitis).</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Urgent same-day or next-day orthopaedic referral if 2 or more Kocher criteria are present, or if clinical suspicion for septic arthritis is high for any reason. Routine paediatric or orthopaedic follow-up at 4-6 weeks is advisable even in uncomplicated cases managed in the community, to document full resolution and exclude Perthes disease. If the child has not fully recovered by 6-8 weeks or a limp persists at follow-up, re-refer to orthopaedics for imaging (X-ray with frog-leg views).<sup>5</sup></p>
</div>
