<h2 class="section-label">Podcasts</h2>

**RNZCGP GP CME Podcast**  -  The college's own continuing medical education series. NZ-specific, peer-reviewed, and counts toward CPD. Short enough for a school-run commute.
[rnzcgp.org.nz](https://www.rnzcgp.org.nz)

**BPAC NZ Clinical Update**  -  Bite-sized audio companion to the *bpacnz* prescribing updates. Practical, NZ-funded, no fluff.
[bpac.org.nz](https://bpac.org.nz)

**The Curbsiders**  -  American internal medicine, but the evidence appraisal is excellent and the format is generous enough to teach you how to think through a consult. Episodes on preventive care and outpatient medicine translate well.
[thecurbsiders.com](https://thecurbsiders.com)

**Primary Care Today (BMJ)**  -  Interview-style updates for GPs. British context, but guidelines often overlap with NZ practice and the clinical reasoning translates directly.
[bmj.com/podcast](https://www.bmj.com/podcast)

**Life in the Fast Lane (LITFL)**  -  Emergency and critical care, not strictly GP  -  but invaluable for the acute presentations that land in your room before they land in ED.
[litfl.com](https://litfl.com)

**The NNT Podcast**  -  Evidence-based medicine applied to everyday clinical decisions. Pairs well with the NNT website. Good antidote to over-treatment.
[thennt.com](https://www.thennt.com)

**Journal Club Podcast (American College of Physicians)**  -  Senior clinicians discuss and critique recent journal articles relevant to practice. Good model for how to read papers critically.
[acponline.org/journals-publications/acp-journal-club](https://www.acponline.org/journals-publications/acp-journal-club)

**The GCP Podcast**  -  Bite-sized clinical updates for GPs. British context but the diagnostic and management reasoning applies widely. Good lunch-break length.
[gp-update.co.uk](https://www.gp-update.co.uk)

**MedPage Today Podcast**  -  Daily briefing-style updates on practice-relevant research and clinical news. US-centric but broadly applicable.
[medpagetoday.com](https://www.medpagetoday.com)

<h2 class="section-label">YouTube Channels</h2>

**MedCram**  -  Dr Seheult's case-based clinical overviews. Clear, concise explanations of pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management. Good for brushing up on topics quickly.
[youtube.com/@medcram](https://www.youtube.com/@medcram)

**Emergency Medicine Ireland**  -  Evidence-based clinical reasoning in acute presentations. The diagnostic flowcharts and decision pathways translate directly to GP practice.
[youtube.com/@emergencymedireland](https://www.youtube.com/@emergencymedireland)

**Clinical Skills Lab**  -  Practical examination technique and clinical reasoning. Useful for refreshing your hands-on approach.
[youtube.com/@ClinicalSkillsLab](https://www.youtube.com/@ClinicalSkillsLab)

<h2 class="section-label">Books</h2>

**Clinical Evidence**  -  BMJ's summary of RCT and systematic review evidence for common conditions. More useful as a reference than a cover-to-cover read.

**Murtagh's General Practice**  -  The Australian GP bible. Sixth edition (2015) is still in wide use; a seventh exists. The diagnostic approach chapters are worth reading properly, not just using as a lookup.

**The Checklist Manifesto**  -  Atul Gawande. Not a clinical reference  -  a book about error, complexity, and the irreducible value of simple systems. Changed how I approach pre-consultation routines.

**How Doctors Think**  -  Jerome Groopman. Readable account of cognitive bias in clinical reasoning. The heuristic traps described are immediately recognisable.

**Being Mortal**  -  Atul Gawande again. Essential reading before any conversation about goals of care, aged care placement, or end-of-life planning. Nothing academic about it.

**Mountains Beyond Mountains**  -  Tracy Kidder's biography of Paul Farmer. Not a how-to, but a reminder of what medicine is for.

**The Ottawa Approach to Clinical Reasoning**  -  Teaches a diagnostic framework that reduces cognitive bias. Practical and immediately applicable to your next consultation.

**Blink: The Power of Thinking Without Thinking**  -  Malcolm Gladwell on intuition and pattern recognition. Relevant to understanding when your gut feeling in a consultation is reliable and when it's not.

**The Diagnosis of Stupidity**  -  Sharp, readable essays on medical error and decision-making. Worth reading before blaming your last misdiagnosis on chance alone.

**Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Dummies**  -  More useful than the title suggests. You don't need to be a therapist to understand CBT principles that help with managing anxiety, insomnia, and medically unexplained symptoms in primary care.

**The Five Dysfunctions of a Team**  -  Fable-style book on teamwork. Less directly clinical but relevant if you're managing a practice or thinking about how your team functions.

<h2 class="section-label">Journals</h2>

**NZMJ** (New Zealand Medical Journal)  -  Free online. The only indexed NZ-specific journal. Worth setting up a contents alert.
[nzmj.com](https://www.nzmj.com)

**BJGP** (British Journal of General Practice)  -  The closest international equivalent to a dedicated GP research journal. Consistently useful on consultation skills, multimorbidity, and preventive care.
[bjgp.org](https://bjgp.org)

**The BMJ**  -  Broad and sometimes sensationalist, but the *Practice* and *Education* sections are reliably good. The rapid responses are occasionally the best part.
[bmj.com](https://www.bmj.com)

**Cochrane Library**  -  Not a journal but the best single source for systematic review evidence. NZ practitioners have free access via the Health Research Council.
[cochranelibrary.com](https://www.cochranelibrary.com)

**AFP** (Australian Family Physician, now *Australian Journal of General Practice*)  -  Australian context translates well to NZ. Strong on practical, guideline-based clinical updates.
[racgp.org.au/ajgp](https://www.racgp.org.au/ajgp)

**CMAJ** (Canadian Medical Association Journal)  -  Similar healthcare context to NZ. Evidence-based, pragmatic, and often faster to publish important updates than other journals.
[cmaj.ca](https://www.cmaj.ca)

**JAMA** (Journal of the American Medical Association)  -  Broad scope, high-impact. The *JAMA* network includes *JAMA Internal Medicine* which is often more relevant to primary care than the main journal.
[jama.jamanetwork.com](https://jama.jamanetwork.com)

**The Lancet**  -  Broad and high-profile, but the *Lancet Gastroenterology*, *Lancet Psychiatry*, etc. can be more focused than the main journal.
[thelancet.com](https://www.thelancet.com)

<h2 class="section-label">Magazines & Periodicals</h2>

**GP Magazine (UK)**  -  News, clinical updates, and practice management. Free online. British context but relevant commentary on primary care challenges.
[gponline.com](https://www.gponline.com)

**Therapeutic Guidelines: Mouth and Oral Health**  -  Most clinicians skip oral health until a patient lands on them with pericoronitis. This short guide covers what you actually need to know.
[tg.org.au](https://www.tg.org.au)

<h2 class="section-label">Websites</h2>

**BPAC NZ**  -  The single most useful NZ clinical resource. Evidence-based, funded, free, and actually written for our prescribing context. Bookmark the search.
[bpac.org.nz](https://bpac.org.nz)

**HealthPathways NZ**  -  Canterbury's HealthPathways system is the gold standard; check what your DHB/locality provides. Essential for referral thresholds and local pathways.
[healthpathwayscommunity.org](https://www.healthpathwayscommunity.org)

**NZ Formulary (NZF)**  -  The national prescribing reference. More relevant to NZ than MIMS or UpToDate for funded drug choices.
[nzf.org.nz](https://nzf.org.nz)

**Medsafe**  -  For datasheets, safety advisories, and checking what's actually registered here.
[medsafe.govt.nz](https://www.medsafe.govt.nz)

**LITFL**  -  Life in the Fast Lane. Excellent clinical overviews, ECG library, toxicology, and CCC (Critical Care Compendium). Produced by Australasian emergency physicians.
[litfl.com](https://litfl.com)

**The NNT**  -  Absolute risk reduction presented cleanly for common interventions. Useful for shared decision-making conversations and calibrating your own prescribing enthusiasm.
[thennt.com](https://www.thennt.com)

**Choosing Wisely New Zealand**  -  NZ version of the international campaign to reduce low-value care. Good source for "what not to order" conversations.
[choosingwisely.org.nz](https://choosingwisely.org.nz)

**UpToDate**  -  Requires a subscription (or hospital access). Comprehensive but US-centric  -  always cross-reference funded options and NZ guidelines.
[uptodate.com](https://www.uptodate.com)

**MDCalc**  -  Clinical calculators for risk stratification, diagnostic criteria, and severity scoring. Faster than looking up formulas by hand.
[mdcalc.com](https://www.mdcalc.com)

**DynaMed**  -  Summarised evidence on clinical topics. More concise than UpToDate, faster to read. Many DHBs provide access.
[dynamed.com](https://www.dynamed.com)

**FPnotebook**  -  Family practitioner's quick reference. Organised by symptom and condition. Good for those moments when you need a quick differential diagnosis checklist.
[fpnotebook.com](https://www.fpnotebook.com)

**NZ Cardiovascular Risk Calculator**  -  Absolute CVD risk estimation tailored to NZ populations. Essential for any BP or cholesterol conversation.
[cvdrisk.co.nz](https://www.cvdrisk.co.nz)

**FRAX (WHO Fracture Risk Assessment Tool)**  -  Osteoporosis risk calculation. Guides DEXA ordering and treatment decisions.
[sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX](https://www.sheffield.ac.uk/FRAX)

**Google Scholar**  -  Free access to abstracts and sometimes full PDFs of research papers. Better than nothing when you don't have journal access.
[scholar.google.com](https://scholar.google.com)

**ResearchGate**  -  Researchers often upload their papers here. You can also request PDFs directly from authors  -  most will send them.
[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net)

**Embase (via EBSCO)**  -  Some DHBs provide free access. Broader indexing than PubMed for European and international journals.
[embase.com](https://www.embase.com)
