## LDL targets by risk

LDL cholesterol is the primary target for lipid-lowering therapy. Targets vary by cardiovascular risk:

- Moderate risk (5–10% 10-year CVD): target LDL <2.6 mmol/L
- High risk (10–20% 10-year CVD): target LDL <1.8 mmol/L
- Very high risk (≥20% 10-year CVD): target LDL <1.4 mmol/L
- Secondary prevention (established CVD): target LDL <1.4 mmol/L (or <1.0 mmol/L if recurrent events)

## Statin choice and dosing

High-intensity statins (atorvastatin 80 mg daily or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg daily) reduce LDL by 45–55%. Use for high-risk or very-high-risk patients. Moderate-intensity statins (atorvastatin 20–40 mg daily or simvastatin 20–40 mg daily) reduce LDL by 30–45%; appropriate for moderate-risk patients.

In NZ, atorvastatin and rosuvastatin are both funded. Simvastatin is lower cost but weaker. Pravastatin is an option for patients with statin intolerance concerns, though intolerance is rare if a different statin is chosen.

## Monitoring and adjustment

Recheck lipids 4–6 weeks after starting or increasing a statin. Continue adjustments until target LDL is reached. If LDL is still >1 mmol/L above target after 6 weeks on high-intensity statin, add ezetimibe 10 mg daily. Recheck lipids again 4–6 weeks later.

## Adding ezetimibe

Ezetimibe reduces LDL by a further 15–20%. It works by blocking cholesterol absorption in the bowel. Add it to any statin if target LDL is not reached. Ezetimibe is well tolerated and funded in NZ when added to statin in high-risk patients.

## PCSK9 inhibitors

PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, bococizumab) reduce LDL by 45–60% and are injected subcutaneously every 2 weeks or monthly. Reserve them for patients with very high risk, familial hypercholesterolaemia, or statin-ezetimibe failure. Refer to a lipid clinic or cardiologist; they are not yet funded routinely in primary care.

## Statin intolerance

True statin intolerance is rare (<1% of patients). Muscle symptoms are common but often unrelated to the statin. If genuine muscle pain or weakness occurs, stop the statin for 4 weeks and retry. If symptoms recur, switch to a different statin (half the patients tolerate a second statin). Use lower doses, less frequent dosing (e.g., three times per week), or ezetimibe alone if statin is not tolerated.

## Triglycerides and HDL

Triglycerides and HDL are secondary targets. If triglycerides remain >2.0 mmol/L despite statin therapy and good lifestyle, add fibrate or bempedoic acid. High triglycerides (>5.6 mmol/L) warrant specialist review to exclude familial triglyceridaemia or secondary causes.

## Adherence

Statin adherence is crucial. Discuss once-daily dosing, side-effect expectations, and long-term benefit. Pill organizers, reminders, or weekly dosette boxes can help. Follow up at 4–6 weeks, 3 months, and then annually. Reinforce that the goal is reducing risk, not eliminating all cholesterol.
