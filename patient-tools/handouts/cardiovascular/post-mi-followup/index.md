## Acute phase management transition

Post-MI management begins in the cardiac unit. By the time patients are discharged, they should be on dual antiplatelet therapy, high-intensity statin, ACE/ARB, and beta-blocker (if tolerated). Your role in primary care is to ensure adherence, optimize doses, manage side effects, and prevent recurrence.

## Dual antiplatelet therapy

**Aspirin 81 mg daily** is given indefinitely after MI. Rarely, aspirin is contraindicated (allergy or active bleeding); discuss alternatives with cardiologist.

**P2Y12 inhibitor** (clopidogrel 75 mg daily, ticagrelor 60–90 mg twice daily, or prasugrel 5–10 mg daily) is continued for 12 months post-MI. Choice depends on MI type, intervention, and bleeding risk; cardiologists usually specify which agent. At 12 months, reassess bleeding and recurrence risk with the patient before stopping. Some high-risk patients (recurrent events, thrombosis on stent) continue dual therapy longer.

Discuss adherence: stopping either antiplatelet early increases stent thrombosis risk. Side effects (dyspepsia, bleeding) may require PPI (omeprazole) or agent change.

## Lipid management

Target LDL <1.0 mmol/L in post-MI patients. Start high-intensity statin immediately: atorvastatin 80 mg daily or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg daily. Recheck lipids 4–6 weeks later. If LDL is still >1.0 mmol/L, add ezetimibe 10 mg daily, then recheck. If still above target, consider PCSK9 inhibitor referral (lipoprotein(a) >50 mg/dL also indicates need for PCSK9 therapy).

Amlodipine may be added if not already on it as part of triple antihypertensive therapy, but lipid control is the priority.

## ACE inhibitor or ARB

ACE inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril 10–40 mg daily) or ARBs (e.g., valsartan 40–160 mg daily) are given to all post-MI patients to protect the left ventricle and prevent remodelling. Continue indefinitely. Monitor creatinine and potassium at baseline, then every 12 months (or sooner if renal function or electrolytes are abnormal).

If ejection fraction is known to be <40%, ACE/ARB is essential. If normal EF but troponin-positive MI, still give ACE/ARB for 2 years minimum.

## Beta-blocker

Beta-blockers reduce mortality post-MI, especially if LV ejection fraction is reduced. Use metoprolol (25–200 mg daily), bisoprolol (1.25–10 mg daily), or carvedilol (3.125–25 mg twice daily). Target heart rate 50–60 bpm if tolerated. Continue indefinitely if EF is reduced; for normal EF, continue for at least 3 years.

Discontinue if bradycardia, hypotension, decompensated heart failure, or intolerance develops. Do not suddenly stop; taper over 1–2 weeks to prevent rebound ischaemia.

## Other medications

- **Nitrates**: short-acting for ongoing angina; long-acting if symptoms persist despite beta-blocker and calcium-channel blocker.
- **Ivabradine**: if heart rate remains >70 bpm despite beta-blocker and ejection fraction is reduced; reduces recurrent MI risk.
- **Aldosterone antagonist** (spironolactone 12.5–25 mg daily): if EF <40% and potassium <5.0 mmol/L. Reduces mortality in heart failure post-MI.

## Cardiac rehabilitation

Refer all MI patients to cardiac rehab within 2 weeks. Rehab includes supervised exercise, education on risk factors, psychological support, and return-to-work advice. Improves outcomes and adherence.

## Risk stratification and follow-up

Assess LV function (echocardiography) if not done in hospital. EF <40% indicates higher risk and may change management (aldosterone antagonist, device therapy). Arrange follow-up: clinic visits at 2 weeks, 3 months, 6 months, and annually. At each visit, assess symptoms, medications, adherence, and risk factor control.

Screen for depression (common post-MI) and refer to mental health services if needed.

## Secondary prevention

Address all modifiable risk factors: smoking (refer to smoking cessation), hypertension (target <130/80), diabetes (tight control), obesity, physical inactivity (encourage 150 min moderate exercise per week), diet (Mediterranean pattern), and stress. Annual influenza and once-lifetime pneumococcal vaccination are advised.

## Red flags for cardiology re-referral

Recurrent angina, persistent dyspnoea or orthopnoea, syncope, palpitations, new murmur, or signs of cardiogenic shock warrant urgent cardiology review.
