Cardiac rehabilitation, or cardiac rehab, is a structured programme that helps you recover after a heart event and reduces the chance of another. People who complete cardiac rehab live longer and are admitted to hospital less than those who do not.&sup1; In New Zealand it is free through DHBs and Heart Foundation, and you do not need to be ultra-fit to take part.

<h2>Who is offered cardiac rehab?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Anyone who has had a heart attack</li>
<li>People who have had a stent or bypass surgery</li>
<li>People with stable angina</li>
<li>People diagnosed with heart failure</li>
<li>People who have had heart valve surgery</li>
<li>Some people who have had a heart transplant</li>
</ul>

<p>Ask your GP or hospital team for a referral if you have not already been offered one.</p>

<h2>What it involves</h2>

<p>Programmes vary slightly between DHBs, but typically include:</p>

<p><strong>Exercise sessions.</strong> Group exercise, supervised by physiotherapists or specialist nurses, for an hour or so, two or three times a week. Activities are paced to your fitness - no one is left behind. Pulse and sometimes blood pressure are monitored.</p>

<p><strong>Education.</strong> Talks on heart disease, medicines, healthy eating, stress, sex and relationships, returning to work, and warning signs.</p>

<p><strong>Peer support.</strong> Meeting other people in the same boat is one of the things people value most.</p>

<p><strong>Individual goal-setting.</strong> Working with a nurse or physio on what matters to you - getting back to gardening, returning to work, walking the dog without breathlessness.</p>

<p>Programmes typically run 6-12 weeks. Many DHBs also offer a follow-on phase or maintenance group.</p>

<h2>What to expect at the first session</h2>

<p>An assessment - including a brief medical review, a six-minute walk test or similar fitness check, and a chat about your goals. You will be given a plan tailored to where you are right now. There is no minimum fitness needed to start.</p>

<p>Wear comfortable clothes and shoes you can move in. Bring your medicines list and any GTN spray you carry.</p>

<h2>Why it works</h2>

<p>Cardiac rehab combines several things that each help:</p>

<ul>
<li>Regular exercise improves heart fitness, blood pressure, cholesterol, and mood</li>
<li>Education improves medicine adherence</li>
<li>Group support reduces isolation and depression</li>
<li>Quitting smoking is supported within the programme</li>
<li>Weight, diet, and stress are all addressed</li>
</ul>

<p>The combination is more powerful than any single piece. Trials show 25% lower risk of cardiovascular death over five years for people who complete cardiac rehab compared with usual care.&sup1;</p>

<h2>Common worries</h2>

<p><strong>"I'm scared exercise will trigger another heart attack."</strong> Cardiac rehab is supervised and very safe - the risk of an event during exercise is extremely low, and far outweighed by the benefits.</p>

<p><strong>"I'm too unfit to start."</strong> The whole point is to start where you are. Some people begin with a few minutes of slow walking.</p>

<p><strong>"I don't have transport."</strong> Many programmes have community options closer to home. Some DHBs offer home-based rehab with phone support and a workbook.</p>

<p><strong>"I work full-time."</strong> Programmes often run early evening or weekends. Home-based options exist.</p>

<p><strong>"I'm too old."</strong> No upper age limit. Studies show benefits across all age groups.</p>

<h2>After the programme</h2>

<p>Maintenance is the key. The aim is to keep up regular activity, healthy eating, and follow-up with your GP. Many areas run "phase 4" community heart support groups - check with your local Heart Foundation branch.</p>

<p>Aim for:</p>

<ul>
<li>150 minutes per week of moderate exercise (or 75 minutes vigorous)</li>
<li>Resistance/strength exercise twice a week</li>
<li>Annual GP review of medicines, blood pressure, and lipids</li>
<li>Annual flu vaccine</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mental health</h2>

<p>Depression and anxiety after a heart event are common - up to 1 in 3 people. Talk about it during cardiac rehab. Most programmes screen for low mood and connect you with support if needed.</p>
