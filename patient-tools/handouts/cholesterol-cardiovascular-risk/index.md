<p>Cholesterol is a fatty substance found in your blood. Some is essential - it helps build cells and produce hormones. But when certain types of cholesterol are too high, fatty deposits can build up in the walls of your arteries, narrowing them and increasing the risk of heart attack and stroke.</p>

<h2>What the results mean</h2>

<p>A full lipid panel measures several things:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Total cholesterol</strong> - the overall level in your blood</li>
  <li><strong>LDL cholesterol</strong> ("bad" cholesterol) - the type that contributes to artery narrowing. Lower is generally better.</li>
  <li><strong>HDL cholesterol</strong> ("good" cholesterol) - helps remove other forms of cholesterol from the bloodstream. Higher is better.</li>
  <li><strong>Triglycerides</strong> - another type of blood fat, raised by excess alcohol, sugar, and refined carbohydrates</li>
</ul>

<p>In NZ, treatment decisions are not usually made on cholesterol numbers alone. What matters more is your overall cardiovascular risk.</p>

<h2>Your cardiovascular risk score</h2>

<p>Your GP uses a tool called the NZ Cardiovascular Risk Calculator to estimate your chance of having a heart attack or stroke over the next five years. It takes into account your:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Age and sex</li>
  <li>Blood pressure</li>
  <li>Cholesterol levels</li>
  <li>Whether you smoke</li>
  <li>Whether you have diabetes</li>
  <li>Whether you have existing heart disease or have had a stroke</li>
  <li>Ethnicity (some ethnic groups carry higher baseline risk)</li>
</ul>

<p>This approach means two people with the same cholesterol level may need very different treatment. Someone aged 65 with high blood pressure and diabetes has a much higher absolute risk than a 40-year-old with the same cholesterol but no other risk factors.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle changes that lower cardiovascular risk</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Eat less saturated fat.</strong> Saturated fat (found in butter, cream, fatty meat, full-fat dairy, coconut oil, and processed foods) raises LDL cholesterol. Replace these with unsaturated fats where possible: olive oil, avocado, nuts, oily fish.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat more fibre.</strong> Soluble fibre (found in oats, legumes, fruit, and vegetables) actively lowers LDL cholesterol. Aim for at least 5 servings of vegetables and 2 of fruit per day.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugar</strong> to lower triglycerides.</li>
  <li><strong>Move more.</strong> Regular physical activity raises HDL ("good") cholesterol and reduces overall cardiovascular risk. Aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week.</li>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking is one of the strongest independent risk factors for heart attack and stroke. Quitting has immediate and long-lasting benefits. Free support: Quitline 0800 778 778.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce alcohol.</strong> Heavy alcohol use raises triglycerides and blood pressure.</li>
  <li><strong>Control blood pressure.</strong> High blood pressure and high cholesterol together multiply each other's damage. Treating both matters.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Statins and other medicines</h2>

<p>Statins (such as atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, or pravastatin) lower LDL cholesterol and significantly reduce the risk of heart attack and stroke. They are recommended when your 5-year cardiovascular risk is above a certain threshold, or if you already have established heart disease, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease.</p>

<p>Statins are safe and well-tolerated by most people. Muscle aches are the most common side effect. If you experience significant muscle pain or weakness, contact your GP - it is almost always manageable with a dose or medication change. Do not stop statins without talking to your doctor first.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is my 5-year cardiovascular risk score?</li>
  <li>Should I be on a statin or other lipid-lowering medicine?</li>
  <li>What changes would make the biggest difference to my risk?</li>
  <li>When should I have my cholesterol rechecked?</li>
</ul>
