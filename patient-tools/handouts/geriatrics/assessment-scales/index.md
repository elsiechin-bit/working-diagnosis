<p>The five instruments below cover different dimensions of geriatric health: disease burden (CIRS-G), frailty phenotype (FRAIL scale), basic self-care (Katz ADL), higher-order independence (Lawton IADL), and holistic frailty judgment (Rockwood CFS). Used in sequence, they give a comprehensive snapshot in under 25 minutes. The interactive version is available in the <a href="/workshop/tools/geriatric-assessment/">Geriatric Assessment Toolkit</a>.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ CIRS-G: disease burden and frailty screening</p>
  <p>CIRS-G measures disease burden and comorbidity complexity across 14 organ systems. It captures the number and severity of chronic conditions, not function. The 14 domains are: cardiovascular, respiratory, GI (upper and lower), hepatic, renal, GU, MSK, neurologic, psychiatric, endocrine, ENT, and eyes. Each domain is rated 0 (none) to 4 (very severe, life-threatening). A score above 40 should prompt an urgent medication audit and deprescribing review. Predicts hospitalisation, mortality, and nursing home placement.<sup>1,2</sup></p>

  | Score | Severity | Clinical implication |
  |-------|----------|----------------------|
  | ≤20 | Mild | Low disease burden; routine preventive care |
  | 21-40 | Moderate | Multiple conditions; monitor for decline; medication review |
  | >40 | Severe | High complexity; polypharmacy risk; geriatric assessment indicated |

  <p>The FRAIL scale is a quick frailty phenotype screen in under 2 minutes. Five yes/no items: Fatigue (tired most of the time), Resistance (cannot climb one flight of stairs unaided), Ambulation (walks slowly), Illness (more than 5 chronic conditions), and Loss of weight (unintentional loss of more than 4.5 kg in the past year). Each positive answer scores 1, total 0-5.<sup>3,4</sup> A score of 3 or above should trigger a falls assessment, sarcopenia screen, and nutritional support discussion.</p>

  | Score | Category | Clinical implication |
  |-------|----------|----------------------|
  | 0-1 | Robust | No frailty concerns; standard preventive care |
  | 2 | Pre-frail | High risk of progression; counsel on exercise, nutrition, medication review |
  | 3-5 | Frail | Frailty phenotype present; intensive review indicated |
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Katz ADL, Lawton IADL, and Rockwood CFS</p>
  <p>The Katz ADL assesses basic self-care independence across six domains: bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, and feeding. Each item is binary (independent or dependent), scored 0-6 with 6 representing full independence.<sup>5</sup> Repeat at each major illness or admission - longitudinal decline is a reliable marker of poor prognosis.</p>

  | Score | Status | Clinical implication |
  |-------|--------|----------------------|
  | 6 | Fully independent | No ADL support needed |
  | 4-5 | Partially independent | Minor assistance needed; consider home modifications |
  | 3 | ADL impairment in 3-4 domains | Needs care coordination; home help or day centre |
  | 0-2 | Severe dependence | Likely requires residential care or 24-hour support |

  <p>Lawton IADL measures higher-order activities: using a telephone, shopping, cooking, housework, laundry, managing transportation, managing money and bills, and managing medications. Scored 0-8 with 8 representing full independence.<sup>6</sup> IADL loss typically precedes Katz ADL loss. Loss of medication management is sensitive for early dementia; loss of financial management raises concern for cognitive impairment and exploitation.</p>

  <p>The Rockwood CFS is a holistic clinical judgment tool: a 9-point ordinal scale assigned after a brief functional history covering mobility, self-care, activity level, and independence. Takes under 2 minutes.<sup>7</sup> CFS 4 is the inflection point: the patient is still independent but at high risk, and targeted intervention (exercise, polypharmacy review, nutritional support) can genuinely alter trajectory.<sup>8</sup></p>

  | Level | Status | Description |
  |-------|--------|-------------|
  | 1-3 | Fit to managing well | Well-controlled conditions; independent; good activity level |
  | 4 | Vulnerable | Not dependent, but showing slowing; weight loss, exhaustion, or mild cognitive impairment |
  | 5 | Mildly Frail | Dependent for some IADLs but independent in all ADLs |
  | 6 | Moderately Frail | Needs help for some ADLs and most IADLs |
  | 7 | Severely Frail | Completely dependent for ADLs; minimal cognition; 24-hour supervision |
  | 8-9 | Very severely frail / Terminally ill | Life expectancy less than 6 months; palliative care appropriate |
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Using them together and NZ context</p>
  <p>A practical sequence for a patient presenting with non-specific decline or complex multimorbidity: start with the FRAIL scale and CFS (4 minutes combined) to establish the frailty picture. Add Katz ADL and Lawton IADL (8 minutes combined) to map functional dependence. If there is polypharmacy or diagnostic complexity, complete CIRS-G last (10 minutes) to quantify disease burden and identify deprescribing opportunities.</p>
  <p>Canterbury HealthPathways includes a frailty assessment pathway. BPAC's polypharmacy guidance pairs well with a high CIRS-G score. PHO expectations include frailty screening for patients aged 75 and over. For referral thresholds: CFS 5-6 typically triggers a geriatrics or aged-care consultation; Katz 0-3 or CFS 6-7 is the usual range for rest home assessment; IADL loss with CFS 4-5 supports a social service referral for community support. The CFS is increasingly used by hospital services for surgical and procedural risk assessment, which means your documentation may follow the patient into secondary care.</p>
