Diabetes is the most common cause of kidney failure in New Zealand. About 1 in 3 people with type 2 diabetes will develop kidney disease.&sup1; The good news: kidney damage usually develops over many years, can be detected early, and modern treatments can slow or even partly reverse it. Annual checks are essential.

<h2>How diabetes affects the kidneys</h2>

<p>The kidneys filter waste from the blood through millions of tiny filters called glomeruli. High blood sugar over years damages these filters, causing protein to leak into the urine and reducing filtering capacity. Over time, the kidneys can fail completely, requiring dialysis or transplant.</p>

<p>Most people with kidney disease feel completely well in the early stages. By the time symptoms appear (tiredness, swelling, nausea), kidney function is already substantially impaired.</p>

<h2>The two main tests</h2>

<p><strong>1. eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate).</strong> A blood test that estimates how well kidneys are filtering. Normal is above 90.</p>

<ul>
<li>Stage 1: eGFR 90+, with abnormal urine</li>
<li>Stage 2: eGFR 60-89</li>
<li>Stage 3a: eGFR 45-59 (mild-moderate)</li>
<li>Stage 3b: eGFR 30-44 (moderate-severe)</li>
<li>Stage 4: eGFR 15-29 (severe)</li>
<li>Stage 5: eGFR below 15 (kidney failure)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>2. ACR (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio).</strong> A urine test for protein leakage - the earliest sign of diabetic kidney damage.</p>

<ul>
<li>Normal: ACR under 3 mg/mmol</li>
<li>Microalbuminuria: 3-30 mg/mmol</li>
<li>Macroalbuminuria: above 30 mg/mmol</li>
</ul>

<p>Higher ACR = more kidney damage and higher cardiovascular risk.</p>

<p>Both tests are done annually for everyone with diabetes.</p>

<h2>What protects kidneys</h2>

<p><strong>Tight blood pressure control.</strong> Target usually below 130/80, often lower (125/75) if proteinuria is present.</p>

<p><strong>ACE inhibitor or ARB.</strong> Cilazapril, candesartan, losartan etc. These specifically protect kidneys, even if blood pressure is normal. Recommended for any diabetes patient with raised ACR. Will lower blood pressure as a bonus.</p>

<p><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors.</strong> Empagliflozin, dapagliflozin. Originally developed for diabetes, but proven to slow kidney decline by 30-40% in people with kidney disease - even those without diabetes.&sup2; PHARMAC-funded for diabetes with kidney disease. One of the most important advances in diabetes care of the past decade.</p>

<p><strong>Glucose control.</strong> HbA1c at target reduces small-vessel damage including kidneys.</p>

<p><strong>Statin.</strong> Reduces cardiovascular risk; cardiovascular disease and kidney disease are linked.</p>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking accelerates kidney damage.</p>

<p><strong>Avoid kidney-toxic medicines</strong> when possible - especially regular non-steroidal anti-inflammatories (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen). Paracetamol is fine.</p>

<p><strong>Limit alcohol.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Treat anaemia, iron deficiency, and vitamin D deficiency</strong> - all common in chronic kidney disease and worsen outcomes.</p>

<h2>Daily life with kidney disease</h2>

<ul>
<li>Drink to thirst - usually 1.5-2 litres daily; more in hot weather. Excess water does not protect kidneys</li>
<li>Eat well - salt below 5 g daily; balanced protein</li>
<li>Stay active</li>
<li>Get vaccinated - flu, COVID, pneumococcal, hepatitis B (in advanced CKD)</li>
<li>Watch for swollen ankles - tell your GP if new</li>
<li>Tell every doctor and pharmacist about your kidney function before any new medicine</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diet</h2>

<p>Most people with early kidney disease (stages 1-3a) do not need a special diet beyond:</p>

<ul>
<li>Lower salt</li>
<li>Mediterranean-style eating</li>
<li>Watch portion sizes if overweight</li>
</ul>

<p>For more advanced kidney disease (stages 3b-5), referral to a renal dietitian is helpful - protein and potassium may need adjustment, and phosphate levels need monitoring.</p>

<h2>Sick day rules</h2>

<p>If you are unwell, vomiting, or have diarrhoea, certain medicines can damage already-stressed kidneys. Pause and contact your GP for:</p>

<ul>
<li>ACE inhibitors and ARBs</li>
<li>SGLT2 inhibitors</li>
<li>Diuretics</li>
<li>Metformin (in some cases)</li>
<li>NSAIDs</li>
</ul>

<p>Restart once you are well, eating, and drinking normally.</p>

<h2>When to see a kidney specialist</h2>

<p>Your GP will refer you to a renal physician if:</p>

<ul>
<li>eGFR drops below 30</li>
<li>eGFR is dropping rapidly</li>
<li>ACR rises rapidly or stays very high</li>
<li>Difficult-to-control hypertension</li>
<li>Unexplained findings</li>
</ul>

<p>Specialist input early - before kidney failure - allows planning for dialysis or transplant if needed and optimising treatment.</p>

<h2>What to expect with advanced disease</h2>

<p>If kidney function drops below 15 (stage 5), options include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Haemodialysis (hospital-based, 3 sessions a week of about 4 hours each)</li>
<li>Peritoneal dialysis (home-based, daily, less restrictive)</li>
<li>Kidney transplant - the best option for those eligible</li>
<li>Conservative care without dialysis - sometimes appropriate, especially in older or frail patients</li>
</ul>

<p>The renal team will discuss options well before this point.</p>
