<p>Snoring is common - somewhere around 40 percent of middle-aged men snore regularly.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Most snoring is harmless to the snorer (if not always to their partner). A smaller but significant group has obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), where the upper airway repeatedly collapses during sleep, oxygen levels drop, and the brain has to briefly wake to restart breathing. This is not benign, and it gets missed for years because the events happen while you are asleep.</p>

<h2>Snoring on its own</h2>

<p>Simple snoring is the noise of vibrating soft tissue in a relaxed airway. Worse with alcohol, sedatives, weight gain, blocked nose, and sleeping on your back. Often improves with a few practical changes: side sleeping, weight loss if relevant, less alcohol in the evening, treating allergic rhinitis. It does not damage your health and does not need investigation unless other features point toward apnoea.</p>

<h2>Obstructive sleep apnoea</h2>

<p>OSA is repeated full or partial blockage of the upper airway during sleep. The diagnosis is made on a sleep study (an overnight recording, either at home or in a lab) and is graded by the apnoea-hypopnoea index (AHI) - the average number of events per hour: 5 to 15 is mild, 15 to 30 is moderate, over 30 is severe.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup></p>

<p>Estimates of how common moderate-to-severe OSA is in adults vary widely depending on the definition used and the population studied, but recent international data suggests that roughly 10 to 20 percent of middle-aged adults have clinically significant OSA, with men affected more commonly than women.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> A substantial fraction is undiagnosed.</p>

<h2>Symptoms that suggest it is more than snoring</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Loud snoring, often with witnessed pauses or gasps reported by your bed partner</li>
  <li>Choking or snorting awakenings</li>
  <li>Unrefreshing sleep, no matter how long you spend in bed</li>
  <li>Daytime sleepiness - falling asleep watching TV, in meetings, or behind the wheel</li>
  <li>Morning headaches</li>
  <li>Difficulty concentrating, low mood, irritability</li>
  <li>Nocturia (waking to pass urine more than once or twice)</li>
  <li>Reduced libido or erectile dysfunction</li>
</ul>

<p>Two simple tools your GP may use are the STOP-BANG questionnaire (eight yes/no questions covering snoring, tiredness, observed apnoeas, blood pressure, BMI, age, neck circumference, and sex) and the Epworth Sleepiness Scale (eight everyday situations rated for likelihood of dozing).</p>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular.</strong> Untreated moderate-to-severe OSA roughly doubles the risk of high blood pressure and is independently linked to atrial fibrillation, stroke, and heart failure.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Driving.</strong> Sleepy drivers cause crashes. NZTA medical fitness-to-drive standards require declaration of moderate-to-severe OSA; commercial drivers must usually demonstrate effective treatment to retain their licence.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn5">5</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Metabolic.</strong> OSA is independently associated with insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes; treatment helps glucose control modestly.</li>
  <li><strong>Quality of life.</strong> Once treated, most men describe a level of daytime alertness and mood they had forgotten was possible.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Getting tested in New Zealand</h2>

<p>Your GP can refer you for a sleep study. Most adults are first offered a Level III home sleep study - a small device worn overnight in your own bed, recording airflow, oxygen, heart rate, and breathing effort. Level I (in-lab, full polysomnography) is reserved for more complex cases. Wait times in the public system vary by region and severity; a private home study costs in the order of $300 to $600.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure).</strong> The gold standard for moderate-to-severe OSA. A small mask delivers a steady flow of air that splints the airway open. Effective in nearly everyone who tolerates it. The first few weeks take getting used to. Public funding for CPAP varies by region and by severity; privately, machines cost in the order of $2,000 to $3,000.</li>
  <li><strong>Mandibular advancement device.</strong> A custom dental splint that holds the lower jaw forward. Useful for mild-to-moderate OSA, primary snoring, or men who cannot tolerate CPAP.</li>
  <li><strong>Positional therapy.</strong> If your OSA is mainly when sleeping on your back, simple devices that prevent supine sleep can be effective on their own or as an adjunct.</li>
  <li><strong>Weight loss.</strong> Even a 10 percent reduction in body weight can substantially improve OSA in many men.</li>
  <li><strong>Surgery.</strong> A last resort, with a narrower role than was once thought. Useful in selected cases (very large tonsils, specific anatomical issues).</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid alcohol and sedatives in the evening.</strong> Both worsen airway collapse.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Loud snoring with witnessed pauses, gasping, or choking awakenings</li>
  <li>Persistent daytime sleepiness, especially if you have nodded off driving or at work</li>
  <li>Unrefreshing sleep alongside hard-to-control blood pressure, atrial fibrillation, or new heart failure</li>
  <li>Wanting to be assessed before your next NZTA medical (especially commercial drivers)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Based on my symptoms, do you think I need a sleep study?</li>
  <li>Public referral or private - what are my options and costs?</li>
  <li>If OSA is confirmed, what would treatment look like for me?</li>
  <li>Do I need to declare anything to NZTA?</li>
</ul>
