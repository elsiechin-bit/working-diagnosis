<p>A heart attack is when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is blocked, usually by a clot in a narrowed artery. Time matters more than almost any other emergency: the longer the muscle is starved of oxygen, the more permanent damage. Calling 111 fast is the single most important thing. This handout covers the warning signs - including the ones that don't match the textbook - and what to do.</p>

<h2>Call 111 first - know this in advance</h2>

<p>If you suspect a heart attack in yourself or someone with you:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Call 111 immediately.</strong> Do not drive yourself to hospital. Ambulances carry equipment and can start treatment on arrival.</li>
  <li><strong>Sit or lie down.</strong> Stay calm. Loosen tight clothing.</li>
  <li><strong>Chew (do not swallow whole) one full-strength aspirin (300mg)</strong> if available and you are not allergic, while waiting for the ambulance.</li>
  <li><strong>Stay on the phone.</strong> The 111 dispatcher will guide you, including starting CPR if you are with someone who has collapsed.</li>
</ol>

<p>If the person collapses and is not breathing normally, start CPR immediately - hands in the centre of the chest, push hard and fast (at the rhythm of "Stayin' Alive"), do not stop until the ambulance arrives. The 111 dispatcher will coach you. Public access defibrillators (AEDs) are now widely available - locations at <a href="https://aedlocations.co.nz/">aedlocations.co.nz</a> - and the device walks you through step by step. You cannot harm someone who is not breathing.</p>

<h2>The classic warning signs</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Chest pain or pressure</strong> - usually central or left-sided, often described as crushing, tightness, heaviness, "an elephant on my chest"</li>
  <li><strong>Pain spreading to arms (especially left), jaw, neck, back, or stomach</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Shortness of breath</strong> - sometimes the dominant symptom</li>
  <li><strong>Cold sweat</strong> - clammy, pale</li>
  <li><strong>Nausea or vomiting</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Light-headedness</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Sense of impending doom</strong> - an oddly specific feature; do not dismiss it</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms typically last more than a few minutes. Heart attack pain is usually unrelieved by rest, position changes, or simple antacids.</p>

<h2>Atypical presentations - the ones that get missed</h2>

<p>Up to a third of heart attacks present without classic chest pain. The patterns to recognise:</p>

<h3>In women</h3>
<p>More likely to present with:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Shortness of breath as the main feature</li>
  <li>Unusual fatigue, sometimes for days before the event</li>
  <li>Nausea, indigestion, abdominal pain</li>
  <li>Pain in jaw, back, or arm without chest involvement</li>
  <li>Sweating without other features</li>
</ul>

<p>Women are more likely than men to delay seeking help, and more likely to be misdiagnosed when they do. If something feels seriously wrong, especially in someone over 50 or with cardiovascular risk factors, lean toward calling 111.</p>

<h3>In people with diabetes</h3>
<p>Diabetic neuropathy can blunt chest pain. Heart attacks may present with:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Shortness of breath alone</li>
  <li>Sudden fatigue</li>
  <li>Sudden confusion or general unwellness</li>
  <li>Sweating</li>
  <li>Less specific symptoms than in people without diabetes</li>
</ul>

<h3>In older adults</h3>
<p>Acute confusion, sudden weakness, falls, or shortness of breath without classic chest pain are common presentations.</p>

<h3>In Maori and Pacific peoples</h3>
<p>Higher rates of heart attack at younger ages and higher rates of presenting late. Lower threshold for seeking care is appropriate.</p>

<h2>What is NOT a heart attack</h2>

<p>Most chest pain is not a heart attack. Things that mimic but usually are not:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Musculoskeletal pain</strong> - reproducible by pressing on the chest wall, worse with movement</li>
  <li><strong>Reflux / heartburn</strong> - burning, often after eating, eased by antacids</li>
  <li><strong>Anxiety / panic attack</strong> - pain is more sharp than crushing, often with hyperventilation, tingling, sense of unreality. Worth knowing: anxiety and heart attack can coexist; be careful about reflex dismissal.</li>
  <li><strong>Costochondritis</strong> - inflamed rib joints; tender to press</li>
  <li><strong>Pulled muscle</strong> - especially after recent exercise or coughing</li>
  <li><strong>Pleurisy</strong> - sharp pain worse with deep breath; often after a chest infection</li>
</ul>

<p>The rule of thumb: if you genuinely cannot tell whether something is a heart attack, it is reasonable to call 111. Paramedics would rather come to a non-emergency than miss one.</p>

<h2>Risk factors that lower the threshold</h2>

<p>If any of these apply to you, the threshold for taking chest pain seriously should be lower:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Age over 50 (men), over 60 (women)</li>
  <li>Smoking</li>
  <li>Diabetes</li>
  <li>High blood pressure</li>
  <li>High cholesterol</li>
  <li>Family history of early heart disease</li>
  <li>Prior heart attack, stent, or bypass surgery</li>
  <li>Maori or Pacific or Indian ethnicity</li>
  <li>Recent COVID infection (transient elevated risk)</li>
  <li>Cocaine or methamphetamine use (especially recent)</li>
</ul>

<h2>While waiting for the ambulance</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sit or lie comfortably; do not pace</li>
  <li>Loosen tight clothing</li>
  <li>Chew (do not swallow whole) a full-strength aspirin (300mg) if you have one and are not allergic</li>
  <li>Take any GTN spray or tablet you have been prescribed</li>
  <li>Open the door for the ambulance if safe</li>
  <li>Have your medication list and ID ready</li>
  <li>Stay on the phone with 111 - they will guide you</li>
</ul>

<h2>What happens when you get to hospital</h2>

<p>Standard NZ care for suspected heart attack:</p>

<ul>
  <li>ECG within 10 minutes of arrival</li>
  <li>Blood test for troponin (a marker of heart muscle damage)</li>
  <li>Aspirin, clopidogrel/ticagrelor, often heparin</li>
  <li>If a major heart attack (STEMI): emergency angiogram with stent insertion ("primary PCI") within 90 minutes where possible</li>
  <li>If a smaller heart attack (NSTEMI): admission for observation, repeat troponins, angiogram within 24-72 hours</li>
  <li>Cardiology review and discharge planning</li>
</ul>

<h2>After</h2>

<p>Recovery from a heart attack is well-organised in NZ - cardiac rehabilitation programmes, structured medication regimens, lifestyle support. See the after-heart-attack handout.</p>
