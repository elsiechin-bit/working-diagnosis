<p>Moving into a rest home is one of the bigger transitions of later life. It often happens at a vulnerable moment - after a hospital admission, a fall, or an exhausted carer's breaking point - and the system in NZ is not always easy to navigate. This handout walks through the process: the assessment, the funding, choosing a home, what to bring, and what to expect in the first weeks.</p>

<h2>The first step: needs assessment</h2>

<p>You cannot move into a publicly funded rest home in NZ without a Needs Assessment Service Coordination (NASC) assessment. NASC is run by your DHB and is the gateway to all government-funded aged-care services - home support, respite, day programmes, and residential care.</p>

<p>The assessment is free. It is usually done in your home, sometimes by phone, by a registered nurse or social worker. They look at:</p>

<ul>
  <li>What you can do safely on your own</li>
  <li>What you need help with</li>
  <li>Your medical conditions and how they affect daily life</li>
  <li>Your home environment and supports</li>
  <li>Your wishes and goals</li>
</ul>

<p>The outcome is a recommended level of care. You can get the process started by asking your GP for a referral, or by contacting NASC yourself.</p>

<h2>The four levels of care</h2>

<h3>Rest home level</h3>
<p>For people who can mostly do everyday tasks but need help with some - dressing, showering, medicines, meal preparation. Staffed by caregivers and a small number of registered nurses. Suits people who are unsteady, frail, or in early dementia, but who do not need round-the-clock nursing.</p>

<h3>Hospital level</h3>
<p>For people with substantial medical or nursing needs - significant mobility loss, complex medical conditions, late-stage chronic illness, more advanced dementia. Higher staffing, more registered nurses, more medical input.</p>

<h3>Dementia care</h3>
<p>Specialised secure units for people with moderate to severe dementia where wandering or behavioural symptoms make standard rest home or hospital care unsafe. Smaller groups, dementia-trained staff, designed environments.</p>

<h3>Psychogeriatric care</h3>
<p>For people with significant psychiatric symptoms alongside dementia or other cognitive impairment - severe agitation, distressing hallucinations, behaviours that put themselves or others at risk. Specialist units, often co-located with a hospital. Less common.</p>

<p>NASC will recommend the level of care based on the assessment. Rest homes can hold contracts to provide one or more of these levels in the same building, so a person whose needs increase may move within the same home.</p>

<h2>How funding works</h2>

<p>This is the part most people find confusing. Two parallel things matter: the cost of the care, and what you contribute toward it.</p>

<h3>The Residential Care Subsidy</h3>
<p>If your assets and income are below thresholds set by the government, the Residential Care Subsidy pays the cost of your care above what you can contribute. You apply to Work and Income.</p>

<p>The asset thresholds are reviewed annually. As of mid-2024, they sit roughly at:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Around NZ$284,000 if you are single or your partner is also in care</li>
  <li>Around NZ$155,000 (excluding the value of the family home and a vehicle) if your partner is still living in the family home</li>
</ul>

<p>The exact thresholds change each July. Check work and income for the current numbers.</p>

<h3>Asset and income testing</h3>
<p>The application asks about all your assets and income: bank accounts, investments, additional property, life insurance with surrender value, gifts above set thresholds in the past five years (some past gifting is "clawed back"). The family home is not counted while a spouse still lives in it.</p>

<h3>What you pay if you are above the threshold</h3>
<p>If your assets exceed the threshold, you pay the full daily rate set by your DHB for the level of care you need. This is usually around NZ$1,200 to $1,700 per week depending on level. You can supplement with a "premium room" fee if you choose a single ensuite room rather than a standard one.</p>

<p>Once your assets drop below the threshold (which can happen as you pay for care), you become eligible for the subsidy. You then contribute most of your income (NZ Super and any other) toward your care, and the subsidy covers the gap.</p>

<h3>Premium rooms and "top-up" fees</h3>
<p>Many rest homes charge an additional weekly "premium" for upgraded rooms - larger, ensuite, garden views. These fees are not covered by the subsidy. They are negotiable and you can choose a standard room.</p>

<h3>Get advice</h3>
<p>The financial side is genuinely complex. Free advice is available through:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Work and Income</strong> - call 0800 999 727 for the residential care subsidy team</li>
  <li><strong>Citizens Advice Bureau</strong> - free, on the phone or in person</li>
  <li><strong>Age Concern NZ</strong> - free advocacy and information</li>
  <li><strong>Eldernet</strong> - online directory and information</li>
</ul>

<p>Some families also engage an aged care navigator (private service, fee) when the situation is complex or contested.</p>

<h2>Choosing a rest home</h2>

<p>Eldernet is the best NZ directory of facilities, with information on level of care, contract status, location, and specialist services. Visit two or three before deciding if you can.</p>

<p>Things to look at:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Atmosphere - is it warm, lived-in, with residents engaged in something? Or quiet, tidy, with people lined up in chairs facing a TV?</li>
  <li>Staff - same faces visit-to-visit, or constant turnover? Friendly with residents by name, or task-focused?</li>
  <li>The smell. A consistently fresh smell is a quiet but reliable sign of good care</li>
  <li>The food - taste it if invited, or visit at a meal time</li>
  <li>Activities programme - what is on the calendar this week?</li>
  <li>Visiting policy and family involvement</li>
  <li>Audit reports - all NZ facilities are audited; look up the most recent report on the Health New Zealand website (formerly Ministry of Health)</li>
  <li>Location - close enough that family can visit easily</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to bring</h2>

<p>Most rest homes have a standard list. Common items:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Clothes for at least a week, all clearly labelled</li>
  <li>Toiletries</li>
  <li>Personal items: photos, a familiar bedspread or quilt, a few books, a clock, glasses, hearing aids</li>
  <li>Mobility aids you already use</li>
  <li>Medicines list (the home will manage medications themselves; bring the list)</li>
  <li>Important documents in a folder: NHI number, EPOA, advance care plan, insurance details, GP contact</li>
</ul>

<p>What not to bring: large amounts of cash, valuable jewellery (most homes recommend small or sentimental rather than precious), original irreplaceable documents.</p>

<h2>Settling in</h2>

<p>The first month is often the hardest. Many people feel disoriented, sad, or angry. This usually settles. Things that help:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Visit often in the first weeks. Even short, frequent visits help</li>
  <li>Familiar objects from home in the room</li>
  <li>A clear daily routine in the early weeks</li>
  <li>Engagement with the activities programme</li>
  <li>Talking openly with the staff about what helps your person settle - sleep patterns, food preferences, anxieties, what calms them</li>
  <li>Patience. Adjustment commonly takes 4 to 8 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>Tell the home about anything you would tell a new GP - falls history, allergies, what helps when they are upset, religious practices, cultural needs.</p>

<h2>If something is not right</h2>

<p>Speak to the senior nurse or facility manager first. If concerns are not resolved, escalate to:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The DHB Aged Care portfolio holder (HQSC has contact lists)</li>
  <li>The Health and Disability Commissioner - 0800 11 22 33</li>
  <li>Aged Care Commissioner</li>
</ul>
