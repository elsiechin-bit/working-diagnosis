A urinary tract infection (UTI) is uncomfortable on its own. Repeated UTIs are exhausting and disruptive. Around 1 in 4 women who have had a UTI will have another within six months, and many women have several a year. The good news: most recurrences are preventable, and not every flare needs antibiotics.

<h2>What counts as recurrent?</h2>

<p>Most clinicians consider UTIs "recurrent" when there are:</p>

<ul>
<li>Three or more in a year, or</li>
<li>Two or more in six months</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms - typical and atypical</h2>

<p>Classic UTI symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Burning or stinging when passing urine</li>
<li>Going more often than usual</li>
<li>Strong urge to go, sometimes with leakage</li>
<li>Lower belly pain or pressure</li>
<li>Cloudy, smelly, or pink-tinged urine</li>
</ul>

<p>Warning signs of upper-tract infection (kidney infection or pyelonephritis):</p>

<ul>
<li>Fever, chills, shaking</li>
<li>Loin pain (one side, between rib and hip)</li>
<li>Nausea or vomiting</li>
<li>Feeling very unwell</li>
</ul>

<p>This needs same-day GP or after-hours review.</p>

<p>In older people, UTIs sometimes show up as confusion, falls, or off-colour. But not every confused older person with bacteria in urine has a UTI - asymptomatic bacteriuria is common and usually does not need antibiotics.</p>

<h2>What gets done at diagnosis</h2>

<p>For typical lower-tract symptoms, your GP often diagnoses on history and a dipstick. For recurrent UTIs, urine should be sent for culture each time, so antibiotic choice can match the bug.</p>

<p>For repeated UTIs, your GP may also check:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bladder ultrasound and post-void residual (to look for incomplete emptying)</li>
<li>Kidney ultrasound</li>
<li>Diabetes screening</li>
<li>Pelvic exam (to check for prolapse, atrophic changes after menopause)</li>
<li>Urology referral if there are red flags - haematuria, severe symptoms, kidney scarring, or men with recurrent UTIs (which always need investigation)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Lifestyle prevention - the biggest gains</h2>

<p><strong>Drink more.</strong> Aim for 1.5-2 litres of water a day, more if it is hot or you exercise. A randomised trial showed an extra 1.5 L per day halved UTI recurrences in women with frequent infections.&sup1;</p>

<p><strong>Pee after sex.</strong> Helps flush bacteria out of the urethra.</p>

<p><strong>Wipe front to back.</strong> Reduces transfer of gut bacteria to the urethra.</p>

<p><strong>Do not hold on for hours.</strong> Pee when you need to.</p>

<p><strong>Avoid douching, scented sprays, and harsh soaps</strong> in the genital area.</p>

<p><strong>Cotton underwear and breathable clothing.</strong></p>

<h2>Vaginal oestrogen for postmenopausal women</h2>

<p>After menopause, oestrogen levels fall and the vaginal and urethral tissues thin. This is one of the strongest risk factors for recurrent UTIs in older women. <strong>Topical vaginal oestrogen</strong> (Estring, Ovestin cream, Vagifem pessaries) restores the tissue and substantially reduces UTI recurrence. It is local-acting, very low systemic absorption, and safe for most women including many breast cancer survivors (discuss with your GP).</p>

<p>This is one of the most underused interventions in NZ general practice and worth asking about.</p>

<h2>Other preventive options</h2>

<p><strong>Cranberry products.</strong> Modest evidence in some studies, mostly negative in others. Not harmful unless on warfarin (interactions). Worth trying if you find them helpful, but do not rely on them as your only prevention.</p>

<p><strong>D-mannose.</strong> A sugar that may stop bacteria sticking to bladder wall. Some evidence for prevention. Available over the counter.</p>

<p><strong>Probiotics</strong> (especially Lactobacillus strains): mixed evidence, generally safe.</p>

<p><strong>Methenamine hippurate</strong> (Hiprex). Releases formaldehyde in acidic urine to kill bacteria. The 2022 ALTAR trial showed it was non-inferior to daily preventive antibiotics for women with recurrent UTI - a major advance.&sup2; Discuss with your GP.</p>

<h2>Antibiotic-based prevention</h2>

<p>If lifestyle measures fail, your GP may consider:</p>

<p><strong>Preventive (prophylactic) low-dose antibiotic</strong> daily for 3-6 months - typically trimethoprim 100 mg, nitrofurantoin 50 mg, or cefalexin 250 mg.</p>

<p><strong>Post-coital antibiotic</strong> for women whose UTIs follow sex - a single low-dose tablet after intercourse. Often very effective.</p>

<p><strong>Self-start antibiotics</strong> - a standby prescription you keep at home and start at the first symptom of a UTI, then send urine for culture. Useful for women with reliable symptoms.</p>

<p>These reduce recurrences but raise concerns about resistance, so they are usually time-limited.</p>

<h2>What about cranberry juice?</h2>

<p>Cranberry juice contains a lot of sugar and is unlikely to be more useful than concentrated tablets. Pure unsweetened cranberry concentrate or capsules are a better option if you want to try cranberry.</p>

<h2>UTIs and sex</h2>

<p>"Honeymoon cystitis" is real - sex can introduce bacteria into the urethra. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Empty your bladder before and within 15 minutes after sex</li>
<li>Drink a glass of water afterwards</li>
<li>Avoid spermicides (including some condom lubricants) - these alter vaginal flora and increase UTI risk</li>
<li>Talk to your GP if you have post-coital UTIs - a post-sex antibiotic dose is highly effective</li>
</ul>

<h2>Men with recurrent UTIs</h2>

<p>UTIs in men are uncommon and recurrent UTIs always warrant urology investigation - prostate problems, structural abnormalities, or stones are often involved. Treatment courses are usually longer.</p>
