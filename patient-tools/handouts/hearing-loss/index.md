<p>Hearing loss is common, affecting about one in five New Zealanders. It can develop gradually and go unnoticed initially, but affects communication, relationships, and quality of life. The good news is that hearing aids and strategies help most people hear better and stay connected.</p>

<h2>Types of hearing loss</h2>

<p>Sensorineural hearing loss (damage to the inner ear nerve) is the most common type and often develops with age, noise exposure, or disease. Conductive hearing loss (blocked middle ear) may be temporary and sometimes treatable. Mixed hearing loss involves both types. Your audiologist will determine which type you have.</p>

<h2>Common signs you might have hearing loss</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Difficulty hearing in conversations, especially in noisy environments</li>
  <li>Asking people to repeat themselves frequently</li>
  <li>Turning up the television or radio louder than others prefer</li>
  <li>Difficulty hearing high-pitched sounds (doorbell, phone, birds)</li>
  <li>Withdrawing from social situations due to communication difficulty</li>
  <li>Feeling isolated or frustrated in conversations</li>
  <li>Family members commenting on your hearing</li>
</ul>

<h2>Getting tested - audiometry</h2>

<p>An audiologist performs a hearing test (audiometry) in a quiet booth. The test is simple, painless, and identifies which frequencies you have difficulty hearing. Your GP can refer you for free hearing assessment (usually funded for ages 50 and over, or for younger people with risk factors). Early detection and treatment help prevent further isolation and decline.</p>

<h2>Hearing aids - what helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Get properly fitted hearing aids.</strong> Modern aids are small, discreet, and significantly improve hearing. Behind-the-ear and in-the-ear styles are available. Proper fitting and adjustment are crucial.</li>
  <li><strong>Be realistic about expectations.</strong> Hearing aids improve hearing but do not restore it to normal. They work best in quiet to moderately noisy environments. Adjusting takes time (weeks to months); your brain needs to readjust to amplified sound.</li>
  <li><strong>Use them consistently.</strong> Hearing aids work best when worn regularly. Leaving them in a drawer helps no one. Persistence and patience during adjustment helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Explore different styles.</strong> If one style is uncomfortable, others may work better. Work with your audiologist to find the right fit and features.</li>
  <li><strong>Other devices and strategies.</strong> Some people benefit from TV listening systems, amplified phones, or visual alert systems for doorbells or alarms.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Communication strategies</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Position yourself well.</strong> Face the speaker and sit where you can see their lips. Good lighting helps lip-reading.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask people to speak clearly and not shout.</strong> Shouting distorts speech; normal clear speech works better.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce background noise.</strong> Move conversations to quieter environments when possible.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask people to rephrase if you do not understand.</strong> Rather than asking them to repeat identically, ask them to rephrase using different words.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell people you have hearing loss.</strong> Letting people know helps them adjust their communication to suit you.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing further hearing loss</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Avoid prolonged exposure to loud noise (concerts, power tools, lawn mowers)</li>
  <li>Wear ear protection in noisy environments</li>
  <li>Keep sound levels at safe volumes (headphones should allow you to hear conversation)</li>
  <li>Manage health conditions that worsen hearing (diabetes, high blood pressure)</li>
  <li>Avoid ototoxic medicines (some antibiotics, chemotherapy agents) if possible</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor or audiologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you suspect you have hearing loss</li>
  <li>For routine hearing assessment if you are over 50</li>
  <li>If hearing suddenly worsens - this needs urgent assessment</li>
  <li>If you have hearing loss with dizziness or ear discharge</li>
  <li>For hearing aid fitting, adjustment, or troubleshooting</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your audiologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What type of hearing loss do I have?</li>
  <li>Will my hearing loss get worse?</li>
  <li>Which hearing aid style is best for me?</li>
  <li>What features do I need?</li>
  <li>How do I care for and maintain the hearing aids?</li>
  <li>What support is available during adjustment?</li>
</ul>
