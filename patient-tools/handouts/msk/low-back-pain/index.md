<p>
  Non-specific low back pain is the most common musculoskeletal complaint in general practice. Around 80% of adults experience at least one episode in their lifetime; the vast majority resolve within 6 weeks with conservative management. The primary role of the GP is rigorous red flag screening, early identification of psychosocial contributors to chronicity, and avoiding unnecessary investigation and over-medicalisation.<sup>1</sup>
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Red flag screening</p>
  <p>Screen for red flags at every presentation. The threshold for action is low:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Cauda equina syndrome:</strong> bilateral leg pain and weakness, saddle anaesthesia, urinary retention, faecal incontinence. Same-day emergency referral.</li>
    <li><strong>Malignancy:</strong> age over 50, unintentional weight loss, constitutional symptoms, history of cancer, unremitting night pain unrelieved by position change.</li>
    <li><strong>Infection:</strong> fever, focal vertebral tenderness, immunosuppression, or IV drug use.</li>
    <li><strong>Fracture:</strong> significant trauma, known osteoporosis, prolonged corticosteroid use, age over 70.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    In the absence of red flags, investigation is not required and should not be offered to patients who ask for it. Explain clearly that a normal scan does not mean normal pain, and that imaging findings rarely change management in uncomplicated presentations.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Yellow flags and psychosocial assessment</p>
  <p>
    Yellow flags are psychosocial factors that predict transition to chronic pain and disability. Assess at first presentation: catastrophising ("this will never get better"), fear-avoidance beliefs (avoiding movement because of anticipated pain), passive coping strategies (waiting to be fixed rather than self-managing), work dissatisfaction or compensation claims, and low mood or anxiety.
  </p>
  <p>
    Patients with multiple yellow flags at 6 weeks who have not improved warrant early referral to a psychologically-informed physiotherapy or pain management service rather than further investigation.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Management: acute and subacute</p>
  <p>
    Reassurance that low back pain is usually self-limiting is the single most important intervention; spend the time to deliver it well. Key messages: stay as active as possible, avoid bed rest, most episodes resolve within 6 weeks, imaging is not needed.
  </p>
  <p>
    Physiotherapy targeting core stability and graduated return to activity is effective and ACC-funded for acute musculoskeletal injury. Refer early. Short-course NSAIDs (5-7 days) provide symptom relief; topical NSAIDs reduce systemic exposure in older patients or those with renal or GI risk. Spinal manipulation has modest evidence for short-term benefit but should not replace exercise prescription.
  </p>
  <p>
    Opioids have no role in non-specific low back pain. Do not introduce them. If a patient presents already on opioids from another prescriber, document a deprescribing plan.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Chronic low back pain and referral</p>
  <p>
    Symptoms persisting beyond 12 weeks warrant reassessment. Consider inflammatory arthropathy screening (ESR, CRP, HLA-B27 if axial spondyloarthropathy is suspected) and review the diagnosis. Imaging is reasonable at this point if not previously done.
  </p>
  <p>
    Duloxetine 60 mg daily has the best evidence base for pharmacological management of chronic non-specific low back pain and is PHARMAC-funded in NZ for this indication (as a Specialist or GP with Special Interest prescription in some DHBs; check local criteria). SNRIs address both pain and the comorbid depression and anxiety common in chronic pain presentations.
  </p>
  <p>
    Referral: red flag features warrant urgent imaging and specialist assessment (orthopedic or spinal). Radiculopat
