<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) affects 2-3% of the population but requires treatment in less than 1%. Female predominance is marked for larger curves (female to male ratio approximately 4:1). The Adam's forward bend test - looking for rib hump asymmetry from behind - is the key screening tool; opportunistic screening during ages 10-16 is appropriate.</li>
    <li>Pain is atypical in idiopathic scoliosis and should prompt investigation for an underlying cause. MRI is indicated for: pain, rapid progression, left thoracic curve, neurological signs, or age under 10 years.<sup>1</sup> Standing posteroanterior and lateral full-spine X-rays measure the Cobb angle and assess skeletal maturity (Risser sign).</li>
    <li>Curves under 20 degrees in skeletally immature adolescents: observation with X-rays every 6 months. Curves 20-40 degrees: bracing has level 1 evidence and reduces progression to surgery-requiring values in 25-40% of cases.<sup>2</sup> Curves over 45 degrees or rapidly progressive: surgical referral.</li>
    <li>Refer to paediatric orthopaedics for Cobb angles exceeding 20 degrees or progressive curves. Urgent referral for curves over 40 degrees, rapid progression (more than 5 degrees per 6-month period), or neurological signs.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Presentation, investigation, and management</p>
  <p>AIS is a lateral spinal curvature diagnosed when the Cobb angle exceeds 10 degrees on standing radiographs, accounting for 85% of all scoliosis cases. Most adolescents are asymptomatic and are detected on routine screening or incidentally during examination for another reason. Physical signs include shoulder asymmetry, unequal waist creases, and an asymmetric rib hump best seen on the Adam's forward bend test (patient bends forward with feet together, knees straight; examiner views from behind for rib or lumbar prominence). Adolescents aged 10-16 should be screened opportunistically. Pain is atypical and should prompt investigation for an underlying cause.</p>

  <p>Standing posteroanterior and lateral full-spine radiographs are the standard investigation. The Cobb angle is measured between the most tilted vertebrae at the top and bottom of the curve. Skeletal maturity (Risser sign) is assessed on the same radiograph and predicts remaining growth. MRI is indicated for atypical features: pain, rapid progression, left thoracic curve (more common in underlying pathology), neurological signs, or age under 10 years.<sup>1</sup></p>

  <p>Curves under 20 degrees in skeletally immature adolescents: observation with X-rays every 6 months during growth. Curves 20-40 degrees in skeletally immature patients: bracing reduces progression to values requiring surgery in 25-40% of cases.<sup>2</sup> Bracing should be worn for 18-23 hours per day. Curves over 45 degrees or rapidly progressive: surgical consultation. Once skeletal maturity is reached (typically age 16-18), curves tend to stabilise and observation alone is appropriate. Physiotherapy using the Schroth method may be used as an adjunct to bracing but evidence is limited. Chiropractic manipulation and electrical stimulation have no evidence.</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Referral criteria</p>
  <p>Refer to paediatric orthopaedic services for Cobb angles exceeding 20 degrees or for progressive curves. Urgent referral for curves over 40 degrees, rapidly progressive curves (more than 5 degrees per 6-month period), or neurological signs. Curves with atypical features (pain, atypical curve pattern, age under 10) should have MRI and specialist referral to exclude underlying pathology. Curves below 20 degrees in skeletally immature adolescents can be monitored with serial X-rays in primary care.</p>
</div>
