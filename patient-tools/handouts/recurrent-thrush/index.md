Vaginal thrush (candida) is a yeast infection that affects most women at some point. For many, a single course of treatment is all they ever need. For around 5-9% of women, thrush keeps coming back - four or more episodes a year is the usual definition of recurrent thrush.&sup1; The good news: most recurrence has identifiable triggers, and longer-term treatment usually works.

<h2>What thrush feels like</h2>

<ul>
<li>Itching, burning, or soreness around the vaginal opening and labia</li>
<li>White, thick, "cottage cheese" discharge</li>
<li>Stinging when passing urine (because urine touches inflamed skin)</li>
<li>Pain with sex</li>
<li>Redness and small splits in the skin</li>
</ul>

<p>Smell is usually mild - if there is a strong fishy or off smell, the cause is more likely bacterial vaginosis or another infection.</p>

<h2>What is not thrush</h2>

<p>Several conditions get treated as thrush but are not:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Bacterial vaginosis (BV)</strong> - thin grey discharge with fishy smell. Treated with metronidazole, not antifungals</li>
<li><strong>Trichomoniasis</strong> - frothy yellow-green discharge, sexually transmitted. Needs metronidazole</li>
<li><strong>Lichen sclerosus or lichen planus</strong> - chronic skin conditions causing itch and skin thinning</li>
<li><strong>Genital herpes</strong> - painful sores, rather than itch and discharge</li>
<li><strong>Allergic or irritant dermatitis</strong> - reaction to soap, lubricant, condoms, or pads</li>
<li><strong>Atrophic vaginitis</strong> after menopause - thinning tissue from low oestrogen</li>
</ul>

<p>If treatments are not working, see your GP for a swab. The wrong diagnosis is the commonest reason "thrush" does not respond.</p>

<h2>Common triggers for recurrence</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Antibiotics.</strong> Wipe out helpful bacteria and let yeast overgrow</li>
<li><strong>High-oestrogen states</strong> - pregnancy, combined oral contraceptive pill, perimenopause hormonal swings</li>
<li><strong>Diabetes</strong> with high blood sugar - check HbA1c if you have recurrent thrush</li>
<li><strong>Immunosuppression</strong> - steroids, chemotherapy, HIV, biologics</li>
<li><strong>Hot, sweaty conditions</strong> - prolonged exercise gear, swimwear, occlusive synthetic underwear</li>
<li><strong>Frequent sex</strong> in some women (microtrauma and friction) - a small minority of women's male partners harbour candida; treat both if symptoms cluster around intercourse</li>
<li><strong>Spermicides and some lubricants</strong> can disrupt vaginal flora</li>
<li><strong>Douching</strong> - never helpful; disrupts the natural balance</li>
</ul>

<h2>Day-to-day prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Cotton underwear; change after exercise or swimming</li>
<li>Avoid tight synthetic clothing</li>
<li>Wipe front to back</li>
<li>Use plain water or a non-fragranced soap-free wash for the vulva. Avoid scented soaps, bubble baths, vaginal sprays, and douches</li>
<li>Try a different brand of pads or tampons; some women react to particular ones</li>
<li>If you use lubricant, choose a water-based unfragranced one</li>
<li>Pat dry rather than rub</li>
</ul>

<h2>Standard treatment - simple thrush</h2>

<p>Over the counter:</p>

<ul>
<li>Clotrimazole 500 mg pessary (single dose) plus 1% cream for the external itch</li>
<li>Or fluconazole 150 mg tablet (single oral dose) - avoid in pregnancy</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms usually settle within 2-3 days.</p>

<h2>Treatment for recurrent thrush</h2>

<p>If you have four or more episodes a year, your GP may recommend:</p>

<p><strong>Initial induction</strong> - higher-dose treatment over 1-2 weeks. For example, fluconazole 150 mg every 72 hours for three doses, or clotrimazole pessary nightly for 5-7 nights.</p>

<p><strong>Maintenance phase</strong> - weekly fluconazole 150 mg for 6 months, or twice-weekly clotrimazole pessary. About 90% of women remain symptom-free during maintenance, and around half stay clear after stopping.&sup2;</p>

<p>Confirm with a vaginal swab before starting long-term treatment - resistant strains (especially non-albicans candida like glabrata) need different antifungals.</p>

<h2>Should partners be treated?</h2>

<p>Routine treatment of male partners does not help most women with recurrent thrush. The exception is if a male partner has obvious balanitis (itchy red rash on the glans) - they can use clotrimazole cream. Same-sex female partners do not need routine treatment but may benefit from screening if symptomatic.</p>

<h2>Diet and probiotics</h2>

<p>The "Candida diet" (eliminating sugar and yeasted foods) has little scientific support for treating vaginal thrush. Reasonable measures:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eat well, with a balanced diet</li>
<li>If you have diabetes, optimise blood sugar - this matters more than specific food avoidance</li>
<li>Probiotic yoghurt or oral probiotics may help some women - evidence modest, harm minimal</li>
<li>Vaginal probiotic insertion (yoghurt, suppositories) is sometimes promoted but evidence is weak</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Thrush is more common in pregnancy due to higher oestrogen. Use clotrimazole pessary or cream - do not take oral fluconazole in pregnancy. Symptoms can be persistent through the pregnancy and usually settle after delivery.</p>

<h2>Postmenopausal women</h2>

<p>True thrush is less common after menopause - most "thrush-like" symptoms in this group are actually atrophic vaginitis from low oestrogen. Topical vaginal oestrogen often resolves chronic itch and irritation that has been treated unsuccessfully as thrush for years.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>First-ever episode (to confirm the diagnosis)</li>
<li>Four or more episodes in a year</li>
<li>Treatment not working within a week</li>
<li>Recurrent symptoms in pregnancy</li>
<li>Unusual or smelly discharge</li>
<li>Bleeding between periods or after sex</li>
<li>Pelvic pain</li>
<li>Persistent itch with skin changes (lichen sclerosus, lichen planus, vulval skin cancer all present this way and are missed)</li>
</ul>
