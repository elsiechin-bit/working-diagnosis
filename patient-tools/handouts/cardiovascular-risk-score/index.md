<p>If your GP has talked to you about your "cardiovascular risk", they are probably referring to a calculated number that estimates your chance of having a heart attack, stroke, or dying from cardiovascular disease in the next 5 years. The NZ-specific calculator is called PREDICT. Understanding the number helps you make informed decisions about what to change in your life and whether starting a medicine like a statin or blood pressure tablet is worthwhile. This handout walks through what the score is, what goes into it, and what to do with it.</p>

<h2>What the score is</h2>

<p>A percentage. "Your 5-year cardiovascular risk is 12%" means: out of 100 people with your profile, around 12 will have a heart attack, stroke, or die from cardiovascular disease in the next 5 years.</p>

<p>The score is not a prediction for you specifically. It is a probability based on what happens to large groups of similar people. Your personal outcome may be better or worse - but the score is the best estimate available, and it changes the recommendation.</p>

<h2>What goes into it</h2>

<p>The PREDICT calculator (developed and validated in NZ) takes:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Age and sex</strong> - the biggest single factors</li>
  <li><strong>Ethnicity</strong> - Maori, Pacific peoples, and Indian people have higher cardiovascular risk on average than European NZers</li>
  <li><strong>Smoking</strong> - current, recent, or never</li>
  <li><strong>Diabetes</strong> - including HbA1c if known</li>
  <li><strong>Blood pressure</strong> - systolic value</li>
  <li><strong>Cholesterol</strong> - total cholesterol and HDL ratio</li>
  <li><strong>Family history</strong> - early heart attack or stroke in close relatives</li>
  <li><strong>Existing cardiovascular conditions</strong> - prior heart attack, stroke, atrial fibrillation, heart failure, angina</li>
  <li><strong>Kidney function</strong> - lower kidney function increases risk</li>
  <li><strong>Mental illness, deprivation, smoking history</strong> - more recent versions of PREDICT include these</li>
</ul>

<p>The calculator combines them mathematically. Online versions exist - <a href="https://www.predict-cvd.com/">predict-cvd.com</a>.</p>

<h2>What the categories mean</h2>

<p>NZ guidance broadly bands risk:[1]</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Less than 5%</strong> - low risk. Lifestyle focus. Medication unusual unless specific reasons.</li>
  <li><strong>5-15%</strong> - moderate risk. Lifestyle still primary. Medication considered, especially toward the upper end of the range and if other factors weigh in.</li>
  <li><strong>15% or higher</strong> - high risk. Medication usually recommended alongside lifestyle. The benefit of statins and antihypertensives is clearest in this group.</li>
  <li><strong>Already had cardiovascular disease</strong> - "secondary prevention". Different conversation - statins and other medicines are routinely recommended regardless of calculated score, because the future risk is high.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who should have a risk assessment</h2>

<p>NZ guidance recommends formal cardiovascular risk assessment from:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Age 30 if Maori, Pacific, or Indian</li>
  <li>Age 30 if any first-degree relative had early heart disease</li>
  <li>Age 30 if other significant risk factors (smoking, diabetes, severe family history)</li>
  <li>Age 45 for everyone else</li>
  <li>Age 55 if assessment was previously low and no new risk factors</li>
</ul>

<p>Repeated every 5 years, more often if risk is moderate or factors change.</p>

<h2>Why the conversation matters</h2>

<p>The same risk score can lead to different decisions for different people. A 12% 5-year risk in a person who already has many lifestyle changes maxed out and a strong family history of premature cardiac death might warrant medication. The same 12% in someone who has not yet tried weight loss, smoking cessation, and exercise might warrant a focused lifestyle intervention first.</p>

<p>Things to factor in alongside the number:</p>

<ul>
  <li>How modifiable are your individual risk factors?</li>
  <li>Family history details - what age, what events</li>
  <li>Personal preferences about taking medicines long-term</li>
  <li>Side effect profile of the medicines being considered</li>
  <li>Other health conditions and overall context</li>
</ul>

<h2>What changes the score - things you can move</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Smoking cessation</strong> - the single biggest modifiable factor. Risk drops within a year and approaches non-smoker levels over a decade.</li>
  <li><strong>Blood pressure</strong> - 10 mmHg lower systolic reduces stroke risk by around 30%, heart attack by around 20%</li>
  <li><strong>LDL cholesterol</strong> - every 1 mmol/L drop reduces events by around 25% over 5 years</li>
  <li><strong>Diabetes control</strong> - well-managed diabetes carries less cardiovascular risk than poorly-managed diabetes</li>
  <li><strong>Weight</strong> - sustained weight loss helps blood pressure, lipid profile, diabetes risk</li>
  <li><strong>Physical activity</strong> - independent reduction in events</li>
  <li><strong>Alcohol</strong> - within guidelines</li>
  <li><strong>Diet</strong> - Mediterranean-style eating has the most evidence</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep apnoea treatment</strong> if relevant</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does not change the score, despite popular belief</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Detoxes, cleanses, supplements (with the rare exception of omega-3 in specific contexts)</li>
  <li>Coconut oil ("good cholesterol" - the evidence does not support cardiovascular benefit and the saturated fat content matters)</li>
  <li>Most "heart healthy" food labels</li>
  <li>Most home blood pressure medications, herbal remedies</li>
</ul>

<h2>If your score is high</h2>

<p>Worth a structured conversation with your GP about which interventions will move it most for you. The combinations matter: lifestyle alone is rarely enough at high baseline risk; medication alone without lifestyle is leaving benefit on the table.</p>

<p>For most people at high risk, this looks like:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Smoking cessation if applicable - largest single benefit</li>
  <li>A statin - even moderate dose makes a real difference</li>
  <li>Blood pressure control to target (around 130/80 in most people, lower if diabetes or kidney disease)</li>
  <li>Aspirin - usually not for primary prevention now (the bleeding risk balance changed in recent years); routine for secondary prevention</li>
  <li>Diabetes control if relevant</li>
  <li>Weight, diet, activity, alcohol changes that suit your life</li>
  <li>Annual review</li>
</ul>
