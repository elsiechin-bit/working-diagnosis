<p>Health screening aims to detect disease early, when treatment is most effective. New Zealand has several national screening programmes, and your doctor may also recommend other screening based on your risk factors. This handout explains the main programmes and how to decide what screening is right for you.</p>

<h2>National screening programmes in New Zealand</h2>

<h3>Cervical screening (for people with a cervix)</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> People aged 25 - 69 years. Screening is now available every 3 years (or every 5 years if HPV testing is negative). Previously cervical smears were offered every 3 years from age 20.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> Cervical cancer precursors and HPV infection. Early detection prevents most cervical cancers.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> A healthcare provider takes a sample of cells from the cervix. This is quick and slightly uncomfortable but not painful. HPV testing on the same sample can now determine who needs closer monitoring.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> Cervical screening has dramatically reduced cervical cancer deaths over decades.</p>

<p><strong>Limitations:</strong> No screening is 100% accurate. Some people with precancerous changes are missed; some without disease are referred for further testing.</p>

<h3>Bowel screening (colorectal cancer)</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> People aged 50 - 74 years are invited for bowel screening every 2 years. Maorilk and Pasifika people are invited from age 45.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> Bowel cancer and precancerous polyps.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> You collect a small stool sample at home (FIT test - faecal immunochemical test). This detects blood in stool, which suggests polyps or cancer. If positive, colonoscopy (camera into the bowel) follows to identify and remove polyps or diagnose cancer.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> Early detection of cancer and removal of polyps prevents most bowel cancers.</p>

<p><strong>Limitations:</strong> Not all polyps or cancers cause bleeding; some may be missed by screening.</p>

<h3>Breast screening (mammography)</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> People aged 45 - 69 years are invited for mammography every 2 years. Maorilk and Pasifika women are invited from age 40. People with high breast cancer risk (family history, genetic predisposition) may need earlier or more frequent screening.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> Breast cancer, usually before symptoms develop.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> A mammogram is an X-ray of the breast, compressing it slightly. It detects lumps and calcifications that might indicate cancer.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> Early detection improves treatment options and survival.</p>

<p><strong>Limitations:</strong> Mammography misses some cancers (especially in dense breast tissue). Some cancers are missed; some suspicious findings turn out not to be cancer (false alarms).</p>

<h3>Blood pressure screening</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> Everyone should have blood pressure checked regularly - at least every 5 years, or more often if it is elevated or you are on blood pressure medicine.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> High blood pressure, which increases stroke and heart attack risk.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> A blood pressure cuff measures pressure in your arteries.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> High blood pressure usually causes no symptoms, so screening is the only way to find it. Treatment prevents heart attacks and strokes.</p>

<h3>Cholesterol screening</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> New Zealand recommends cholesterol screening at age 45 for men and 50 for women (earlier if risk factors for heart disease exist - family history, smoking, diabetes). Repeat testing depends on initial results.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> High cholesterol, which increases heart disease risk.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> A blood test measures total cholesterol, HDL, LDL, and triglycerides.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> Early detection allows lifestyle changes or statins to reduce heart disease risk.</p>

<h3>Diabetes screening</h3>

<p><strong>Who and when:</strong> People at risk (overweight, family history, Maorilk/Pasifika ethnicity, over 45 years) should be screened. Fasting glucose or HbA1c testing is used.</p>

<p><strong>What it detects:</strong> Diabetes and prediabetes.</p>

<p><strong>How it works:</strong> A blood test measures glucose control.</p>

<p><strong>Benefit:</strong> Early identification of prediabetes allows lifestyle changes to prevent diabetes. Early diabetes treatment prevents complications.</p>

<h2>Other screening your doctor might recommend</h2>

<p><strong>Bone density (DEXA scan):</strong> For women over 65, men over 70, or anyone with risk factors for osteoporosis or previous fracture.</p>

<p><strong>Prostate screening (PSA test):</strong> For men over 50. This is optional - discuss with your doctor whether it is right for you, as the benefit is debated and false positives are common.</p>

<p><strong>Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) screening:</strong> For men aged 65 - 74 who have ever smoked. This is a single ultrasound to check for aortic aneurysm.</p>

<p><strong>Vision and hearing screening:</strong> For older people or anyone with symptoms.</p>

<h2>Should you have screening?</h2>

<p>Screening can find serious conditions early and prevent deaths. However, all screening has limitations:
  No test is perfect - some results are false alarms; some diseases are missed
  Screening can find harmless conditions that would never have caused problems, leading to unnecessary treatment
  Waiting for screening results causes anxiety
  Some screening involves discomfort or side effects</p>

<p>Good screening balances the benefit of detecting important disease against the risks of overdiagnosis and false positives. National programmes are designed with this in mind.</p>

<h2>Informed decision-making about screening</h2>

<p>Before accepting screening, consider:
  What disease does it detect?
  How common is the disease?
  How often does screening miss the disease?
  If abnormal, what happens next?
  Will finding the disease change your treatment?
  Are you comfortable with the screening process?</p>

<h2>Questions for your doctor about screening</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Which screening programmes apply to me?</li>
  <li>When should I be screened?</li>
  <li>What are the benefits and limitations of this screening?</li>
  <li>What happens if the result is abnormal?</li>
  <li>Should I have screening given my personal risk factors?</li>
  <li>Are there any reasons I should not have this screening?</li>
</ul>
