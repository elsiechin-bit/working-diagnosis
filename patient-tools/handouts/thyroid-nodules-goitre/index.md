A thyroid nodule is a lump in the thyroid gland. They are very common - around 5% of adults can be felt by the doctor, and over half are seen on ultrasound when looked for. The vast majority are benign. The job of investigation is to identify the few that need attention without putting most people through unnecessary surgery or worry.

<h2>Goitre</h2>

<p>"Goitre" simply means an enlarged thyroid. It can be:</p>

<ul>
<li>Diffuse (whole gland) - often hormonal (Graves' or Hashimoto's)</li>
<li>Multinodular - several lumps</li>
<li>Solitary nodule</li>
</ul>

<p>Most goitres are visible only as a fullness in the neck and have no symptoms.</p>

<h2>What people notice</h2>

<ul>
<li>Lump in the neck (often noticed by family or in the mirror)</li>
<li>Pressure when swallowing</li>
<li>Difficulty breathing when lying flat (very large goitres)</li>
<li>Hoarse voice (rare; needs investigation)</li>
<li>Symptoms of overactive or underactive thyroid in some</li>
</ul>

<p>Many nodules are found incidentally on a CT or ultrasound done for another reason.</p>

<h2>The two main questions</h2>

<ol>
<li>Is the thyroid working normally?</li>
<li>Is there any sign that this nodule is malignant?</li>
</ol>

<h2>Initial tests</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>TSH</strong> - to assess function</li>
<li><strong>Thyroid ultrasound</strong> - the key test for nodules. Describes size, characteristics, suspicious features</li>
<li>Sometimes <strong>thyroid uptake scan</strong> - to see if a nodule is "hot" (autonomously producing hormone, almost always benign) or "cold" (most cancers are cold, but most cold nodules are still benign)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Ultrasound features that prompt attention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Solid (rather than cystic)</li>
<li>Hypoechoic (darker than surrounding tissue)</li>
<li>Microcalcifications</li>
<li>Irregular borders</li>
<li>Taller than wide</li>
<li>Extra-thyroidal extension</li>
<li>Suspicious lymph nodes nearby</li>
</ul>

<p>Most NZ centres use the TIRADS or ATA classification to score risk.</p>

<h2>Fine needle aspiration (FNA)</h2>

<p>The next step for nodules with suspicious features or above a certain size threshold. A thin needle is inserted under ultrasound guidance, takes a sample of cells, and is sent to the lab. Quick, relatively painless, no stitches.</p>

<p>Results categorise the nodule:</p>

<ul>
<li>Benign - no further action; periodic ultrasound</li>
<li>Atypia of undetermined significance - repeat FNA</li>
<li>Suspicious for follicular neoplasm - usually surgery</li>
<li>Suspicious for malignancy - surgery</li>
<li>Malignant - surgery</li>
<li>Non-diagnostic - repeat</li>
</ul>

<h2>Watch and wait</h2>

<p>Most benign nodules need only periodic ultrasound (usually 1-2 yearly). Significant change in size, new symptoms, or new suspicious features prompts repeat FNA.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Benign nodules</strong> - usually nothing. Surgery is considered if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pressure symptoms (swallowing, breathing)</li>
<li>Cosmetic concern (visible large lump)</li>
<li>Suspicious changes</li>
<li>Hot nodule causing hyperthyroidism (radioiodine alternative)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Thyroid cancer</strong> - usually surgery (lobectomy or total thyroidectomy), sometimes radioactive iodine, levothyroxine afterwards. The most common types (papillary, follicular) have excellent cure rates - over 90% 10-year survival even with lymph node involvement.&sup1;</p>

<h2>Types of thyroid cancer</h2>

<p>From most to least common:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Papillary thyroid cancer</strong> - most common; very treatable</li>
<li><strong>Follicular thyroid cancer</strong> - second most common; treatable</li>
<li><strong>Medullary thyroid cancer</strong> - rare; sometimes inherited (MEN2 syndrome)</li>
<li><strong>Anaplastic thyroid cancer</strong> - rare; aggressive; older patients</li>
<li><strong>Lymphoma of the thyroid</strong> - rare</li>
</ul>

<p>Even with cancer diagnosis, most thyroid cancers are slow-growing and very curable.</p>

<h2>Risk factors for thyroid cancer</h2>

<ul>
<li>Childhood radiation exposure to neck</li>
<li>Family history of thyroid cancer or MEN syndromes</li>
<li>Female sex (3 times more common in women, but cancer rate similar)</li>
<li>Some inherited conditions (Cowden syndrome, FAP)</li>
</ul>

<p>Most thyroid cancers occur with no known risk factor.</p>

<h2>After thyroid surgery</h2>

<ul>
<li>Lifelong levothyroxine if total or near-total removal</li>
<li>TSH targets often slightly suppressed for cancer (TSH 0.1-0.5)</li>
<li>Calcium monitoring after surgery (parathyroid glands can be affected)</li>
<li>Voice rest for a few days</li>
<li>Return to normal activity within 2-4 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>Specialist follow-up depends on cancer type and stage; usually thyroglobulin level, ultrasound, sometimes whole-body scans.</p>

<h2>Thyroid scares - reassurance</h2>

<p>Many people feel anxious between diagnosis and FNA result. Worth knowing:</p>

<ul>
<li>About 90-95% of all thyroid nodules are benign</li>
<li>Even nodules with some "suspicious" features on ultrasound are mostly benign</li>
<li>Most thyroid cancers, when found, are highly curable</li>
<li>Modern ultrasound and FNA give clearer answers earlier</li>
</ul>
