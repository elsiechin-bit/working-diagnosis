<p>Sleep and mental health move together. Bad sleep makes mood worse. Low mood worsens sleep. Anxiety drives both. The good news is that improving one tends to improve the other. This handout covers what is going on, what to try first, and the situations where treating one without the other is unlikely to work.</p>

<p>For the broader sleep handout (what is normal, sleep hygiene basics, common sleep problems), see <a href="/handouts/sleep/">Sleep - what is normal, what is not, and what helps</a>. This handout focuses on the mental-health-specific overlap.</p>

<h2>The two-way relationship</h2>

<h3>How poor sleep affects mental health</h3>
<ul>
  <li>One bad night reduces emotional regulation - small things feel bigger; arguments are quicker</li>
  <li>A week of poor sleep significantly raises anxiety baseline</li>
  <li>Two weeks of severely restricted sleep produces depressive symptoms even in people without underlying depression</li>
  <li>Chronic insomnia is associated with around twofold higher risk of developing depression</li>
  <li>Suicide risk peaks in people who have been sleeping badly for weeks</li>
  <li>Manic episodes in bipolar disorder are often preceded by sleep loss</li>
</ul>

<h3>How mental illness affects sleep</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Depression</strong> - typically early-morning waking (3-5am), unable to return to sleep. Sometimes hypersomnia (sleeping too much, especially in atypical depression).</li>
  <li><strong>Anxiety</strong> - difficulty falling asleep, racing thoughts at bedtime, multiple awakenings</li>
  <li><strong>PTSD</strong> - nightmares, hypervigilance preventing sleep, fear of sleep itself</li>
  <li><strong>Bipolar</strong> - reduced need for sleep during hypomania/mania, increased sleep during depression</li>
  <li><strong>Schizophrenia</strong> - severely disrupted sleep architecture, reversed day-night cycles</li>
  <li><strong>ADHD</strong> - delayed sleep phase (cannot get to sleep until late, then cannot wake up), restless sleep</li>
</ul>

<h2>The things that help both</h2>

<h3>Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I)</h3>
<p>The single most evidence-based treatment for chronic insomnia, with effects that often spill into mood and anxiety. More effective than sleep medication in the medium term, with no side effects, and benefits persist after the course ends.[1]</p>

<p>Available free in NZ via:</p>
<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/courses/insomnia">Just A Thought - Insomnia course</a></li>
  <li><a href="https://www.sleepio.com/">Sleepio</a> - subscription, validated digital programme</li>
  <li>Some psychologists in NZ offer in-person CBT-I</li>
</ul>

<h3>Regular wake time</h3>
<p>The single most powerful sleep variable. Same wake time every day including weekends. Anchors the body clock and reduces the irregularity that worsens both sleep and mood.</p>

<h3>Morning light</h3>
<p>10-20 minutes of bright outdoor light within an hour of waking. Sets the body clock and has independent antidepressant effects. Works in NZ even in winter.</p>

<h3>Movement during the day</h3>
<p>Aerobic exercise improves both sleep depth and mood. Aim for 150 minutes a week of moderate activity. Even short walks help.</p>

<h3>Wind-down before bed</h3>
<p>30-60 minutes of quiet, low-stimulation time before sleep. No screens for the last 30 minutes ideally. The brain needs warning that sleep is coming.</p>

<h3>Caffeine, alcohol, nicotine</h3>
<p>All worsen sleep AND mental health. Reduce caffeine, especially after midday. Reduce alcohol - it might help you fall asleep but fragments the second half of the night and worsens anxiety. Nicotine is a stimulant.</p>

<h2>The things that work for one but can hurt the other</h2>

<h3>Sleeping tablets</h3>
<p>Zopiclone, zolpidem, lorazepam, temazepam. Effective short-term but:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Tolerance develops within weeks</li>
  <li>Dependence - withdrawal can be unpleasant</li>
  <li>Falls and fractures, especially in older adults</li>
  <li>Memory problems</li>
  <li>Daytime sedation worsens depression</li>
  <li>Can blunt anxiety treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ guidance is short courses (1-2 weeks) for acute situational insomnia, not long-term use.</p>

<h3>Antidepressants</h3>
<p>Some sedating antidepressants (mirtazapine, amitriptyline, doxepin in low doses) can be useful when depression and insomnia coexist. Some SSRIs (fluoxetine especially) can worsen insomnia in some people. The choice matters - discuss with your GP.</p>

<h3>"Just one drink to wind down"</h3>
<p>The most common bad sleep prescription in NZ. Reliable in the short term, increasingly destructive over time.</p>

<h2>The patterns to recognise</h2>

<h3>If sleep is the bigger problem</h3>
<p>You sleep badly, the bad sleep causes mood and anxiety symptoms, and treating sleep settles things. Often a course of CBT-I plus sleep hygiene is enough.</p>

<h3>If mental health is the bigger problem</h3>
<p>Anxiety, depression, or trauma is the engine; sleep disturbance is downstream. Treating sleep alone helps a bit; treating the mental health condition resolves both.</p>

<h3>If both are present and feeding each other</h3>
<p>Often need to address both in parallel. Brief sleep intervention plus therapy or medication for the mental health condition.</p>

<h2>Specific situations</h2>

<h3>Postnatal</h3>
<p>Sleep loss is the most common contributor to postnatal mental illness. Even one or two longer protected sleep blocks per week (someone else takes a feed; partner does the night) makes a measurable difference. See the postnatal depression handout.</p>

<h3>Shift work</h3>
<p>Inevitable sleep disruption raises mental health risk. Strategies that help: anchor sleep at the same time when on the same shift pattern, strategic light exposure, melatonin timing, structured naps, eat at consistent times. Talk to occupational health if available.</p>

<h3>Bipolar disorder</h3>
<p>Sleep is medication. Reduced sleep can trigger mania. Anyone with bipolar should treat sleep regularity as one of the core stabilising practices. Your GP and psychiatrist will discuss this in detail.</p>

<h3>PTSD and trauma</h3>
<p>Specific approaches like Imagery Rehearsal Therapy reduce nightmares. Prazosin can help. CBT-I can be tailored to PTSD-related sleep disturbance.</p>

<h3>Adolescents</h3>
<p>Naturally delayed sleep phase plus screens plus school start times = chronic sleep restriction. The bidirectional link with depression and anxiety is strong. The intervention matters: parents enforcing earlier bedtimes rarely works; reducing pre-bed screens and protecting morning light usually does.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Insomnia plus low mood, anxiety, or thoughts of self-harm</li>
  <li>Insomnia lasting more than 3 months despite trying the basics</li>
  <li>Excessive daytime sleepiness affecting safety (driving, working)</li>
  <li>Witnessed pauses in breathing during sleep, severe snoring, choking arousals</li>
  <li>You suspect a mental health condition is driving the sleep problem</li>
  <li>You are using alcohol or sleeping tablets to cope</li>
  <li>Significant change in sleep pattern that is not settling</li>
</ul>
