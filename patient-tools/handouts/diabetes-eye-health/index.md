Diabetes can damage the small blood vessels at the back of the eye - the retina - leading to a condition called diabetic retinopathy. It is one of the most common causes of preventable blindness in working-age adults. The good news: with annual photo screening and prompt treatment when needed, most sight loss is avoidable.

<h2>What diabetic retinopathy is</h2>

<p>High blood sugar weakens the small vessels in the retina. Over years they can leak, become blocked, or grow abnormally. Stages:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mild non-proliferative</strong> - tiny dots and microaneurysms. No symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Moderate</strong> - more leaks; sometimes mild blurring</li>
<li><strong>Severe non-proliferative</strong> - extensive blockages</li>
<li><strong>Proliferative</strong> - abnormal new vessels grow, can bleed into the eye</li>
<li><strong>Diabetic macular oedema</strong> - swelling in the central retina, can occur at any stage</li>
</ul>

<p>You can have advanced retinopathy without symptoms. By the time vision is affected, damage may be hard to reverse.</p>

<h2>Annual retinal screening - free in NZ</h2>

<p>Everyone with diabetes is eligible for free retinal photography every 1-2 years through the National Diabetes Retinal Screening Programme. You sit at a camera, drops dilate your pupils, and photos are taken of each retina. The whole appointment takes about 20-30 minutes.</p>

<p>Photos are graded centrally and you receive results by mail. Most photos show no or minimal change. If anything significant is found, you will be referred to an ophthalmologist.</p>

<p>If you have not had screening, ask your GP or practice nurse to make a referral.</p>

<h2>Symptoms of significant disease</h2>

<p>Most early retinopathy is silent. Symptoms suggest something more advanced:</p>

<ul>
<li>Floaters (small spots, lines, or cobwebs in vision) - especially sudden showers</li>
<li>Blurred vision</li>
<li>Distorted vision (straight lines look wavy)</li>
<li>Dark patches in the visual field</li>
<li>Sudden vision loss in one eye - emergency, possibly retinal detachment or vitreous haemorrhage</li>
</ul>

<p>See your GP or eye specialist same-day for any sudden vision change.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Background retinopathy</strong> usually needs no treatment, just continued screening and good diabetes control.</p>

<p><strong>Pre-proliferative or proliferative retinopathy</strong> may need:</p>

<ul>
<li>Laser treatment (panretinal photocoagulation) - reduces abnormal vessel growth</li>
<li>Anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept) - given into the eye monthly initially. Highly effective; PHARMAC funded for eligible cases</li>
<li>Surgery (vitrectomy) - for bleeding into the eye or retinal detachment</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Diabetic macular oedema</strong> is treated with anti-VEGF injections, sometimes combined with laser. Treatment can substantially preserve vision.</p>

<h2>Other eye complications of diabetes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cataract</strong> - more common and earlier in diabetes. Treated with surgery</li>
<li><strong>Glaucoma</strong> - more common in diabetes. Annual eye pressure checks recommended</li>
<li><strong>Blurry vision</strong> when sugar is changing - usually settles within weeks of stable glucose. Do not get new glasses while sugar is unstable</li>
</ul>

<h2>What protects vision</h2>

<p><strong>Glucose control.</strong> Tight HbA1c control over years reduces retinopathy progression by half.&sup1;</p>

<p><strong>Blood pressure control.</strong> Hypertension accelerates retinopathy.</p>

<p><strong>Cholesterol management.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Annual screening.</strong> Even if your vision is perfect.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy and retinopathy</h2>

<p>Retinopathy can worsen during pregnancy. Women with diabetes planning pregnancy should have a baseline retinal photo, and be screened in each trimester. Tell your GP early if you are planning pregnancy.</p>

<h2>If diagnosed - the future</h2>

<p>Most people with retinopathy keep useful vision for life with regular screening, treatment when needed, and good diabetes control. The trajectory used to be much worse - intensive monitoring and modern treatment have transformed outcomes.</p>

<h2>Tips for adapting</h2>

<p>If vision is reduced:</p>

<ul>
<li>Magnifying aids and good lighting at home</li>
<li>Large-print materials</li>
<li>Talking glucose meters and CGM apps</li>
<li>Audiobooks and screen readers</li>
<li>Driving assessment if vision is borderline</li>
<li>Blind Low Vision NZ provides services for people with significant vision loss</li>
</ul>
