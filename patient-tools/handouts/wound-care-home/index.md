Most cuts, grazes, and minor surgical wounds heal well with simple home care. Knowing how to clean, dress, and watch a wound, and the signs that mean something is going wrong, will help you heal faster and avoid complications.

<h2>The first hour: cleaning the wound</h2>

<p>For a fresh cut or graze:</p>

<ol>
<li>Wash your hands with soap and water</li>
<li>Apply firm direct pressure with a clean cloth for 10-15 minutes to stop bleeding (do not keep peeking)</li>
<li>Once bleeding has slowed, rinse the wound under running tap water for several minutes - the pressure helps wash out grit and bacteria. Cool, not hot</li>
<li>If grit, gravel, or glass remains, gently wipe with a clean wet cloth or pick out small fragments with clean tweezers. Do not use cotton wool - fibres stick to wounds</li>
<li>Pat the area dry with a clean cloth or gauze</li>
<li>Apply a non-stick dressing or plaster</li>
</ol>

<p>You do not need antiseptic solutions like Dettol or hydrogen peroxide for most wounds - they can damage healing tissue. Plain tap water is enough.</p>

<h2>When to use antibiotic ointment</h2>

<p>For uncomplicated minor wounds, plain petroleum jelly (Vaseline) keeps the wound moist, which helps healing. Antibiotic ointments (mupirocin, fusidic acid) are not routinely needed and contribute to antibiotic resistance. Reserve them for clearly infected wounds and only on GP advice.</p>

<h2>Dressings</h2>

<p>Choosing a dressing:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Plasters (sticking plasters)</strong> - good for small cuts and grazes</li>
<li><strong>Non-stick gauze pads</strong> (e.g. Melolin, Telfa) - good for larger areas; tape down or cover with a bandage</li>
<li><strong>Hydrocolloid dressings</strong> (e.g. Duoderm) - useful for shallow wounds and pressure sores; can stay on for several days</li>
<li><strong>Tegaderm</strong> or other film dressings - waterproof, useful for sites that get wet</li>
</ul>

<p>Change dressings:</p>

<ul>
<li>Daily for the first few days, or whenever they get wet, dirty, or come loose</li>
<li>Less often for hydrocolloid or film dressings - follow the package instructions</li>
<li>Stop dressing once the wound is dry, scabbed, and not weeping</li>
</ul>

<h2>Surgical wound care</h2>

<p>After a procedure (skin biopsy, mole removal, minor surgery), follow your GP's specific instructions. Typical advice:</p>

<ul>
<li>Keep the dressing on and dry for 24-48 hours</li>
<li>After that, you can shower - pat dry; do not rub</li>
<li>Avoid swimming or soaking baths until stitches are out and the wound is fully dry</li>
<li>Stitches: usually removed at 5-7 days for face, 7-10 days for body, 10-14 days for back, scalp, and extremities</li>
<li>Steri-strips: leave on until they fall off naturally (usually within 7-10 days)</li>
<li>Once healed, apply daily SPF 50+ to the scar for 6-12 months - sun darkens scars</li>
</ul>

<p>Avoid heavy lifting, strenuous exercise, and stretching the wound area for the time period your GP advised. Reopening a wound from over-stretching is common, especially around joints.</p>

<h2>Signs of infection - see your GP</h2>

<p>Most wounds become slightly red and tender for a day or two as part of normal healing. Genuine infection looks like:</p>

<ul>
<li>Increasing redness spreading beyond the wound edges</li>
<li>Pain getting worse rather than better after 48 hours</li>
<li>Yellow or green pus, or strong unpleasant smell</li>
<li>Increasing swelling and warmth</li>
<li>Red streaks running up the limb away from the wound</li>
<li>Fever, chills, or feeling unwell</li>
<li>Tender swollen lymph nodes upstream of the wound (e.g. armpit for an arm cut)</li>
</ul>

<p>Any of these warrants a same-day GP visit. The earlier antibiotics start, the simpler the treatment.</p>

<h2>Tetanus</h2>

<p>Wounds contaminated with soil, manure, or rust raise tetanus risk. Check your tetanus status:</p>

<ul>
<li>Most NZ adults have had Tdap or boostrix in the last 10 years through pregnancy, work, or routine boosters</li>
<li>If your last booster was over 10 years ago, or you are unsure, see your GP within 24-48 hours</li>
<li>For deep, dirty wounds with a booster over 5 years ago, a top-up may be advised</li>
</ul>

<h2>Wounds that need professional attention</h2>

<p>See a GP or after-hours clinic the same day for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cuts that gape, are deeper than 1 cm, or expose fat or muscle</li>
<li>Cuts on the face that may need fine stitches</li>
<li>Cuts over a joint, especially if movement opens them</li>
<li>Bites - human and animal bites have a high infection rate</li>
<li>Puncture wounds (nail, fish hook) - hard to clean, often need antibiotics</li>
<li>Wounds with embedded foreign material that will not rinse out</li>
<li>Bleeding that will not stop after 15-20 minutes of firm pressure</li>
<li>Numbness, weakness, or pale colour beyond the wound (suggests nerve or blood vessel damage)</li>
<li>Burns more than the size of the palm, or any burn that blisters or chars</li>
</ul>

<h2>Slow-healing wounds</h2>

<p>If a wound has not made progress in 2 weeks, see your GP. Reasons it may stick:</p>

<ul>
<li>Diabetes - poor blood sugar slows healing; check HbA1c</li>
<li>Poor circulation (peripheral arterial disease) - especially on lower legs</li>
<li>Pressure (heel ulcer in someone bed- or chair-bound)</li>
<li>Venous insufficiency (varicose veins, swelling)</li>
<li>Repeated knocks at the same site</li>
<li>Foreign body still inside</li>
<li>Skin cancer disguised as a non-healing sore</li>
</ul>

<p>District nurse referral is often available through your GP for complex or chronic wounds.</p>

<h2>Practical tips</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stock a basic kit: plasters, gauze, micropore tape, saline sachets or distilled water for rinsing, scissors, tweezers</li>
<li>Replace expired items annually</li>
<li>Keep nails short and clean if doing dressing changes</li>
<li>Photograph daily for wounds you are watching - easier to compare than relying on memory</li>
</ul>
