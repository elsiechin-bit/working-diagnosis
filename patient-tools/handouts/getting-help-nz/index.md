<p>Reaching out is hard. The system is confusing. The first thing to know is that there are several things you can do tonight, free, without an appointment. Below the urgent options, there is a longer guide to GP-based and publicly funded options. None of this replaces a conversation with someone who knows you, but it is a starting point at the moment when starting feels too hard.</p>

<h2>Right now, free, available 24/7</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>1737</strong> - free call or text any time. Trained counsellors. The most NZ-specific generalist mental health line. <a href="https://1737.org.nz">1737.org.nz</a></li>
  <li><strong>Lifeline NZ</strong> 0800 543 354 or text 4357 (HELP). Suicide prevention and general mental health.</li>
  <li><strong>Suicide Crisis Helpline</strong> 0508 828 865 (0508 TAUTOKO). Specifically for people thinking about suicide and people supporting someone who is.</li>
  <li><strong>Youthline</strong> 0800 376 633 or text 234. For under-25s.</li>
  <li><strong>Healthline</strong> 0800 611 116. Free 24/7 nurse advice for any health concern.</li>
  <li><strong>Emergency: 111</strong> if you or someone else is in immediate danger.</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these will judge you for "not being unwell enough". They are not just for people in crisis. They are for the days when something is off and you need to talk to a human who is not a friend or family member.</p>

<h2>If you are thinking about suicide or self-harm</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Call <strong>1737</strong> or <strong>0508 828 865</strong></li>
  <li>If you are in immediate danger, call <strong>111</strong> or go to the nearest hospital ED</li>
  <li>Tell someone - a friend, family member, your GP, your hauora provider</li>
  <li>If you have means of self-harm at home (firearms, large quantities of medication), getting them out of the house can be a meaningful step. Police hold firearms safely; family or pharmacy can hold medication.</li>
</ul>

<p>Asking for help is not weakness. People who ask for help recover, on average, faster and more completely than people who do not.</p>

<h2>Going to your GP</h2>

<p>For most non-crisis mental health concerns, your GP is the right starting point. Worth knowing what to expect.</p>

<h3>Booking the appointment</h3>
<p>Tell the receptionist you would like a longer appointment "for something that needs proper time" - many practices reserve longer slots for mental health and chronic conditions. You do not need to give details to the receptionist.</p>

<p>Subsidies: in NZ, mental health consultations are subsidised in the same way as other appointments. If cost is a barrier, ask the practice about Care Plus, Very Low Cost Access, or community service card discounts. Some primary care practices have free brief intervention counselling on site.</p>

<h3>What to bring</h3>
<ul>
  <li>A short note of how you have been feeling, the duration, and anything specific (sleep, appetite, energy, thoughts)</li>
  <li>Any medications you are on, including supplements and over-the-counter</li>
  <li>If you have a partner or whanau member you would like with you, that is fine</li>
</ul>

<h3>What your GP can do</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Listen and assess - including ruling out physical contributors (thyroid, anaemia, sleep apnoea, vitamin deficiencies, medication side effects)</li>
  <li>Diagnose and explain</li>
  <li>Prescribe medication if appropriate, and explain what to expect</li>
  <li>Refer to brief intervention counselling - free at most NZ practices, usually 4-6 sessions</li>
  <li>Refer to community mental health team or specialist services for more complex needs</li>
  <li>Provide a sickness certificate for work or study</li>
  <li>Connect you with cultural, peer, or community support relevant to your needs</li>
</ul>

<h2>Free or low-cost services in NZ</h2>

<h3>Brief intervention counselling at your GP</h3>
<p>Most NZ practices have on-site or partnered counsellors offering 4-6 free sessions. Often called "Health Improvement Practitioner" or "HIP" services. No referral needed in some - ask your reception or GP.</p>

<h3>Online programmes (free, no waitlist)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/">Just A Thought</a> - free NZ-specific online cognitive behavioural therapy for depression, anxiety, social anxiety, perinatal mental health, and more. Built and clinically run.</li>
  <li><a href="https://thelowdown.co.nz/">The Lowdown</a> - free NZ resource for young people with depression and anxiety. Text-based support, 0800 111 757.</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.depression.org.nz/">depression.org.nz</a> - the John Kirwan-led NZ resource. Free Journal app, self-help, support.</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.smallsteps.org.nz/">Small Steps</a> - free NZ digital tools for stress, low mood, anxiety, sleep.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Specialist mental health services</h3>
<p>For more severe or persistent mental health concerns, your GP can refer to your DHB Community Mental Health team. These services see people with significant mental illness (severe depression, psychosis, bipolar, eating disorders, complex trauma). Waitlists vary; urgent referrals are seen quickly.</p>

<h3>Cultural and community-specific services</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Maori mental health and addiction services</strong> - kaupapa Maori providers in most regions. Te Kahui Tauawhi, Mahitahi Hauora, Hapai Te Hauora, regional iwi providers.</li>
  <li><strong>Pacific mental health services</strong> - Pasifika providers like Vaka Tautua, Le Va, MoanaConnect.</li>
  <li><strong>Rainbow community</strong> - <a href="https://outline.org.nz/">OUTLine</a> 0800 688 5463; rainbow-affirming therapists at <a href="https://rainbowtherapists.org.nz/">rainbowtherapists.org.nz</a></li>
  <li><strong>Asian mental health</strong> - Asian Family Services 0800 862 342 (multilingual).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Private therapy</h3>
<p>NZ Association of Counsellors (<a href="https://www.nzac.org.nz/">nzac.org.nz</a>) and NZ Association of Psychotherapists (<a href="https://www.nzap.org.nz/">nzap.org.nz</a>) have searchable directories. NZ Psychological Society (<a href="https://www.psychologistnz.org/">psychologistnz.org</a>) for clinical psychologists. Cost typically NZ$120-200 per session. Some employers have free Employee Assistance Programmes (EAP) - ask HR.</p>

<h2>What if I have tried before and it did not work?</h2>

<p>Some patterns worth knowing:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The relationship with the therapist matters more than the type of therapy. If it did not click last time, that does not mean therapy is not for you - try someone different.</li>
  <li>Antidepressants can take 4-6 weeks to work. They sometimes need adjustment of dose or switching. The first medicine working is more like a 50/50 chance; the second works for most who did not respond to the first.</li>
  <li>If you have given up on traditional services, peer support and lived-experience services can be different and very effective. <a href="https://www.peer-support.org.nz/">peer-support.org.nz</a> has a directory.</li>
  <li>Most mental health conditions are treatable. Severe and chronic disease is real, but the modal outcome is recovery or significant improvement.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to say if you do not know what is wrong</h2>

<p>Some openings that work in a GP appointment:</p>

<ul>
  <li>"I have not been feeling like myself for a few months."</li>
  <li>"I have been more anxious / sad / tired / angry than usual."</li>
  <li>"I am drinking / using more than I used to."</li>
  <li>"My sleep is not working and I do not know why."</li>
  <li>"I want to talk about my mental health."</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not have to have a diagnosis in mind. That is what your GP is there for.</p>
