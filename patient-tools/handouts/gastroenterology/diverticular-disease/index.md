## Diverticular Disease

Diverticular disease encompasses asymptomatic diverticulosis and symptomatic diverticulitis. With ageing populations, acute diverticulitis is increasingly common. Primary care must distinguish uncomplicated from complicated disease and refer appropriately.

## Definition and epidemiology

Diverticulosis is the presence of small outpouchings (diverticula) of the colonic mucosa and submucosa through the muscular wall, occurring at points of weakness where blood vessels penetrate the wall. Diverticulitis is inflammation or infection within a diverticulum. Prevalence increases with age: 5–10% by age 40, 50% by 60, and 70% by 80 years. More common in developed Western countries.

## Pathophysiology

Increased intraluminal colonic pressure (from low dietary fibre, abnormal motility, or small colonic diameter) causes herniation of mucosa through muscular defects. Most diverticula are asymptomatic. Inflammation occurs if a diverticulum becomes obstructed by faecal matter or food particle; bacterial overgrowth causes inflammation and infection.

Low dietary fibre is implicated; higher fibre intake reduces risk and recurrence. Smoking and NSAIDs may increase risk of diverticulitis.

## Clinical presentation

**Asymptomatic diverticulosis**
80% of people with diverticulosis are asymptomatic; discovered incidentally on imaging done for other reasons.

**Symptomatic uncomplicated diverticulosis**
Intermittent LLQ pain, bloating, altered bowel habit (constipation or diarrhoea), and flatus without fever or systemic symptoms. Symptoms may persist for years without progression to acute diverticulitis.

**Acute uncomplicated diverticulitis**
Acute LLQ pain (sometimes RLQ if right colon involved), fever (usually 38–38.5°C), and localised peritonism (LLQ tenderness ± guarding). Systemic symptoms (tachycardia, tachypnoea) may be mild or absent. Nausea and vomiting are common.

**Acute complicated diverticulitis**
Severe pain, fever >38.5°C, generalised peritonism or severe localised peritonism, systemic toxicity (tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status), and elevated inflammatory markers (WCC >11K, CRP >50 mg/L). Complications (abscess, free perforation) are suggested by severe sepsis or peritoneal signs.

## Assessment and investigations

History: character and location of pain, onset, fever, nausea, vomiting, bowel habit changes, and prior episodes of diverticulitis. Examine abdomen for tenderness, guarding, rebound, and peritoneal signs. Assess vital signs for signs of systemic toxicity.

**Mild symptoms in outpatient setting**
If uncomplicated diverticulitis is suspected (LLQ pain, mild fever, minimal peritonism, no systemic toxicity), outpatient management is often appropriate. CT imaging confirms diagnosis and excludes complications. Blood tests (FBC, CRP, LFTs) assess severity.

**Severe symptoms or systemic toxicity**
Admit for urgent investigation and treatment. CT imaging (CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast) confirms diverticulitis, assesses extent, and identifies complications (abscess, free perforation, fistula). Scoring systems (e.g., modified Hinchey score) classify severity.

**Colonoscopy**
Avoid colonoscopy acutely during diverticulitis (risk of perforation). Perform colonoscopy ≥4–6 weeks after resolution to exclude colorectal malignancy (especially if first episode in age >50 or change in bowel habit).

## Severity classification (modified Hinchey score)

- **Stage Ia:** Confined to paracolic fat; no peritoneal involvement
- **Stage Ib:** Abscess <4 cm
- **Stage II:** Abscess ≥4 cm or fistula
- **Stage III:** Free perforation with purulent peritonitis
- **Stage IV:** Free perforation with faecal peritonitis

Stage I (uncomplicated) diverticulitis may be managed in outpatient or admission depending on clinical severity. Stages II–IV (complicated) require admission, IV antibiotics, and possibly percutaneous drainage or surgery.

## Management

**Uncomplicated diverticulitis (mild to moderate)**
Mild fever (<38°C), minimal peritonism, normal/mildly elevated WCC, and stable vital signs suggest uncomplicated disease manageable in outpatient.

Liquid diet (water, broths, clear fluids) for 3–5 days. Oral antibiotics: cephalosporin (cephalexin 500 mg QID) + metronidazole 400 mg TDS for 5–7 days, or oral fluoroquinolone (ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD) + metronidazole 400 mg TDS. Recent evidence suggests mild uncomplicated diverticulitis without systemic fever may resolve without antibiotics; however, antibiotics are typically prescribed for infection risk reduction.

Analgesia: paracetamol 1 g four times daily (avoid NSAIDs initially, which may worsen inflammation). Follow-up at 48 hours: if symptoms resolve, continue diet and antibiotics. If worsening, admit for imaging and IV therapy.

**Uncomplicated diverticulitis (moderate to severe, or outpatient failure)**
Fever >38°C, significant peritonism, elevated WCC, or failed outpatient management warrant admission. IV hydration, fasting, and IV antibiotics: cefotaxime 2 g TDS + metronidazole 500 mg TDS (or piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g TDS). CT imaging confirms diagnosis and excludes complications. Switch to oral antibiotics once improving (typically 48–72 hours); complete course for 7–10 days total.

Repeat imaging is not routinely needed if clinical improvement occurs. Resume diet gradually after symptoms resolve.

**Complicated diverticulitis**
Perforation (free or contained), abscess, or fistula requires admission. CT imaging guides management.

Abscess <4 cm: managed conservatively with IV antibiotics and close monitoring; resolution is expected with antibiotics alone in most cases.

Abscess ≥4 cm: percutaneous drainage under CT or ultrasound guidance, plus IV antibiotics. Drainage significantly reduces morbidity and mortality and may avert emergency surgery.

Free perforation: emergency surgery required. Hartmann's procedure (colostomy with delayed reversal) or primary resection with anastomosis depending on patient stability and surgeon preference.

## Elective surgery considerations

After one uncomplicated episode of diverticulitis, elective colonic resection is not routinely recommended (recurrence risk 15–30%). After two episodes of uncomplicated diverticulitis, consider elective resection. After one complicated episode (abscess, perforation, fistula), elective resection is advised to prevent recurrence with higher morbidity.

In immunocompromised patients (immunosuppressive therapy, HIV), elective resection is considered after first episode due to increased risk of complicated recurrence.

## Monitoring and follow-up

At 1–2 weeks post-acute episode, review symptom resolution and antibiotic completion. Ensure outpatient follow-up for imaging if not done.

At 4–6 weeks, perform colonoscopy to exclude colorectal malignancy (essential if first episode, age >50, or change in bowel habit).

Counsel on recurrence prevention: increase dietary fibre to 20–35 g/day (gradually to avoid bloating), increase fluid intake to ≥2 L/day, and encourage physical activity. Evidence for fibre in prevention is moderate; however, fibre is beneficial for overall colonic health.

## Complications

**Perforation**
Free perforation into peritoneal cavity causes acute peritonitis and septic shock; requires emergency surgery. Mortality is 10–30%.

**Abscess**
Walled-off collection of pus; presents with localised pain, fever, and mass effect. Percutaneous drainage + antibiotics is standard; averts surgery in most cases.

**Fistula**
Chronic inflammation erodes into adjacent organs (colovesical fistula most common, causing pneumaturia and recurrent UTIs; colovaginal, colocutaneous). Diagnosis is by imaging (CT, fistulography). Elective resection is indicated.

**Stricture**
Recurrent inflammation and scarring causes colonic stenosis. Presents with obstruction (pain, nausea, vomiting, constipation). Elective resection is indicated if severe.

## Special populations

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
More common; present with less dramatic symptoms but higher complication rates. Lower threshold for admission and imaging. Manage per guidelines above; surgery may be higher risk.

**Immunocompromised patients**
HIV, on immunosuppressive therapy, or post-transplant have higher risk of complicated diverticulitis. Elective resection is considered after first episode due to high recurrence and complication rates.

## Field notes

Diverticulosis is exceedingly common and usually benign; reassure patients who are asymptomatic. Acute diverticulitis is eminently treatable; mild uncomplicated cases are often managed in outpatient with antibiotics and diet. CT imaging is gold standard; use it liberally to confirm diagnosis and assess complications. Perforated diverticulitis is an emergency—early recognition and referral save lives. Colonoscopy post-resolution is essential to exclude malignancy, especially if first episode. Recurrence rates are moderate after first uncomplicated episode; prophylactic resection is not justified. Fibre supplementation is a reasonable preventive measure with overall health benefits.
