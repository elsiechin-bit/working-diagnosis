## Hepatitis C

Hepatitis C affects 71 million people globally and 0.5–1% of developed populations. The majority are unaware of their infection. Crucially, hepatitis C is now curable with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs), achieving cure rates >95%. Primary care screening and early referral for treatment are essential.


## Definition and epidemiology

Hepatitis C is an RNA virus transmitted via blood exposure, primarily through IV drug use (largest risk group in developed countries). Sexual transmission is rare; perinatal transmission is possible but uncommon. Chronic hepatitis C develops in 70–80% of infected individuals. There are 6 major genotypes; genotype determines treatment duration. NZ has genotype 1 predominating (40%), followed by genotypes 2, 3, and others. Spontaneous clearance occurs in 20–30% within 6 months; the remainder require treatment.

## Pathophysiology

HCV replicates in hepatocytes and circulates in blood as virions. Host immune response is insufficient to clear infection in most. Chronic infection drives progressive hepatic inflammation, fibrosis, and eventually cirrhosis in 15–20% over 20–30 years. Progression is faster in men, those with concurrent HBV or HIV, and those with heavy alcohol consumption. Once cirrhosis develops, risk of hepatocellular carcinoma is 5% annually.

## Clinical presentation

**Acute hepatitis C**
Most (70–80%) are asymptomatic or have mild symptoms: malaise, fatigue, anorexia, occasional jaundice. Acute severe hepatitis is rare; fulminant hepatic failure is very uncommon.

**Chronic hepatitis C**
Majority are asymptomatic and unaware of infection. Fatigue is the most common symptom, but non-specific. Most are discovered incidentally on screening or when complications of cirrhosis appear (ascites, bleeding, encephalopathy).

**Cirrhosis**
Presents with decompensation: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, or hepatorenal syndrome. Signs include spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, splenomegaly.

## Diagnosis

**Serology**
Anti-HCV antibody indicates past or current infection (develops 4–8 weeks after exposure). Does NOT distinguish active from resolved infection.

**Viral load**
HCV RNA PCR quantifies active infection. Detectable HCV RNA confirms current infection and indicates need for treatment. HCV RNA− (despite anti-HCV+) indicates resolved infection (either spontaneous clearance or prior successful treatment).

**Genotyping**
HCV genotype is determined by HCV RNA sequencing. Genotype guides treatment duration and choice of DAA regimen.

**Liver function assessment**
LFTs (ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin) assess hepatic injury. ALT elevation is mild (1–3× ULN) in most; normal LFTs do not exclude cirrhosis.

**Fibrosis staging**
FibroScan (transient elastography) measures liver stiffness. <5.8 kPa suggests F0-F1 (no/minimal fibrosis); 5.8–12 kPa suggests F2-F3; >12 kPa suggests F4 (cirrhosis). FIB-4 index aids non-invasive assessment: (Age × AST) / (Platelet count × √ALT). Score <1.3 (age ≤65) or <2.67 (age >65) suggests low cirrhosis risk.

## Severity classification

**Non-cirrhotic chronic hepatitis C**
F0-F3 fibrosis (FibroScan <12 kPa). HCV RNA detectable but no clinical signs of cirrhosis.

**Cirrhosis (F4)**
FibroScan >12 kPa or clinical/biochemical evidence (ascites, INR >1.2, albumin <35 g/L, platelets <150K). Compensated cirrhosis has no decompensation; decompensated cirrhosis shows ascites, bleeding, or encephalopathy.

## Management

**Direct-acting antiviral therapy**
All HCV RNA+ patients are candidates for DAA therapy, regardless of genotype, cirrhosis stage, or prior treatment. DAAs are highly effective (>95% sustained virological response, i.e., cure, defined as undetectable HCV RNA 12 weeks post-treatment).

Regimens vary by genotype; specialist guidance is standard, but many regimens are simple 8–12 week courses of oral once-daily agents. Examples: sofosbuvir/velpatasvir (all genotypes, 12 weeks) or sofosbuvir/velpatasvir/voxilaprevir (highly resistant genotypes, 12 weeks). Cost is significant; in NZ, most DAAs are funded by the Ministry of Health for HCV RNA+ individuals.

Treatment can be initiated in primary care or specialist setting depending on local protocols and patient factors.

**Monitoring**
LFTs, HCV RNA at end of treatment and 12 weeks post-treatment (SVR12, sustained virological response at 12 weeks post-treatment) confirms cure. HCV RNA− at SVR12 = cure.

**Cirrhosis surveillance**
Patients with cirrhosis require annual HCC surveillance (AFP and ultrasound, or CT if ultrasound inadequate). Endoscopy screening for oesophageal varices if evidence of portal hypertension (splenomegaly, low platelets, ascites).

**Lifestyle advice**
Avoid alcohol (accelerates fibrosis). Vaccination against HAV and HBV if non-immune (HBV if HBsAg− and anti-HBs−).

## Monitoring and follow-up

**Pre-treatment**
Baseline HCV RNA, genotype, and HCV RNA quantification. FibroScan or FIB-4 to stage fibrosis. LFTs, platelets, INR, albumin to assess synthetic function. Screen for HBV (HBsAg) and HIV (if high-risk).

**During treatment**
Adherence support. Most regimens require no monitoring during therapy; some require LFTs at week 2 and week 6. Reassure that side effects are minimal with modern DAAs (previous interferon-based regimens were poorly tolerated).

**Post-treatment**
HCV RNA 12 weeks post-treatment (SVR12) confirms cure. If SVR12− (treatment failure), repeat HCV RNA, confirm compliance, and consider re-treatment (rates of treatment failure are <5% with current DAAs). Once SVR confirmed, HCV RNA need not be repeated; patient is cured.

**Cirrhosis surveillance**
Annual ultrasound and AFP for patients with cirrhosis, indefinitely (HCC risk remains even after cure).

## Complications

**Cirrhosis and decompensation**
Ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome. Requires specialist management. DAA therapy halts progression but does not reverse established cirrhosis.

**Hepatocellular carcinoma**
Develops in cirrhotic livers at 5% annually. DAA therapy reduces but does not eliminate HCC risk in cirrhotic patients; surveillance continues post-cure.

**Mixed cryoglobulinaemia**
Rare vasculitis from immune complex deposition; associated symptoms resolve with DAA therapy.

## Special populations

**IV drug users**
Highest-risk population (seroprevalence 50–80%). Screen opportunistically; access to DAA therapy is often a barrier; ensure equitable provision and adherence support. Reinfection after cure is possible if ongoing blood exposure; counsel on harm reduction.

**HIV co-infection**
HCV/HIV co-infected patients have accelerated fibrosis progression. DAA therapy is safe in HIV; coordinate with HIV specialist. Cure rates are similar to mono-infected.

**HBV co-infection**
Screen all HCV+ patients for HBsAg. HBV/HCV co-infected patients require assessment by specialist; DAAs are generally safe, but HBV reactivation can occur with TDF-based antiretrovirals if HBV DNA detectable.

**Pregnancy**
Vertical transmission of HCV occurs in 5–10% of HCV RNA+ pregnancies. DAA therapy is contraindicated in pregnancy due to limited safety data; defer DAA therapy until post-partum. Pregnant HCV+ women should be counseled on transmission risk; infant testing (HCV RNA or anti-HCV) at 3 months confirms or excludes transmission.

**Cirrhotic decompensation**
Patients with ascites or other decompensation can receive DAAs safely; specialist initiation is recommended. DAA therapy does not reverse established decompensation but halts further progression.

## Field notes

Screen all IV drug users (active and former) and healthcare workers with significant blood exposure; seroprevalence in these groups is substantial. Most HCV+ individuals are asymptomatic; incidental discovery is common. Modern DAAs (sofosbuvir/velpatasvir or similar) are simple, well-tolerated, and highly effective (>95% cure). Treatment duration is 8–12 weeks regardless of genotype, making regimens simple and cost-effective. Cure is now universal; there is no reason to defer treatment. Post-cure surveillance for HCC continues in cirrhotic patients. Reinfection is possible in IV drug users; counsel accordingly. HBV and HAV vaccination is essential for non-immune HCV+ patients to prevent superinfection and accelerated liver disease.
