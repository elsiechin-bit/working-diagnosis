## Peptic Ulcer Disease

Peptic ulcer disease (PUD) is a mucosal breach (≥5 mm) in the stomach or duodenum. While declining in developed countries, PUD remains significant in NSAID users and H. pylori-infected populations. Most cases are now preventable or easily treated with appropriate therapy.


## Definition and epidemiology

Peptic ulcer disease is defined as a mucosal defect ≥5 mm in the stomach or duodenum visible on endoscopy. Duodenal ulcers account for 60–80% of PUD; gastric ulcers comprise 20–40%. Overall prevalence in developed countries is 1–2%, declining from peak rates in the mid-20th century due to H. pylori screening and PPI use.

H. pylori causes 60% of PUD; NSAIDs account for 25–30% (higher in older age groups). Zollinger-Ellison syndrome and stress-related ulceration are rare (<5%).

## Pathophysiology

H. pylori establishes chronic inflammation of the gastric antrum, causing epithelial damage, reduced mucosal bicarbonate secretion, and impaired acid neutralisation. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin synthesis (COX-1 and COX-2 inhibition), reducing mucosal blood flow, mucus secretion, and bicarbonate production. Both mechanisms overwhelm mucosal defences, leading to ulceration.

Duodenal ulcers typically occur in the first and second portions. Gastric ulcers favour the lesser curve and antrum. Acute stress (severe illness, burns, trauma) causes acute mucosal erosions; chronic PUD usually reflects H. pylori or NSAID exposure.

## Clinical presentation

Epigastric pain is cardinal; character and timing vary by location. Duodenal ulcer pain is typically relieved by food or antacids and occurs 2–3 hours after meals or nocturnally. Gastric ulcer pain is often precipitated by food and may worsen with eating. Associated nausea, early satiation, bloating, and occasionally vomiting occur.

Bleeding presents with haematemesis (bright red or coffee-ground), melaena, or anaemia discovered incidentally. Perforation causes acute severe epigastric pain radiating to shoulders, with peritoneal irritation, board-like abdomen, and shock.

## Diagnosis

Endoscopy is the gold standard and diagnostic + therapeutic for bleeding ulcers. Appearance: clean base suggests old ulcer; adherent clot or visible vessel indicates active or recent bleeding. Biopsy of gastric ulcers excludes malignancy.

H. pylori serology, breath test, stool antigen, or urease test on biopsy (during endoscopy) confirms infection. Serology detects exposure but not active infection; breath test and stool antigen are preferred for active infection confirmation. Urea breath test is gold standard for confirmation of cure (≥4 weeks post-eradication).

Full blood count detects anaemia from chronic or acute blood loss. Urea and electrolytes assess renal function if PPI therapy prolonged.

## Severity classification

**Active bleeding (Forrest classification for endoscopy):**
- Forrest Ia: arterial spurting
- Forrest Ib: oozing bleeding
- Forrest IIa: adherent clot (high rebleeding risk)
- Forrest IIb: flat pigmented spot or visible vessel without oozing
- Forrest III: clean base (low rebleeding risk)

Ulcers with Forrest Ia–IIb receive endoscopic haemostasis (adrenaline injection, thermal coagulation, clips). Forrest III and clean-base ulcers are managed medically.

## Management

**Acute bleeding and resuscitation**
Admit immediately. Two large-bore IVs, blood bank notification, transfusion if Hb <70 g/L or ongoing bleeding. PPI high-dose (omeprazole 80 mg IV bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion, or lansoprazole 60 mg IV daily) commences before endoscopy. Endoscopy within 24 hours for diagnosis and treatment. For Forrest Ia–IIb, perform adrenaline injection (1:10,000, 1–2 mL boluses), thermal coagulation (laser, argon plasma, or heater probe), or clip placement (preferred in New Zealand). Repeat dose of high-dose PPI (40 mg BD IV or 60 mg daily) for 72 hours, then oral PPI.

**Non-bleeding ulcers (acute or chronic)**
High-dose PPI: omeprazole 40 mg BD or lansoprazole 60 mg daily for 4–8 weeks promotes healing. Ulcer size and depth determine healing time (small ulcers heal in 4 weeks; large or penetrating ulcers may require 8–12 weeks).

Test for and treat H. pylori (see below). Cease NSAIDs. If NSAID continuation necessary, co-prescribe PPI (omeprazole 20 mg daily or equivalent) for gastroprotection.

Repeat endoscopy is not needed for healing confirmation in H. pylori-positive or NSAID-related ulcers after successful eradication/NSAID cessation and PPI therapy. Repeat endoscopy is required for gastric ulcers (to exclude malignancy after 8 weeks of PPI).

**H. pylori eradication**
First-line therapy in New Zealand: omeprazole 20 mg BD + amoxycillin 1 g BD + clarithromycin 500 mg BD for 7 days (or lansoprazole 30 mg BD equivalent). Alternative regimens exist for clarithromycin resistance. Confirm eradication with breath test or stool antigen ≥4 weeks post-therapy (do not use serology).

Eradication of H. pylori cures the ulcer and prevents recurrence (recurrence <5% in successfully eradicated patients vs. 80% in untreated H. pylori).

**NSAID-related ulcers**
Cease NSAID if possible. If essential for ongoing indication, switch to COX-2 selective inhibitor (celecoxib 100–200 mg BD) plus PPI (omeprazole 20 mg daily, lansoprazole 30 mg daily), or continue traditional NSAID + PPI at gastroprotective dose. Full-dose PPI is required; standard antacids are insufficient. Check H. pylori status; if positive, eradicate (combines NSAID + H. pylori risk).

## Complications of PUD

**Bleeding**
Active or recent bleeding occurs in 15–20% of PUD cases. Mortality is 5–10%, higher in older patients (>65 years) and those with comorbidities. Endoscopic haemostasis achieves haemostasis in >90% of cases. Transfusion requirements vary with bleeding severity.

**Perforation**
Occurs in 5–10% of PUD cases; more common with duodenal than gastric ulcers. Presents with acute severe pain, peritoneal signs, and shock. Diagnosis is confirmed by pneumoperitoneum on upright CXR or CT. Requires emergency surgical repair (oversewing with Graham patch or primary closure). Mortality is 5–30% depending on delay to surgery and patient comorbidities.

**Gastric outlet obstruction**
Chronic ulcers cause scarring and stricturing of the pylorus or duodenum. Presents with persistent vomiting, early satiation, weight loss, and dehydration. Diagnosis is by endoscopy (shows narrowing); contrast imaging shows delayed gastric emptying. Medical management with high-dose PPI may heal ulcer and reduce stricturing. Pyloric dilation or surgical bypass (pyloroplasty, gastrojejunostomy) is reserved for refractory cases.

**Penetration**
Deep ulcers erode into adjacent structures (liver, pancreas, biliary tree). Causes severe pain poorly responsive to therapy. Diagnosis requires imaging (CT with contrast). Management is medical (high-dose PPI) in most cases; surgery reserved for uncontrolled pain or bleeding.

## Monitoring and follow-up

For H. pylori-positive PUD, confirm eradication at ≥4 weeks post-therapy using breath test or stool antigen. Retest if symptoms persist.

For NSAID-related ulcers, review NSAID necessity; if essential, prescribe gastroprotection indefinitely (PPI at full dose). Monitor for complications (bleeding, perforation).

For gastric ulcers, repeat endoscopy at 8 weeks of PPI therapy to confirm healing and exclude malignancy (biopsies from initial endoscopy are required; healing of a gastric ulcer does not exclude early cancer).

## Special populations

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
PUD is more common; NSAIDs are common cause. Lower threshold for endoscopy in dyspepsia. Monitor PPI side effects (hypomagnesaemia, fracture risk, B12 deficiency, C. difficile). H. pylori eradication is equally effective.

**Aspirin and dual antiplatelet therapy**
Patients requiring ongoing aspirin or dual antiplatelet therapy (clopidogrel + aspirin) with prior PUD should continue therapy with high-dose PPI (omeprazole 20 mg daily) for gastroprotection. Do not cease antiplatelet therapy without cardiology input.

## Field notes

Endoscopy remains gold standard for diagnosis and treatment of bleeding PUD. Never assume PUD in the absence of endoscopic confirmation—dyspepsia can mimic PUD. H. pylori eradication is curative; emphasise adherence to full 7-day course. NSAID users are at lifelong risk; identify safer alternatives when possible. Gastric ulcers require repeat endoscopy to exclude malignancy—this is not optional. Rebleeding after endoscopic therapy occurs in 15–20% of cases; close monitoring is essential. For perforation, surgery cannot wait—early diagnosis saves lives.
