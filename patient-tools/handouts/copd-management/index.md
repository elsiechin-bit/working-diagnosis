The medicines do part of the work in COPD. The other part is up to you. Pacing yourself, learning breathing techniques, exercising regularly, and acting quickly on flares are what keeps people out of hospital.

<h2>Breathing techniques</h2>

<p>When you feel breathless, panic and quick shallow breathing make things worse. Two techniques help:</p>

<p><strong>Pursed-lip breathing.</strong> Breathe in slowly through your nose. Purse your lips as if blowing out a candle, and breathe out slowly through pursed lips - take twice as long as the in-breath. This keeps small airways open and lets more air out.</p>

<p><strong>Diaphragmatic (belly) breathing.</strong> Place a hand on your belly. Breathe in slowly through your nose, feeling the belly rise. Breathe out gently through pursed lips, feeling the belly fall. Practice 5-10 minutes a day when not breathless.</p>

<h2>Positions that help breathlessness</h2>

<ul>
<li>Sit forward, leaning on a table or your knees</li>
<li>Stand and lean against a wall</li>
<li>Walk slowly with arms supported (e.g. on a trolley or walker)</li>
<li>In bed: prop up with several pillows or use a wedge cushion</li>
</ul>

<p>Practise these so they come naturally during flares.</p>

<h2>Pacing</h2>

<p>The principle: do less in any given burst, do it more often.</p>

<ul>
<li>Break tasks into smaller steps with rests between</li>
<li>Sit on a stool to shower, shave, or prepare food</li>
<li>Pull rather than push; slide objects rather than carry</li>
<li>Keep frequently-used items at waist height</li>
<li>Plan demanding tasks for when you feel best (often after morning sputum clearance)</li>
<li>Group errands; consolidate trips</li>
<li>Use a wheeled trolley/walker for shopping</li>
</ul>

<h2>Stay active</h2>

<p>Avoiding activity makes COPD worse. Lung function does not improve, but heart, muscle, and breathing efficiency all do.</p>

<ul>
<li>Aim for daily walks - 10 minutes is a fine start; build to 30</li>
<li>Stop and rest when needed, then continue</li>
<li>Use your reliever 15 minutes before exercise if breathlessness limits you</li>
<li>Pulmonary rehabilitation programmes are the gold standard - free, supervised, group-based. Ask for a referral</li>
<li>Maintain strength: gentle resistance exercises (sit-to-stand, light weights) reduce frailty and falls</li>
</ul>

<h2>Clearing sputum</h2>

<p>Some people produce a lot of sputum. Daily clearance helps prevent infections.</p>

<p><strong>Active cycle of breathing technique:</strong></p>

<ol>
<li>3-5 normal relaxed breaths</li>
<li>3-4 deep breaths, holding briefly at the top</li>
<li>1-2 huffs (forced exhalations like fogging a mirror)</li>
<li>Cough if sputum is loose</li>
<li>Repeat for 10-15 minutes morning and evening</li>
</ol>

<p>Stay well hydrated - water thins sputum.</p>

<p>Devices like the Acapella or Aerobika can help if recommended by a respiratory physiotherapist.</p>

<h2>Recognising a flare-up early</h2>

<p>Signs that a flare is starting:</p>

<ul>
<li>More breathlessness than usual</li>
<li>More cough or phlegm</li>
<li>Phlegm changing colour - yellow, green, brown</li>
<li>Wheeze or tight chest</li>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Disturbed sleep</li>
</ul>

<p>Acting in the first 24 hours can prevent a hospital admission.</p>

<h2>Action plan</h2>

<p>Your GP can give you a written COPD action plan, similar to an asthma plan. Typical steps:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mild flare:</strong> increase reliever, stay home, monitor</li>
<li><strong>Moderate flare:</strong> start prednisone 30 mg daily for 5 days (if you have a standby supply at home), with or without antibiotics if phlegm is purulent</li>
<li><strong>Severe flare:</strong> call your GP same day, or 111 if very breathless</li>
</ul>

<p>Many people with frequent flares are given a "rescue pack" - prednisone tablets and antibiotics to start at home, with rules on when.</p>

<h2>Diet and weight</h2>

<p>Both ends of weight are problems:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Underweight</strong> in advanced COPD weakens breathing muscles. Aim for protein at every meal, smaller frequent meals if appetite is poor, smoothies or oral supplements (Ensure, Fortisip) if eating is difficult</li>
<li><strong>Overweight</strong> increases the work of breathing. Modest weight loss helps</li>
</ul>

<p>Avoid large meals before activity - a full stomach pushes up on the diaphragm.</p>

<h2>Mental health</h2>

<p>Anxiety and depression are common in COPD. Untreated, both worsen breathlessness and admissions. Talk to your GP. Cognitive behavioural therapy, anti-anxiety techniques, and antidepressants when appropriate all help.</p>

<h2>Smoking and vaping</h2>

<p>Quitting at any stage:</p>

<ul>
<li>Slows the rate of lung function decline</li>
<li>Reduces flares</li>
<li>Improves response to inhalers</li>
</ul>

<p>If you have not been able to quit, keep trying. Quitline 0800 778 778 offers free support, NRT, and (in some areas) varenicline. Many DHBs have free smoking-cessation programmes.</p>

<h2>Vaccinations</h2>

<ul>
<li>Annual flu vaccine - free for COPD</li>
<li>COVID-19 boosters as recommended</li>
<li>Pneumococcal vaccine - free for those 65+ or eligible chronic conditions</li>
<li>Shingrix (shingles) if 65+ or immunocompromised</li>
</ul>

<h2>Travel and altitude</h2>

<p>Most people with COPD can travel safely. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Carry inhalers in hand luggage</li>
<li>Take more than you need</li>
<li>For air travel, your GP can advise if you need a hypoxia assessment - some people need in-flight oxygen</li>
<li>High altitude (above 2000 m) reduces oxygen - check with your GP before mountain holidays</li>
</ul>

<h2>End-of-life conversations</h2>

<p>For people with advanced COPD, talking with your GP about advance care planning is helpful - what treatment you would want or not, where you would want to be cared for. The conversation usually relieves rather than burdens. Resources at <a href="https://www.myacp.org.nz">myacp.org.nz</a>.</p>
