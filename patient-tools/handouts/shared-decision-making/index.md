<p>Shared decision-making means you and your doctor work together to choose the best treatment or approach for you, taking into account both the medical evidence and what matters to you personally. It is not the doctor deciding alone, and it is not you deciding alone - it is a partnership. This handout explains how shared decision-making works and why it matters.</p>

<h2>Why shared decision-making matters</h2>

<p>When you are involved in decisions about your care, you are more likely to follow through with treatment, feel satisfied with your choice, and have better health outcomes. Many treatment options exist for the same condition, and the best choice depends on your individual circumstances, values, and preferences. Your doctor has medical expertise; you know yourself best. Together you make better decisions.</p>

<h2>The steps of shared decision-making</h2>

<h3>1. Your doctor tells you that a decision is needed</h3>

<p>Your doctor explains that treatment options exist and that your preference matters. They might say "There are several ways we could manage this - let me tell you about them and what you think is best."</p>

<h3>2. Your doctor explains the options</h3>

<p>For each option, your doctor explains:
  What the treatment involves and how it is done
  How likely it is to help
  What side effects or risks are possible
  What happens if you do not have treatment
  Whether there is research comparing different options</p>

<h3>3. You share what matters to you</h3>

<p>Tell your doctor about your priorities and concerns. These might include:
  Whether you prefer to avoid medicines
  Concerns about side effects
  Whether you can manage complex regimes
  Whether you want to try the simplest approach first
  Family history or cultural values that influence your choice
  Cost concerns
  Work or lifestyle factors that matter</p>

<h3>4. You and your doctor discuss pros and cons</h3>

<p>Together you weigh up the advantages and disadvantages of each option for your particular situation. Your doctor might say "Given that you are worried about side effects, let's think about trying the gentlest option first and seeing if it helps."</p>

<h3>5. You make a decision together</h3>

<p>You agree on a plan that makes sense to you both. This might be a treatment, a "watchful waiting" approach, or trying something temporarily to see if it helps. You should feel comfortable with the decision.</p>

<h2>Shared decision-making in practice</h2>

<h3>For a new diagnosis</h3>

<p>Your doctor explains the condition and then discusses treatment options. For example, with high blood pressure, you might discuss starting medicines now versus trying lifestyle changes first. Your doctor explains the benefits and risks of each approach and you decide together.</p>

<h3>For ongoing conditions</h3>

<p>As your condition changes or new options become available, decisions may need revisiting. Your doctor should regularly check: "Is this still working for you? Do you want to try something different?"</p>

<h3>For prevention</h3>

<p>Sometimes doctors recommend screening or preventive treatment (like cholesterol medicine or vaccines). Shared decision-making means explaining why it is recommended, what the benefits and risks are, and whether you choose to have it based on your values.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What options do we have for treating this?</li>
  <li>What are the benefits of each option for my situation?</li>
  <li>What are the risks or side effects of each?</li>
  <li>What happens if I do not have treatment?</li>
  <li>Are there other options we have not discussed?</li>
  <li>What does the research say - is one option clearly better than others?</li>
  <li>Which option would you choose if you were in my situation?</li>
  <li>Can I have time to think about this or get a second opinion?</li>
  <li>Can we try this approach and see if it works before moving to something more intensive?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Expressing your values and preferences</h2>

<p>You might say:
  "I would prefer to try lifestyle changes before medicines."
  "I am worried about side effects - what is the mildest option?"
  "I need something simple because I often forget to take medicines."
  "Cost is important - what are the most affordable options?"
  "I want to avoid surgery if possible."
  "My family has a history of cancer - should I consider screening?"
  "I am planning to have children - are any treatments risky in pregnancy?"</p>

<h2>What to do if your doctor does not listen</h2>

<p>If your doctor dismisses your concerns, does not listen to your preferences, or makes a decision without discussing options, you can:
  Express this clearly: "I feel rushed and would like more time to discuss options"
  Ask for a follow-up appointment to discuss properly
  Request a second opinion
  See a different GP in the practice
  Change to a different GP practice if you consistently feel unheard</p>

<h2>Shared decision-making is not always appropriate</h2>

<p>In medical emergencies (cardiac arrest, severe trauma, severe allergic reaction), there is no time for discussion - your doctor makes decisions to save your life. Once the emergency is stabilized, shared decision-making resumes. For many common health problems though, shared decision-making is the right approach.</p>

<h2>Making decisions when you lack capacity</h2>

<p>If you become unable to make decisions (unconsciousness, advanced dementia, severe mental illness), healthcare decisions are made by someone else - usually a family member or legal representative - guided by your known wishes or best interests.</p>

<h2>Working with written decision aids</h2>

<p>For some conditions, your doctor may provide decision aids - written or online information comparing treatment options. These are designed to help you understand options and think about what matters to you. Using these alongside discussion with your doctor supports good shared decision-making.</p>
