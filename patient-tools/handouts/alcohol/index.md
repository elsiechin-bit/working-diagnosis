<p>A "standard drink" in Aotearoa is a unit of measurement, not a serving size. It is defined as 10 grams of pure alcohol<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>, and most of the practical advice about safer drinking is expressed in standard drinks per week or per session - so it pays to know what one actually looks like. Almost no one pours one when they pour themselves a drink. Wine glasses have grown, beers have got stronger, and a generous restaurant pour is often closer to two.</p>

<h2>What one standard drink looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Beer (4% ABV):</strong> 330 mL bottle or can, or about two-thirds of a pint</li>
  <li><strong>Beer (5% ABV):</strong> 250 mL - a small handle, not a full pint</li>
  <li><strong>Wine (13% ABV):</strong> 100 mL - around a third of a normal wine glass</li>
  <li><strong>Spirits (40% ABV):</strong> 30 mL - a single nip or shot</li>
  <li><strong>RTDs and seltzers:</strong> check the label - many are 1.5 to 2 standard drinks per can</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ alcohol containers must show the number of standard drinks they hold. A 750 mL bottle of 13% wine contains around seven to eight standard drinks. A 1 litre bottle of 40% spirits holds about thirty-three.</p>

<h2>The low-risk drinking advice</h2>

<p>The current NZ low-risk advice, aligned with the 2020 Australian NHMRC guidelines<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>, is:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>No more than 10 standard drinks in a week</strong></li>
  <li><strong>No more than 4 standard drinks in any single session</strong></li>
  <li><strong>At least two alcohol-free days each week</strong></li>
  <li><strong>No alcohol at all</strong> if you are pregnant, planning pregnancy, breastfeeding, under 18, or have a liver condition</li>
</ul>

<p>"Low-risk" is exactly that - lower risk, not no risk. There is no level of alcohol intake that is completely risk-free, and the more recent the evidence, the more this point is emphasised.</p>

<h2>What the evidence says about the harms</h2>

<p>Alcohol is classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer as a Group 1 carcinogen - the same category as tobacco and asbestos<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup>. It raises the risk of cancers of the mouth, throat, oesophagus, liver, bowel, and breast. The breast-cancer link starts at low levels: even a single drink a day measurably increases lifetime risk.</p>

<p>The other harms most relevant in primary care include:</p>

<ul>
  <li>High blood pressure and atrial fibrillation</li>
  <li>Liver disease, from fatty liver through to cirrhosis</li>
  <li>Disrupted sleep - alcohol fragments deep sleep even at modest doses</li>
  <li>Worsening of depression and anxiety, particularly the morning after</li>
  <li>Falls, injuries, and road harm</li>
  <li>Foetal alcohol spectrum disorder if drunk in pregnancy</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical ways to cut down</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Track what you actually drink</strong> for a week, in standard drinks not glasses. Most people are surprised. The Cheers website has a free tracker.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan two or three alcohol-free days each week</strong> and stick to them. The body needs the recovery.</li>
  <li><strong>Use smaller glasses at home</strong> - a 150 mL wine glass instead of a 250 mL one halves your effective dose without changing your habit.</li>
  <li><strong>Switch to lower-strength options:</strong> 4% beer instead of 6%, 11% wine instead of 14%, soda water as a long mixer.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat first, alternate with water</strong>, and slow the rate. The first drink of the night does the most work.</li>
  <li><strong>Take a longer break.</strong> A month off (Dry July, FebFast, Sober October) is a useful reset, and reveals how much your sleep, mood, and weight were quietly carrying.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you think you have a problem</h2>

<p>Two informal signs that drinking has become harmful: you find yourself drinking earlier in the day than you used to, or you feel physically unwell - shaky, sweaty, anxious, nauseous - on a morning after not drinking. Both are worth talking to your GP about.</p>

<p>If you are physically dependent on alcohol, do not stop suddenly without medical advice - alcohol withdrawal can include seizures and is one of the few withdrawal syndromes that can kill you. Your GP can help plan a safe taper or refer you to specialist support, and there are funded medications (naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram) that genuinely help once you have stopped drinking.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are drinking above the low-risk advice and finding it hard to cut back</li>
  <li>You have shakes, sweating, anxiety, or nausea on mornings you have not drunk</li>
  <li>You are pregnant, planning pregnancy, or breastfeeding</li>
  <li>You have a liver condition, high blood pressure, or are on medicines that interact with alcohol (warfarin, opioids, sedatives, paracetamol at higher doses)</li>
  <li>Your mood, sleep, or relationships are being affected by your drinking</li>
  <li>You want to stop and would like help with medication options or specialist referral</li>
</ul>
