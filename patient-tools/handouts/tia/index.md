A transient ischaemic attack, or TIA, is a brief disturbance in blood supply to the brain. Symptoms look just like a stroke but resolve within minutes to a few hours, with no permanent damage on scans. The temptation is to feel relieved and move on. Don't. A TIA is a clear warning that a full stroke may be only days away. Urgent assessment cuts that risk by around 80%.&sup1;

<h2>What a TIA looks like</h2>

<p>Same symptoms as stroke - they just go away:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the face, arm, or leg</li>
<li>Sudden trouble speaking or understanding speech</li>
<li>Sudden vision loss in one eye, often described as a "curtain coming down"</li>
<li>Sudden imbalance, vertigo, or trouble walking</li>
<li>Sudden severe headache (less common)</li>
</ul>

<p>Most TIAs last 5-15 minutes. By definition they last under 24 hours, but anything beyond an hour is unusual and may be a stroke that recovers.</p>

<h2>What to do</h2>

<p><strong>Call 111 immediately</strong> if you are still having symptoms. The ambulance system cannot tell the difference between a stroke and a TIA at the door, and you should not be deciding either.</p>

<p>If symptoms have completely resolved, contact your GP or the urgent stroke clinic <strong>the same day</strong>. After hours, go to the emergency department. A TIA is not something to "see how it goes" overnight.</p>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>About 1 in 12 people who have a TIA will have a stroke within seven days, and 1 in 5 within three months, if untreated.&sup2; Almost half of those strokes happen within 48 hours.</p>

<p>The good news: prompt assessment and treatment cut that risk dramatically. A TIA is a chance to act before disaster strikes.</p>

<h2>What happens at assessment</h2>

<p>Most centres run a rapid TIA pathway. You can expect:</p>

<ul>
<li>Detailed history and examination</li>
<li>Brain imaging (CT or MRI) - to look for any actual damage and exclude bleeds</li>
<li>Carotid ultrasound - to look for narrowing of the neck arteries</li>
<li>ECG and sometimes a longer heart rhythm monitor - to look for atrial fibrillation</li>
<li>Echocardiogram in some cases - to look for heart sources of clots</li>
<li>Blood tests - cholesterol, glucose, kidney function</li>
</ul>

<p>The aim is to identify the cause so the right preventive treatment can begin.</p>

<h2>Treatment after a TIA</h2>

<p>You will usually leave with several medicines:</p>

<p><strong>Antiplatelet:</strong> aspirin alone, or aspirin plus clopidogrel for the first 21 days, then a single agent. If atrial fibrillation is found, an anticoagulant replaces antiplatelets - see our <a href="/handouts/anticoagulants/">anticoagulants handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>High-dose statin:</strong> atorvastatin 40-80 mg, even if cholesterol seemed normal. The aim is LDL below 1.4 mmol/L. See our <a href="/handouts/statins/">statins handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Blood pressure medicines:</strong> tightly controlled, target usually below 130/80.</p>

<p>If a tight carotid narrowing is found, you may be referred for surgery (carotid endarterectomy) or a stent.</p>

<h2>Driving</h2>

<p>In NZ, you must not drive for at least 1 month after a TIA, until you have had medical clearance. Commercial drivers need longer (at least 6 months and full neurological clearance). Tell NZTA - it is your legal responsibility.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle - the long-term work</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking - the single biggest improvement</li>
<li>Limit alcohol to under 10 standard drinks per week</li>
<li>Treat high blood pressure - check at home and aim under 130/80</li>
<li>Exercise - 150 minutes per week, even brisk walking</li>
<li>Eat Mediterranean-style - vegetables, fruit, fish, olive oil, less red meat</li>
<li>Treat diabetes carefully - HbA1c targets discussed with your GP</li>
<li>Treat sleep apnoea if present</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mood and energy</h2>

<p>Many people feel shaken or low-mood after a TIA, even though the symptoms went away. This is normal. Anxiety about a possible stroke is common. Tell your GP if it is not settling - support and treatment work well.</p>
