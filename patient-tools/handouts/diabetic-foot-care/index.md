Diabetes can damage nerves (so you do not feel small injuries) and reduce blood supply (so wounds heal slowly). The result: a small blister, scratch, or pressure point can become an ulcer that takes months to heal, and in some cases leads to infection and amputation. Around 1 in 4 people with diabetes will have a foot ulcer in their lifetime.&sup1; Daily care is the strongest prevention.

<h2>Daily foot check - 2 minutes</h2>

<p>Look at your feet every day. If you cannot see your soles, use a mirror or ask someone to look.</p>

<p>Check for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cuts, scratches, blisters</li>
<li>Redness, especially over pressure points</li>
<li>Swelling</li>
<li>Cracks (especially heels)</li>
<li>Calluses or corns</li>
<li>Discolouration or dark patches</li>
<li>Ingrown toenails</li>
<li>Tinea between toes (athlete's foot) - common entry point for cellulitis</li>
<li>Any change in shape or temperature</li>
</ul>

<p>If you notice anything new, especially that does not start healing in 24-48 hours, see your GP or podiatrist.</p>

<h2>Daily foot care</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wash feet daily in lukewarm water (test temperature with elbow first - nerve damage may not detect hot water)</li>
<li>Pat dry thoroughly, especially between toes</li>
<li>Moisturise tops and bottoms but not between toes (excess moisture there leads to fungal infection)</li>
<li>Cut nails straight across after a bath when soft. Do not dig into corners - file rough edges</li>
<li>If hard or thick nails, see a podiatrist</li>
<li>Treat athlete's foot promptly - antifungal cream and dry feet</li>
<li>Wear clean socks daily, change after exercise</li>
</ul>

<h2>Footwear</h2>

<p>Shoes are your protection. Choose:</p>

<ul>
<li>Well-fitting, deep-toed shoes with cushioned soles</li>
<li>Soft leather or breathable fabric</li>
<li>Adjustable laces or velcro - feet can swell during the day</li>
<li>Try on shoes in the afternoon when feet are largest</li>
<li>Replace worn shoes - thinning soles lose protection</li>
</ul>

<p>Avoid:</p>

<ul>
<li>Going barefoot, even at home</li>
<li>Open-toed sandals or flip flops in unfamiliar terrain</li>
<li>High heels and pointy toes</li>
<li>Hot water bottles, electric blankets, hot fireplaces directly against feet (burns risk if neuropathy)</li>
</ul>

<p>Always check inside shoes before putting them on - small stones, drawing pins, or insect bites can cause unnoticed injury. People with significant neuropathy regularly find foreign bodies they did not feel.</p>

<h2>Annual foot check with the GP or podiatrist</h2>

<p>Once a year, your GP or practice nurse will:</p>

<ul>
<li>Check sensation with a 10g monofilament (a soft fibre touched to specific points)</li>
<li>Check vibration sense with a tuning fork</li>
<li>Check pulses</li>
<li>Look for shape changes, calluses, ulcers</li>
<li>Categorise your foot risk</li>
</ul>

<p>Higher-risk feet (with neuropathy, poor circulation, foot deformity, or previous ulcer) need more frequent review and often free podiatrist visits.</p>

<h2>Risk levels</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Low risk:</strong> normal sensation and pulses. Annual review</li>
<li><strong>Moderate risk:</strong> reduced sensation OR pulses. 6-monthly podiatrist</li>
<li><strong>High risk:</strong> reduced sensation AND pulses, or callus, or deformity, or previous ulcer. 1-3 monthly podiatrist</li>
<li><strong>Active foot disease:</strong> ulcer, infection, or Charcot foot. Specialist team referral - same week</li>
</ul>

<h2>Free podiatry in NZ</h2>

<p>Most DHBs offer free or subsidised podiatry for people with diabetes at moderate or higher risk. Ask your GP for a referral.</p>

<h2>What an early foot ulcer looks like</h2>

<ul>
<li>Often starts as a callus or pressure spot, especially under the ball of the foot or heel</li>
<li>Skin breaks down underneath, sometimes with blood-stained callus</li>
<li>Once open, may not hurt at all if neuropathy is present</li>
<li>Can deepen rapidly</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>If you find any new break in the skin:</strong></p>

<ol>
<li>Clean with saline or running water</li>
<li>Cover with a clean dressing</li>
<li>Stay off it - reduce walking</li>
<li>See your GP or podiatrist same-day or next day</li>
</ol>

<h2>Warning signs - call same-day</h2>

<ul>
<li>Spreading redness around an ulcer or any wound</li>
<li>Pus or strong smell</li>
<li>New foot pain</li>
<li>Increasing swelling</li>
<li>Foot turning red, warm, and swollen for no obvious reason - could be Charcot arthropathy, an emergency in diabetes</li>
<li>Sudden coldness or pallor in a foot</li>
<li>Fever with foot symptoms</li>
</ul>

<p>Untreated infection can spread to bone within days.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking - smokers have far worse circulation and ulcer outcomes</li>
<li>Keep blood sugar in target range</li>
<li>Treat blood pressure and cholesterol</li>
<li>Stay active - walking improves circulation</li>
<li>Manage weight</li>
</ul>

<h2>Charcot foot - the unusual emergency</h2>

<p>Charcot arthropathy is a sudden destruction of foot bones in someone with diabetic neuropathy. The foot becomes hot, red, and swollen, often without pain or trauma. It is often mistaken for cellulitis or gout. Early diagnosis and immobilisation prevent permanent deformity. Any unexplained hot swollen foot in someone with diabetic neuropathy needs same-day specialist review.</p>
