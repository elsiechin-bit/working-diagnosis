<p>Tremor (involuntary shaking) is common and usually benign, but different types have different causes and treatments. This handout explains the main types of tremor and when to see a doctor.</p>

<h2>Types of tremor</h2>

<p><strong>Essential tremor:</strong> Shaking that occurs when your hands are held in a position (holding a cup, writing, eating). Usually affects both hands equally and runs in families. Not dangerous and does not worsen into Parkinson's disease.</p>

<p><strong>Parkinsonian tremor:</strong> Shaking at rest (when your hands are relaxed in your lap), often with other symptoms like slowness, stiffness, or balance problems. Suggests possible Parkinson's disease and requires evaluation.</p>

<p><strong>Physiological tremor:</strong> Fine shaking that occurs naturally in everyone when holding a position (especially visible when arms are outstretched). Usually not noticeable unless exaggerated by fatigue, caffeine, or cold.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<h3>For Essential Tremor:</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Reduce caffeine and stimulants.</strong> Caffeine worsens tremor - reducing coffee, tea, and energy drinks often helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress and fatigue.</strong> Tremor worsens with stress and tiredness. Relaxation and good sleep reduce severity.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid alcohol before important occasions.</strong> While alcohol temporarily reduces tremor, it is not a reliable treatment.</li>
  <li><strong>Occupational adaptation.</strong> Weighted utensils, splints, or modified techniques may help with daily tasks.</li>
  <li><strong>Medication if severe.</strong> Propranolol (a beta-blocker) effectively reduces essential tremor if it interferes with daily activities. Primidone is another option.</li>
</ul>

<h3>For Parkinsonian Tremor:</h3>

<p>Treatment depends on whether Parkinson's disease is confirmed. If confirmed, dopaminergic medicines significantly reduce tremor. Your neurologist will discuss options based on your symptoms.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You have developed new tremor</li>
  <li>Tremor is affecting your daily activities</li>
  <li>You have tremor at rest (not just with movement)</li>
  <li>You have tremor accompanied by slowness, stiffness, or balance problems</li>
  <li>Your tremor is affecting one side of your body more than the other</li>
  <li>Tremor has changed character or worsened significantly</li>
  <li>You want treatment to reduce tremor</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What type of tremor do I have?</li>
  <li>Do I need investigations (blood tests, imaging)?</li>
  <li>Could this be Parkinson's disease?</li>
  <li>Will it get worse over time?</li>
  <li>Are there treatments that might help?</li>
</ul>
