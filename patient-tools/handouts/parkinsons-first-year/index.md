<p>A Parkinson's disease diagnosis can feel overwhelming, but the first year is about understanding your condition, starting appropriate medicines, and learning what helps. Most people live well for many years with Parkinson's. This handout covers what to expect and how to manage in the first year.</p>

<h2>What Parkinson's disease is</h2>

<p>Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurological condition affecting movement and mood. It develops when nerve cells that produce dopamine (a chemical that helps control movement) gradually fail. This causes the characteristic movement problems and other symptoms. It is not immediately life-threatening, but symptoms gradually worsen over years and affect quality of life.</p>

<h2>First-year symptoms you may notice</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Tremor - usually a pill-rolling tremor (thumb and fingers) at rest, worse with stress</li>
  <li>Slowness of movement (bradykinesia) - difficulty initiating movement, taking longer to do things</li>
  <li>Muscle rigidity - stiffness and resistance to movement</li>
  <li>Balance problems - increased fall risk, difficulty turning</li>
  <li>Stooped posture - tendency to lean forward</li>
  <li>Mask-like expression - reduced facial expression</li>
  <li>Small handwriting - writing becoming smaller (micrographia)</li>
  <li>Speech changes - quieter, monotone voice</li>
  <li>Non-motor symptoms - fatigue, mood changes, sleep problems, constipation</li>
</ul>

<h2>First-year medication - starting dopamine replacement</h2>

<p>The cornerstone of first-year treatment is dopamine replacement. Levodopa (L-DOPA) combined with carbidopa is the most effective medicine and remains the gold standard. Many people tolerate it well in the first year. Other options include dopamine agonists (like pramipexole) and MAO-B inhibitors (like selegiline). Your neurologist will choose based on your age, symptoms, and other medicines. The goal in year one is symptom control and maintaining function, not preventing disease progression.</p>

<h2>What actually helps in the first year</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Start physiotherapy early.</strong> A physiotherapist trained in Parkinson's can teach movement strategies, balance exercises, and posture correction. These become increasingly valuable as time goes on.</li>
  <li><strong>Stay physically active.</strong> Exercise slows disease progression. Aim for regular activity - walking, swimming, tai chi, or dancing. Even moderate activity helps tremor and rigidity.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage non-motor symptoms.</strong> Sleep quality, mood, and constipation need attention. Address these early rather than waiting; they affect quality of life significantly.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan ahead for driving.</strong> If Parkinson's affects your reflexes or confidence, discuss driving safety with your doctor. Many people drive safely for several years, but honesty about your abilities matters.</li>
  <li><strong>Take medicines consistently.</strong> Dopamine medicines work best with regular timing. Set reminders and keep a medicines schedule.</li>
  <li><strong>Modify your home for safety.</strong> Remove trip hazards, improve lighting, install grab bars where needed. Falls are a significant risk as balance worsens.</li>
  <li><strong>Seek psychological support.</strong> Depression is common in Parkinson's and worsens in year one. Talking to a therapist or your doctor helps.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Non-motor symptoms - often overlooked but important</h2>

<p>Depression, anxiety, sleep disturbance, and constipation are common and treatable. Fatigue can be profound. These are not psychological but part of the disease and respond to treatment. Address them early; do not assume you must just manage them. Sleep problems improve with dopamine medicines. Constipation responds to stool softeners and increased fibre. Mood problems improve with antidepressants or dopamine-optimizing medicines.</p>

<h2>When to see your neurologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>To establish diagnosis and start medicines</li>
  <li>To adjust dopamine medicine if symptoms worsen</li>
  <li>If you develop side effects from medicines</li>
  <li>If new symptoms develop (speech problems, balance worsening, falls)</li>
  <li>To plan for future needs (physiotherapy, referrals, driving assessment)</li>
  <li>If depression or anxiety develops</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to expect beyond the first year</h2>

<p>Symptoms gradually progress, but the rate varies widely. Some people remain stable for many years; others progress faster. Motor complications (dyskinesia, wearing-off) emerge in the second or third year as dopamine medicine effects shorten. Adjusting medicines and timing doses helps manage this. The first year is about understanding your individual pattern and building a management plan with your neurologist and physiotherapist that you can adapt as time goes on.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your neurologist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What stage is my Parkinson's at?</li>
  <li>Which medicine should I start, and what are the side effects?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist or speech therapist?</li>
  <li>How often will we adjust medicines?</li>
  <li>What lifestyle factors help slow progression?</li>
  <li>Should I be concerned about my driving?</li>
  <li>What support is available for depression or anxiety?</li>
</ul>
