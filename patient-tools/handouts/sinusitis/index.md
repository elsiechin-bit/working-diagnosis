Sinusitis is inflammation of the air-filled cavities in your face. Most cases follow a viral cold and clear up by themselves within 10-14 days. Antibiotics are usually not needed and rarely shorten the illness. Knowing how to manage symptoms at home and what warning signs to watch for is more useful than chasing prescriptions.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Pain or pressure across cheeks, forehead, or between the eyes</li>
<li>Pain worse leaning forward</li>
<li>Blocked nose</li>
<li>Thick yellow or green nasal discharge</li>
<li>Reduced sense of smell</li>
<li>Mild fever, fatigue</li>
<li>Tooth pain (upper teeth, from maxillary sinus pressure)</li>
<li>Cough, especially at night (postnasal drip)</li>
</ul>

<p>Coloured mucus does not mean bacterial infection - it just means immune cells are working. Most of these are still viral.</p>

<h2>Acute vs chronic</h2>

<p><strong>Acute sinusitis</strong> lasts under 4 weeks. Most viral, usually after a cold. Settles in 10-14 days.</p>

<p><strong>Chronic sinusitis</strong> lasts 12 weeks or more. Often involves nasal polyps, allergy, or anatomical issues. Needs longer-term management and often ENT input.</p>

<h2>Self-care</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Saline nasal rinses</strong> (NeilMed, Sterimar, or homemade with sterile or boiled-and-cooled water plus a teaspoon of salt). Twice daily. The single best home measure - clears mucus and reduces inflammation</li>
<li><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for pain and fever</li>
<li><strong>Steam inhalation</strong> - sit in a steamy bathroom or use a bowl of hot water with a towel over your head. Be careful not to scald</li>
<li><strong>Decongestant nasal sprays</strong> (oxymetazoline, xylometazoline) - use for no more than 5 days, as longer use causes rebound congestion</li>
<li><strong>Drink plenty of fluids</strong></li>
<li><strong>Warm compresses</strong> on the face</li>
<li><strong>Sleep with the head propped up</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Steroid nasal sprays</h2>

<p>For more troublesome or recurrent sinusitis, steroid nasal sprays (fluticasone, budesonide, mometasone) reduce inflammation and help drainage. Available without prescription. Start at the first sign and use daily for 2-4 weeks. Technique matters - aim slightly outwards, not at the septum, and breathe gently through the nose without sniffing hard.</p>

<h2>When are antibiotics useful?</h2>

<p>Most sinusitis does not need antibiotics. Consider them if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Symptoms have not improved by day 10</li>
<li>Symptoms initially improved then got worse again ("double sickening")</li>
<li>Severe symptoms with high fever and intense facial pain</li>
<li>Spread to the eye or signs of complications (see below)</li>
</ul>

<p>If antibiotics are needed, amoxicillin is usually first line. A 5-7 day course is usually enough.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - same-day care</h2>

<p>See a GP urgently or call 111 for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe headache, neck stiffness, drowsiness or confusion</li>
<li>Vision changes, eye swelling, or eye pain on movement</li>
<li>Swelling of the forehead</li>
<li>High fever with feeling very unwell</li>
<li>Symptoms lasting over 3-4 weeks despite treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>Rare but serious complications include orbital cellulitis, intracranial spread, and bone infection.</p>

<h2>Chronic sinusitis</h2>

<p>If symptoms persist beyond 12 weeks, see your GP. Triggers and contributors include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Allergic rhinitis - see our <a href="/handouts/allergic-rhinitis/">hayfever handout</a></li>
<li>Nasal polyps</li>
<li>Deviated nasal septum</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Dental disease (especially upper molars)</li>
<li>Immune problems</li>
</ul>

<p>Investigations may include CT scan and ENT referral. Treatment commonly includes long-term saline rinses with steroid spray, and sometimes surgery (functional endoscopic sinus surgery).</p>

<h2>Prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Treat hayfever well</li>
<li>Stop smoking</li>
<li>Wash hands during cold and flu season</li>
<li>Stay up to date with flu and COVID vaccinations</li>
<li>Saline rinses daily during the cold season for those with frequent sinusitis</li>
</ul>
