Allergic rhinitis, often called hayfever, affects around 1 in 4 New Zealanders.&sup1; The classic pattern is sneezing, blocked nose, and itchy red eyes. It is often dismissed as trivial but can ruin sleep, school performance, and exercise. The good news: a stepwise treatment plan controls symptoms in almost everyone.

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
<li>Sneezing, often in clusters</li>
<li>Runny clear nasal discharge</li>
<li>Blocked nose</li>
<li>Itchy nose, mouth, throat, or ears</li>
<li>Itchy, watery, red eyes</li>
<li>Postnasal drip</li>
<li>Tiredness, irritability, poor sleep</li>
<li>"Allergic salute" in children (rubbing the nose upward, leaving a horizontal crease)</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms are bilateral. One-sided symptoms suggest something else (foreign body in children, polyp, or rarely tumour in adults).</p>

<h2>Common NZ triggers</h2>

<p><strong>Seasonal:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Spring tree pollens (silver birch, oak)</li>
<li>Summer grass pollens (rye, cocksfoot) - peak in November-January</li>
<li>Autumn weeds and moulds</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Year-round:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>House dust mite</li>
<li>Pet dander (cat especially)</li>
<li>Cockroaches</li>
<li>Indoor moulds</li>
</ul>

<p>Many people are sensitised to multiple triggers.</p>

<h2>Identifying triggers</h2>

<p>Diary works for most people:</p>

<ul>
<li>Time of year</li>
<li>Outdoor vs indoor exposure</li>
<li>Specific contacts (mowing, gardening, animals, certain houses)</li>
</ul>

<p>Allergy testing (skin prick or specific IgE blood test) is useful when:</p>

<ul>
<li>Symptoms are severe and not controlled with treatment</li>
<li>Considering immunotherapy</li>
<li>Asthma is also poorly controlled</li>
</ul>

<h2>Avoidance - what helps and what doesn't</h2>

<p><strong>Helps:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Pollen: keep windows closed during peak pollen times; shower and change clothes after coming inside; sunglasses outside</li>
<li>Pet dander: keep pets out of bedrooms; HEPA filter; regular bathing of pets reduces dander modestly</li>
<li>House dust mite: hot wash bedding weekly (60°C+); allergen-proof mattress and pillow covers; reduce soft toys and carpet in bedroom; ventilate</li>
<li>Mould: fix leaks; ventilate bathrooms; clean obvious mould</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Less effective:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Air purifiers - help only modestly</li>
<li>Routine carpet replacement (often impractical and overrated)</li>
<li>Eliminating one ingredient from diet</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - the step-up plan</h2>

<p><strong>Step 1: Daily intranasal steroid spray.</strong> The single most effective treatment - far better than antihistamines for nasal symptoms. Start 1-2 weeks before peak season if you know your trigger. Daily, every day in season.</p>

<ul>
<li>Fluticasone (Flixonase, Flixotide) - over the counter</li>
<li>Mometasone (Nasonex)</li>
<li>Budesonide (Rhinocort)</li>
<li>Beclomethasone</li>
</ul>

<p>Technique: tilt head slightly forward, aim spray to outer wall of nostril (not septum), gentle breath - do not sniff hard.</p>

<p><strong>Step 2: Add a non-sedating oral antihistamine.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Cetirizine 10 mg, fexofenadine 180 mg, or loratadine 10 mg daily</li>
<li>Available over the counter</li>
<li>Older antihistamines (chlorpheniramine, promethazine) cause drowsiness and impaired thinking - avoid for daytime use</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Step 3: Add intranasal antihistamine</strong> (azelastine - Otrivine Allergy) - works within 15 minutes. Combination spray with fluticasone (Dymista) is very effective.</p>

<p><strong>Step 4: Add eye drops</strong> - olopatadine, ketotifen, or sodium cromoglycate for itchy red eyes.</p>

<p><strong>Step 5: Specialist input</strong> - allergy testing, immunotherapy, montelukast for severe disease.</p>

<h2>Allergen immunotherapy</h2>

<p>Sublingual tablets or drops (taken under the tongue daily for 3-5 years) can desensitise the immune system. Available privately in NZ for grass pollen and dust mite. Effective in many people but a substantial commitment.</p>

<h2>Hayfever and asthma</h2>

<p>About 80% of people with asthma also have hayfever, and treating the nose well improves asthma control. If you have both, your GP will treat them together.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Most steroid nasal sprays and second-generation antihistamines (loratadine, cetirizine) are considered safe in pregnancy. Discuss with your GP or pharmacist.</p>

<h2>Children</h2>

<p>Allergic rhinitis in children is often missed. It causes mouth-breathing, snoring, poor sleep, and reduced school concentration. Treatment is the same stepped plan; nasal sprays are licensed from age 4-6 depending on product.</p>
