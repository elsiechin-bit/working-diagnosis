<p>Stress is a normal response to demand. Burnout is what happens when stress has been on too long without enough recovery. Around 1 in 4 NZ workers report burnout symptoms in any given year, and rates are higher in healthcare, education, and frontline service jobs.[1] This handout walks through the difference, the warning signs, and the things that genuinely help, including the things that do not.</p>

<h2>Stress vs burnout</h2>

<p>Stress is acute - a response to specific demands. You feel it during the busy week, settle when the project ends. Burnout is a state - the body and mind have shifted into a new baseline of exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced capacity.</p>

<table>
<tr><th>Stress</th><th>Burnout</th></tr>
<tr><td>Over-engaged, hyper-aroused</td><td>Disengaged, blunted</td></tr>
<tr><td>Emotions are loud</td><td>Emotions are flat</td></tr>
<tr><td>Loss of energy</td><td>Loss of motivation, ideals, hope</td></tr>
<tr><td>Anxiety predominates</td><td>Detachment and depression predominate</td></tr>
<tr><td>"I cannot keep this up"</td><td>"I do not care anymore"</td></tr>
<tr><td>Recovers with rest</td><td>Does not fully recover with a long weekend</td></tr>
</table>

<h2>The three components of burnout</h2>

<p>Christina Maslach's framework, the most-used clinical model:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Exhaustion</strong> - depleted physical and emotional resources. Tired in a way sleep does not fix.</li>
  <li><strong>Cynicism / depersonalisation</strong> - distancing yourself from work, becoming negative about it, treating people as objects rather than humans</li>
  <li><strong>Reduced personal accomplishment</strong> - feeling ineffective, that nothing you do matters, that you are not good at the job</li>
</ul>

<p>You can have one of these prominently and not the others - but typically by the time someone is genuinely burnt out, all three are present in some form.</p>

<h2>What burnout looks like physically</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Fatigue that does not lift with weekends or even a week off</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance - either insomnia, or sleeping a lot and waking unrefreshed</li>
  <li>Headaches, neck and back pain</li>
  <li>Gut symptoms - reflux, change in bowel habits</li>
  <li>Frequent minor illnesses - immune system runs down</li>
  <li>Reduced exercise tolerance</li>
  <li>Appetite change, weight change</li>
  <li>Reduced libido</li>
  <li>Worsening of any chronic condition you have</li>
</ul>

<h2>What burnout looks like emotionally and cognitively</h2>

<ul>
  <li>"Sunday night dread" extending to most nights</li>
  <li>Cynicism, sarcasm, irritability about work and the people in it</li>
  <li>Sense of futility - "what's the point"</li>
  <li>Difficulty concentrating, making decisions, finishing tasks</li>
  <li>Forgetfulness - leaving things at home, missing appointments</li>
  <li>Emotional flatness - things that used to be enjoyable feel beige</li>
  <li>Increased dread, low-grade anxiety, but also a numbness</li>
  <li>Drinking more, eating worse, exercising less</li>
  <li>Withdrawing from friends and partner</li>
</ul>

<h2>What burnout is NOT</h2>

<ul>
  <li>"Just" being tired</li>
  <li>A character weakness</li>
  <li>A sign you are bad at your job</li>
  <li>Something a long weekend will fix</li>
  <li>Something you can think your way out of with positive thinking</li>
</ul>

<p>Burnout is largely about the gap between the demands placed on you and the resources you have to meet them. People in low-control, high-demand jobs (healthcare, teaching, social work, customer service) have the highest rates regardless of personal resilience. The person is not the problem.</p>

<h2>What genuinely helps</h2>

<h3>Acknowledge the shift</h3>
<p>The first step is naming it. "This is not normal tiredness; I am burnt out." Not as a diagnosis to wear, but as a description of state that warrants different action than just trying harder.</p>

<h3>Reduce the load if you possibly can</h3>
<p>Recovery from burnout requires actual reduction in demand for a period of weeks to months. Options on a spectrum:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Take leave - annual leave, sick leave, long service leave</li>
  <li>Reduce hours temporarily</li>
  <li>Drop one significant external commitment</li>
  <li>Renegotiate workload with your manager</li>
  <li>Move to a different role within the same organisation</li>
  <li>Change jobs</li>
</ul>

<p>Most people resist reducing the load because the work depends on them. Sometimes that is true. Sometimes it just feels that way. Either way, the work usually continues whether you are there or not.</p>

<h3>Sleep, food, movement</h3>
<p>Boring, foundational, work. Aim for 7-8 hours of sleep, regular meals, daily walking outside. The body cannot recover without these.</p>

<h3>Reconnect</h3>
<p>Burnt-out people withdraw. Reversing that helps. Lunch with a friend. Phone your sister. Sit on the porch with the partner. Time with people whose presence is restorative rather than demanding.</p>

<h3>Find one thing</h3>
<p>One activity that has nothing to do with productivity. Garden, music, drawing, fishing, cooking for pleasure. The thing burnout most reliably steals is the capacity to do things for their own sake. Reclaiming a tiny amount of that is medicinal.</p>

<h3>Talk to someone</h3>
<p>Your GP can rule out the things that look like burnout (thyroid, anaemia, sleep apnoea, depression), and can suggest counselling or medical leave if needed. Some employee assistance programmes (EAP) offer 4-6 free sessions of counselling - ask HR. Your union if you have one.</p>

<h3>Therapy</h3>
<p>CBT-based and acceptance-based therapies help when burnout overlaps with depression and anxiety, which is common. Short courses often enough.</p>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>If burnout has tipped into clinical depression or significant anxiety, antidepressants can help while the rest of the recovery happens. Discuss with your GP.</p>

<h2>Things that look helpful but mostly are not</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Resilience training at work.</strong> Useful in mild cases; an insult to the burnt-out who need the workload changed, not their attitude.</li>
  <li><strong>Yoga retreats and detoxes.</strong> Pleasant. Don't fix the working conditions you return to.</li>
  <li><strong>Pushing through.</strong> Burnout is not laziness. Pushing harder increases the deficit.</li>
  <li><strong>Quitting impulsively without a plan.</strong> Sometimes necessary, often regretted. Talk to your GP, partner, financial adviser before making big decisions.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Recovery takes time</h2>

<p>Most people recover from burnout, fully. Timeframes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Mild: 1-3 months with reduced load, sleep, support</li>
  <li>Moderate: 3-9 months, often with formal time off</li>
  <li>Severe: 6-18 months, often with significant career changes</li>
</ul>

<p>The mistake people make is going back to full demand too early. The body recovers slower than motivation does.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You suspect burnout and want help working out what to do</li>
  <li>Persistent low mood, hopelessness, or thoughts of self-harm</li>
  <li>You need a sickness certificate for time off</li>
  <li>Physical symptoms that need ruling out (chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, severe fatigue)</li>
  <li>You are using alcohol or drugs to cope</li>
  <li>Your relationships, parenting, or driving safety is being affected</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specific to healthcare workers</h2>

<p>Burnout in NZ healthcare is endemic. Specialist support exists:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Doctor's Health Programme</strong> - free confidential counselling for doctors, dentists, medical students. <a href="https://www.dhc.org.nz/">dhc.org.nz</a></li>
  <li><strong>Nurses' Health Programme</strong> - through the College of Nurses</li>
  <li><strong>Allied health peer support</strong> - through professional bodies</li>
  <li><strong>1737</strong> - confidential, NZ-based, doctors and nurses use it too</li>
</ul>
