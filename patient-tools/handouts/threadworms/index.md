<p>Threadworms are harmless intestinal parasites that are common in children and easily spread within families. While uncomfortable, they are treatable with simple medicines and hygiene measures. This handout explains what to look for and how to treat them effectively.</p>

<h2>What are threadworms</h2>

<p>Threadworms (pinworms) are small white parasites about the size of a thread that live in the large intestine. They cause no serious harm but are annoying because the female worms crawl out of the anus at night to lay eggs, causing itching. They spread via contaminated fingers, bedding, and dust.</p>

<h2>Signs of threadworm infection</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Itching around the anus, especially at night</li>
  <li>Restlessness or sleep disturbance due to itching</li>
  <li>Visible small white worms in stool or nappy</li>
  <li>Visible worms around the anus</li>
  <li>Girls may have vulval itching or irritation</li>
  <li>Sometimes poor appetite or slight tummy discomfort</li>
  <li>No fever or serious illness</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis and checking</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Visual inspection:</strong> Look at stool or around the anus, especially at night or first thing in the morning, for tiny white thread-like worms.</li>
  <li><strong>Scotch tape test:</strong> Press clear adhesive tape to the skin around the anus first thing in the morning (before defecating or wiping), then stick it to a slide to look for eggs under a microscope. Your GP can arrange this if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>No blood tests needed:</strong> Threadworms are diagnosed by visual inspection or tape test, not by blood tests.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment with medicine</h2>

<p><strong>Medicines are simple and effective.</strong> Mebendazole is the usual first choice - a single dose tablet or liquid that kills adult worms. A second dose after 2 weeks kills any worms that have hatched from surviving eggs.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dose:</strong> A single dose of mebendazole (usually 100 mg), repeated after 2 weeks.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat the whole family:</strong> All household members should be treated at the same time, even if asymptomatic, to prevent reinfection.</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> Mebendazole is very safe with minimal side effects.</li>
  <li><strong>Availability:</strong> Available over-the-counter from pharmacies without a prescription.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Hygiene measures alongside medicine</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Wash hands regularly.</strong> Hand-washing after toilet use and before meals is the most important prevention measure.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep nails short.</strong> Prevents harbouring eggs under the nails.</li>
  <li><strong>Shower or bathe in the morning.</strong> Removes eggs laid during the night.</li>
  <li><strong>Wash bedding and towels.</strong> In hot water after treatment to remove any eggs.</li>
  <li><strong>Clean the bathroom.</strong> Wipe down toilet seat and handle with disinfectant.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid scratching.</strong> Scratching spreads eggs on fingers to mouth and fomites.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When reinfection happens</h2>

<p>Reinfection is common, especially if hygiene measures are not followed or if other family members have untreated infections. If symptoms return after a few weeks, repeat treatment. Persistent reinfection suggests ongoing transmission from an untreated family member or inadequate hygiene.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You suspect threadworms but are unsure of the diagnosis</li>
  <li>Treatment does not work after two doses</li>
  <li>Your child develops other symptoms (fever, vomiting, severe pain)</li>
  <li>You need confirmation of diagnosis for school or other purposes</li>
</ul>

<h2>Notifying schools or childcare</h2>

<p>Most schools and childcare centres do not require notification of threadworms as they are not serious and treatment is straightforward. However, it may be helpful to notify if your child will be absent or to ask about their hygiene policies.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your pharmacist or GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Is treatment needed for the whole family?</li>
  <li>What dose should each family member take?</li>
  <li>When is the best time to take the repeat dose?</li>
  <li>What hygiene measures are most important?</li>
  <li>How can I prevent my child from getting threadworms again?</li>
</ul>
