A personalised written asthma action plan is one of the most powerful tools in asthma care. Studies show that people with a written plan are roughly half as likely to be admitted to hospital and have fewer flare-ups overall.&sup1; Despite this, only around 1 in 4 NZ adults with asthma have one. If you do not, ask your GP at your next visit.

<h2>What an action plan is</h2>

<p>A simple two-sided document that tells you exactly:</p>

<ul>
<li>What medicines to take when you are well</li>
<li>What to do when symptoms get worse</li>
<li>When to seek urgent help</li>
</ul>

<p>It uses a traffic-light system - green, yellow (or orange), and red - based on symptoms, sometimes peak flow.</p>

<h2>Green zone - well</h2>

<p>You are in the green zone when:</p>

<ul>
<li>No symptoms or only mild and short-lived</li>
<li>Sleeping well, no night waking</li>
<li>Reliever needed less than twice a week</li>
<li>Normal activity</li>
</ul>

<p>What to do: take your usual preventer inhaler every day, even though you feel well. The preventer is what is keeping you well.</p>

<h2>Yellow zone - getting worse</h2>

<p>You are in the yellow zone when any of:</p>

<ul>
<li>Increased reliever need (over 3-4 times a day)</li>
<li>Symptoms not fully relieved by reliever</li>
<li>Wheeze, cough, or tight chest most days</li>
<li>Waking at night with symptoms</li>
<li>Activity affected</li>
<li>Peak flow drops to 60-80% of your best</li>
</ul>

<p>What to do (typical plan):</p>

<ul>
<li>Use reliever as needed</li>
<li>Increase preventer 4-fold for 7-14 days, OR start a course of oral prednisone if your plan says so (typically 30-50 mg daily for 5-7 days)</li>
<li>If on Symbicort MART, increase your daily Symbicort and use it for relief too</li>
<li>Watch closely. If not better within 24-48 hours, see your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>Red zone - severe</h2>

<p>You are in the red zone when any of:</p>

<ul>
<li>Reliever not lasting 3 hours</li>
<li>Cannot speak full sentences</li>
<li>Severe breathlessness</li>
<li>Lips or fingernails turning blue</li>
<li>Drowsy or confused</li>
<li>Peak flow below 60% of your best</li>
</ul>

<p>What to do:</p>

<ol>
<li>Sit upright; do not lie flat</li>
<li>Take 6 puffs of reliever via spacer (one puff at a time, with a breath)</li>
<li>Call <strong>111</strong></li>
<li>Repeat 6 puffs every 5 minutes until help arrives</li>
<li>Take oral prednisone if you have it</li>
</ol>

<p>This is a medical emergency. Do not wait to see if it improves.</p>

<h2>Getting your plan</h2>

<p>Ask your GP or practice nurse for a written action plan at your next asthma review. The Asthma + Respiratory Foundation NZ has free templates - download one and complete it together. Some practices print them on coloured A4 with your name and personal medicine details.</p>

<h2>Making the plan work</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Keep it visible.</strong> Stick it on the fridge, take a photo on your phone</li>
<li><strong>Share it.</strong> Give a copy to your spouse, parents, kids, employer if relevant</li>
<li><strong>Carry your reliever everywhere.</strong> Symbicort MART users always have their preventer-reliever on them</li>
<li><strong>Have a flare kit.</strong> Reliever, spacer, oral prednisone if your GP has prescribed standby. Keep at home, not just in your handbag</li>
<li><strong>Review it yearly</strong> with your GP, or after any flare</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is in a good plan</h2>

<ul>
<li>Your usual preventer with the dose</li>
<li>Your reliever with how to use it</li>
<li>Your peak flow personal best (if used)</li>
<li>Yellow zone instructions specific to your inhalers</li>
<li>Standby prednisone instructions if appropriate</li>
<li>Triggers to avoid</li>
<li>Vaccination reminders (annual flu, COVID, pneumococcal)</li>
<li>Phone number for your GP and after-hours service</li>
<li>"Call 111" criteria clearly written</li>
</ul>

<h2>Peak flow monitoring</h2>

<p>Not everyone needs a peak flow meter. Useful for:</p>

<ul>
<li>People who do not feel symptoms early</li>
<li>People with frequent flares</li>
<li>People starting a new treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>How: stand up, full breath in, blow out hard and fast into the meter. Best of three. Compare to your personal best (the highest reading you can produce when well). Plan zones are written as percentages of personal best.</p>

<h2>Children</h2>

<p>Children should have their own action plan, especially if they take their own medicines or attend school. Schools and ECEs can keep a copy with the child's reliever and spacer at the office.</p>

<h2>After a hospital visit</h2>

<p>Always see your GP within 1-2 weeks of any ED or hospital visit for asthma. The plan often needs updating: stronger preventer, allergen check, technique review, trigger discussion.</p>
