<p>There is no single best contraceptive. The right one depends on your medical history, your lifestyle, whether you might want children in the future, and what side effects you can live with. This handout is a comparison overview. Each method has its own dedicated handout with more detail.</p>

<h2>The three things that matter most</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>How effective is it</strong> - both with perfect use and with typical use</li>
  <li><strong>How long it lasts and how reversible it is</strong></li>
  <li><strong>What does the routine look like</strong> - daily, weekly, monthly, multi-year, one-off</li>
</ol>

<h2>The "long-acting reversible contraceptives" (LARCs) - the most effective</h2>

<p>LARCs are over 99% effective with typical use. They are the most reliable methods because once they are in, you do not need to do anything daily.</p>

<h3>Hormonal IUD (Mirena, Jaydess)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> &gt;99% (1 in 500 chance of pregnancy per year)</li>
  <li><strong>Lasts:</strong> 5-8 years (Mirena), 3 years (Jaydess)</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Releases small amounts of progestogen locally in the uterus. Thickens cervical mucus, thins the uterine lining, sometimes stops ovulation</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Very effective. Periods often become much lighter or stop entirely. Treats heavy bleeding. Funded in NZ.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Requires insertion (5-10 minute procedure, can be uncomfortable). Spotting in the first 3-6 months. Not protective against STIs.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Mirena fully funded since 2019. Jaydess funded with Special Authority.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Copper IUD</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> &gt;99%</li>
  <li><strong>Lasts:</strong> 5-10 years depending on type</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Copper is toxic to sperm; also creates an inflammatory environment in the uterus that prevents fertilisation and implantation</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Hormone-free. Very effective. Can also be used as emergency contraception up to 5 days after sex.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Periods can be heavier and crampier. Requires insertion. Not protective against STIs.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Yes, fully funded.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Contraceptive implant (Jadelle)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> &gt;99%</li>
  <li><strong>Lasts:</strong> 5 years</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Two small rods inserted under the skin of the upper arm; release progestogen continuously</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Very effective. No daily routine. Quick removal, fertility returns within weeks.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Insertion and removal procedures. Irregular bleeding common, especially in the first year. Not protective against STIs.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Fully funded.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Depot injection (Depo-Provera)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> 94% with typical use, 99% with perfect use (every-12-week injection)</li>
  <li><strong>Lasts:</strong> 12 weeks per dose</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> No daily routine. Periods often stop. Useful in some women with heavy or painful periods.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Possible weight gain. Possible mood effects. Bone density may decrease with long-term use (regains on stopping). Fertility return can take 6-12 months. Not STI-protective.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Yes.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Hormonal methods that need a daily or weekly routine</h2>

<h3>Combined pill (oestrogen + progestogen)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> 91% typical use, &gt;99% perfect use</li>
  <li><strong>Routine:</strong> One pill daily</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Lighter and more predictable periods. Reduces acne. Can be skipped for special occasions. Reduces ovarian and endometrial cancer risk.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Easy to forget. Small increase in blood clot risk (especially smokers, over 35, migraine with aura, BMI &gt;35, recent surgery). Not for everyone.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Yes - several brands (Ava, Levlen, Mercilon, Mercilon-30, Microgynon).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Progestogen-only pill ("mini-pill")</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> 91% typical use, 99% perfect use</li>
  <li><strong>Routine:</strong> One pill daily, ideally at the same time</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Safe in many situations where combined pill is not (breastfeeding, smokers over 35, migraine with aura, history of clot). Suitable from postpartum onward.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Time-sensitive (window narrower than combined pill for older formulations; the newer drospirenone pill has a wider window). Irregular bleeding common, especially early on.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Yes (Microlut, Cerazette, Slinda).</li>
</ul>

<h3>Vaginal ring (NuvaRing)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> 91% typical use, 99% perfect use</li>
  <li><strong>Routine:</strong> Insert vaginally, leave for 3 weeks, remove for 1 week</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Less daily routine than the pill. Effective.</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Not funded in NZ - around NZ$80-100 per ring.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Barrier methods - the only ones that protect against STIs</h2>

<h3>Condoms (external, on the penis)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> 87% typical use, 98% perfect use</li>
  <li><strong>Pros:</strong> Available everywhere, no prescription, protects against STIs</li>
  <li><strong>Cons:</strong> Routine at the moment. Can break or slip. Latex allergy.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded:</strong> Free at Family Planning, sexual health clinics, some GP practices, and through Public Health.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Internal (insertable) condoms</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Worn by the receptive partner. Effectiveness similar to external condoms with practice. STI protection.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Diaphragms</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Less commonly used in NZ. Less effective than other methods. Requires fitting and use with spermicide.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Permanent methods</h2>

<h3>Vasectomy (male sterilisation)</h3>
<p>Day procedure under local anaesthetic. Highly effective once confirmed by sperm-free semen sample at 3 months. Reversal possible but not always successful. Funded with referral in some DHBs; most often paid privately around NZ$1,500-2,500.</p>

<h3>Tubal ligation (female sterilisation)</h3>
<p>Surgical procedure under general anaesthetic. Either tubes are blocked, cut, or removed. Highly effective. Reversal possible but difficult. Limited public funding.</p>

<h2>Natural family planning and withdrawal</h2>

<p>Less effective methods. Withdrawal is around 78% effective with typical use. Fertility awareness methods range from 76-99% depending on technique and consistency. Useful for some couples but not generally first-line for high-effectiveness contraception.</p>

<h2>Things to talk about with your GP or Family Planning</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your medical history (clots, migraine, breast cancer, liver disease, high blood pressure)</li>
  <li>Whether you might want children in the next 1, 5, or 10 years</li>
  <li>Whether STI protection matters in your situation</li>
  <li>How you feel about hormones</li>
  <li>What your daily routine looks like and how reliable a daily method would be for you</li>
  <li>Any concerns from previous methods</li>
</ul>
