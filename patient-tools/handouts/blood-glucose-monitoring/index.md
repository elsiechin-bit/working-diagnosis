Home blood glucose monitoring lets you see how your diabetes is responding to food, activity, illness, and medicines. Not everyone with diabetes needs to check every day - the right approach depends on which medicines you take and what your goals are.

<h2>Who needs to check?</h2>

<p><strong>Often (multiple times daily):</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Anyone on insulin (type 1 or type 2)</li>
<li>Pregnant women with gestational diabetes</li>
<li>Anyone unstable or unwell</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Sometimes:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>People on sulfonylureas (gliclazide, glipizide) - check before driving and if hypo symptoms</li>
<li>People starting a new medicine and watching its effect</li>
<li>People with very variable readings</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Often not needed:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Type 2 diabetes managed by lifestyle alone or metformin alone with good HbA1c</li>
<li>Stable, low-risk medication regimens</li>
</ul>

<p>Discuss with your GP what is right for you.</p>

<h2>What the targets are</h2>

<p>Most adults with type 2 diabetes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Fasting (before breakfast): 4-7 mmol/L</li>
<li>2 hours after meals: 5-10 mmol/L (sometimes up to 8)</li>
</ul>

<p>Targets vary by individual. Older or frail people may have higher targets to avoid hypos. Pregnant women have tighter targets. Your GP or diabetes nurse will tell you yours.</p>

<h2>How to check</h2>

<ol>
<li>Wash hands with soap and water; dry well. Avoid alcohol wipes (alters reading)</li>
<li>Insert a fresh test strip into the meter</li>
<li>Use the lancing device on the side of a fingertip - less painful than the pad</li>
<li>Touch a small drop of blood to the test strip</li>
<li>Wait for the result (5-10 seconds)</li>
<li>Record in the meter, app, or paper logbook with the time and any context (before/after meals, exercise, illness)</li>
</ol>

<p>Rotate finger sites. Avoid the index finger and thumb if possible - they get used most. Use the side, not the tip pad.</p>

<h2>Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs)</h2>

<p>CGMs (Freestyle Libre, Dexcom) attach a small sensor to your arm or abdomen and read glucose continuously through tiny capillary fluid measurements. They have transformed diabetes care, especially for people on insulin.</p>

<p>In NZ, CGM is funded for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Type 1 diabetes (PHARMAC funded since 2024)</li>
<li>Some type 2 diabetes patients on insulin (specific criteria)</li>
<li>Pregnancy with diabetes</li>
</ul>

<p>Otherwise CGMs can be bought privately. The Libre 2 starter kit is around $100-150, sensors around $90 each lasting 14 days.</p>

<h2>Reading the data</h2>

<p>Look at <strong>patterns</strong>, not single readings:</p>

<ul>
<li>Are fasting numbers consistently high or low?</li>
<li>Are post-meal spikes mostly after one particular meal?</li>
<li>Do you go low at the same time of day?</li>
<li>Are weekends different from weekdays?</li>
</ul>

<p>Bring your readings (book, phone app, downloaded report) to every diabetes review. Patterns guide medicine adjustments.</p>

<h2>What changes the numbers</h2>

<p><strong>Raises blood sugar:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Carbohydrate-heavy meals</li>
<li>Sugary drinks</li>
<li>Stress</li>
<li>Illness or infection</li>
<li>Some medicines (steroids, some psychiatric medicines)</li>
<li>Lack of sleep</li>
<li>Inactivity</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Lowers blood sugar:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Exercise (immediate and lasting effect for hours)</li>
<li>Skipping meals (especially on insulin or sulfonylureas - hypo risk)</li>
<li>Alcohol (can cause delayed lows hours later)</li>
<li>Hot weather (insulin absorbs faster)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Sick day rules</h2>

<p>When unwell:</p>

<ul>
<li>Check more often (4 times a day or more)</li>
<li>Drink plenty of fluids</li>
<li>Do not stop your insulin if on it - illness usually raises blood sugar even when not eating</li>
<li>Test for ketones if type 1 diabetes and sugar is high (over 14)</li>
<li>Have a sick day plan written by your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<p>Same-day:</p>

<ul>
<li>Persistent readings over 20 mmol/L</li>
<li>Persistent readings under 4 mmol/L despite eating</li>
<li>Vomiting and unable to keep fluids or medicines down</li>
<li>Frequent unexpected hypos</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li>Confusion, drowsiness, or unresponsiveness</li>
<li>Ketones present and feeling very unwell (DKA risk)</li>
<li>Severe hypo with seizure or unconsciousness</li>
</ul>

<h2>Driving</h2>

<p>If you take insulin or sulfonylureas, check before driving:</p>

<ul>
<li>Above 5 mmol/L: safe</li>
<li>4-5 mmol/L: have a snack first</li>
<li>Below 4: do not drive. Treat the hypo and wait 45 minutes</li>
</ul>

<p>Carry fast-acting carbs (jelly beans, glucose tablets) in the car.</p>
