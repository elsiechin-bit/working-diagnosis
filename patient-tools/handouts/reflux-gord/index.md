<p>Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD, often called "reflux" or "heartburn") happens when stomach acid flows back up into the oesophagus (the tube that connects your throat to your stomach). This causes a burning feeling in the chest or throat, often after eating or when lying down.</p>

<p>GORD is very common - around one in five New Zealanders experiences reflux symptoms regularly. For most people, it is a nuisance rather than a danger, and responds well to treatment.</p>

<h2>Common symptoms</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Burning sensation in the chest (heartburn), especially after meals or at night</li>
  <li>Sour or bitter taste in the mouth, or acid coming up into the throat</li>
  <li>Belching</li>
  <li>Feeling of food sticking in the chest</li>
  <li>Chronic cough, hoarse voice, or sore throat (from acid reaching the throat)</li>
  <li>Bloating or nausea</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<p>The following tend to worsen reflux - try to identify your personal triggers:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Large meals - especially fatty, rich, or spicy food</li>
  <li>Alcohol</li>
  <li>Coffee and other caffeinated drinks</li>
  <li>Citrus fruit and tomatoes</li>
  <li>Chocolate and mint</li>
  <li>Eating within 2-3 hours of lying down</li>
  <li>Being overweight - extra abdominal pressure pushes acid up</li>
  <li>Tight clothing around the waist</li>
  <li>Smoking</li>
  <li>Stress</li>
</ul>

<h2>Lifestyle changes that help</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Eat smaller, more frequent meals</strong> rather than large meals at once.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not lie down for 2-3 hours after eating.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Elevate the head of your bed</strong> by 15-20 cm (place blocks under the bedhead legs, or use a wedge pillow). Extra pillows alone do not help - they tilt your head but not your whole upper body.</li>
  <li><strong>Lose weight</strong> if you are overweight - even modest loss reduces symptoms significantly.</li>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking</strong> - nicotine relaxes the valve between the stomach and oesophagus.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce alcohol.</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Antacids</strong> (such as Gaviscon, Mylanta) work quickly for mild or occasional symptoms. They neutralise acid but do not treat the underlying cause. Available from your pharmacy without a prescription.</li>
  <li><strong>Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)</strong> - such as omeprazole, esomeprazole, or pantoprazole - are the most effective medicine for GORD. They reduce the amount of acid the stomach makes. They work best taken 30-60 minutes before the first meal of the day. Your GP will advise on the dose and how long to take them.</li>
  <li><strong>H2 blockers</strong> (such as famotidine) are less potent than PPIs but can help for milder symptoms or as a second option.</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not use antacids containing aspirin (such as Alka-Seltzer) long term, as the aspirin can irritate the stomach lining.</p>

<h2>How long will I need treatment?</h2>

<p>For many people, PPIs are used for 4-8 weeks to allow the oesophagus to heal, then stopped or used only on-demand when symptoms return. Some people with more significant GORD need long-term treatment. Your GP will guide you on what is right for your situation.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP urgently</h2>

<p>Most reflux is straightforward, but some symptoms need prompt assessment. Contact your GP if you have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Difficulty or pain swallowing</li>
  <li>Unintentional weight loss</li>
  <li>Vomiting blood or black vomit</li>
  <li>Black or tarry bowel motions</li>
  <li>Reflux symptoms that are new and severe, particularly if you are over 55</li>
  <li>Symptoms that are not improving after 4-6 weeks of treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>These symptoms need investigation (usually a gastroscopy - a camera examination of the oesophagus and stomach) to rule out more serious conditions including Barrett's oesophagus or cancer.</p>
