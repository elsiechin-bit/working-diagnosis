<p>Around 1 in 7 five-year-olds and 1 in 20 ten-year-olds wet the bed.[1] It is one of the most common, most distressing, and most misunderstood childhood conditions. It is not the child's fault. It is not laziness. It is not bad parenting. Most cases sort themselves out with time, but for the child the wait can be hard, and many things genuinely help. This handout is the practical version - what is going on, what to try, and the things to stop doing.</p>

<h2>What is going on</h2>

<p>Bedwetting (nocturnal enuresis) is when a child who would otherwise be expected to be dry wets at night. There are usually one or more of three things at play:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The bladder is small or overactive</strong> - it cannot hold a full night's urine, or it contracts when it should be relaxed</li>
  <li><strong>The brain does not wake to the signal</strong> - many bedwetting children are very deep sleepers</li>
  <li><strong>Too much urine made overnight</strong> - some children do not make enough of the antidiuretic hormone vasopressin during sleep</li>
</ul>

<p>It often runs in families. If one parent wet the bed as a child, around 40% of children will. If both parents did, around 70%.[2]</p>

<p>Bedwetting is described as "primary" if the child has never been reliably dry at night for more than 6 months, "secondary" if they were dry and have started wetting again. Secondary enuresis is more often associated with stress, infection, constipation, or medical issues, and benefits from earlier assessment.</p>

<h2>What is normal</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Most children are reliably dry at night by age 5</li>
  <li>Around 1 in 7 five-year-olds still wet</li>
  <li>Around 1 in 20 ten-year-olds still wet</li>
  <li>Spontaneous resolution rate is around 15% per year</li>
  <li>Boys outnumber girls 2:1</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things to start doing</h2>

<h3>Treat constipation if it is there</h3>
<p>Constipation is the single most overlooked contributor. A loaded rectum presses on the bladder. Up to a third of bedwetting children are constipated, sometimes without obvious symptoms. See the constipation handout. Treating it often resolves bedwetting without any other intervention.</p>

<h3>Adequate daytime fluids</h3>
<p>Children who under-drink in the day pee more concentrated urine and the bladder does not learn to hold normal volumes. Aim for 6-8 drinks across the day, more in summer or with sport. Most should be water. Less in the evening, more earlier.</p>

<h3>Reduce evening fluids - but not too much</h3>
<p>Stop fluids 1-2 hours before bed. Avoid bedtime drinks of caffeine, fizzy drinks, milk in large volumes, and very salty snacks (which trigger thirst).</p>

<h3>Empty the bladder before bed</h3>
<p>Twice if possible - once during the bedtime routine, once just before lights out.</p>

<h3>Comfortable sleeping environment</h3>
<p>Easy access to the toilet. A nightlight in the hallway. Steps if the toilet is high. Pyjamas that come off easily. Older children might prefer a potty in the bedroom.</p>

<h3>Protect the bed</h3>
<p>Waterproof mattress protector. Layered: fitted sheet over mattress protector, then towel, then another fitted sheet. When wet, peel off the top two layers and the second dry layer is ready.</p>

<h2>Things to stop doing</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Don't be cross.</strong> The child cannot help it. They are usually as upset about it as you are.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't punish, shame, or compare to siblings.</strong> Beyond ineffective, it makes things worse.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't restrict daytime fluids hard.</strong> The bladder needs adequate filling to learn its capacity.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't lift the child to the toilet asleep.</strong> Old advice. Does not improve learning the wake-up signal and just disturbs everyone's sleep.</li>
  <li><strong>Don't put a 5-year-old back into nappies for shame reasons.</strong> But for an older child who finds it less distressing to use overnight pull-ups (Goodnites, Drynites) while waiting things out, that is fine.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<p>Reasonable to see your GP about bedwetting once your child is over 6, especially if it is bothering them. See sooner if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Secondary enuresis - dry then wet again</li>
  <li>Daytime wetting too</li>
  <li>Symptoms of urinary tract infection (burning, frequency, pain)</li>
  <li>Symptoms of constipation</li>
  <li>Difficult or painful weeing, weak stream</li>
  <li>Weight loss, increased thirst (rule out diabetes)</li>
  <li>Bedwetting paired with snoring or pauses in breathing during sleep (consider obstructive sleep apnoea, which can cause bedwetting)</li>
  <li>Significant distress for the child or family</li>
</ul>

<h2>What your GP might suggest</h2>

<h3>Enuresis alarm</h3>
<p>The most effective long-term treatment, with around 65-75% success rate.[3] A small alarm with a sensor in the underwear that sounds at the first drop of urine, training the brain to wake to bladder signals.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Used every night for at least 6-8 weeks, often 3 months</li>
  <li>Initially: parents need to wake too and help</li>
  <li>Gradually: the child wakes themselves, then begins to wake before the alarm</li>
  <li>Cure rate is high but it requires commitment</li>
  <li>Available in NZ from pharmacies, around NZ$120-180 to buy; some can be hired</li>
</ul>

<h3>Desmopressin (Minirin)</h3>
<p>A tablet or melt taken at bedtime that reduces overnight urine production. Works on the night taken; does not retrain.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Useful for sleepovers, school camps, holidays</li>
  <li>Can be used long-term; cure rate around 30% if used continuously over months</li>
  <li>Funded in NZ on prescription</li>
  <li>Important: do not drink large fluids 1 hour before to 8 hours after - rare risk of low sodium</li>
</ul>

<h3>Combined approach</h3>
<p>Sometimes alarm + desmopressin together for severe cases. Discuss with your GP.</p>

<h3>Specialist referral</h3>
<p>Considered if treatment has not worked after 3-6 months, or if there are concerning features. Paediatrics, urology, or continence services depending on local pathways.</p>

<h2>Sleepovers, camps, and travel</h2>

<p>One of the most distressing aspects for older children. Some workarounds:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pre-bed desmopressin works for most children for a single night</li>
  <li>Talk to the host family or camp leader in advance - most are kind and discreet</li>
  <li>Use overnight pull-ups discreetly (Drynites, Goodnites)</li>
  <li>Pack spare bedding, plastic bags for wet items</li>
</ul>

<h2>The emotional bit</h2>

<p>Bedwetting affects self-esteem, sleepovers, school camps, sibling relationships. The pattern that helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Tell your child it is common, not their fault, and not forever</li>
  <li>Involve them in the management - they choose pull-ups vs alarm vs wait</li>
  <li>Reward effort (drinking through the day, taking the medication, getting up for the alarm), not dryness</li>
  <li>If it is a battle, get help. The bedwetting is rarely the actual problem - the family stress around it is.</li>
</ul>
