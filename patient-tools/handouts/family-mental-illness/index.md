<p>Mental illness is a family experience. The person with the diagnosis carries the central weight, but everyone in the home is affected - partners, children, siblings, parents, sometimes extended whanau. This handout is for the people around the diagnosis: what tends to happen, what helps over time, the patterns that protect family wellbeing, and the NZ services specifically for families. The aim is not to "fix" your family member, which is not your job. It is to help your family live well alongside the illness.</p>

<h2>What family members commonly experience</h2>

<h3>The early period</h3>
<p>Often confusion, fear, denial. "Maybe it is just stress." "Maybe it will pass." Then a crisis, often, that forces recognition. Hospital admissions, suicide attempts, manic episodes, eating disorder collapse, alcohol consequences. The diagnosis comes, sometimes years late.</p>

<h3>The grieving</h3>
<p>The loss of who you thought your loved one was, or who you thought they would become. The loss of family rhythms, of feeling safe at home, of being able to predict tomorrow. The loss of your own life, which has had to bend around theirs. This grief is rarely named but is often the heaviest part of the experience.</p>

<h3>The stretching</h3>
<p>Family members often take on roles they were not prepared for: medication monitor, crisis manager, financial protector, advocate with services. Carers report:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sleep loss - lying awake worrying or responding to nighttime crises</li>
  <li>Hypervigilance - constantly scanning for warning signs</li>
  <li>Emotional flooding - smaller things provoking bigger reactions because the reservoir is full</li>
  <li>Withdrawal from friends - hard to explain, easier to avoid</li>
  <li>Reduced own self-care - "I cannot afford to fall apart"</li>
  <li>Resentment, then guilt about the resentment</li>
  <li>Anxiety, depression, physical illness - rates 2-3 times higher than the general population in long-term carers</li>
</ul>

<h3>The longer term</h3>
<p>Most families find a new equilibrium, though it takes time. The illness becomes part of the family landscape rather than the centre of every day. The family member with the illness recovers to varying degrees. Family relationships shift, sometimes deepen, sometimes strain.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Get good information</h3>
<p>Understanding the illness reduces fear and guides response. The condition's name matters less than the specific features: what triggers episodes, what warning signs look like, what helps in a crisis, what the prognosis tends to be. Your GP, the treating psychiatrist, and condition-specific organisations (Balance NZ for bipolar, Supporting Families NZ for general, EDANZ for eating disorders) are good sources.</p>

<h3>Find your people</h3>
<p>Other family members of people with mental illness understand things others do not. Peer support is one of the most consistently helpful interventions. NZ options:</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.supportingfamilies.org.nz/">Supporting Families NZ</a> - regional groups, education programmes</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.balance.org.nz/">Balance NZ</a> - bipolar family support</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.ed.org.nz/">EDANZ</a> - eating disorder family support</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.al-anon.org.nz/">Al-Anon</a> for families affected by alcohol</li>
  <li>Online and in-person groups for specific conditions</li>
</ul>

<h3>Look after yourself, deliberately</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Sleep, food, exercise, friendships, time alone, your own GP - all of these matter more, not less, while supporting someone</li>
  <li>Build small predictable things into your week that are non-negotiable - exercise, a meal with a friend, an evening to yourself</li>
  <li>If you are struggling, see your own GP. Carer mental health is a recognised concern and not less worthy than the person you are supporting.</li>
  <li>If finances are stretched, free options exist - 1737, Supporting Families peer groups, EAP through some employers</li>
</ul>

<h3>Set limits</h3>

<p>This is the hardest part. Limits are not abandonment. They are how you stay capable of being there over the long haul. Examples:</p>

<ul>
  <li>"I cannot talk about this after 10pm. We can pick it up in the morning."</li>
  <li>"I am not going to lend you money for this."</li>
  <li>"I am happy to drive you to the GP. I cannot represent you in the appointment."</li>
  <li>"I love you and I will be here. I am also seeing my own therapist for my own reasons."</li>
</ul>

<p>Carers who never set limits burn out and become unable to support anyone. Carers who set thoughtful limits often last longer and stay more present.</p>

<h3>Encourage professional help, do not be it</h3>

<p>You are family, not the treatment team. The boundary helps both of you. Useful roles:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Coming to GP and psychiatry appointments if invited</li>
  <li>Knowing the medication and the warning signs of relapse</li>
  <li>Being the person who notices when things start changing</li>
  <li>Coordinating with services in a crisis</li>
  <li>Providing the predictable home environment that supports recovery</li>
</ul>

<p>Less useful roles:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Being the only therapist</li>
  <li>Trying to argue them out of beliefs that are part of their illness</li>
  <li>Constantly checking in - hypervigilance is exhausting and often counterproductive</li>
  <li>Taking on responsibility that is not yours</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specific scenarios</h2>

<h3>Children of a parent with mental illness</h3>

<p>Children are often the silent affected family members. They watch closely, understand more than adults expect, and often blame themselves. What helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Age-appropriate explanation. "Mum has an illness called depression. It makes her very sad sometimes. It is not your fault. She still loves you and she is getting help."</li>
  <li>Reassurance about what stays the same - school, meals, who picks them up, that someone is in charge</li>
  <li>Permission to ask questions and feel their feelings</li>
  <li>A trusted adult outside the immediate family - aunty, grandparent, neighbour, teacher, sports coach</li>
  <li>School informed to a degree that supports the child without exposing the family</li>
  <li>Children's mental health support if they are showing signs of struggling - GP referral, school counsellor, Skylight (for grief), KidsLine</li>
</ul>

<h3>Partner / spouse</h3>

<p>The relationship will be tested. The partner role often expands to include carer, advocate, financial manager, parent of mutual children. Couple therapy when things are stable can help; specialist services for partners exist.</p>

<h3>Adult siblings</h3>

<p>Often the second-most-affected. May find themselves expected to be the "responsible" one, the support, the future caregiver after parents. Their own mental health worth attending to deliberately.</p>

<h3>Older parents of an adult child with mental illness</h3>

<p>Often carry the weight for decades. NZ has specific peer support for this group. Worth noting: the question of who supports the person with mental illness when the parents are no longer able to is real and worth discussing earlier rather than later.</p>

<h2>In a crisis</h2>

<p>Things to know in advance:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The contact details for their treating team and how to reach them after-hours</li>
  <li>Local crisis numbers - DHB Mental Health Crisis Team, 1737, Lifeline</li>
  <li>That 111 is appropriate when there is immediate risk</li>
  <li>That police may be involved in mental health emergencies; this is sometimes necessary, often non-traumatic when handled well, but worth preparing for</li>
  <li>Whether your family member has an Advance Directive or Wellness Plan that records their preferences</li>
  <li>Where their medication is, what they are allergic to, any history that emergency staff need</li>
</ul>

<p>Many families benefit from a written crisis plan agreed when things are stable. It includes who to call, what helps, what makes things worse, and the family member's own preferences.</p>

<h2>The long game</h2>

<p>Some patterns that distinguish families who do reasonably well over years:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The illness is named and treated as one part of family life, not the centre</li>
  <li>The person with the diagnosis is treated as a whole person, not as their illness</li>
  <li>The other family members maintain their own lives - friends, hobbies, work, identity</li>
  <li>Conflict is allowed - protecting the person with mental illness from all difficult conversation is its own problem</li>
  <li>Grief, frustration, and exhaustion are talked about</li>
  <li>Relapses are expected and not catastrophised - the illness is not a moral failure when it returns</li>
  <li>Recovery is supported when it happens, slowly and with patience</li>
</ul>
