<p>Shoulder pain is very common and usually comes from problems with the rotator cuff (the muscles and tendons that stabilise your shoulder) or from impingement (pinching) of tendons. The good news is that most shoulder pain improves significantly with conservative treatment, rest, and exercise. This handout covers what helps.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain at the front, side, or back of the shoulder</li>
  <li>Weakness when lifting your arm, especially sideways or overhead</li>
  <li>Pain that worsens when reaching, throwing, or lifting</li>
  <li>Difficulty sleeping on the affected side</li>
  <li>Clicking or popping sensation in the shoulder</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Rotator cuff problems usually develop from repetitive overhead activities, poor posture, or weakness in the shoulder muscles. When the rotator cuff is irritated or weak, it can become impinged (pinched) as you move your arm. This triggers inflammation and pain. Most cases are caused by overuse rather than a single injury and are very treatable.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest the painful activity.</strong> Avoid overhead movements, heavy lifting, and activities that trigger pain. This gives inflammation time to settle - usually 2 to 4 weeks.</li>
  <li><strong>Use ice and pain relief.</strong> Ice packs for 15 minutes, several times daily in the first few days help reduce inflammation. Paracetamol or ibuprofen taken regularly helps manage pain so you can gently move your shoulder.</li>
  <li><strong>Do gentle range-of-motion exercises.</strong> Even while resting the painful movement, doing gentle pendulum swings and passive range exercises (letting gravity do the work) prevents stiffness. Ask your GP for guidance or ask about physiotherapy.</li>
  <li><strong>Strengthen your rotator cuff.</strong> Once acute pain settles, specific exercises (like internal and external rotation exercises with resistance) rebuild strength and prevent recurrence. Physiotherapy is very effective for this.</li>
  <li><strong>Fix your posture.</strong> Poor posture (slouching, forward shoulders) contributes to impingement. Keep your shoulders relaxed and back, and avoid hunching over screens.</li>
  <li><strong>Return to activity gradually.</strong> Once pain is improving, slowly reintroduce the activities that bothered you, but with better technique or reduced load.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief (paracetamol or ibuprofen) helps in the early stages. Some people benefit from a short course of an anti-inflammatory such as ibuprofen. If pain persists, your GP may consider a steroid injection into the shoulder to reduce inflammation, though physiotherapy is equally or more effective for long-term recovery.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is severe or sudden</li>
  <li>You cannot move your shoulder at all</li>
  <li>Symptoms are not improving after 4 - 6 weeks despite self-care</li>
  <li>You have significant weakness in your arm</li>
  <li>Pain is preventing you from working or doing activities you need to do</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most rotator cuff problems resolve completely with conservative treatment and exercises. Recurrence is common if you return to the triggering activity without addressing muscle weakness, so maintaining rotator cuff strength long-term helps prevent problems coming back.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Would physiotherapy help? Can you refer me?</li>
  <li>Is imaging (ultrasound or MRI) needed?</li>
  <li>What activities should I avoid or modify?</li>
  <li>How do I know if I need surgery?</li>
</ul>
