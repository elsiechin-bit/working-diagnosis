Pneumonia is an infection of the lung tissue itself, usually caused by bacteria, sometimes by viruses or fungi. Most adults with pneumonia can be treated at home with antibiotics and recover within 1-3 weeks. Some need hospital. Knowing what is normal in recovery and what is a warning sign helps you take action at the right time.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Cough, often productive of yellow, green, brown, or blood-tinged sputum</li>
<li>Fever, often high, with chills and shaking</li>
<li>Sharp chest pain, worse on deep breath or coughing</li>
<li>Breathlessness, even at rest in severe cases</li>
<li>Fast heart rate</li>
<li>Tiredness and feeling very unwell</li>
<li>Loss of appetite</li>
<li>In older adults: confusion or falls may be the first sign</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Your GP will examine you (listening to the chest, checking pulse, breathing rate, oxygen level) and may order:</p>

<ul>
<li>Chest X-ray</li>
<li>Blood tests (including CRP)</li>
<li>Sputum culture in some cases</li>
<li>Pulse oximetry to check oxygen</li>
</ul>

<p>The CRB-65 score (confusion, respiratory rate, blood pressure, age 65+) helps decide whether home treatment is safe.</p>

<h2>Treatment at home</h2>

<p><strong>Antibiotics.</strong> Usually amoxicillin for 5-7 days, with doxycycline as an alternative or addition for atypical pneumonia. Take the full course even if you feel better.</p>

<p><strong>Rest.</strong> Bed rest is fine for the first few days. Avoid strenuous activity for 1-2 weeks.</p>

<p><strong>Fluids.</strong> Drink plenty of water, soup, juice. Aim for pale urine.</p>

<p><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for fever and chest pain.</p>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Even temporary cessation helps recovery.</p>

<p><strong>Cough.</strong> Expect a cough lasting weeks. Most cough mixtures do not help much. Honey can soothe.</p>

<h2>What recovery looks like</h2>

<ul>
<li>1 week: fever should settle. You should be feeling better, though still tired</li>
<li>2-4 weeks: most chest pain has gone. Cough is improving but not gone</li>
<li>4-6 weeks: cough has settled in most people. Energy is returning but not yet full</li>
<li>3-6 months: full recovery. Some people, especially older or with chronic disease, take longer</li>
</ul>

<p>Many people underestimate how long full recovery takes. Be patient with yourself.</p>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<p>See your GP urgently if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Symptoms not improving by 48-72 hours of antibiotics</li>
<li>Fever returning after settling</li>
<li>Increasing breathlessness</li>
<li>Chest pain getting worse</li>
<li>Coughing up large amounts of blood</li>
<li>Persistent cough beyond 6-8 weeks - chest X-ray usually repeated to rule out underlying cause</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe breathlessness or unable to talk in sentences</li>
<li>Lips, fingers blue</li>
<li>Confusion or drowsiness</li>
<li>Severe chest pain</li>
<li>Collapsing or fainting</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why hospital is sometimes needed</h2>

<p>Hospital admission is recommended for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Older adults (often over 65) with significant illness</li>
<li>Low oxygen saturation (under 92% on room air)</li>
<li>Severe pneumonia or sepsis</li>
<li>Inability to keep antibiotics down</li>
<li>People with serious underlying disease (immunosuppression, severe COPD, heart failure)</li>
<li>Those living alone unable to care for themselves</li>
</ul>

<p>Hospital treatment includes oxygen, intravenous antibiotics, and sometimes intensive care.</p>

<h2>After pneumonia</h2>

<p>Follow-up matters:</p>

<ul>
<li>GP review at 2 weeks - check progress, repeat exam</li>
<li>Repeat chest X-ray at 6 weeks if smoker, over 50, or symptoms persist - to ensure complete clearance and rule out an underlying cause (rarely, lung cancer presents as a non-resolving pneumonia)</li>
<li>Pulmonary rehabilitation if recovery is slow, especially in older adults or those with COPD</li>
</ul>

<h2>Prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Annual flu vaccine</strong> - reduces pneumonia risk</li>
<li><strong>Pneumococcal vaccine</strong> - free in NZ for those 65+ and eligible chronic conditions</li>
<li><strong>COVID vaccine</strong> - reduces severe respiratory illness</li>
<li><strong>Stop smoking</strong> - smokers have around 4 times the pneumonia risk</li>
<li><strong>Treat dental disease</strong> - poor oral hygiene increases aspiration pneumonia risk</li>
<li><strong>Manage chronic diseases</strong> - diabetes, COPD, heart failure all increase risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Children and older people</h2>

<p>In children, pneumonia is common and most cases recover well with antibiotics and rest. Watch for fast breathing, indrawing of the ribs, and reduced feeding.</p>

<p>In older adults, the warning signs can be subtle - new confusion, falls, reduced appetite, off-food. A low threshold for medical review is wise.</p>

<h2>Aspiration pneumonia</h2>

<p>People with swallowing problems (after stroke, advanced dementia, motor neurone disease, severe reflux) are at risk of aspirating food, drink, or saliva into the lungs. Speech therapy, modified textures, and good oral care reduce risk.</p>
