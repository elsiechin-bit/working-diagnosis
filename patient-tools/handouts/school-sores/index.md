<p>School sores - <em>impetigo</em>, sometimes called <em>paipai</em> in te reo Maori - are one of the most common skin infections in tamariki in Aotearoa. They happen when bacteria get into broken skin (a scratch, an insect bite, an eczema patch), and they spread easily between children where bedrooms or towels are shared. With simple treatment, most sores heal in about a week. They are not a sign of poor parenting - they happen because bacteria are everywhere and tamariki get knocks, bites, and rashes.</p>

<h2>What they look like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A small red spot or blister, often around the nose, mouth, hands, or legs</li>
  <li>Quickly forms a honey-coloured crust</li>
  <li>Often itchy, sometimes a little tender, but usually not painful</li>
  <li>New sores can appear nearby as the child scratches and spreads the bacteria</li>
  <li>With treatment, most sores improve in 3 to 5 days and heal within 7 to 10 days, usually without scarring</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why we take school sores seriously in NZ</h2>

<p>Childhood skin infection rates in Aotearoa are among the highest in the developed world, and they affect tamariki Maori and Pacific tamariki disproportionately - driven by housing and access to care, not by anything inherent<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>. Two of the bacteria involved - <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> and <em>Streptococcus pyogenes</em> - matter beyond the skin: strep skin infections are part of the rheumatic fever picture in NZ, and treating them well helps prevent rare but serious complications, including rheumatic fever and post-strep kidney inflammation. This is why we treat impetigo a little more actively here than in many other countries.</p>

<h2>What helps at home</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Soak the crusts off gently</strong> with a warm, wet cloth, then pat dry.</li>
  <li><strong>Cover the sores</strong> with a plaster or dressing where you can - this stops the bacteria spreading and reduces scratching.</li>
  <li><strong>Wash hands often</strong>, for everyone in the whare, especially after touching the sores or changing dressings.</li>
  <li><strong>Use a separate towel and flannel</strong> for the affected child, and wash sheets, towels, and clothes in hot water until the sores have healed.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep fingernails short</strong> to reduce scratching and reinfection.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat eczema underneath.</strong> Well-moisturised skin is much harder for bacteria to break into, and eczema is the most common entry point.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What your GP can give</h2>

<p>A few small sores may only need an antiseptic wash and good wound care. For more widespread sores, or if there are concerns about strep, your GP will usually prescribe oral antibiotics - most commonly <strong>flucloxacillin for 5 days</strong><sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>. If your tamaiti is allergic to penicillin, erythromycin or co-trimoxazole are alternatives. Topical fusidic acid is no longer a first-choice option in NZ because resistance has risen. <strong>Finish the full course</strong>, even when the sores look better - stopping early lets the infection come back and may slightly increase the risk of complications. GP visits are free for tamariki under 14 at most clinics enrolled with a PHO, and most antibiotics are fully funded by Pharmac.</p>

<h2>Going back to kura</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay home until sores are dry and crusted over, or fully covered with a dressing</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Once antibiotics have been taken for 24 hours</strong> and the sores are covered, most tamariki can return to kura, kohanga reo, or daycare<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Let the kura or daycare know</strong> so other tamariki can be checked</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid kapa haka, sports, and swimming pools</strong> until the sores are fully healed</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to come back to your GP</h2>

<p>Contact your clinic, after-hours service, or call Healthline (<strong>0800 611 116</strong>, free, 24/7) if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sores are spreading or new ones keep appearing despite treatment</li>
  <li>Your tamaiti has a fever, is off food, or seems generally unwell</li>
  <li>The skin around a sore is hot, swollen, or has red streaks spreading from it</li>
  <li>Sores are very painful or near the eyes</li>
  <li>A pepi under 3 months is affected</li>
  <li>The wee turns dark, smoky, or tea-coloured in the weeks after - this can be an early sign of post-strep kidney inflammation</li>
  <li>Sore joints, chest pain, or shortness of breath in the weeks after - this can be an early sign of rheumatic fever</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> if your tamaiti is very drowsy, breathing fast, has a stiff neck, or has a spreading purple rash that does not fade when you press a glass against it.</p>

<h2>If your home is cold, damp, or crowded</h2>

<p>If skin infections keep coming back in your whanau, it is worth talking to us about your housing. NZ's Healthy Homes Initiative can connect families with practical support around insulation, heating, and reducing crowding. Skin infections are part of a bigger picture, and warm dry housing makes a real difference.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Should we swab the sores, or anyone else in the whanau, to check what bacteria we are dealing with?</li>
  <li>Is anyone else in the home likely to need treatment?</li>
  <li>Could this be linked to my tamaiti's eczema, and should we review their skin care plan?</li>
  <li>If the sores keep coming back, is a referral to a paediatrician or dermatologist worth considering?</li>
</ul>
