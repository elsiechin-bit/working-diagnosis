<p>Children's eyes are still developing well into the school years. Most vision problems in childhood are easily treatable when caught early and much harder to fix later. The good news is that New Zealand has a free national vision screen at age 4 (the B4 School Check), free or subsidised eye care for children in many regions, and a clearer evidence base than ever about what helps. Two messages stand out: outdoor time genuinely protects against short-sightedness, and most "screens are ruining children's eyes" headlines do not survive a careful look at the data - though screens still matter, just for slightly different reasons than people assume.</p>

<h2>How vision develops</h2>

<p>Babies are born with poor vision and gradually develop sharp focus, depth perception, and the coordination between the two eyes over the first few years of life. The visual brain is still wiring itself up until roughly age 7 or 8. If one eye is significantly out of focus, turned, or covered during this critical window, the brain learns to ignore it and the eye becomes lazy (amblyopia). Treating amblyopia is straightforward in early childhood and much harder once the visual system has matured, which is why early detection matters so much.</p>

<h2>Common conditions in childhood</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Refractive error.</strong> Short-sightedness (myopia), long-sightedness (hyperopia), and astigmatism. Glasses correct these. Mild long-sightedness is normal in young children and often improves on its own.</li>
  <li><strong>Amblyopia (lazy eye).</strong> One eye does not develop normal vision because of a difference in prescription, a turn, or a blockage. Treated with glasses, patching, or atropine drops.</li>
  <li><strong>Strabismus (eye turn or squint).</strong> One eye points in a different direction. Some are present from birth, others develop. Always worth assessing.</li>
  <li><strong>Colour vision deficiency.</strong> Affects roughly 1 in 12 boys and 1 in 200 girls.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Not treatable but worth knowing about for school and later career planning.</li>
  <li><strong>Conjunctivitis, blocked tear ducts, stye, chalazion.</strong> Common, usually self-limiting, occasionally need treatment.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The B4 School Check</h2>

<p>Every NZ child is offered a free vision and hearing screen at around age 4 through the B4 School Check, run by Te Whatu Ora and partner providers.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> If concerns are picked up, the child is referred for a full eye assessment with an optometrist, free of charge. If your child has missed this check, ask your GP, Plunket nurse, or school how to catch up. Many regions also offer free or subsidised eye examinations for children under 16 enrolled in school dental services or with community service cards.</p>

<h2>Signs to watch for at home</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sitting very close to the TV, or holding books and devices unusually close to the face</li>
  <li>Squinting, screwing up the eyes, or covering one eye to see</li>
  <li>Tilting the head consistently to one side when looking at things</li>
  <li>Frequent eye rubbing, especially with reading or screen use</li>
  <li>Headaches after school or homework</li>
  <li>An eye that turns in or out, even occasionally</li>
  <li>One eye that looks different from the other in photographs (a different reflection in the pupil)</li>
  <li>Difficulty catching balls, frequent clumsiness, or trouble copying from the board at school</li>
  <li>Avoiding tasks that need sustained near vision (reading, drawing) when peers are happy with them</li>
</ul>

<p>Any of these is a reason for an optometrist visit. You do not need a GP referral.</p>

<h2>Myopia, outdoor time, and screens</h2>

<p>Short-sightedness has been increasing in children worldwide over recent decades, with prevalence in school-age children doubling or more in many countries.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> The strongest reproducible environmental factor is the amount of time children spend outdoors. Roughly two hours of outdoor time a day, every day, reduces both the chance of becoming short-sighted and the rate at which existing myopia progresses.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup> The mechanism is not entirely settled, but bright outdoor light, the wider range of focal distances outside, and possibly less prolonged near work all seem to contribute.</p>

<p>Screens are part of this picture mainly because time on screens displaces time outdoors and tends to be done up close. The evidence that screens themselves directly damage eyes - rather than acting through reduced outdoor time and digital eye strain - is much weaker than the headlines suggest.</p>

<p>Practical translation:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Two hours of outdoor time a day.</strong> The single most evidence-based thing you can do for your child's eyes.</li>
  <li><strong>20-20-20 rule for older children doing prolonged near work.</strong> Every 20 minutes of close work, look at something 20 feet (6 metres) away for 20 seconds. Reduces digital eye strain - the gritty, tired-eye feeling - though does not change myopia risk.</li>
  <li><strong>Watch posture and lighting, not minutes on a screen.</strong> Hunched over a tablet on a dark sofa is harder on a young visual system than the same screen held at a sensible distance with the room well lit.</li>
  <li><strong>If your child is becoming short-sighted, ask the optometrist about myopia control.</strong> Specific contact lenses (orthokeratology, soft multifocal) and atropine eye drops can slow progression and are increasingly available in NZ. They do not prevent myopia from starting, but they can substantially reduce how short-sighted a child becomes.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn5">5</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your optometrist or doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Any of the warning signs above</li>
  <li>A family history of strong glasses prescriptions, lazy eye, or eye turn</li>
  <li>If your child has missed the B4 School Check</li>
  <li>If your child has trouble at school that might be vision-related</li>
  <li>An eye injury, sudden eye redness with pain, or sudden visual change - these need same-day assessment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask the optometrist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Does my child need glasses, and if so, full-time or just for some activities?</li>
  <li>Is there any sign of a lazy eye or eye turn?</li>
  <li>If my child is short-sighted, are we candidates for myopia control?</li>
  <li>How often should we come back?</li>
</ul>
