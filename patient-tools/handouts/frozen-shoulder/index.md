<p>Frozen shoulder is a condition where the tissue around your shoulder joint becomes inflamed and tight, severely limiting movement. It typically progresses through three stages and usually resolves on its own over 1 to 3 years, though physiotherapy and exercise significantly speed recovery. This handout explains what to expect and what helps.</p>

<h2>The three stages</h2>

<p><strong>Freezing stage (weeks 1 - 9):</strong> Gradual onset of pain in the shoulder, especially with certain movements. Pain is often worse at night and can be severe. Movement becomes increasingly restricted.</p>

<p><strong>Frozen stage (months 4 - 12):</strong> Pain may actually decrease, but stiffness becomes the main problem. Your shoulder feels stuck - you cannot reach overhead, behind your back, or across your chest.</p>

<p><strong>Thawing stage (months 12 - 36):</strong> Movement slowly returns. This can take months but usually continues to improve until you regain most or all normal movement.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Keep moving within pain limits.</strong> Even though your shoulder is stiff, gentle movement actually prevents the tissues from becoming more stuck. Pendulum swings, passive stretching (using your other arm to assist), and sleeping positions that allow gentle movement all help.</li>
  <li><strong>Physiotherapy is very effective.</strong> A physiotherapist can provide specific exercises and manual therapy that significantly speed recovery compared to no treatment. Regular physiotherapy in the early stages can shorten the condition substantially.</li>
  <li><strong>Pain relief helps you exercise.</strong> Regular paracetamol or ibuprofen taken as prescribed allows you to tolerate movement and exercise, which is the treatment that works.</li>
  <li><strong>Heat before exercise.</strong> Apply heat to your shoulder for 10 - 15 minutes before doing your exercises - it helps tissues relax and allows greater movement.</li>
  <li><strong>Gentle stretching daily.</strong> Doing your stretches several times daily, even in small bouts, is more effective than occasional intense sessions. Be gentle - pushing too hard into pain can trigger muscle guarding that makes stiffness worse.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep positioning matters.</strong> Sleep on your back or on the opposite side. Lying on the frozen shoulder can limit movement overnight and increase stiffness.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Anti-inflammatory medicines like ibuprofen can help in the painful freezing stage by reducing inflammation. Paracetamol helps manage pain. Some GPs consider short-term oral corticosteroids in the early freezing stage to reduce inflammation, though evidence is modest. Your GP will advise if appropriate. Steroid injections into the shoulder joint are sometimes used; they may help pain and allow better exercise.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You develop sudden severe shoulder pain</li>
  <li>Pain is accompanied by fever or you feel unwell</li>
  <li>You develop significant weakness or numbness in your arm</li>
  <li>Pain or stiffness is not improving after several months despite physiotherapy</li>
</ul>

<h2>Surgery</h2>

<p>Surgery (manipulation under anaesthetic or arthroscopic release) is rarely needed, as most frozen shoulders resolve with time and exercise. It may be considered if you have severe stiffness that hasn't improved after 12 months despite aggressive physiotherapy.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Can you refer me to physiotherapy?</li>
  <li>What exercises should I do daily?</li>
  <li>When should I be concerned that this is not improving?</li>
  <li>Do I need imaging or other investigations?</li>
</ul>
