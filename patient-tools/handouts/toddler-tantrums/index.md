<p>Tantrums are a normal and universal part of toddler development. They reflect your child's frustration with their limitations and developing emotions - not bad behaviour or poor parenting. Understanding why tantrums happen and using calm, consistent strategies helps everyone manage them better.</p>

<h2>Why tantrums happen</h2>

<p>Toddlers have big emotions but limited language and self-control to express or manage them. When frustrated, tired, hungry, or overwhelmed, they cannot reason their way out - they erupt. Tantrums peak between 18 months and 4 years as children develop language and independence. They are a sign of development, not failure.</p>

<h2>What tantrums look like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Crying, screaming, or yelling</li>
  <li>Throwing yourself on the ground</li>
  <li>Kicking, hitting, or flailing limbs</li>
  <li>Holding breath or gagging (rarely causes harm)</li>
  <li>Rigid body or thrashing</li>
  <li>Regression in toileting or behaviour</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay calm.</strong> Your calmness is contagious. Take a breath, speak quietly, and model the emotional control you want your child to develop. Yelling or losing your temper escalates tantrums.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep your child safe.</strong> Move them away from hazards if needed, but avoid physical punishment or restraint unless they are a danger to themselves or others.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not give in to unreasonable demands.</strong> If you said no to sweets, stay consistent even during a tantrum. Giving in teaches tantrums work.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid reasoning or explaining during the tantrum.</strong> Your child cannot hear you during emotional flooding. Wait until they are calm to discuss what happened.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not shame your child.</strong> Avoid saying "stop crying" or "you're being bad." Instead: "I see you're upset. I'm here."</li>
  <li><strong>Offer comfort when they are ready.</strong> Some children want comfort during a tantrum; others need space. Follow your child's lead.</li>
  <li><strong>Prevent when possible.</strong> Ensure adequate sleep, meals, and notice before transitions. Avoid setting up situations where you must say no to everything.</li>
</ul>

<h2>After the tantrum</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Reconnect with your child.</strong> Once calm, reconnect with a hug or kind words. Tantrums are exhausting for everyone.</li>
  <li><strong>Teach the skill they were missing.</strong> If your child had a tantrum because they could not ask for help, teach them words or signs: "Next time, say 'help please.'"</li>
  <li><strong>Move on.</strong> Do not dwell on the tantrum or punish retrospectively. Your child has learned the lesson they are capable of learning.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Managing your own emotions</h2>

<p>Tantrums can be triggering for parents. Feeling angry, embarrassed, or helpless is normal. Take breaks when needed - step outside, call a friend, take deep breaths. Your wellbeing matters. If you find yourself unable to stay calm, step away briefly if safe to do so.</p>

<h2>When tantrums are a concern</h2>

<p>Occasional tantrums are normal. Concerns arise if:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Your child regularly hurts themselves or others during tantrums</li>
  <li>Tantrums last more than 25 - 30 minutes regularly</li>
  <li>Your child seems to be in genuine distress beyond normal frustration</li>
  <li>Tantrums are happening many times daily or seem to be worsening significantly</li>
  <li>Your child cannot be calmed or distracted at all</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are concerned about the severity or frequency of tantrums</li>
  <li>Your child is hurting themselves or others regularly</li>
  <li>Tantrums seem to reflect underlying distress rather than typical frustration</li>
  <li>You need support managing your own emotional response to tantrums</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your health visitor or GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Are my child's tantrums normal for their age?</li>
  <li>What strategies might work best for my child?</li>
  <li>When should I be concerned about the frequency or intensity of tantrums?</li>
  <li>Is there support available for parents managing difficult behaviour?</li>
</ul>
