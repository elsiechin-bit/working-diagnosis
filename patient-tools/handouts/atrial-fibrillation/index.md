Atrial fibrillation, or AF, is the most common abnormal heart rhythm. The upper chambers of the heart quiver instead of beating regularly, so the pulse becomes fast and irregular. AF affects about 1 in 20 people over 65 and 1 in 10 over 80. It is treatable, and modern care can usually keep you well.

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<p>AF can cause:</p>

<ul>
<li>Palpitations (fluttering, racing, or pounding heartbeat)</li>
<li>Breathlessness with mild exertion</li>
<li>Tiredness or reduced exercise capacity</li>
<li>Light-headedness or near-faints</li>
<li>Chest discomfort</li>
</ul>

<p>But about 1 in 3 people have no symptoms at all. AF is sometimes picked up incidentally on a pulse check, smartwatch, or routine ECG.</p>

<h2>Why AF matters</h2>

<p>Two main risks:</p>

<p><strong>Stroke.</strong> When the atria do not contract properly, blood pools and can clot. A clot can travel to the brain and cause a stroke. Untreated AF roughly increases stroke risk by five times.&sup1; The good news: blood thinners reduce this risk by about two-thirds.</p>

<p><strong>Heart failure.</strong> A heart that runs fast and irregularly for months or years can weaken. Controlling the rate or rhythm prevents this.</p>

<h2>Three pillars of AF treatment</h2>

<p><strong>1. Anticoagulation (blood thinners) to prevent stroke.</strong></p>

<p>Most people with AF benefit from a blood thinner. Your GP uses the CHA<sub>2</sub>DS<sub>2</sub>-VASc score to decide. Points are added for age 65+ (1), age 75+ (2 total), female sex (1), high blood pressure (1), diabetes (1), heart failure (1), prior stroke (2), and vascular disease (1). A score of 2 or more (or 1 in men/2 in women excluding the female-sex point) means anticoagulation is recommended.</p>

<p>Modern blood thinners (DOACs - dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban) are funded in NZ for AF. They are simpler to use than warfarin and do not need regular blood tests for most people. See our <a href="/handouts/anticoagulants/">anticoagulants handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>2. Rate control - keeping the pulse from being too fast.</strong></p>

<p>Beta blockers (metoprolol, bisoprolol) or calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil) are first-line. The aim is a resting pulse below 110 beats per minute, or stricter if you still have symptoms.</p>

<p><strong>3. Rhythm control - trying to restore normal rhythm.</strong></p>

<p>Some people are offered rhythm control, especially if they are younger, very symptomatic, or new to AF. Options include:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cardioversion</strong> - electric shock under brief sedation to reset the rhythm</li>
<li><strong>Antiarrhythmic medicines</strong> - flecainide, sotalol, or amiodarone</li>
<li><strong>Catheter ablation</strong> - a procedure that scars the small areas of atrial tissue triggering AF; increasingly used and very effective for some patients</li>
</ul>

<h2>Daily life with AF</h2>

<p><strong>Check your pulse.</strong> Press two fingers to the side of your wrist or neck and count for 30 seconds. Irregular and fast (over 110) suggests AF is not well controlled. See your GP.</p>

<p><strong>Smartwatches and home ECGs.</strong> KardiaMobile and Apple Watch can record an ECG strip. They are useful for documenting episodes - bring the recordings to your appointments.</p>

<p><strong>Triggers.</strong> Common ones include alcohol, caffeine excess, dehydration, lack of sleep, and acute illness. Some people identify a personal trigger. Reducing alcohol to under 3 standard drinks per week roughly halves AF episodes in regular drinkers.&sup2;</p>

<p><strong>Weight, sleep apnoea, blood pressure.</strong> Treating these reduces AF burden substantially. If you snore loudly or feel sleepy in the day, ask about a sleep study.</p>

<p><strong>Exercise.</strong> Moderate aerobic exercise is helpful. Endurance athletes can paradoxically have more AF, but this rarely applies to most patients.</p>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe chest pain</li>
<li>Severe breathlessness or unable to lie flat</li>
<li>Fainting</li>
<li>Stroke symptoms (face droop, arm weakness, speech difficulty - FAST)</li>
</ul>

<p>See your GP within a few days if your usual rhythm changes, palpitations are worse, or you feel more tired than usual.</p>
