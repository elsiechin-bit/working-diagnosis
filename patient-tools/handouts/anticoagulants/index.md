Blood thinners (more accurately, anticoagulants) are medicines that reduce the chance of dangerous clots forming in your blood. They are commonly used for atrial fibrillation, after a clot in the leg or lung, after some types of heart surgery, or with mechanical heart valves. They can be life-saving, but they need a little understanding to use safely.

<h2>How they work</h2>

<p>Your blood naturally clots to stop bleeding. Clotting goes wrong when blood pools (as in atrial fibrillation) or sits still in a vein (as in deep vein thrombosis). Anticoagulants slow the clotting process so dangerous clots are less likely to form.</p>

<p>They do not "thin" the blood in any literal sense - they do not change how thick or runny it is. They simply slow the chemistry of clotting.</p>

<h2>The two main types</h2>

<p><strong>Warfarin</strong> has been used for over 70 years. Effective and cheap, but needs regular INR blood tests to keep the dose right. Many foods and medicines interact with warfarin.</p>

<p><strong>DOACs</strong> (direct oral anticoagulants) are newer. In NZ the funded DOACs for atrial fibrillation and clots are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Dabigatran</strong> (Pradaxa) - twice daily</li>
<li><strong>Rivaroxaban</strong> (Xarelto) - once daily with food</li>
<li><strong>Apixaban</strong> (Eliquis) - twice daily</li>
</ul>

<p>DOACs do not need INR monitoring, have fewer food and drug interactions, and have a slightly lower risk of brain bleeds compared with warfarin. For most people with atrial fibrillation, DOACs are now first-line. Warfarin is still preferred for people with mechanical heart valves or some types of antiphospholipid syndrome.</p>

<h2>Common side effect: bleeding</h2>

<p>The main risk of any anticoagulant is bleeding. Most bleeds are minor (gum bleeding, easy bruising, longer bleeding from small cuts). Some are serious.</p>

<p><strong>See your GP soon</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Heavier or longer periods than usual</li>
<li>Frequent or large bruises with no cause</li>
<li>Persistent nosebleeds</li>
<li>Blood in the stool that looks dark or tarry, or visible red blood</li>
<li>Pink or red urine</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe headache, especially after a head knock</li>
<li>Vomiting blood or coffee-ground material</li>
<li>Sudden weakness, slurred speech, or confusion</li>
<li>Bleeding that will not stop after 20 minutes of pressure</li>
<li>Fall onto your head, or any significant head injury</li>
</ul>

<h2>Daily living tips</h2>

<p><strong>Take it every day, on time.</strong> Missing doses leaves you unprotected, especially with DOACs (which clear from the body in 12-24 hours). Set a phone alarm.</p>

<p><strong>Tell every clinician.</strong> Dentists, surgeons, podiatrists, and pharmacists all need to know. Carry a card or wear a medic alert.</p>

<p><strong>Care with new medicines.</strong> Anti-inflammatories (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen) substantially raise bleeding risk - avoid unless your GP says otherwise. Paracetamol is safe. Some antibiotics interact with warfarin.</p>

<p><strong>Alcohol.</strong> Heavy drinking destabilises warfarin and raises bleeding risk on any anticoagulant. Up to 1-2 standard drinks per day is usually fine.</p>

<p><strong>Activity.</strong> Most sports are fine. Avoid contact sports (rugby, boxing) where head injury is likely.</p>

<p><strong>Travel.</strong> Take more than enough medication. Long flights are not a problem on anticoagulants - they reduce, not increase, clot risk.</p>

<h2>Warfarin specifics</h2>

<p>Aim for INR usually between 2.0 and 3.0 (some valves need 2.5 to 3.5).</p>

<p><strong>Foods that affect INR:</strong> Sudden changes in vitamin K intake matter most. Big serves of leafy greens (kale, spinach, broccoli) lower INR; alcohol binges raise it. Aim for consistency rather than avoidance.</p>

<p><strong>Missed dose:</strong> If less than 12 hours late, take it. If more, skip and continue normally; never double up. Tell the warfarin clinic.</p>

<h2>DOAC specifics</h2>

<p>No routine blood tests. Annual kidney function and full blood count are usually all that is needed.</p>

<p><strong>Missed dose (twice-daily DOACs - dabigatran, apixaban):</strong> If within 6 hours of usual time, take it. If later, skip and take the next one as normal.</p>

<p><strong>Missed dose (rivaroxaban, once-daily):</strong> If within 12 hours, take it. After 12 hours, skip. Take with food.</p>

<p><strong>Reversal agents</strong> exist for emergencies (idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet for rivaroxaban and apixaban) and are stocked in major hospitals.</p>

<h2>Stopping for procedures</h2>

<p>Some procedures (most dental work, cataract surgery, simple skin biopsies) do not require stopping.</p>

<p>Bigger procedures usually need anticoagulants paused 24-72 hours before. Always tell your GP and surgeon - never stop on your own initiative. Your GP or pharmacist will give you a personalised plan.</p>
