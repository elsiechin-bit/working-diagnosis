<p>The combined oral contraceptive pill ("the pill") has been around since 1961. It is taken by roughly one in four women of reproductive age in NZ. Taken correctly it is highly effective. Taken imperfectly (which most of us do) it is around 91% effective in real-world use. This handout walks through how to start, what to expect, and the things worth knowing.</p>

<h2>How it works</h2>

<p>The combined pill contains two hormones, oestrogen and progestogen. They work together to:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Stop ovulation - no egg released, no pregnancy possible</li>
  <li>Thicken cervical mucus - sperm cannot get through</li>
  <li>Thin the lining of the uterus - implantation less likely</li>
</ul>

<p>The pill comes in a 28-day pack. Most are 21 active pills + 7 placebo (or pill-free) days; some are 24 active + 4 placebo (newer formulations, often less hormone fluctuation). During the placebo or pill-free days you have a "withdrawal bleed", which is similar to a period but not actually one.</p>

<h2>NZ-funded options</h2>

<p>Several brands are fully funded in NZ:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ava 30, Microgynon 30</strong> - levonorgestrel + ethinylestradiol 30mcg - common starting choice</li>
  <li><strong>Levlen ED</strong> - same active ingredients</li>
  <li><strong>Mercilon, Mercilon-30</strong> - desogestrel-containing pill</li>
  <li><strong>Microgynon 50</strong> - older, higher-dose</li>
  <li><strong>Ava 20</strong> - lower-dose option</li>
</ul>

<p>If a particular pill does not suit, there is usually another funded option to try. Yasmin (drospirenone) is not currently funded but is sometimes prescribed for specific reasons.</p>

<h2>Who can take it</h2>

<p>Most healthy women under 35 who do not smoke and do not have specific risk factors. Your GP will check:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Blood pressure</li>
  <li>BMI</li>
  <li>Personal and family history of blood clots</li>
  <li>Smoking history</li>
  <li>Migraine type (with or without aura)</li>
  <li>Breast cancer history</li>
  <li>Liver disease, lupus, certain other conditions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who should not take it</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Smokers over 35</li>
  <li>Anyone over 35 with multiple cardiovascular risk factors (high blood pressure, diabetes, obesity, family history)</li>
  <li>Migraine with aura - a stroke risk concern</li>
  <li>Personal history of blood clot (DVT, PE)</li>
  <li>Some inherited clotting disorders (Factor V Leiden, etc.)</li>
  <li>Active or recent breast cancer</li>
  <li>Severe liver disease</li>
  <li>BMI over 35 - relative contraindication</li>
  <li>Up to 6 weeks postpartum (longer if breastfeeding)</li>
</ul>

<p>If the combined pill is not safe for you, the progestogen-only pill, IUD, implant, or depot injection are usually all options.</p>

<h2>Starting the pill</h2>

<h3>Day 1 start (within first 5 days of period)</h3>
<p>Take the first pill on day 1 of your period. Effective immediately - no backup needed.</p>

<h3>Quick start (any other day)</h3>
<p>Start the pill the day you collect it. Use condoms or abstain for the first 7 days. Pregnancy test in 3-4 weeks if there has been any recent unprotected sex.</p>

<h2>Taking it</h2>

<ul>
  <li>One pill a day, ideally at the same time</li>
  <li>The order on the pack matters; follow the arrows</li>
  <li>You will have a withdrawal bleed during the pill-free or placebo week (or, with newer 24+4 packs, during the 4 inactive days)</li>
  <li>Start the next pack the day after the placebo week ends, even if you are still bleeding</li>
</ul>

<h2>Missed pills</h2>

<p>The rules depend on how many you missed and where in the pack:</p>

<h3>One pill missed (less than 24 hours late)</h3>
<p>Take it as soon as you remember, even if it means taking two in one day. Continue as normal. No backup needed.</p>

<h3>Two or more pills missed</h3>
<p>Take the most recent missed pill now. Discard any earlier missed pills. Continue with the rest of the pack as usual. <strong>Use condoms for 7 days.</strong> If the missed pills were in week 1 of the pack and you had unprotected sex, consider emergency contraception. If they were in week 3, skip the placebo week and start the next pack immediately.</p>

<h2>The first three months</h2>

<p>Most people get used to the pill within 2-3 months. In that window, common things to expect:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Breakthrough bleeding or spotting between periods - usually settles</li>
  <li>Lighter, more predictable withdrawal bleeds</li>
  <li>Possible mood changes (better in some, worse in others)</li>
  <li>Possible breast tenderness</li>
  <li>Possible headaches</li>
  <li>Slight nausea, often improved by taking with food or at night</li>
  <li>No reliable evidence the pill causes weight gain in most people, despite popular belief</li>
</ul>

<p>If side effects persist beyond 3 months, talk to your GP. Switching to a different formulation (lower oestrogen, different progestogen) often helps.</p>

<h2>Worrying side effects - seek care immediately</h2>

<p>The combined pill slightly increases the risk of blood clots. The absolute risk is low (around 5-12 per 10,000 women per year on the pill, vs 2-3 per 10,000 not on it; pregnancy itself raises the risk to around 30 per 10,000)[1]. But signs of a clot need urgent attention.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Calf or leg pain</strong>, especially with swelling, warmth, redness - possible DVT</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, coughing blood</strong> - possible PE</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden severe headache, weakness on one side, slurred speech</strong> - possible stroke</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden chest pain, especially crushing or radiating</strong> - possible MI</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden one-sided vision loss</strong> - possible retinal vein thrombosis</li>
  <li><strong>Severe abdominal pain</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Things that reduce effectiveness</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Vomiting within 3 hours of taking the pill - take another</li>
  <li>Severe diarrhoea</li>
  <li>Some medicines: rifampicin (TB treatment), some anti-epileptics (carbamazepine, phenytoin, topiramate at higher doses, lamotrigine bidirectionally), St John's Wort, some HIV medicines</li>
</ul>

<p>Common antibiotics (amoxicillin, doxycycline, etc.) do <em>not</em> reduce pill effectiveness despite older advice. The exception is rifampicin and rifabutin.</p>

<h2>Stopping the pill</h2>

<p>Fertility usually returns within the first or second cycle after stopping. There is no need to "take a break" or "let your body rest" between packs.</p>
