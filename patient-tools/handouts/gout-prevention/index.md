<p>If you have had a gout attack, the good news is that you can significantly reduce the risk of future attacks through lifestyle changes and, if needed, medicines that lower uric acid levels. This handout explains what prevents gout and how to use allopurinol effectively.</p>

<h2>Why gout attacks happen</h2>

<p>Gout occurs when uric acid levels in your blood become too high, causing uric acid crystals to form in your joints. High uric acid (hyperuricaemia) comes from a combination of your genetics (how much uric acid your kidneys excrete) and lifestyle factors (diet, alcohol, weight). Most people who have one gout attack will have more unless uric acid is managed.</p>

<h2>Dietary changes that lower uric acid</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Limit high-purine foods.</strong> Purines (found in red meat, organ meats, certain seafood, and yeast extracts) break down into uric acid. You do not need to eliminate these entirely, but reducing portions helps. Beef, lamb, liver, kidneys, and anchovies are highest.</li>
  <li><strong>Limit alcohol, especially beer.</strong> Beer is particularly bad for gout because it contains purines and interferes with uric acid excretion. Limit to no more than 2 standard drinks per day, and fewer on days with high-purine meals.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid sugary drinks.</strong> Drinks sweetened with fructose (regular soft drinks, fruit juice) increase uric acid. Water is best.</li>
  <li><strong>Lose weight if overweight.</strong> Weight loss directly lowers uric acid levels. Aim for gradual loss (0.5 - 1 kg per week) rather than rapid crash diets, which can paradoxically trigger attacks.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat more plant-based foods.</strong> Vegetables, legumes, and grains are generally protective. Most people with gout can safely eat foods like peas and lentils (despite containing purines, their overall effect is protective).</li>
  <li><strong>Drink plenty of water.</strong> Aim for 2 - 3 litres daily. Good hydration helps your kidneys excrete uric acid.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Allopurinol - lowering uric acid with medicine</h2>

<p>Allopurinol is a tablet that lowers uric acid by reducing how much your body makes. It is very effective but requires careful use:<strong> Start low and go slow.</strong> Allopurinol is started at a low dose (usually 50 mg daily) and increased slowly every 2 - 4 weeks until your uric acid is at target (usually below 360 micromol/L). Starting too high can trigger attacks, so patience is important.</p>

<p><strong>Prevent attack flares when starting.</strong> Many people take a low-dose anti-inflammatory (like indomethacin) or colchicine for the first 3 - 6 months while uric acid is being lowered, to prevent attacks triggered by the uric acid changes. Ask your GP about this.</p>

<p><strong>Take it long-term.</strong> Once you are on an effective dose, allopurinol is usually taken indefinitely. Stopping it causes uric acid to rise again and attacks recur.</p>

<p><strong>Monitor uric acid levels.</strong> Your GP will check your uric acid levels regularly (usually every 2 - 4 weeks while adjusting dose, then every 6 - 12 months once stable) to ensure you are at target.</p>

<h2>Side effects of allopurinol</h2>

<p>Allopurinol is generally well tolerated. Mild side effects (rash, nausea) occur in about 5% of people. Rarely, a severe skin reaction can occur - if you develop an extensive rash, stop allopurinol and see your doctor urgently. People with certain genetic variants (HLA-B*5801, more common in people of Thai, Han Chinese, and Korean descent) have higher risk of severe reactions - your doctor may test for this if relevant.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You develop symptoms of another gout attack</li>
  <li>You develop a rash while taking allopurinol</li>
  <li>Uric acid is not reaching target despite increasing doses</li>
  <li>You have side effects affecting your quality of life</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is my target uric acid level?</li>
  <li>Do I need a medicine to prevent attacks while starting allopurinol?</li>
  <li>How long will I need to take allopurinol?</li>
  <li>Should I be tested for genetic risk factors?</li>
</ul>
