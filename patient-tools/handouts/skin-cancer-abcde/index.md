New Zealand has the highest rate of melanoma in the world.&sup1; The good news: most skin cancers are visible on the surface, and almost all are curable when caught early. Once a year, do a slow head-to-toe check. Knowing what is normal for you is half the battle.

<h2>The three main types</h2>

<p><strong>Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)</strong> - the most common. Slow-growing, almost never spreads, but can grow deeper if ignored. Usually appears as a pearly lump, a small ulcer that scabs and bleeds, or a flat scaly patch. Common on face, ears, scalp, and back.</p>

<p><strong>Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)</strong> - faster-growing, can spread if neglected. Usually a firm, scaly red lump or non-healing ulcer. Often on sun-damaged skin (face, ears, lips, hands, lower legs).</p>

<p><strong>Melanoma</strong> - less common but more serious. Can spread quickly. Usually pigmented (brown, black, sometimes red, blue, or skin-coloured). See the ABCDE rule below.</p>

<h2>The ABCDE rule for moles and pigmented spots</h2>

<p><strong>A - Asymmetry.</strong> One half does not match the other half.</p>

<p><strong>B - Border.</strong> Irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges.</p>

<p><strong>C - Colour.</strong> More than one shade - browns, blacks, reds, whites, blues all in the same spot.</p>

<p><strong>D - Diameter.</strong> Larger than 6 mm (about the size of a pencil eraser). Some melanomas are smaller, so do not let size alone reassure you.</p>

<p><strong>E - Evolving.</strong> Changing in size, shape, colour, or symptoms (itch, bleed, crust). The most important sign of all.</p>

<p>Any spot that ticks one or more of these boxes deserves a GP review.</p>

<h2>The "ugly duckling" sign</h2>

<p>Look for the spot that does not look like the others on your skin. Most people's moles look broadly similar. The odd-one-out (different colour, shape, or growth pattern) is the one to show your doctor.</p>

<h2>Other warning signs</h2>

<ul>
<li>A new spot in someone over 30</li>
<li>A sore that does not heal within 4 weeks</li>
<li>A lump that grows over weeks to months</li>
<li>A spot that itches, bleeds, crusts, or scabs repeatedly</li>
<li>A black or dark streak under a fingernail or toenail (especially if widening)</li>
<li>A pigmented spot on the palm, sole, or in the mouth</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to check yourself</h2>

<p>Once a year, ideally on the same date (e.g. your birthday):</p>

<ol>
<li>Stand in front of a full-length mirror in good light</li>
<li>Check face, ears, scalp (use a comb to part hair, or ask someone)</li>
<li>Check neck, chest, abdomen (women: under breasts)</li>
<li>Check arms - all sides, hands (palms, backs, between fingers, under nails)</li>
<li>Use a hand mirror to check the back of the neck, back, buttocks</li>
<li>Sit and check legs - all sides, feet (tops, soles, between toes, under nails)</li>
<li>Use a hand mirror to check genital area</li>
</ol>

<p>Photographing pigmented spots over time helps detect change. Apps like SkinVision are not a substitute for medical review but can help you log spots.</p>

<h2>Who is at higher risk?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Fair skin, fair eyes, red or blonde hair</li>
<li>History of sunburn, especially in childhood</li>
<li>Many moles (over 50) or atypical moles</li>
<li>Personal or family history of skin cancer</li>
<li>Immunosuppression (transplant medicines, methotrexate, biologics)</li>
<li>Outdoor occupations (farming, construction, sport)</li>
<li>Use of sunbeds</li>
</ul>

<p>Maori, Pacific, and Asian patients have lower melanoma rates but often present later and at deeper stages, partly because the lesions appear in unusual sites (palms, soles, under nails) and are missed.</p>

<h2>What your GP will do</h2>

<p>Examination of the spot, often with a dermatoscope (magnifying lens with a light). If suspicious, the spot will be biopsied or excised under local anaesthetic. Results take about a week.</p>

<p>If skin cancer is confirmed, treatment depends on the type, size, and location. Most are cured by simple excision. Melanoma may need a wider excision and sometimes lymph node assessment.</p>

<h2>Mole maps and skin checks</h2>

<p>People at higher risk are sometimes offered "mole mapping" - a series of photographs to track moles over time. This is mostly private in NZ. Whether you have mole mapping or not, an annual GP skin check is worthwhile if you are at higher risk.</p>
