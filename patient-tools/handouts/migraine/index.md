<p>Migraines are severe, often one-sided headaches often accompanied by light sensitivity, nausea, or vomiting. While painful and disruptive, migraines are not dangerous and are highly treatable. This handout covers what triggers migraines, how to manage attacks, and when prevention is needed.</p>

<h2>What migraines feel like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Throbbing pain usually on one side of the head</li>
  <li>Often worse with movement or activity</li>
  <li>Light sensitivity (photophobia) - bright light worsens pain</li>
  <li>Sound sensitivity (phonophobia) - loud noise worsens pain</li>
  <li>Nausea, sometimes vomiting</li>
  <li>Some people experience aura - visual disturbances (flashing lights, zigzag lines, loss of part of visual field) 15 - 30 minutes before the headache</li>
  <li>Migraines typically last 4 - 72 hours without treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Hormonal changes (menstrual cycle, oral contraceptives, hormone therapy)</li>
  <li>Stress and stress relief (weekends, holidays after high stress)</li>
  <li>Sleep changes (too little, too much, irregular sleep)</li>
  <li>Food triggers (chocolate, aged cheese, cured meats, citrus, caffeine withdrawal)</li>
  <li>Skipping meals or fasting</li>
  <li>Dehydration</li>
  <li>Weather changes and barometric pressure changes</li>
  <li>Sensory triggers (bright lights, strong smells, loud noise)</li>
  <li>Medication (oral contraceptives, vasodilators)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps during an attack</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Treat early.</strong> Pain relief works best if taken at the first sign of a migraine, before pain becomes severe.</li>
  <li><strong>Use pain relief effectively.</strong> Paracetamol, ibuprofen, or aspirin taken early can abort mild migraines. For moderate to severe migraines, prescription triptans (sumatriptan, zolmitriptan) are highly effective - they narrow blood vessels and reduce pain and associated symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Rest in a dark, quiet room.</strong> Darkness and quiet reduce sensory triggers and help recovery.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply heat or cold.</strong> Some people find heat (warm shower, heating pad) helpful; others prefer cold (cold compress).</li>
  <li><strong>Stay hydrated.</strong> Dehydration worsens migraines and delays recovery.</li>
  <li><strong>Anti-nausea medicine.</strong> If nausea prevents you swallowing pain relief, anti-nausea medication (metoclopramide) taken first can help.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing migraines</h2>

<p>If you have frequent migraines (more than 4 per month), preventive medicines significantly reduce frequency and severity. Preventive medicines are taken daily even without a migraine present. Options include beta-blockers (propranolol), antidepressants (amitriptyline), or anti-epileptic drugs. Your doctor will choose based on your specific triggers and other health factors.</p>

<h2>Identifying and avoiding your personal triggers</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Keep a migraine diary.</strong> Record when migraines occur, what you ate, your stress level, sleep, and any other factors. Patterns often emerge showing your personal triggers.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid identified triggers when possible.</strong> If certain foods trigger migraines, avoid them. If stress triggers them, stress management helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain regular routines.</strong> Consistent sleep, meal times, and exercise reduce overall migraine frequency.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your migraines are frequent or worsening</li>
  <li>Your migraine pattern has changed (different location, severity, associated symptoms)</li>
  <li>You have a migraine with aura and want preventive medicines</li>
  <li>You are having difficulty managing migraines with over-the-counter pain relief</li>
  <li>A migraine is unusually severe or accompanied by fever, stiff neck, or confusion</li>
</ul>

<h2>Red flags - when it is not migraine</h2>

<p>See a doctor urgently if you experience sudden severe headache, headache with fever and stiff neck, headache with weakness or vision changes, or a change in your usual migraine pattern - these may indicate something other than migraine.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Are these definitely migraines?</li>
  <li>Should I take a preventive medicine?</li>
  <li>Which pain relief is best for me?</li>
  <li>What are my trigger factors?</li>
  <li>When should I seek urgent care?</li>
</ul>
