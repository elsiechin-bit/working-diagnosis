<p>Osteoporosis is a condition where your bones become weakened and more likely to break from a minor fall or bump. The good news is that you can significantly reduce your fracture risk through bone-healthy habits and, if needed, medicines. This handout explains what helps protect your bones.</p>

<h2>What it is</h2>

<p>Osteoporosis develops when bones lose density and become more fragile. You usually have no symptoms until a fracture happens, which is why screening and prevention are so important. Risk increases with age, and women face higher risk after menopause due to falling oestrogen levels.</p>

<h2>Risk factors you should know about</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Age (especially over 50 for women, over 70 for men)</li>
  <li>Female sex or history of early menopause</li>
  <li>Family history of osteoporosis or fractures</li>
  <li>Low vitamin D levels</li>
  <li>Inadequate calcium intake</li>
  <li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
  <li>Smoking and heavy alcohol use</li>
  <li>Certain medicines (long-term corticosteroids)</li>
  <li>Rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory conditions</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Get enough calcium.</strong> Aim for 1000 - 1200 mg daily from dairy, leafy greens, fortified plant milks, or supplements if needed. Spread calcium intake throughout the day for better absorption.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain adequate vitamin D.</strong> Vitamin D helps your body absorb calcium. Aim for 15 - 20 micrograms (600 - 800 IU) daily, more if you have limited sun exposure or dark skin tone. Your GP can check your level.</li>
  <li><strong>Do weight-bearing and muscle-strengthening exercise.</strong> Walking, running, dancing, and resistance training stimulate bone growth. Aim for at least 30 minutes most days, including 2 days of strength training per week.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid smoking and limit alcohol.</strong> Smoking accelerates bone loss; heavy drinking (more than 3 standard drinks daily) weakens bones.</li>
  <li><strong>Prevent falls.</strong> Remove hazards at home, wear proper footwear, check your vision, and consider balance training if you have risk factors for falling.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain a healthy weight.</strong> Being underweight increases fracture risk; being overweight increases fall risk. Aim for a BMI in the healthy range.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Screening and diagnosis</h2>

<p>A bone density scan (DEXA) measures your bone strength and can predict fracture risk. Your GP may recommend screening if you have risk factors, are over 50 (especially women post-menopause), or have had a fracture. A fracture risk calculator (like FRAX) helps decide if treatment is needed.</p>

<h2>Medicines for bone health</h2>

<p>If lifestyle measures are not enough, medicines like bisphosphonates (alendronate) can slow bone loss and reduce fracture risk. These are usually started if you have low bone density or have already had a fracture. Your GP will discuss whether medication is right for you based on your individual risk.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You have risk factors for osteoporosis and want screening</li>
  <li>You have had a fracture from a minor fall or bump</li>
  <li>You are experiencing height loss or stooped posture</li>
  <li>You are concerned about your bone health</li>
  <li>You are starting or stopping long-term corticosteroid therapy</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I need bone density screening?</li>
  <li>What is my fracture risk?</li>
  <li>Should I take vitamin D supplements?</li>
  <li>Do I need medication for bone health?</li>
  <li>What type of exercise is best for my bones?</li>
</ul>
