A stroke is a brain attack. Treatment exists that can dissolve clots or remove them, but only in the first few hours. Every minute of delay loses about 1.9 million brain cells.&sup1; Knowing the signs and acting immediately can be the difference between full recovery and lifelong disability.

<h2>FAST</h2>

<p><strong>F - Face.</strong> Ask the person to smile. Does one side of the face droop?</p>

<p><strong>A - Arms.</strong> Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift down or stay weak?</p>

<p><strong>S - Speech.</strong> Ask them to repeat a simple sentence. Is it slurred, jumbled, or are they struggling to find words?</p>

<p><strong>T - Time.</strong> If any of these are present, dial <strong>111</strong> immediately and say "I think this is a stroke."</p>

<h2>Other stroke symptoms</h2>

<p>FAST catches most strokes but not all. Other warning signs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden numbness or weakness, especially down one side</li>
<li>Sudden confusion or difficulty understanding speech</li>
<li>Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes</li>
<li>Sudden severe headache with no clear cause ("worst headache of my life")</li>
<li>Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, or loss of balance</li>
</ul>

<p>The key word is <strong>sudden</strong>. Stroke symptoms come on over seconds to minutes, not hours.</p>

<h2>What to do</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Dial 111</strong> - tell the operator you think it is a stroke</li>
<li><strong>Note the time</strong> the symptoms started (or when the person was last seen well)</li>
<li><strong>Stay with the person.</strong> Lay them on their side if drowsy</li>
<li><strong>Do not give food, drink, or aspirin</strong> until paramedics arrive (some strokes are bleeds, where aspirin makes things worse)</li>
<li><strong>Unlock the front door</strong> if you are alone, so paramedics can enter</li>
<li>If possible, gather a list of medicines or your medic alert card</li>
</ol>

<p>Do not drive yourself or the person to hospital. Ambulances pre-notify the hospital so the stroke team is ready when you arrive.</p>

<h2>Why time matters</h2>

<p>Two main treatments depend on timing:</p>

<p><strong>Clot-dissolving medicine (alteplase, tenecteplase):</strong> usually given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Earlier is better.</p>

<p><strong>Clot retrieval (thrombectomy):</strong> a tiny catheter pulls the clot out of a blocked artery. Available at major NZ centres. Effective up to 6 hours, sometimes 24, in selected patients.</p>

<p>Both treatments only work for ischaemic strokes (clots, about 85% of strokes). The remaining 15% are bleeds and need different management. The hospital sorts this out with a CT scan within minutes of arrival.</p>

<h2>Mini-strokes (TIAs)</h2>

<p>A transient ischaemic attack (TIA, or "mini-stroke") has the same symptoms as stroke but resolves within hours. It is a warning that a real stroke may be coming - around 1 in 12 people have a stroke within a week of a TIA. Treat any FAST symptom the same way: call <strong>111</strong>. See our <a href="/handouts/tia/">TIA handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Who is at higher risk?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Anyone over 55, especially over 75</li>
<li>People with high blood pressure (the biggest single risk factor)</li>
<li>Smokers</li>
<li>People with atrial fibrillation, diabetes, high cholesterol</li>
<li>People who have already had a stroke or TIA</li>
<li>Maori and Pacific people - earlier age of stroke and worse outcomes, partly due to inequities in risk factor management</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of this applies, see your GP regularly to keep risk factors under control. Most strokes are preventable.</p>

<h2>After the call</h2>

<p>If symptoms settle before the ambulance arrives, do not relax - still go to hospital. A TIA needs urgent investigation in the same way.</p>
