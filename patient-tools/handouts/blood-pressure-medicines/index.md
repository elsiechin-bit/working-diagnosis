<p>If you have been prescribed a medicine for high blood pressure, you are in good company - around one in five NZ adults takes one. The medicines are well-studied, generally well-tolerated, and meaningfully reduce the risk of stroke, heart attack, kidney failure, and dementia.[1] This handout walks through the main classes, what to expect when starting, common concerns, and why many people need more than one.</p>

<h2>Why we treat blood pressure</h2>

<p>High blood pressure usually causes no symptoms. The treatment is preventative - lowering pressure now reduces the chance of damage years later. The benefit is real but invisible. This is why people sometimes stop their medicines: nothing seems wrong. The damage was already being prevented; stopping unmasks the original risk.</p>

<h2>The main classes</h2>

<h3>ACE inhibitors</h3>
<p>Names ending in "-pril": cilazapril, enalapril, lisinopril, perindopril, quinapril. Funded in NZ.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How they work:</strong> block an enzyme that makes blood vessels narrow</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> dry tickly cough (around 10-15%, often weeks after starting), occasional dizziness on standing, raised potassium</li>
  <li><strong>Especially useful in:</strong> diabetes, kidney disease, heart failure, after heart attack</li>
</ul>

<h3>Angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs)</h3>
<p>Names ending in "-sartan": candesartan, losartan, valsartan. Funded.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How they work:</strong> similar mechanism to ACE inhibitors but at a different point in the chain</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> similar to ACEs but no cough. Generally well-tolerated.</li>
  <li><strong>Used:</strong> often as the alternative if ACE inhibitor causes cough</li>
</ul>

<h3>Calcium channel blockers</h3>
<p>Two main types:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Dihydropyridines</strong> - amlodipine, felodipine. Lower BP by relaxing arteries. Common side effects: ankle swelling, mild flushing, headache. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Non-dihydropyridines</strong> - diltiazem, verapamil. Slow heart rate as well. Used in specific situations like angina or atrial fibrillation. Funded.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Thiazide diuretics</h3>
<p>Bendroflumethiazide, indapamide. Funded.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How they work:</strong> mild "water tablet" effect through the kidneys</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> increased urination (settles after a few weeks), low potassium (monitored), occasional gout flares, raised blood sugar in some</li>
  <li><strong>Often combined</strong> with ACE inhibitors or ARBs in fixed-dose tablets</li>
</ul>

<h3>Beta blockers</h3>
<p>Metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol, carvedilol. Funded.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How they work:</strong> slow the heart rate and reduce its force of contraction</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> tiredness, cold hands and feet, sleep changes, occasional reduced exercise tolerance, potential to mask hypoglycaemia in diabetes, asthma flare in some</li>
  <li><strong>Used more for:</strong> heart failure, after heart attack, atrial fibrillation, angina - less commonly first-line for hypertension alone now</li>
</ul>

<h3>Other classes</h3>
<p>Spironolactone (especially for resistant hypertension), alpha-blockers (doxazosin), and centrally-acting agents are used in specific situations.</p>

<h2>Why two or three medicines</h2>

<p>Blood pressure has multiple drivers - the heart, the arteries, the kidneys, the nervous system, hormone systems. Single medicines target one driver. For about half of people, one medicine is not quite enough. The modern approach combines two medicines at low-to-moderate doses rather than maxing out one - typically more effective with fewer side effects.</p>

<p>Common combinations:</p>
<ul>
  <li>ACE/ARB + thiazide diuretic</li>
  <li>ACE/ARB + calcium channel blocker</li>
  <li>All three for moderate-severe hypertension</li>
  <li>Add spironolactone for resistant cases</li>
</ul>

<p>Many fixed-dose combination tablets in NZ contain two medicines in one pill, which is helpful for adherence.</p>

<h2>Starting a new medicine - what to expect</h2>

<ul>
  <li>The first dose is usually low. Your GP often adjusts upward over weeks.</li>
  <li>Side effects, if any, are usually most noticeable in the first 1-2 weeks. Many settle.</li>
  <li>Stand up slowly for the first few days - some medicines cause a brief drop on standing.</li>
  <li>Home BP monitoring is helpful - aim for an average around 135/85 at home, lower in some situations.</li>
  <li>Most people need 4-8 weeks before the medicine's full effect on average blood pressure is clear.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common concerns</h2>

<h3>"I do not feel anything different"</h3>
<p>Most people do not. The medicine is preventing future damage you cannot feel.</p>

<h3>"Will I be on this forever?"</h3>
<p>Usually yes for high blood pressure, but doses can sometimes be reduced if weight, diet, alcohol, or activity changes substantially.</p>

<h3>"Do I really need this if I feel fine?"</h3>
<p>This is the question that hides 30 years of stroke and heart attack risk. The answer is usually yes, especially after age 50.</p>

<h3>Sex life</h3>
<p>Most modern blood pressure medicines have minimal effect on sex life. Some older ones (and high-dose beta blockers, thiazides) can contribute to erectile difficulties. Worth raising with your GP - alternatives exist.</p>

<h3>Pregnancy</h3>
<p>ACE inhibitors and ARBs are not safe in pregnancy. If you might become pregnant, your GP will switch to alternatives (labetalol, methyldopa, nifedipine).</p>

<h2>Lifestyle alongside medication</h2>

<p>Lifestyle works alongside medication, not instead. Combined effects are bigger than either alone.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Salt</strong> - reducing salt by 1g/day lowers BP by around 2 mmHg average; bigger effect if your starting intake was high</li>
  <li><strong>Weight</strong> - around 1 mmHg drop per kg of weight loss</li>
  <li><strong>Alcohol</strong> - reducing heavy drinking has substantial effects</li>
  <li><strong>Exercise</strong> - 5-10 mmHg drop with regular aerobic activity</li>
  <li><strong>DASH-style diet</strong> (vegetables, fruit, low-fat dairy, less red meat) - 5-10 mmHg in trials</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep</strong> - poor sleep and sleep apnoea raise BP; treating them helps</li>
  <li><strong>Stress</strong> - real but smaller effect than the others</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to call your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Side effects in the first weeks that are bothering you</li>
  <li>Persistent dry cough on an ACE inhibitor</li>
  <li>Significant ankle swelling on amlodipine</li>
  <li>Dizziness on standing that does not settle</li>
  <li>Fainting</li>
  <li>Home blood pressure consistently below 100/60 in someone who is feeling unwell</li>
  <li>Considering pregnancy</li>
  <li>Starting a new medicine - some interact with BP medicines</li>
</ul>
