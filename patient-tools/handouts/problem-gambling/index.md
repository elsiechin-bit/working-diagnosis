<p>Problem gambling is the term for gambling that causes significant harm - financial, relational, occupational, mental, or physical. Around 1-2% of NZ adults are problem gamblers, and another 4-5% are at moderate risk.[1] The harm extends to families - around 5 people are affected for every problem gambler. NZ has well-funded, free, 24/7 services. The hardest part is recognising the problem and asking for help. This handout walks through the signs and the path to support.</p>

<h2>What problem gambling is</h2>

<p>Gambling becomes a problem when it persists despite causing significant negative consequences. The DSM-5 lists nine criteria; meeting four or more constitutes "gambling disorder":</p>

<ul>
  <li>Needing to gamble with increasing amounts to achieve excitement</li>
  <li>Restlessness or irritability when trying to cut down</li>
  <li>Repeated unsuccessful attempts to control gambling</li>
  <li>Preoccupation with gambling</li>
  <li>Gambling when distressed (relief gambling)</li>
  <li>"Chasing losses" - returning to gamble after losing</li>
  <li>Lying about gambling</li>
  <li>Jeopardising or losing relationships, jobs, opportunities</li>
  <li>Relying on others for money to relieve gambling-related financial situations</li>
</ul>

<p>Problem gambling is recognised as a behavioural addiction. It activates the same brain reward systems as substance addictions. The neural and psychological mechanisms are similar; treatment approaches overlap.</p>

<h2>How it shows up</h2>

<h3>The person gambling</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Increasing amounts of time spent gambling, including at unusual times (early morning, late night)</li>
  <li>Increasing financial stakes</li>
  <li>Borrowing or selling things to gamble</li>
  <li>Lying about losses or whereabouts</li>
  <li>Mood deteriorating - more anxious, depressed, irritable</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance, increased alcohol use</li>
  <li>Withdrawing from family activities</li>
  <li>Job performance dropping or attendance issues</li>
  <li>Health complaints - headaches, gut issues, chest tightness</li>
  <li>Suicidal thoughts (problem gamblers have around 4x higher suicide risk than the general population)</li>
</ul>

<h3>What family members notice</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Money disappearing, household savings draining</li>
  <li>Unexplained credit card debt or new accounts</li>
  <li>Unpaid bills despite income</li>
  <li>Unfamiliar transactions</li>
  <li>Constant phone use, secrecy</li>
  <li>Mood swings related to wins and losses</li>
  <li>Defensiveness or denial when asked</li>
  <li>Children sensing something is wrong</li>
</ul>

<h2>NZ context - what gets gambled on</h2>

<p>NZ-specific patterns:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pokie machines (EGMs)</strong> - around 80% of problem gambling harm in NZ is associated with pokies. Concentrated in lower-decile communities.</li>
  <li><strong>Online sports betting</strong> - rising rapidly, especially among younger men</li>
  <li><strong>Online casinos and apps</strong> - typically based offshore, not regulated by NZ law</li>
  <li><strong>TAB</strong> - in-person and online sports and racing</li>
  <li><strong>Lotto</strong> - lower harm potential but a real factor for some</li>
  <li><strong>Casinos</strong> - SkyCity, Christchurch Casino</li>
</ul>

<p>The disproportionate harm from pokies and online sports betting is well-known to the regulator. There are NZ rules around exclusion programmes, and the gambling industry pays a problem gambling levy that funds free national services.</p>

<h2>Free national services in NZ</h2>

<h3>Gambling Helpline</h3>
<p><strong>0800 654 655</strong> or text 8006 (free, 24/7). Free counselling over the phone, intervention planning, support for families. Specialist Maori, Pacific, and Asian lines available. <a href="https://gamblinghelpline.co.nz/">gamblinghelpline.co.nz</a></p>

<h3>Free face-to-face counselling</h3>
<p>Around 30 contracted providers across NZ offering free in-person or video counselling for gambling and family. Find through the helpline or at <a href="https://choicenotchance.org.nz/">choicenotchance.org.nz</a></p>

<h3>Specialist providers</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Asian Family Services</strong> 0800 862 342 - multilingual including Mandarin, Cantonese, Korean, Vietnamese</li>
  <li><strong>Maori provider services</strong> - kaupapa Maori approaches</li>
  <li><strong>Pacific provider services</strong> - culturally specific support</li>
  <li><strong>Mapu Maia</strong> - Pacific gambling harm services</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - what works</h2>

<h3>Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)</h3>
<p>The most evidence-based treatment for problem gambling.[2] Helps with:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Recognising and challenging thinking errors (gambler's fallacy, illusions of control)</li>
  <li>Identifying personal triggers</li>
  <li>Developing coping strategies for urges</li>
  <li>Exposure to triggering situations without gambling (relapse prevention)</li>
  <li>Addressing co-existing depression, anxiety, alcohol use</li>
</ul>

<p>Available free through the contracted gambling counselling services.</p>

<h3>Practical financial measures</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Self-exclusion</strong> from venues - both physical (pokie venues, casinos) and online (TAB self-exclusion). Operates for fixed periods, signed at the venue or via the helpline. Effective for many.</li>
  <li><strong>Banking restrictions</strong> - many NZ banks now allow card blocks on gambling merchant codes (gambling block). Ask your bank.</li>
  <li><strong>Putting money management in someone else's hands</strong> - partner, family member, financial mentor</li>
  <li><strong>Removing apps</strong> - delete betting apps; use parental controls or app blockers if needed</li>
  <li><strong>Limit-setting on online accounts</strong> - daily, weekly, monthly deposit limits</li>
</ul>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>Less established than for some other addictions, but:</p>

<ul>
  <li>SSRIs - useful when co-existing depression or anxiety</li>
  <li>Naltrexone - opioid antagonist used for alcohol dependence; some evidence in gambling, particularly with strong urges</li>
</ul>

<h3>Peer support</h3>
<p>Gamblers Anonymous - free 12-step group, in-person and online meetings in NZ. <a href="https://gamblersanonymous.org.nz/">gamblersanonymous.org.nz</a></p>

<h3>Family support</h3>
<p>Gam-Anon - support for family and friends affected. Many NZ counselling services also work with affected family members in their own right - the harm is significant and worth addressing.</p>

<h2>When the situation is critical</h2>

<p>Some warning signs that need urgent help:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Thoughts of suicide or self-harm</li>
  <li>Borrowing or stealing from others</li>
  <li>Imminent loss of housing or job</li>
  <li>Unable to pay essential bills</li>
  <li>Significant relationship breakdown</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these are present, call the Gambling Helpline (0800 654 655) or 1737, or your GP. In a crisis call 1737 or 111. Free emergency budget advice is available from financial mentors, contactable via the helpline or <a href="https://www.fincap.org.nz/">fincap.org.nz</a>.</p>

<h2>For family members</h2>

<p>Living with someone who gambles problematically is exhausting. The pattern that helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Get support yourself - the helpline supports affected family members too, free</li>
  <li>Protect your own finances - separate accounts where possible</li>
  <li>Avoid bailing them out repeatedly - well-intentioned, often counterproductive</li>
  <li>Encourage them toward professional help; do not try to be the only support</li>
  <li>Look after children in the household - explain age-appropriately, do not pretend everything is fine</li>
  <li>Look after your own mental health</li>
</ul>
