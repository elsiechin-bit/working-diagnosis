<p>Acute neck pain (wry neck, cervical strain) is very common and usually develops suddenly - often after an awkward movement, poor posture, or tension. Although it can be painful and limiting, most people recover well with self-care within days to weeks. This handout covers what you can do at home and when to see your doctor.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sharp or stiff pain on one side of the neck, sometimes with restriction of movement</li>
  <li>Muscle tightness and spasm, which may make turning your head painful</li>
  <li>Pain that may travel into the shoulder or upper back</li>
  <li>Symptoms that often worsen with certain head positions or movements</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why this happens</h2>

<p>Acute neck pain is usually caused by muscle strain or minor irritation of the joints between the neck vertebrae. This triggers protective muscle spasm, which makes movement painful. Despite how uncomfortable it feels, underlying damage is usually minimal, and the body's natural healing process is very effective. Most acute neck pain resolves completely within 1 to 4 weeks.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Keep moving.</strong> Although it feels counterintuitive, gentle movement and activity speeds recovery better than complete rest. Avoid sudden jerky movements, but gentle neck stretching and normal daily activities are encouraged. Start moving as soon as pain allows.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply heat or ice.</strong> Some people find heat (hot shower, heat pack) helpful for muscle tightness; others prefer ice in the first 24 hours. Try both and use whichever feels better. There is no single "correct" option.</li>
  <li><strong>Adjust your posture.</strong> If your pain was triggered by poor posture or desk work, take frequent breaks, keep your screen at eye level, and avoid cradling your phone between your ear and shoulder.</li>
  <li><strong>Take simple pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol (1000 mg every 6 hours) or ibuprofen (400 - 600 mg every 6 - 8 hours) can help you stay active by managing pain. Take them regularly rather than waiting until pain is severe.</li>
  <li><strong>Try gentle stretching.</strong> Once acute pain settles (usually within a few days), gentle neck stretches help restore movement. Slowly and carefully tilt your ear towards your shoulder, hold for 15 - 30 seconds, and repeat several times daily.</li>
  <li><strong>Neck pillows are optional.</strong> A properly supporting pillow (not too high, not too flat) can help, but there is no evidence that special neck pillows speed recovery more than a regular pillow.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief is often all you need. Paracetamol and ibuprofen are equally effective; choose based on what suits you and whether ibuprofen is safe for you (avoid if you have a history of stomach ulcers or kidney problems). Muscle relaxants are sometimes prescribed by GPs for acute neck strain, but evidence for their benefit is modest, and they can cause drowsiness.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is severe or worsening despite self-care after 3 - 5 days</li>
  <li>You develop pins and needles, numbness, or weakness in your arms or hands</li>
  <li>You have difficulty swallowing, hoarseness, or headache with vision changes</li>
  <li>You have fever or feel systemically unwell</li>
  <li>The pain follows significant trauma or a fall</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of these apply, contact your GP or call 111 for advice. Imaging (X-rays or MRI) is rarely needed for simple acute neck pain; your doctor will examine you and advise based on your symptoms.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Is imaging needed, or can I manage this with self-care at home?</li>
  <li>When should I be concerned that this is not improving?</li>
  <li>Would physiotherapy help once acute pain settles?</li>
</ul>
