<p>Fibromyalgia is a chronic condition characterised by widespread musculoskeletal pain, fatigue, and often sleep problems. It is not a progressive disease and does not cause permanent joint damage, but it can significantly affect quality of life. The good news is that self-management strategies, exercise, and appropriate medicines can help reduce symptoms and improve function.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Widespread musculoskeletal pain and aching across the body</li>
  <li>Persistent fatigue that does not improve with rest</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance - unrefreshing sleep, difficulty falling or staying asleep</li>
  <li>Cognitive difficulties (brain fog, memory problems, poor concentration)</li>
  <li>Tender points on examination</li>
  <li>Often accompanied by mood changes (anxiety or depression)</li>
  <li>Symptoms fluctuate and may worsen with stress, poor sleep, or increased activity</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Fibromyalgia involves changes in how your nervous system processes pain signals, resulting in amplified pain sensation. This is not psychological, though stress and poor sleep can worsen symptoms. The exact cause is not fully understood, but factors include genetics, physical trauma, infections, and psychological stress. It affects women more than men.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Gradual, consistent exercise.</strong> Regular physical activity is one of the most effective treatments. Start gently (walking, swimming, tai chi) and progress gradually. Exercise should be low-impact and paced to avoid post-exertional worsening. Even 10 - 15 minutes daily helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Improve sleep.</strong> Establish a consistent bedtime routine, keep your room cool and dark, avoid screens before bed, and limit caffeine. Sleep medications may be needed short-term to break the sleep-pain cycle.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress.</strong> Stress and emotional strain worsen symptoms. Mindfulness, relaxation techniques, or cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) can help.</li>
  <li><strong>Pace your activities.</strong> Avoid boom-bust cycles (overactivity followed by crashes). Keep activity consistent day-to-day, and build in rest periods even if you feel able to do more.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain good posture and ergonomics.</strong> Adjust your workspace and sleeping position to reduce physical strain.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep a symptom diary.</strong> Track what worsens or improves your symptoms. This helps identify patterns and adjust your management accordingly.</li>
  <li><strong>Join a support group.</strong> Connecting with others who have fibromyalgia provides validation and practical tips.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines that help</h2>

<p>Pain relief options include paracetamol or low-dose NSAIDs for pain. Some people benefit from low-dose antidepressants (like amitriptyline) which help both pain and sleep. Pregabalin (Lyrica) is specifically approved for fibromyalgia and helps reduce pain in some people. Your GP will work with you to find what works best with the fewest side effects.</p>

<h2>What does not help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Bed rest - this worsens deconditioning and fatigue</li>
  <li>Avoiding all activity due to fear of symptom worsening</li>
  <li>Waiting for a cure before starting rehabilitation</li>
  <li>High-dose opioid painkillers - these are ineffective and carry addiction risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You suspect you have fibromyalgia (diagnosis requires careful assessment)</li>
  <li>Your symptoms are worsening or not improving with self-management</li>
  <li>You develop red flags (fever, weight loss, progressive weakness) that suggest another condition</li>
  <li>You feel depressed or anxious and need support</li>
  <li>You need help adjusting medicines or dosing</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Fibromyalgia is a long-term condition, but most people improve with consistent self-management. Symptoms fluctuate and may worsen with stress or life changes, but they often improve with time and appropriate strategies. Recovery is gradual and may take months, but many people achieve significant improvement in pain and function.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>How is fibromyalgia diagnosed, and what tests are needed?</li>
  <li>Which medicines are most likely to help my symptoms?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist or pain specialist?</li>
  <li>Is cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) available?</li>
  <li>What can I do to prevent symptom flares?</li>
</ul>
