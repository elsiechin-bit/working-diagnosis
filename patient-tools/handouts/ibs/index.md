Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) is a common gut disorder where bowel function is upset without any visible damage on tests. Around 1 in 7 New Zealanders has IBS at some point. Symptoms can be miserable, but with the right combination of diet, stress management, and sometimes medications, most people get back to a comfortable baseline.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Abdominal pain or discomfort, often related to bowel habits</li>
<li>Change in bowel habits - constipation, diarrhoea, or alternating</li>
<li>Bloating and wind</li>
<li>Sense of incomplete emptying</li>
<li>Mucus in stool</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms come and go and often vary with stress, food, and life events.</p>

<h2>Three main subtypes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>IBS-C</strong> - constipation predominant</li>
<li><strong>IBS-D</strong> - diarrhoea predominant</li>
<li><strong>IBS-M</strong> - mixed bowel habit</li>
</ul>

<h2>What IBS is not</h2>

<p>IBS does not damage the bowel and does not turn into cancer or inflammatory bowel disease. The diagnosis is based on typical symptoms once serious conditions have been excluded.</p>

<h2>Red flags - see your GP</h2>

<p>Symptoms that need investigation before assuming IBS:</p>

<ul>
<li>Onset over age 50</li>
<li>Blood in the stool</li>
<li>Unintentional weight loss</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Family history of bowel cancer or inflammatory bowel disease</li>
<li>Persistent diarrhoea, especially at night</li>
<li>Anaemia</li>
<li>Sudden change in bowel habit</li>
</ul>

<h2>Investigations</h2>

<p>Your GP usually checks:</p>

<ul>
<li>Full blood count and CRP</li>
<li>Coeliac antibodies (tTG)</li>
<li>Faecal calprotectin (rules out inflammatory bowel disease)</li>
<li>Stool culture if recent diarrhoea</li>
<li>Thyroid function</li>
<li>Sometimes colonoscopy if red flags</li>
</ul>

<p>If these are normal and symptoms fit, IBS is the diagnosis.</p>

<h2>Self-management - the foundations</h2>

<p><strong>Eat regularly.</strong> Don't skip meals. Eat slowly, sitting down. Eat in calm settings.</p>

<p><strong>Reduce trigger foods.</strong> Common ones:</p>

<ul>
<li>Caffeine</li>
<li>Alcohol</li>
<li>Spicy or fatty foods</li>
<li>Carbonated drinks</li>
<li>Artificial sweeteners (sorbitol, mannitol)</li>
<li>Some fibres - especially insoluble like wheat bran in IBS-D, but soluble fibre (oats, psyllium) often helps</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Drink enough water.</strong> 1.5-2 litres a day, especially with a high-fibre diet.</p>

<p><strong>Exercise regularly.</strong> Walking, yoga, swimming all help gut motility and stress.</p>

<p><strong>Manage stress.</strong> Mindfulness, CBT, gut-directed hypnotherapy all have good evidence for IBS.</p>

<h2>The low-FODMAP diet</h2>

<p>FODMAPs are short-chain carbohydrates that ferment in the gut, causing bloating and pain in some people. The low-FODMAP diet involves:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Elimination phase</strong> - cut high-FODMAP foods (some onions, garlic, wheat, certain fruits, certain vegetables, lactose, some legumes) for 4-6 weeks</li>
<li><strong>Reintroduction</strong> - test each FODMAP group methodically</li>
<li><strong>Personalisation</strong> - find your tolerance levels for each</li>
</ol>

<p>The full benefit comes from doing all three phases, ideally with a registered dietitian. Around 50-75% of people with IBS get substantial improvement.&sup1;</p>

<p>Avoid staying on a strict elimination phase indefinitely - it can be nutritionally restrictive. The Monash University FODMAP app is the best practical guide.</p>

<h2>Medications</h2>

<p>Targeted by symptom:</p>

<p><strong>For pain and bloating:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Antispasmodics (mebeverine, hyoscine) - take 30 minutes before meals</li>
<li>Peppermint oil capsules - good evidence</li>
<li>Tricyclic antidepressants in low dose (amitriptyline 10-25 mg at night) - very effective for IBS pain, helps sleep</li>
<li>SSRIs sometimes used</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>For diarrhoea:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Loperamide (Imodium) for occasional use - especially before social events or work</li>
<li>Bile acid binders (cholestyramine) for some patients</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>For constipation:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Soluble fibre (psyllium, ispaghula) - start low, increase slowly</li>
<li>Macrogol (osmotic laxative)</li>
<li>Linaclotide (specialised IBS-C medicine; private)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Probiotics</strong> - mixed evidence; some strains may help. Multi-strain products taken consistently for 4-12 weeks are reasonable to trial.</p>

<h2>Mind-gut therapies</h2>

<p>The gut and brain are deeply connected. Several psychological approaches have strong evidence:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Cognitive behavioural therapy</strong> targeted at IBS - effective</li>
<li><strong>Gut-directed hypnotherapy</strong> - 6-10 sessions; remarkable effect for many</li>
<li><strong>Mindfulness-based stress reduction</strong></li>
<li><strong>Apps</strong> like Nerva (gut-directed hypnotherapy) and Mahana</li>
</ul>

<p>None of this means "it's all in your head" - the gut-brain axis is biology, and treatments that target it work biologically.</p>

<h2>Living with IBS</h2>

<ul>
<li>Plan ahead for travel and events - know where toilets are</li>
<li>Pack emergency kit - loperamide, baby wipes, change of underwear</li>
<li>Communicate with employer if needed - flexible toilet access usually helps</li>
<li>Be patient - finding what works takes weeks to months of trial and error</li>
</ul>
