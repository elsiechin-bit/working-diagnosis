<p>Gastroenteritis - vomiting, diarrhoea, or both - is one of the most common illnesses in young children. In NZ, most cases are caused by viruses (rotavirus, norovirus, adenovirus) and resolve within 3-7 days. The main risk is dehydration. With careful fluid management at home, most children recover without needing medical care. This handout walks through the practical steps and the signs that need a doctor.</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>A typical course:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Vomiting starts first, often dramatically. Lasts 24-48 hours.</li>
  <li>Diarrhoea follows. Up to 10-15 watery motions a day at peak.</li>
  <li>Fever may come and go.</li>
  <li>Tummy pain, off food, miserable.</li>
  <li>Diarrhoea typically lasts 5-7 days, sometimes 2 weeks.</li>
</ul>

<p>The vomiting is usually scarier than the diarrhoea but settles faster. The diarrhoea is what tends to drain a child of fluids.</p>

<h2>Keeping your child hydrated - the most important thing</h2>

<p>Fluids are the treatment for almost every routine case of gastro. Antibiotics, anti-vomiting medicines, and most over-the-counter remedies are usually unhelpful or harmful. The aim is small, frequent fluids that the gut can absorb without provoking another vomit.</p>

<h3>What to use</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Oral rehydration solution (ORS)</strong> - Pedialyte, Gastrolyte, Hydralyte. The gold standard. Designed to balance fluid, salt, and sugar in the right ratios for absorption. Available in pharmacies and supermarkets. Sachets to mix or ready-to-drink.</li>
  <li><strong>Water</strong> - fine for older children with mild illness</li>
  <li><strong>Breast milk</strong> - keep going. Continue offering breastfeeds at usual frequency or more.</li>
  <li><strong>Formula</strong> - keep going at usual strength. Do not dilute.</li>
  <li><strong>Diluted juice or sports drinks</strong> - acceptable if ORS not available; dilute apple juice 1:1 with water</li>
</ul>

<p>Avoid: full-strength juice, fizzy drinks, sports drinks neat - the high sugar concentration can worsen diarrhoea.</p>

<h3>How to offer fluids during vomiting</h3>

<p>The trick is small amounts often. The stomach can absorb a teaspoon every 5 minutes when it would reject a whole cup. Specifically:</p>

<ul>
  <li>5ml (one teaspoon) every 5 minutes for 15-30 minutes</li>
  <li>If they keep that down, double it - 10ml every 5 minutes</li>
  <li>Build up gradually as tolerance returns</li>
  <li>If they vomit, wait 30 minutes and start again from teaspoon size</li>
</ul>

<p>This is tedious. It works. Set a timer if you need to. Persistence matters more than the volume per dose.</p>

<h2>Food</h2>

<p>Once vomiting settles (12-24 hours of keeping fluids down), reintroduce food when your child is hungry. There is no need for the old "BRAT diet" (bananas, rice, apple, toast). The current advice is to offer a normal, age-appropriate diet:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Continue breastfeeds throughout</li>
  <li>Continue formula at full strength</li>
  <li>Toast, plain biscuits, crackers, plain pasta or rice, banana, mashed potato, soup - whatever they will eat</li>
  <li>Avoid very fatty or very sugary foods for the first day or two</li>
  <li>Restart usual diet within 24-48 hours</li>
</ul>

<h2>Warning signs of dehydration</h2>

<p>The single most important parental skill in gastro is recognising dehydration. See your GP urgently or go to ED if your child has any of:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dry mouth, no tears when crying</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Sunken eyes</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Sunken fontanelle</strong> (the soft spot on top of a baby's head)</li>
  <li><strong>Fewer wet nappies than usual</strong> - less than 4 wet nappies in 24 hours in babies, no wee for 8-12 hours in older children</li>
  <li><strong>Very dark or strong-smelling urine</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Lethargy, drowsy, hard to wake, floppy</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Cold or mottled hands and feet</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Fast breathing or fast heart rate</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Skin that stays "tented" when gently pinched</strong> (loss of skin elasticity)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Other reasons to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Baby under 6 months with vomiting and diarrhoea - lower threshold</li>
  <li>Vomiting that is bright green (bilious) - urgent</li>
  <li>Severe abdominal pain, especially in the right lower abdomen (consider appendicitis)</li>
  <li>Blood in the diarrhoea - more than a streak</li>
  <li>Swollen, tense, or unusually painful tummy</li>
  <li>Diarrhoea lasting more than 7 days</li>
  <li>Recent overseas travel - some causes need specific treatment</li>
  <li>Recent antibiotics - rule out C. difficile</li>
  <li>Vomiting without diarrhoea, especially with headache or different behaviour - could be more than gastro</li>
</ul>

<h2>What about anti-vomiting medicines?</h2>

<p>Ondansetron (a single dose dissolving wafer) is sometimes prescribed in NZ EDs and GP clinics for children over 6 months whose vomiting is preventing them keeping any fluid down. It is effective and reduces hospital admission rates. It is not a routine first-line at home but worth knowing about - if you have struggled with the small-sips approach for several hours, it is reasonable to ask your GP or after-hours service whether ondansetron would help.</p>

<p>Anti-diarrhoeal medicines (loperamide) are not recommended for children under 12 with infectious diarrhoea.</p>

<h2>Preventing spread</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Handwashing with soap and water (not alcohol gel - less effective against norovirus and rotavirus) after every nappy change, after toileting, before food</li>
  <li>Keep your child off daycare or school until 48 hours after the last vomit or diarrhoea episode</li>
  <li>Disinfect surfaces and toys that get into mouths</li>
  <li>Adults caring for sick children get sick themselves often. Plan accordingly.</li>
  <li>The rotavirus vaccine in the NZ schedule has substantially reduced severe rotavirus gastro since 2014.</li>
</ul>
