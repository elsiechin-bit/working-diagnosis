<p>Until recently, loneliness was treated as a private feeling rather than a health issue. The evidence has shifted. Long-running studies now show that being lonely or socially isolated is associated with about a 30% increase in early mortality - effect sizes comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day, and larger than those for obesity or low physical activity<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>. The biology is plausible: chronic loneliness raises cortisol, alters immune function, and disturbs sleep, on top of the obvious downstream effects on diet, movement, and substance use. None of this means loneliness is a personal failing. It is a feeling, and a signal, like hunger.</p>

<h2>Loneliness is not the same as being alone</h2>

<p>Plenty of people live alone and feel deeply connected; plenty of people are surrounded by others and feel acutely lonely. The thing that makes loneliness loneliness is the gap between the connection you want and the connection you have. That gap can come from quantity (not enough contact), quality (contact that does not feel safe or seen), or context (a recent move, bereavement, retirement, separation, having a baby, being a young carer).</p>

<h2>Who is most affected in Aotearoa</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Young adults (15 to 24)</strong> - consistently the loneliest age group in NZ wellbeing data, contrary to the older stereotype<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Older adults living alone</strong>, especially after a partner has died or moved into care</li>
  <li><strong>Recent migrants and refugees</strong>, particularly in the first 2 to 3 years</li>
  <li><strong>Solo parents</strong>, particularly with very young tamariki and limited respite</li>
  <li><strong>People with disabilities or chronic illness</strong> that reduce mobility</li>
  <li><strong>Rangatahi questioning sexuality or gender</strong> in unsupportive environments</li>
  <li><strong>People grieving</strong> - the hardest months are often months 3 to 9, after the formal support has thinned out</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<p>The honest answer is that small, regular doses of low-effort connection do most of the work. Not friendship retreats; not big new initiatives. The aim is to push the threshold of "minimum viable contact" above zero on most days.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>One person, regularly.</strong> Pick one person you already like and message or call them weekly. Predictable beats spontaneous. Frequency beats depth.</li>
  <li><strong>One regular thing, weekly.</strong> A class, a kapa haka group, a marae event, a club, a shared volunteer slot, a walking group, a board games night. A standing commitment removes the activation energy.</li>
  <li><strong>Move your meeting outdoors</strong> where you can. Walking conversations are easier than sit-down ones for many people, and you get exercise and time outside as a bonus.</li>
  <li><strong>Lean on weak ties.</strong> The barista, the librarian, the dog walker you nod at - "weak ties" research shows these brief, friendly recognitions reliably lift mood. They are not substitutes for close ties; they are part of the diet.</li>
  <li><strong>Reach into your community.</strong> If you have lost touch, marae, churches, sports clubs, neighbourhood groups, parents' coffee groups, libraries, and Men's Sheds are how Aotearoa rebuilds connection.</li>
  <li><strong>Accept that loneliness can be lying.</strong> When you are deeply lonely, your nervous system reads social risk everywhere - other people seem judgemental, exhausting, or not worth bothering. The bias resolves with contact, not with rumination.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If a particular life event has hit you</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bereavement:</strong> Skylight NZ and Hospice NZ both run free grief support. Skylight has tamariki resources too.</li>
  <li><strong>Recent move or migration:</strong> local libraries, English Language Partners, and community centres are the lowest-friction starting points.</li>
  <li><strong>New parents:</strong> Plunket, kohanga reo, Pasifika Plunket, and your nearest playgroup. Postnatal depression and anxiety are common and very treatable - see your GP if it is not lifting.</li>
  <li><strong>After retirement:</strong> SeniorNet, Age Concern, U3A, Probus, and most councils run "active ageing" programmes.</li>
  <li><strong>Rangatahi feeling alone or unsafe:</strong> Youthline 0800 376 633, Outline 0800 688 5463 for rainbow rangatahi.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Loneliness has lasted months and you have lost interest in things that used to feel good</li>
  <li>You are sleeping poorly, drinking more, or struggling at work because of how you are feeling</li>
  <li>You are caring for someone full-time and have lost most of your own social contact</li>
  <li>You have thoughts of harming yourself or feel unsafe</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are in crisis or having thoughts of suicide, call <strong>1737</strong> (free, 24/7, phone or text), <strong>Lifeline 0800 543 354</strong>, or go to your nearest emergency department.</p>
