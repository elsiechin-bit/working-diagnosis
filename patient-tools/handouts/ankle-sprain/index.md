<p>Ankle sprains are one of the most common lower limb injuries. While they can be painful and frustrating, most ankle sprains heal well with appropriate care, activity modification, and rehabilitation exercises. This handout covers what helps recovery and when to see a doctor.</p>

<h2>What happens with an ankle sprain</h2>

<p>An ankle sprain occurs when you twist or turn your ankle awkwardly, stretching or tearing the ligaments that hold your ankle joint together. Most sprains affect the outer (lateral) ligaments. Sprains range from mild (small tears with minimal swelling) to severe (complete ligament rupture). Even mild sprains can feel unstable and painful, but they respond well to rehabilitation.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Immediate pain on the outer side of the ankle</li>
  <li>Swelling that develops within hours</li>
  <li>Bruising, often appearing a day or two later</li>
  <li>Difficulty or pain with weight-bearing</li>
  <li>Feeling of ankle instability or "giving way"</li>
  <li>Pain with certain movements (inversion - turning the sole of your foot inward)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Immediate care (first 48 hours)</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest and immobilisation.</strong> Avoid walking on the injured ankle. Use crutches if needed to keep weight off the foot.</li>
  <li><strong>Ice.</strong> Apply ice packs for 15 - 20 minutes, several times daily for the first 48 hours to reduce swelling.</li>
  <li><strong>Compression.</strong> Wrap the ankle firmly with an elastic bandage or compression stocking to limit swelling.</li>
  <li><strong>Elevation.</strong> Keep your foot raised above heart level as much as possible to reduce swelling.</li>
  <li><strong>Pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen helps manage pain and inflammation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Recovery and rehabilitation</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Return to activity gradually.</strong> After 48 hours, gentle movement (within pain limits) promotes healing. Avoid the movement that caused the injury, but otherwise move your ankle gently.</li>
  <li><strong>Use an ankle support or brace.</strong> A lace-up ankle brace or strapping provides support and confidence for weight-bearing. Many people benefit from bracing for 4 - 6 weeks.</li>
  <li><strong>Do balance and proprioception exercises.</strong> Once swelling settles, exercises like standing on one leg, balance board work, or wobble cushion training rebuild stability and prevent recurrent sprains. These are crucial for full recovery.</li>
  <li><strong>Strengthen calf and foot muscles.</strong> Once acute pain settles, gentle calf raises, toe curls, and resistance band exercises rebuild strength.</li>
  <li><strong>Gradually return to sport or high-demand activities.</strong> Return should progress: walking, then light jogging, then sport-specific movements. This typically takes 4 - 12 weeks depending on sprain severity.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing recurrent sprains</h2>

<p>Recurrent sprains are common if the ankle is not properly rehabilitated. Completing balance training, maintaining strength, wearing supportive shoes, and being cautious on uneven surfaces all help prevent re-injury. Once you have sprained your ankle, you are at higher risk of spraining it again unless rehabilitation is thorough.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You cannot bear weight at all or pain is severe</li>
  <li>Swelling is extreme or not improving after 48 hours</li>
  <li>You suspect a broken bone (severe pain, inability to move foot, severe swelling)</li>
  <li>Your ankle feels unstable even with careful movement</li>
  <li>Symptoms are not improving after 2 - 3 weeks despite rest and care</li>
  <li>You are having recurrent sprains</li>
</ul>

<h2>Imaging and diagnosis</h2>

<p>Most ankle sprains do not need X-rays; diagnosis is based on history and examination. X-rays are needed if you cannot bear weight at all or your doctor suspects a fracture. Ultrasound or MRI are occasionally used if your doctor is concerned about a complete ligament rupture or other structural damage, but this is uncommon in straightforward sprains.</p>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most ankle sprains heal within 4 - 12 weeks. The key is early rest and ice to control swelling, followed by progressive rehabilitation with balance and strength training. Recurrence is common in those who do not fully rehabilitate, so completing exercises is important even after pain settles.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I need an X-ray?</li>
  <li>What type of brace or support should I use?</li>
  <li>When can I start bearing weight?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist for balance training?</li>
  <li>When can I return to sport or my usual activities?</li>
</ul>
