A cough lasting more than 8 weeks counts as chronic. It is one of the most common reasons for GP visits and one of the most frustrating - both for patients and clinicians. The causes are usually treatable, but identifying the right one takes patience and sometimes a few rounds of trial treatment.

<h2>The four most common causes</h2>

<p>About 90% of chronic cough in non-smokers fits one of four diagnoses, often in combination:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Postnasal drip syndrome</strong> (upper airway cough syndrome) - mucus from the nose dripping down the back of the throat</li>
<li><strong>Asthma</strong> - including cough-variant asthma where cough is the only symptom</li>
<li><strong>Reflux (GORD)</strong> - stomach acid irritating the throat and lower airways</li>
<li><strong>ACE inhibitor cough</strong> - a side effect of cilazapril, perindopril, lisinopril, etc.</li>
</ol>

<p>For smokers, chronic bronchitis (smokers' cough) and COPD are common.</p>

<h2>Postnasal drip</h2>

<p>Clues:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sensation of mucus dripping down the throat</li>
<li>Frequent throat clearing</li>
<li>Cough worse on lying down</li>
<li>Hay fever symptoms</li>
<li>Cobblestone appearance of the back of the throat</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment: nasal saline rinses, intranasal steroid spray (fluticasone, mometasone) for 4-6 weeks, antihistamine if allergic. See our <a href="/handouts/allergic-rhinitis/">hayfever handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Asthma (cough-variant)</h2>

<p>Clues:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cough worse at night or early morning</li>
<li>Triggered by exercise, cold air, laughter</li>
<li>Family history of asthma or allergy</li>
<li>Variable peak flow</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment: trial of inhaled steroid (fluticasone, budesonide) for 6-8 weeks. Spirometry can confirm the diagnosis. See our <a href="/handouts/asthma-adults/">asthma handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Reflux (GORD)</h2>

<p>Clues:</p>

<ul>
<li>Heartburn or acid taste, but often silent</li>
<li>Cough worse after meals or lying down</li>
<li>Sour taste, hoarse voice in the morning</li>
<li>Worse with rich, fatty, or large meals</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment: weight loss if overweight, avoid late meals, raise head of bed. Trial of proton pump inhibitor (omeprazole, pantoprazole) for 8-12 weeks. See our <a href="/handouts/reflux-gord/">reflux handout</a>.</p>

<h2>ACE inhibitor cough</h2>

<p>Clues:</p>

<ul>
<li>You take cilazapril, perindopril, lisinopril, ramipril, enalapril, captopril, or similar</li>
<li>Dry, tickly cough, often within months of starting (can be later)</li>
<li>Often worse at night</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment: switch to an ARB (candesartan, losartan, valsartan) - same class of action, no cough side effect. Discuss with your GP. The cough usually settles within 2-4 weeks of stopping.</p>

<h2>Other causes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Smoking</strong> - the cough usually settles months after quitting</li>
<li><strong>COPD</strong> - especially with sputum and breathlessness; spirometry confirms</li>
<li><strong>Bronchiectasis</strong> - chronic productive cough, often with infections; needs CT</li>
<li><strong>Whooping cough</strong> (pertussis) - prolonged barking cough lasting weeks, often with vomiting after coughing fits</li>
<li><strong>Lung cancer</strong> - rare but important. New persistent cough especially in smokers needs chest X-ray</li>
<li><strong>Tuberculosis</strong> - rising in NZ; cough plus weight loss, night sweats, fever</li>
<li><strong>Heart failure</strong> - cough with breathlessness on exertion or lying down, ankle swelling</li>
<li><strong>Habit cough or chronic refractory cough</strong> - some cough persists after the trigger has been treated. Specialist input may be needed</li>
</ul>

<h2>Investigations</h2>

<p>Your GP may arrange:</p>

<ul>
<li>Chest X-ray - the first investigation in any chronic cough</li>
<li>Spirometry</li>
<li>Bloods - full blood count, CRP, sometimes IgE</li>
<li>Trial of treatment for the most likely cause</li>
<li>Referral to respiratory or ENT specialist if cough persists despite reasonable treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Red flags - see your GP urgently</h2>

<ul>
<li>Coughing up blood</li>
<li>Unintentional weight loss</li>
<li>Chest pain</li>
<li>Severe breathlessness</li>
<li>Fever lasting more than a few days</li>
<li>Persistent hoarseness for more than 3 weeks</li>
<li>Difficulty swallowing</li>
<li>New cough in a smoker over 50</li>
<li>Night sweats</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps - any chronic cough</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking and avoid second-hand smoke</li>
<li>Treat any nasal congestion with saline rinse</li>
<li>Stay well hydrated</li>
<li>Avoid known triggers (perfumes, cleaning chemicals, dusty environments)</li>
<li>Honey can soothe (over age 1)</li>
<li>Throat lozenges (older than 4)</li>
<li>Speech therapy - cough suppression techniques work for many</li>
</ul>

<h2>The frustrating reality</h2>

<p>Most chronic cough has a treatable cause, but finding it can take 2-3 trial treatments over weeks to months. Persistence pays off. Keep notes of what helps and what does not - this guides the next step.</p>

<h2>Newer treatments</h2>

<p>For refractory chronic cough where standard treatments fail, newer agents (gefapixant, low-dose morphine) have been studied. These are mostly specialist-level and not all available in NZ.</p>
