<p>Glaucoma is a group of conditions in which the optic nerve - the cable connecting the eye to the brain - is gradually damaged, usually because the pressure inside the eye is too high for that particular nerve. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide and affects around 2 percent of New Zealand adults over 40, with prevalence rising sharply with age.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The reason it is sometimes called the silent thief of sight is the central problem of glaucoma: the most common type causes no pain, no redness, and no early visual symptoms, because peripheral vision is lost first and the brain quietly fills in the gap. By the time you notice, you have lost something that does not come back.</p>

<h2>The two main types</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Primary open-angle glaucoma.</strong> By far the most common. Painless, gradual, and usually picked up at a routine optometry check rather than because the patient noticed anything. Both eyes are usually affected, though often unequally.</li>
  <li><strong>Acute angle-closure glaucoma.</strong> A medical emergency. The drainage angle of the eye closes suddenly, the pressure rises rapidly, and the eye becomes red, painful, and the vision blurred or haloed. Often associated with nausea and vomiting. This needs assessment in an emergency department or ophthalmology service the same day.</li>
</ul>

<p>There are other less common forms (normal-tension glaucoma, congenital, secondary glaucomas from injury, steroids, or other eye disease) but the principles below mostly apply to the primary open-angle type.</p>

<h2>Who is at higher risk</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Age over 40, with risk rising every decade</li>
  <li>A first-degree relative with glaucoma (risk roughly four to nine times baseline)<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup></li>
  <li>High short-sightedness (myopia)</li>
  <li>Diabetes</li>
  <li>Long-term steroid use, including some inhalers and creams used for years</li>
  <li>Previous eye injury or surgery</li>
  <li>Certain ethnicities have higher rates of specific glaucoma types - your optometrist will factor this in</li>
</ul>

<h2>Screening in New Zealand</h2>

<p>There is no national glaucoma screening programme. Most glaucoma in NZ is picked up by optometrists during routine eye checks. The advice from Glaucoma NZ is for everyone over 45 to have an eye check at least every five years, and those with a family history or other risk factors to start earlier and check more often.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> An optometrist visit for glaucoma typically includes a pressure check, optic nerve examination, visual field test, and a scan of the optic nerve (OCT). It takes about 30 to 45 minutes and is usually booked privately - costs vary by region.</p>

<h2>Symptoms to know about</h2>

<p>Most open-angle glaucoma is symptomless until late. The visual field defect typically begins as a small blind area to one side, expands slowly, and only becomes obvious to the patient when it is well advanced. Acute angle-closure glaucoma is the opposite - sudden severe eye pain, blurred vision, halos around lights, headache, and nausea, in one eye. If that picture appears, get to an emergency department or call <strong>111</strong>.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>The aim of treatment is to lower the pressure in the eye to a level that protects the optic nerve from further damage. Glaucoma cannot be reversed, but with consistent treatment most people keep useful vision for life.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Eye drops.</strong> First-line for most people. Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost, bimatoprost) are the most commonly used and many are PHARMAC-funded.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> Drops are often lifelong - missing doses lets pressure rise.</li>
  <li><strong>Selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT).</strong> A 5-minute laser procedure that improves drainage. Now often offered as a first-line alternative to drops, with comparable effectiveness.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup> SLT is available in NZ public and private services.</li>
  <li><strong>Surgery.</strong> For glaucoma that progresses despite drops and laser. Trabeculectomy and minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS) are the main options.</li>
</ul>

<p>Acute angle-closure glaucoma is treated urgently with intensive pressure-lowering medication and a small laser hole in the iris (laser iridotomy) to relieve the angle closure. The other eye is usually treated with the same laser to prevent the same thing happening on that side.</p>

<h2>Living with glaucoma</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Take your drops every day, on time.</strong> Set a phone alarm if needed. Pressure does not stay low if drops are missed.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell your GP and other prescribers you have glaucoma.</strong> Some medicines (some antidepressants, some asthma inhalers, some blood pressure tablets, certain steroids) can affect pressure or interact with glaucoma drops.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep optometry and ophthalmology appointments.</strong> Visual field testing and optic nerve imaging track progression in a way you cannot self-assess.</li>
  <li><strong>Driving.</strong> Significant visual field loss can affect your fitness to drive; your specialist will let you know if and when this becomes relevant.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor or optometrist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are over 45 and have not had an eye check in the last five years</li>
  <li>You have a parent, sibling, or child with glaucoma</li>
  <li>You have noticed any change in your peripheral or side vision</li>
  <li>You are using long-term steroid medication (oral, inhaled, or topical)</li>
</ul>

<p>For a sudden painful red eye with blurred vision, halos, and nausea: get to an emergency department or call <strong>111</strong>.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your eye specialist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What type of glaucoma do I have, and how advanced is it?</li>
  <li>What is my target pressure?</li>
  <li>Are drops or laser the better starting point for me?</li>
  <li>What should my close family do about screening?</li>
</ul>
