Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) is a common condition where the throat repeatedly narrows or closes during sleep, briefly stopping breathing. Each pause ends with a brief arousal that disturbs sleep without you knowing. The effects show up in the day - fatigue, low mood, brain fog, and in the long run higher risk of high blood pressure, stroke, and heart disease. Treatment can transform daily life.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p><strong>Night-time:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Loud snoring (usually first noticed by a partner)</li>
<li>Witnessed pauses in breathing followed by gasping or choking</li>
<li>Restless sleep, frequent waking</li>
<li>Need to pass urine several times</li>
<li>Dry mouth in the morning</li>
<li>Morning headaches</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Day-time:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Excessive daytime sleepiness</li>
<li>Falling asleep watching TV, reading, or in conversation</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating</li>
<li>Memory problems</li>
<li>Irritability or low mood</li>
<li>Reduced sex drive</li>
<li>Sleeping at the wheel - very dangerous</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who is at higher risk?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Overweight or obese - the strongest risk factor</li>
<li>Male (women catch up after menopause)</li>
<li>Age over 40</li>
<li>Thick neck (over 43 cm in men, 38 cm in women)</li>
<li>Maori and Pacific people - higher prevalence</li>
<li>Family history</li>
<li>Crowded throat anatomy (large tonsils, small jaw, set-back jaw)</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Heavy alcohol use, especially evening</li>
<li>Use of sedatives</li>
<li>Hypothyroidism</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>Untreated OSA increases risk of:</p>

<ul>
<li>Hypertension - sometimes resistant to medications</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation</li>
<li>Stroke and heart attack</li>
<li>Type 2 diabetes</li>
<li>Motor vehicle crashes (drowsy driving)</li>
<li>Workplace accidents</li>
<li>Depression</li>
</ul>

<p>The good news: treatment reverses much of this risk.</p>

<h2>How OSA is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Your GP will use a screening questionnaire - the STOP-BANG or Epworth Sleepiness Scale - and may arrange:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Home sleep study</strong> - a portable device worn overnight at home that measures airflow, oxygen, and effort</li>
<li><strong>Hospital sleep study (polysomnography)</strong> - more detailed, in-lab</li>
</ul>

<p>OSA severity is graded by the Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI):</p>

<ul>
<li>Mild: 5-14 events per hour</li>
<li>Moderate: 15-29</li>
<li>Severe: 30 or more</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - lifestyle</h2>

<p>For all OSA, regardless of severity:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Lose weight</strong> if overweight - around 10% loss can cut AHI by 30-50%</li>
<li><strong>Avoid alcohol</strong> within 3-4 hours of bed</li>
<li><strong>Avoid sedatives</strong> (sleeping pills, benzodiazepines, opioids)</li>
<li><strong>Stop smoking</strong></li>
<li><strong>Sleep on your side</strong> - some OSA is positional. A tennis ball sewn into the back of pyjamas, or wedge pillow, can help</li>
<li><strong>Treat hayfever</strong> - blocked nose worsens OSA</li>
<li><strong>Regular sleep schedule</strong> - consistent bed and wake times</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - CPAP</h2>

<p>CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) is a small machine that delivers pressurised air through a mask, splinting the airway open. It is the most effective treatment for moderate to severe OSA.</p>

<p>What to expect:</p>

<ul>
<li>Adjustment period of 1-3 months - many people find it strange initially</li>
<li>Mask choice matters - nasal pillows, nasal mask, or full-face. Try several</li>
<li>Modern machines are quiet and have humidifiers</li>
<li>Most users wear it most nights and feel transformed within weeks</li>
<li>NZ access is mostly through DHB sleep clinics or private providers; cost varies</li>
</ul>

<p>Tips for success:</p>

<ul>
<li>Use it every night including naps</li>
<li>Adjust the mask if leaks or skin irritation</li>
<li>Use the humidifier if your nose dries</li>
<li>Stay engaged with your sleep service - they can adjust pressures and masks</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - mandibular advancement device (MAD)</h2>

<p>A custom mouthpiece, fitted by a dentist, that holds the lower jaw forward. Useful for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Mild to moderate OSA</li>
<li>People who cannot tolerate CPAP</li>
<li>Snoring without significant OSA</li>
</ul>

<p>Cost is around $1500-2500 and not currently funded. May cause jaw discomfort initially.</p>

<h2>Treatment - surgery</h2>

<p>Surgical options exist (uvulopalatopharyngoplasty, tonsillectomy in children with enlarged tonsils, jaw advancement). Reserved for selected patients - ENT or maxillofacial referral if relevant.</p>

<h2>Treatment - newer options</h2>

<p>Hypoglossal nerve stimulators are an emerging option for moderate-severe OSA in selected patients - mostly private and very expensive in NZ.</p>

<p>Some weight-loss medications and surgery can substantially improve OSA in obese patients.</p>

<h2>Driving</h2>

<p>If you are very sleepy or have had near-misses, do not drive until OSA is treated. NZTA requires you to declare OSA on licence renewal. Once treated effectively, driving is safe.</p>

<p>Commercial drivers (heavy vehicle, taxi, bus) need formal CPAP compliance proof to keep licences.</p>

<h2>Children and OSA</h2>

<p>OSA in children is usually due to large tonsils and adenoids. Symptoms include snoring, witnessed pauses, restless sleep, mouth-breathing, bedwetting, daytime tiredness, and behavioural problems sometimes mistaken for ADHD. Tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy resolve most cases.</p>

<h2>Partner's perspective</h2>

<p>Partners often suffer alongside - sleeping in spare rooms, listening to apnoeas, worrying. Effective treatment helps their sleep too.</p>
