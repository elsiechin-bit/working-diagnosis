<p>If you receive healthcare that you believe was not good enough, you have the right to complain. Complaints help healthcare providers improve and protect other patients. This handout explains how to make a complaint and what happens next.</p>

<h2>First step - complain locally</h2>

<p>Start by speaking to the healthcare provider or service involved. This might be your GP, practice nurse, hospital, or health service. You can complain verbally or in writing - in writing is often better so there is a record. Be clear about what went wrong and what you would like to happen (apology, explanation, compensation, service improvement). Many complaints are resolved quickly at this level.</p>

<h2>If the local complaint does not help</h2>

<p>If you are not satisfied with how the local complaint was handled, or if the service does not resolve it, you can complain to the Health and Disability Commissioner (HDC). The HDC is free, independent, and separate from the healthcare service. They investigate complaints fairly and can recommend remedies.</p>

<h2>Making a complaint to the HDC</h2>

<p>You can complain online, by phone, or by mail. There is usually a time limit - you generally must complain within 12 months of the event, though the HDC has discretion to accept complaints outside this timeframe in some circumstances. When you complain, provide as much detail as possible: dates, names of staff involved, what happened, and how it affected you. Attach copies of relevant documents like appointment letters or test results.</p>

<h2>What happens during HDC investigation</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The HDC gathers information.</strong> They ask the healthcare provider for their account of what happened and any relevant documents.</li>
  <li><strong>You get a chance to respond.</strong> The provider's version is shared with you, and you can add more information if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>The HDC may get expert advice.</strong> If your complaint is complex, they may ask another healthcare professional for their opinion on whether care met acceptable standards.</li>
  <li><strong>A decision is made.</strong> The HDC decides whether the provider breached the Code of Consumer Rights. This usually takes 3 - 12 months depending on complexity.</li>
  <li><strong>You get a report.</strong> The HDC sends you a detailed written report with their findings. If they uphold your complaint, they may recommend actions like an apology, compensation, or service changes.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What can the HDC recommend?</h2>

<p>If your complaint is upheld, the HDC can recommend an apology, compensation for emotional harm or loss, or changes to how the service operates to prevent similar problems. The healthcare provider is expected to follow HDC recommendations, though they can disagree with findings. If they do not comply, you have legal options.</p>

<h2>How to prepare your complaint</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Write down what happened.</strong> Include dates, times, and names of staff involved. Be factual and specific - avoid emotional language though it is okay to describe how it affected you emotionally.</li>
  <li><strong>Explain the impact.</strong> Describe how the situation harmed you - physically, emotionally, financially, or in terms of lost opportunities.</li>
  <li><strong>State what you want to happen.</strong> Do you want an explanation, apology, compensation, or changes to services?</li>
  <li><strong>Gather evidence.</strong> Collect appointment letters, medical records, test results, emails, or any documents relevant to your complaint.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep copies.</strong> Make copies of everything you send and keep originals for your own records.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Examples of what can be complained about</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Being treated disrespectfully or your cultural beliefs not being respected</li>
  <li>Not being given information needed to make decisions about your care</li>
  <li>Being denied access to your medical records</li>
  <li>Receiving unnecessary or harmful treatment</li>
  <li>Significant delays in diagnosis or treatment</li>
  <li>Breaches of privacy or confidentiality</li>
  <li>Not being listened to about your symptoms or concerns</li>
  <li>Charges that seem excessive or were not explained upfront</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things that are not usually complaints matters</h2>

<p>The HDC handles complaints about care quality and conduct. They do not usually handle disputes about fees alone (though excessive or non-transparent charges may be part of a broader complaint). Complaints about general unhappiness with a decision, even if you disagree with it, are not usually upheld unless there was a breach of rights. If clinical judgment was involved, the HDC only finds a breach if the provider acted unreasonably.</p>

<h2>After the HDC decision</h2>

<p>If you are not satisfied with the HDC's decision or if the provider does not follow HDC recommendations, you have legal options. You can seek legal advice about taking a claim to the District Court or higher courts, though this involves costs and legal complexity. Some community law centres offer free or low-cost legal advice.</p>

<h2>Support while complaining</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bring a support person.</strong> You can have a friend, family member, or advocate help you make your complaint and deal with the process.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask for help writing.</strong> If English is not your first language or you struggle with writing, ask someone to help you. The HDC can also help communicate your complaint in plain language.</li>
  <li><strong>Access free legal advice.</strong> Community law centres offer free advice about complaints. Contact your local Citizens Advice Bureau for contact details.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask before complaining</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Have I tried complaining to the healthcare provider first?</li>
  <li>Can I explain clearly what went wrong and how it affected me?</li>
  <li>Do I have evidence (documents, records, dates)?</li>
  <li>Is my complaint within 12 months of the event?</li>
  <li>What would I like to happen as a result of my complaint?</li>
</ul>
