Hepatitis C is a viral infection of the liver. For decades it was a difficult condition to live with and treat. Since 2016 things have changed completely - direct-acting antiviral medicines cure over 95% of cases with 8-12 weeks of well-tolerated tablets. Treatment is fully funded in New Zealand. Around 50,000 New Zealanders are estimated to have or have had hepatitis C, but many do not know it. If you are at risk, get tested.

<h2>How it spreads</h2>

<ul>
<li>Blood-to-blood contact - the main route</li>
<li>Sharing needles, syringes, or other drug equipment</li>
<li>Tattoos or piercings done with non-sterile equipment</li>
<li>Blood transfusions before 1992 (when screening began in NZ)</li>
<li>Mother to baby (uncommon, around 5% transmission)</li>
<li>Needlestick injury</li>
<li>Less commonly: sexual transmission, sharing razors or toothbrushes if blood contact</li>
</ul>

<p>Hepatitis C is not spread through casual contact, sneezing, hugging, sharing food, or breastfeeding (unless cracked nipples bleed).</p>

<h2>Who should be tested?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Anyone who has injected drugs, ever - even once, even decades ago</li>
<li>Anyone who received blood products or had medical procedures overseas in countries where blood is not screened</li>
<li>Anyone who received a blood transfusion or organ transplant in NZ before 1992</li>
<li>Tattoos or piercings done in non-regulated settings</li>
<li>People in prison or who have been in prison</li>
<li>Long-term dialysis patients</li>
<li>Healthcare workers with needlestick injuries</li>
<li>Babies of mothers with hepatitis C</li>
<li>Sexual partners of people with hepatitis C, especially if blood contact</li>
<li>Anyone with persistently raised liver enzymes</li>
<li>Anyone wishing to know their status</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ ran screening campaigns to find undiagnosed cases. Free testing is available - no GP visit charge in many regions for hepatitis C testing.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Most people with chronic hepatitis C have no symptoms for years. When symptoms occur:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Vague abdominal discomfort</li>
<li>Joint aches</li>
<li>Mood and concentration problems</li>
<li>Brain fog</li>
</ul>

<p>Late stage (cirrhosis):</p>

<ul>
<li>Jaundice</li>
<li>Swelling of the abdomen or legs</li>
<li>Easy bruising</li>
<li>Confusion</li>
<li>Vomiting blood</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<ol>
<li><strong>Anti-HCV antibody test</strong> - shows if you have ever been exposed</li>
<li><strong>HCV RNA</strong> - if antibody is positive, this confirms current infection. Some people clear the virus naturally and have positive antibody but no RNA</li>
<li><strong>Genotype</strong> - was important historically; less so with modern pan-genotypic treatments</li>
<li><strong>FibroScan</strong> - assesses liver scarring (fibrosis)</li>
<li><strong>Liver function tests</strong> - often mildly raised but not always</li>
<li><strong>Liver ultrasound</strong> if cirrhosis present</li>
</ol>

<h2>Treatment - direct-acting antivirals (DAAs)</h2>

<p>The current NZ-funded options:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Maviret (glecaprevir/pibrentasvir)</strong> - 8 weeks for most people, 16 weeks for cirrhosis</li>
<li><strong>Epclusa (sofosbuvir/velpatasvir)</strong> - 12 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>Both are once-daily tablets. They work for almost all genotypes (pan-genotypic). Cure rates over 95%.</p>

<p>Side effects are usually mild - mild headache, fatigue, nausea. No injections, no interferon, no severe side effects of older treatments.</p>

<h2>Eligibility</h2>

<p>Funded for all New Zealanders with confirmed chronic hepatitis C. No restrictions on disease stage or alcohol/drug use. Treatment can be initiated by GPs or nurse practitioners (with appropriate training) in most NZ regions.</p>

<h2>Cure - what does it mean?</h2>

<p>Cure (sustained virological response, or SVR) is confirmed by an HCV RNA test 12 weeks after finishing treatment. Once cured, the virus is gone for life unless you are re-exposed.</p>

<p>Liver function usually improves substantially. Fibrosis can partly reverse. The risk of liver cancer remains slightly elevated for some years post-cure if cirrhosis is present, so surveillance continues.</p>

<h2>Re-infection</h2>

<p>You do not develop lasting immunity. Re-infection is possible if you are re-exposed. People who have been cured can be re-infected and re-treated.</p>

<h2>Daily life with chronic hepatitis C</h2>

<p>Even before treatment:</p>

<ul>
<li>Avoid alcohol or limit substantially - alcohol speeds liver damage</li>
<li>Maintain healthy weight</li>
<li>Get vaccinated against hepatitis A and B</li>
<li>Be careful with paracetamol; avoid herbs that may be liver-toxic</li>
<li>Tell every healthcare provider</li>
<li>Use condoms with new partners</li>
<li>Do not share razors, toothbrushes, nail clippers</li>
<li>Cover cuts; clean blood spills with bleach</li>
<li>Do not donate blood, organs, or sperm</li>
</ul>

<h2>Drug user-friendly services</h2>

<p>NZ has invested in needle and syringe exchange programmes, with peer-led services in many cities. Treatment can be delivered alongside opioid substitution therapy. Past or current drug use does not prevent treatment.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Pregnant women with hepatitis C are not currently treated during pregnancy (DAAs are not approved in pregnancy). Treatment is given before pregnancy or after breastfeeding ends. Vertical transmission risk is around 5%; babies are tested at 18 months.</p>
