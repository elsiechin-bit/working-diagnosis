<p>Iron deficiency is common, especially in women of childbearing age. It causes fatigue, shortness of breath, and pale skin. Increasing dietary iron, taking supplements if needed, and treating the underlying cause helps most people feel better.</p>

<h2>Types of iron and absorption</h2>

<p>Dietary iron exists in two forms: haem iron (from animal sources) and non-haem iron (from plant sources). Haem iron is absorbed better - about 15 - 35% of dietary haem iron is absorbed. Non-haem iron is absorbed less efficiently (2 - 20%), but absorption improves with vitamin C and decreases with calcium, tea, and coffee. Timing matters: drink tea or coffee after meals, not with them, to improve iron absorption.</p>

<h2>Iron-rich foods</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Good haem iron sources (animal sources - absorbed better):</strong> Red meat (beef, lamb), liver, poultry, fish and shellfish (oysters, mussels, canned fish with bones).</li>
  <li><strong>Plant-based iron sources (absorbed less well but still valuable):</strong> Leafy greens (spinach, silverbeet), legumes (lentils, chickpeas, beans), fortified cereals and bread, nuts and seeds, wholegrain products.</li>
  <li><strong>Iron absorption boosters:</strong> Vitamin C from citrus, berries, tomatoes, capsicum. Eat vitamin C-rich foods with iron-containing meals to boost absorption.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Dietary iron - practical tips</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Include iron at every meal.</strong> Small amounts from multiple sources add up through the day.</li>
  <li><strong>Combine plant and animal sources.</strong> A small amount of meat with legumes and vegetables boosts overall iron absorption.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat vitamin C with iron-rich meals.</strong> Orange juice with breakfast, tomato with lentils, berries with cereal all boost absorption.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid tea and coffee with meals.</strong> These inhibit iron absorption. Drink them after meals instead.</li>
  <li><strong>Cook in cast iron pans.</strong> Some iron leaches into food, especially acidic dishes.</li>
  <li><strong>Include liver occasionally.</strong> Liver is very high in iron (though some people dislike it).</li>
  <li><strong>Choose iron-fortified foods.</strong> Fortified cereals, breads, and plant-based milks add iron to your diet.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Iron supplements</h2>

<p>If dietary iron is not enough, supplements help. Ferrous iron (ferrous sulfate, fumarate, or gluconate) is absorbed better than ferric iron. Take supplements on an empty stomach or with vitamin C for best absorption. Side effects (constipation, nausea, dark stools) are common. Taking every other day rather than daily sometimes reduces side effects while maintaining effectiveness. Liquid supplements may be better tolerated than tablets.</p>

<h2>Daily iron requirements</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Adult women (18 - 50 years): 18 mg daily</li>
  <li>Adult women (over 50 years): 8 mg daily</li>
  <li>Adult men: 8 mg daily</li>
  <li>Pregnant women: 27 mg daily</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you have symptoms of anaemia (fatigue, shortness of breath, pale skin, dizziness)</li>
  <li>To check your iron levels if you are fatigued</li>
  <li>If you are struggling to absorb iron (such as with coeliac disease or inflammatory bowel disease)</li>
  <li>If iron supplements are not helping</li>
  <li>To identify the cause of iron deficiency (heavy periods, gut bleeding, etc.)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I have iron deficiency anaemia?</li>
  <li>What is causing my iron deficiency?</li>
  <li>Do I need iron supplements?</li>
  <li>Which iron supplement should I take?</li>
  <li>What side effects should I expect?</li>
  <li>How often should I have my iron levels checked?</li>
</ul>
