<p>Endometriosis is a chronic condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows in places it should not - usually in the pelvis, sometimes elsewhere. It affects roughly one in ten women of reproductive age in NZ.[1] On average, women report symptoms for 7-9 years before being diagnosed - a delay that often involves seeing multiple doctors and being told the pain is normal. It is not. This handout is a starting point: what we know, what helps, and where to push back when needed.</p>

<h2>What endometriosis is</h2>

<p>Endometrial-like tissue can grow on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, ligaments holding the uterus in place, the bowel, the bladder, the abdominal wall - occasionally even in the lung or diaphragm. This tissue still responds to monthly hormonal cycles, swelling and bleeding into surrounding tissues. Because there is no exit (unlike the uterus), this causes inflammation, scarring (adhesions), and pain.</p>

<p>The exact cause is not fully understood. Theories include retrograde menstruation (some menstrual blood flows backward through the tubes), embryonic cells that develop incorrectly, and immune system factors. There is a genetic component - having a first-degree relative with endometriosis raises your risk.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Symptoms vary enormously. Two people can have the same severity on imaging and very different experiences. Common features:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Painful periods</strong> - the pain is typically worse than the period pain of friends, often disabling. Painkillers do not always help.</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic pain at other times of the cycle</strong> - around ovulation, with bowel motions, with sex (deep dyspareunia), or just constant background pain</li>
  <li><strong>Heavy or irregular bleeding</strong> in some women</li>
  <li><strong>Bowel symptoms</strong> - cyclical bloating, constipation, diarrhoea, painful bowel motions especially around periods (often misdiagnosed as IBS)</li>
  <li><strong>Bladder symptoms</strong> - frequency, urgency, painful urination, especially around periods</li>
  <li><strong>Fatigue</strong> - chronic, often disproportionate to other symptoms</li>
  <li><strong>Difficulty conceiving</strong> - around 30-50% of women with endometriosis experience subfertility</li>
</ul>

<p>What is not endometriosis: mild period pain that responds to ibuprofen, isolated heavy bleeding without pain, occasional cramps. Plenty of women have annoying but normal periods.</p>

<h2>Why diagnosis takes so long</h2>

<p>Several reasons:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms are often dismissed as "just bad periods"</li>
  <li>No simple test - no blood test, ultrasound is helpful but not definitive (only finds endometriomas, not implants)</li>
  <li>The only definitive diagnosis is surgical (laparoscopy with biopsy) - which is invasive, has waitlists, and is reserved for severe cases</li>
  <li>Symptoms can mimic IBS, urinary tract problems, musculoskeletal pain, leading to detours through other specialties</li>
  <li>Variable severity means some women have classic findings, others have minimal visible disease but severe pain</li>
</ul>

<p>Internationally, average time to diagnosis is 6-9 years. In NZ, the picture is similar.[1]</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>The current approach in NZ is:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Clinical history</strong> with a high index of suspicion - menstrual pain plus any of: dyspareunia, dyschezia (painful bowel motions), dysuria with periods, infertility, family history</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic exam</strong> - may reveal tenderness, fixed retroverted uterus, palpable nodules</li>
  <li><strong>Transvaginal ultrasound</strong> - can find ovarian endometriomas and severe rectovaginal nodules; cannot rule out endometriosis if normal</li>
  <li><strong>MRI</strong> - useful for deep infiltrating endometriosis, surgical planning</li>
  <li><strong>Trial of empirical hormonal treatment</strong> - relatively new approach, increasingly accepted: if symptoms fit, start hormonal treatment without waiting for surgical confirmation</li>
  <li><strong>Diagnostic laparoscopy</strong> - direct visualisation, with biopsy and excision of disease where possible. Considered the gold standard but reserved for cases where empirical treatment fails or fertility is an issue</li>
</ol>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>There is no cure. The goal is symptom management, fertility preservation where relevant, and prevention of progression.</p>

<h3>Pain relief</h3>
<ul>
  <li>NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, mefenamic acid) - most effective taken before pain starts and continued through period. Mefenamic acid (Ponstan) is particularly useful for period pain</li>
  <li>Paracetamol - works for some, in combination with NSAIDs</li>
  <li>TENS machines, heat packs, magnesium - some women find these helpful</li>
  <li>Stronger pain relief (codeine, tramadol) - sometimes needed but with caution about long-term use</li>
</ul>

<h3>Hormonal treatment</h3>
<p>Suppressing the menstrual cycle reduces inflammation in endometriotic tissue and slows progression.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Combined pill, used continuously (no pill-free week)</strong> - first-line for many. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Progestogen-only options</strong> - mini-pill, dienogest (Visanne, NZ-funded since 2024 for endometriosis), depot injection, hormonal IUD (Mirena, fully funded)</li>
  <li><strong>GnRH analogues</strong> (goserelin, leuprorelin) - cause temporary medical menopause. Reserved for severe cases or surgical planning. NZ-funded with Special Authority.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Surgery</h3>
<p>Laparoscopic excision of endometriotic tissue, removal of endometriomas, division of adhesions. Symptom relief in around 70% in the short term, but recurrence is common (around 50% within 5 years). Excision (cutting out) is preferred over ablation (burning off) for deep disease. Best done in centres with surgeons experienced in endometriosis.</p>

<h3>Fertility</h3>
<p>If you are trying to conceive: surgical treatment improves natural conception rates in moderate disease. IVF may be appropriate. Endometriosis NZ has a useful section on fertility planning. Discuss with your GP, fertility specialist, or gynaecologist.</p>

<h3>Multidisciplinary care</h3>
<p>For severe or persistent symptoms, the most effective care combines: gynaecology, pelvic floor physiotherapy, pain medicine specialist, dietitian (for the bowel symptom overlap), psychologist or counsellor (for the long-term impact). Some NZ centres now have dedicated endometriosis clinics that provide this.</p>

<h2>What to do if you are not being heard</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Track your symptoms - a 3-month diary of pain, bleeding, what affects work and relationships, what painkillers you have tried</li>
  <li>Bring this to your appointment</li>
  <li>Ask specifically: "Could this be endometriosis?" - the question itself often shifts the conversation</li>
  <li>Request referral to gynaecology if symptoms have not improved on first-line hormonal treatment after 3-6 months</li>
  <li>Endometriosis NZ has a directory of clinicians with particular interest in the condition</li>
  <li>Get a second opinion if needed</li>
</ul>

<h2>Living with it</h2>

<p>Beyond the medical, the practical:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Talk to your employer if symptoms affect work - many endometriosis patients benefit from workplace adjustments. The NZ Endometriosis NZ "Listen to Your Body" toolkit has guidance.</li>
  <li>Diet - while there is no "endo diet" with strong evidence, anti-inflammatory eating patterns help some people. Worth a structured trial with a dietitian rather than DIY elimination.</li>
  <li>Exercise - regular gentle activity is associated with better symptom control. Yoga, walking, swimming for many.</li>
  <li>Mental health - chronic pain conditions are associated with depression and anxiety. The link goes both ways. Treat both if both are present.</li>
  <li>Community - Endometriosis NZ runs support groups; online communities can be valuable but vary in quality.</li>
</ul>
