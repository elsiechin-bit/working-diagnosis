<p>Croup is the dramatic 2am cough that takes new parents by surprise. The classic story: a child who was perfectly fine at bedtime wakes a few hours later barking like a seal, with a strained inward gasp on every breath. Parents understandably go straight to the emergency department. The good news is that most croup is mild, treatable with a single dose of steroid, and over within a few days. This handout walks through what is happening and what to do.</p>

<h2>What croup is</h2>

<p>Croup is a viral infection (most often parainfluenza virus) that causes swelling in the upper airway, particularly the larynx and trachea. The narrowing of the airway produces:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>A barking, seal-like cough</strong> - unmistakable once you have heard it</li>
  <li><strong>A hoarse voice</strong> - sometimes barely audible</li>
  <li><strong>Stridor</strong> - a high-pitched harsh noise on breathing IN, caused by air rushing through the narrowed airway</li>
  <li>A few days of cold-like symptoms beforehand in most cases</li>
  <li>Often worse at night, often dramatically</li>
</ul>

<p>Most children with croup are aged 6 months to 3 years. Older children sometimes get it but the airway is wider so symptoms tend to be milder.</p>

<h2>What to do tonight</h2>

<h3>Stay calm</h3>
<p>The single most useful thing you can do. Croup sounds terrifying. Most children become more distressed when their parent is panicking, and the increased agitation tightens the airway further.</p>

<h3>Sit your child up</h3>
<p>On your lap, leaning gently forward. Open clothing around the neck. Cuddle.</p>

<h3>Cool, moist air</h3>
<p>Take them outside if it is cool. Or open a freezer briefly. Or sit with them in the bathroom with the cold tap running. Cool air shrinks the swelling slightly. (Steamy bathroom is a popular old remedy but the evidence does not support it; cool air is more likely to help.)</p>

<h3>Fluids and paracetamol if uncomfortable</h3>
<p>Standard supportive care.</p>

<h3>Watch the breathing</h3>
<p>What you are watching for is whether the airway is narrowing or settling. Most cases settle in 30-60 minutes with calm and cool air.</p>

<h2>When to call 111</h2>

<p>Get urgent help if your child has any of these:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stridor at rest</strong> - the harsh inward noise on breathing in, even when calm</li>
  <li><strong>Working hard to breathe</strong> - chest sucking in, ribs visible on each breath, nostrils flaring, head bobbing</li>
  <li><strong>Pale, blue-tinged, or grey</strong> - especially around lips and fingertips</li>
  <li><strong>Drooling, unable to swallow</strong> - in croup this is unusual; consider epiglottitis (rare since Hib vaccination but possible)</li>
  <li><strong>Drowsy, floppy, hard to wake</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Drooling and very unwell</strong> - especially with high fever and a sicker-looking child</li>
  <li><strong>Severe distress not settling within 20-30 minutes of being upright in cool air</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Phone Healthline 0800 611 116 (24/7) for guidance if you are unsure but the child is not in extremis.</p>

<h2>What ED or your GP will do</h2>

<p>The standard treatment for croup is a single dose of an oral steroid - usually dexamethasone 0.6mg/kg, occasionally prednisolone. It works by reducing the airway swelling. Effects start within 1-2 hours and last around 2-3 days. Almost every child with anything more than mild croup gets one dose.[1]</p>

<p>For more severe cases, nebulised adrenaline gives rapid relief while the steroid is taking effect. This is given in ED, not at home.</p>

<p>Antibiotics do not help. Croup is viral.</p>

<h2>What to expect over the next few days</h2>

<ul>
  <li>The barking cough usually peaks on day 2-3 and settles by day 4-5</li>
  <li>Symptoms tend to be worse at night for the duration</li>
  <li>Mild stridor with crying or activity (but not at rest) is reasonably common in the recovery phase</li>
  <li>Some children have several mild croup episodes per winter; recurrent severe croup is worth discussing with your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is probably <em>not</em> a problem</h2>

<ul>
  <li>The barking cough on its own, without working hard to breathe</li>
  <li>Stridor only when the child is upset or active, settling when calm</li>
  <li>Mild fever, runny nose, off food</li>
  <li>A second mild night a week later</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP later</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Recurrent croup (more than 2-3 episodes a year)</li>
  <li>Suspected post-croup cough lingering more than 2 weeks</li>
  <li>Stridor between episodes (not just during illness) - needs ENT assessment</li>
  <li>Croup in an infant under 6 months - lower threshold to seek same-day review</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common questions</h2>

<h3>Is it croup or asthma?</h3>
<p>Asthma causes wheeze, which is a noise on breathing OUT. Croup causes stridor, which is a noise on breathing IN, and a barking cough. They are different conditions, treated differently. If you are unsure, see your GP or call Healthline.</p>

<h3>Will it happen again tomorrow night?</h3>
<p>It might. Croup typically peaks on the second night. Steroids reduce the chance of significant recurrence, but a milder repeat is common.</p>

<h3>Can I prevent croup?</h3>
<p>Not easily. Handwashing and the usual virus precautions help marginally. There is no vaccine for parainfluenza viruses. Some children just seem to be prone.</p>

<h3>Should I get the humidifier out?</h3>
<p>The evidence does not support steam treatment for croup, and there is a small burn risk with hot-mist humidifiers. A cool-mist humidifier is fine but not specifically helpful. Cool outside or freezer-cool air is more likely to help during the acute episode.</p>
