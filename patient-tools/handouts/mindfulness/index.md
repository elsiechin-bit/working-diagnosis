<p>Mindfulness has been overhyped, oversold, and turned into a wellness brand. The underlying practice, stripped of all that, is genuinely useful for some specific things. The evidence base is reasonable but more modest than many headlines suggest. This handout is the practical version: what it actually is, what it does and does not do, and how to start without spending money or buying anything.</p>

<h2>What mindfulness actually is</h2>

<p>Two-line definition: paying attention to what is happening in the present moment, on purpose, without immediately judging or trying to change it. That is it.</p>

<p>The skill is two parts:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Noticing</strong> - what is happening in body, mind, surroundings</li>
  <li><strong>Returning</strong> - when attention drifts (which it always does), gently bringing it back</li>
</ul>

<p>The "non-judging" part matters because the brain's automatic response to most things is evaluation - good, bad, like, dislike, threat, opportunity. Mindfulness practices the alternative: noticing without immediately doing anything about it.</p>

<h2>What the evidence supports</h2>

<p>Reasonable evidence:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stress reduction</strong> - mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) reduces self-reported stress and physiological stress markers in healthy adults[1]</li>
  <li><strong>Recurrent depression prevention</strong> - mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT) reduces relapse in people who have had three or more depressive episodes, comparable to maintenance antidepressants[2]</li>
  <li><strong>Chronic pain</strong> - reduces pain interference (how much pain affects function), though not always pain intensity</li>
  <li><strong>Anxiety</strong> - moderate effects, useful especially as adjunct to other treatment</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep</strong> - mindfulness-based interventions help insomnia, though specific CBT-I has stronger evidence</li>
</ul>

<p>Weaker or contradictory evidence:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Workplace productivity claims - mostly oversold</li>
  <li>Cognitive enhancement in healthy adults - small effects, not the dramatic claims</li>
  <li>Specific physical health outcomes (cardiovascular, immune) - mixed</li>
  <li>Treatment for severe mental illness as a primary intervention - not appropriate</li>
</ul>

<h2>What it is NOT</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Religion (though it has Buddhist origins, the secular practice is not religious)</li>
  <li>Emptying your mind - impossible, not the goal</li>
  <li>Always feeling calm - mindfulness includes noticing distress, not eliminating it</li>
  <li>A cure for severe mental illness - can be helpful adjunct but not primary treatment</li>
  <li>A solution to actual problems in your life - paying attention to a difficult relationship does not fix the relationship</li>
  <li>Something that works through one session - the benefit comes from regular practice over weeks</li>
</ul>

<h2>What can go wrong</h2>

<p>Worth knowing because it is rarely discussed in the marketing material. A minority of practitioners experience adverse effects:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Increased anxiety in some people, especially with longer or more intense practice</li>
  <li>Re-experiencing of trauma in those with PTSD - mindfulness needs adapting (or avoiding) for active trauma</li>
  <li>Worsening of dissociation in some people with dissociative tendencies</li>
  <li>Triggering of psychotic symptoms in those with vulnerability</li>
  <li>Spiritual or existential distress in long retreats</li>
</ul>

<p>If you have significant trauma, dissociative symptoms, or psychosis history, talk to a mental health professional before starting an intensive practice.</p>

<h2>How to start - the simplest possible practice</h2>

<p>You do not need an app, a guru, a cushion, or 30 minutes a day. The minimum useful dose is around 5-10 minutes most days.</p>

<h3>The basic exercise</h3>

<ol>
  <li>Sit comfortably. Eyes closed or softly focused on a point.</li>
  <li>Notice your breath - the feeling of air at the nostrils, or the rise and fall of your chest. Pick one.</li>
  <li>When your mind wanders (it will, every few seconds at first), notice that it has wandered, and bring attention back to the breath. No judgement.</li>
  <li>Repeat for 5-10 minutes.</li>
</ol>

<p>That is the practice. The goal is not to keep attention on the breath - that is impossible. The goal is to notice when attention has wandered and return it. The "noticing and returning" IS the practice. Mind wandering is not failure; it is the opportunity.</p>

<h3>Variants worth trying</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Body scan</strong> - moving attention slowly through different parts of the body, noticing sensations</li>
  <li><strong>Walking meditation</strong> - attention to the feet, the contact with the ground, the rhythm of walking</li>
  <li><strong>Sounds</strong> - noticing whatever sounds are present without trying to identify or interpret them</li>
  <li><strong>Eating</strong> - one piece of fruit, eaten very slowly, noticing taste, texture, temperature</li>
  <li><strong>Three-breath check-in</strong> - any time during the day, three slow breaths with full attention. Particularly useful before stressful events.</li>
</ul>

<h2>For specific purposes</h2>

<h3>Stress and overwhelm</h3>
<p>The "noticing and returning" muscle is what helps. The 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique is a quick mindfulness exercise: 5 things you see, 4 you touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.</p>

<h3>Anxiety</h3>
<p>Mindfulness teaches the difference between a thought and reality. "I am having the thought that something terrible will happen" is different from "something terrible is about to happen". Anxiety often runs on a confusion of the two. Mindfulness creates space for the distinction.</p>

<h3>Depression - particularly recurrent</h3>
<p>MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy) is structured for this. Available as 8-week courses in NZ through some psychologists and mindfulness centres. Reduces relapse rate in those with prior depression.</p>

<h3>Pain</h3>
<p>The technique is not to escape pain but to relate to it differently. Pain has two components - the sensation and the suffering about the sensation. Mindfulness reduces the second more reliably than the first.</p>

<h3>Sleep</h3>
<p>Mindfulness done at bedtime as a wind-down can help, though CBT-I is more specifically effective for chronic insomnia. The body scan in particular can be useful for sleep.</p>

<h2>NZ options</h2>

<h3>Free</h3>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.melon.org.nz/">Mentemia / Melon</a> - free NZ wellbeing app with mindfulness practices</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.smallsteps.org.nz/">Small Steps</a> - free NZ mental wellbeing tools, brief mindfulness practices</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/">Just A Thought</a> - free NZ online CBT, includes mindfulness elements</li>
  <li>YouTube has many free guided practices - search "mindfulness for beginners" or specific teachers (Tara Brach, Jon Kabat-Zinn, Mark Williams)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Apps (paid)</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Insight Timer - free with paid premium; large free library</li>
  <li>Headspace, Calm - subscription apps, polished, structured courses</li>
  <li>Waking Up - more secular, contemplative tradition</li>
</ul>

<h3>Structured courses</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>MBSR (Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction)</strong> - 8-week structured course, originally developed by Jon Kabat-Zinn. Available in main NZ centres - around NZ$400-700. Strongest evidence base.</li>
  <li><strong>MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy)</strong> - 8-week course specifically for recurrent depression. Some psychologists in NZ offer it; some DHBs.</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.mindfulnessworks.com/">Mindfulness Works NZ</a> - 4-week introductory courses across NZ, low cost</li>
</ul>

<h3>For Maori</h3>
<p>Some kaupapa Maori mindfulness approaches connect mindfulness with whakapapa, te ao Maori, and tikanga - distinct from secular Western adaptations. Worth seeking these out if more aligned to your worldview.</p>

<h2>NZ resources</h2>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.melon.org.nz/">Melon</a> - free wellbeing app</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.mindfulnessworks.com/">Mindfulness Works NZ</a> - low-cost intro courses</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.mentalhealth.org.nz/">Mental Health Foundation NZ</a> - mindfulness resources</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.healthify.nz/health-a-z/m/mindfulness/">Healthify NZ - Mindfulness</a></li>
  <li>Your GP can refer to MBCT or other mindfulness-based therapies if you have recurrent depression</li>
</ul>
