Eating well on real terms

Good nutrition is not about restriction, perfection, or expensive superfoods. It is about sustainable patterns that support energy, mood, and long-term health. Here is what the evidence actually shows.

What does a good diet actually look like? ›

Focus on patterns rather than individual foods or nutrients. The evidence consistently supports diets that emphasise: plenty of vegetables and fruit (at least 5 servings daily), wholegrains rather than refined grains, legumes (lentils, chickpeas, beans), moderate amounts of fish and lean meat, dairy or calcium-fortified alternatives, nuts and seeds, and limited ultra-processed foods.

The Mediterranean-style diet and the DASH diet both have strong evidence for reducing cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality.1,4 No single "superfood" can substitute for an overall good dietary pattern. The quality of what you eat most of the time matters far more than occasional indulgences.

Ultra-processed foods: what they are and why they matter ›

Ultra-processed foods (UPFs) are industrial formulations made from extracted or refined food substances, with additives including preservatives, emulsifiers, artificial flavours, and colours. Examples: packaged snacks, most breakfast cereals, soft drinks, flavoured yoghurts, instant noodles, commercially produced bread.

They now account for the majority of energy intake in many high-income countries including NZ.2 Research shows higher UPF consumption is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, depression, and all-cause mortality, even when calorie intake is matched.3 The mechanism is not fully understood but likely involves disrupted satiety signals, additive combinations, and displacement of whole foods.

The most impactful change many NZ families can make is reducing consumption of sugar-sweetened drinks (including juice), takeaway meals, and packaged snack foods, while increasing home cooking.

Eating well on a budget ›

Healthy eating does not require expensive products. The most nutritious and cost-effective foods include: dried lentils and chickpeas, eggs, canned fish (sardines, tuna), frozen vegetables (nutritionally equivalent to fresh), oats, seasonal local produce, plain yoghurt.

Expensive items often marketed as "superfoods" (acai, goji berries, spirulina) offer no meaningful advantage over cheaper whole foods. A banana provides similar micronutrients to a goji berry at a fraction of the cost.

Meal planning and cooking at home are the highest-leverage changes most people can make to both cost and nutrition. Even simple meals, cooked from basic ingredients, are typically more nutritious and cheaper than takeaway.

Common nutrition myths addressed ›

Several widely believed nutrition claims are not well supported by evidence:

  • Low-fat foods are healthier. Low-fat products often contain added sugar to compensate. Total dietary fat is less important than fat type (avoid trans fats, favour unsaturated).
  • You need to eat protein immediately after exercise. The so-called "anabolic window" is smaller than commonly claimed. Total daily protein intake matters more than precise timing.
  • Detox diets cleanse your body. The liver and kidneys perform continuous detoxification. No food or drink meaningfully accelerates this.
  • Carbohydrates are bad. Wholegrains, legumes, and fruit are all carbohydrate-containing foods with strong evidence of benefit. It is refined and processed carbohydrates that warrant reduction.
  • Gluten-free is healthier for everyone. Only relevant for people with coeliac disease or non-coeliac gluten sensitivity. Gluten-free processed foods are often lower in fibre and higher in sugar and fat than their conventional equivalents.
NZ-specific considerations ›

NZ has higher rates of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease in Maori and Pacific populations, driven by complex socioeconomic and food environment factors, not individual choice. Traditional Maori and Pacific foods (kaimoana/seafood, kumara, taro, breadfruit) are generally nutritious and worth incorporating into your regular diet if accessible.

The NZ Eating and Activity Guidelines (2020) are freely available from the Ministry of Health and provide practical, evidence-based guidance tailored to the NZ food environment and population.

Consider seeing a GP or dietitian if you
  • Have type 2 diabetes or are at high risk (review your diet with professional support)
  • Have had unexplained weight loss
  • Experience symptoms that may be related to diet (bloating, fatigue, anaemia)
  • Are pregnant or planning pregnancy (folic acid, iodine requirements change)
