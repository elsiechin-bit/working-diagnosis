The medicines do most of the work, but daily habits make a real difference. Watching your weight, looking after your fluid balance, exercising within your limits, and acting early on warning signs can keep you out of hospital.

<h2>Weigh yourself every morning</h2>

<p>Sudden weight gain is almost always fluid, not fat. The kilos appear faster than fat ever could. Aim to weigh:</p>

<ul>
<li>Same time each day (best: after waking and toileting, before breakfast)</li>
<li>Same scales</li>
<li>Similar clothing (or none)</li>
</ul>

<p>Write the number down or note it on your phone. Look at the trend.</p>

<p><strong>Action thresholds (typical):</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Up 1 kg in a day or 1.5 kg over two days - watch closely, reduce salty foods, see if it settles</li>
<li>Up 2 kg in three days - contact your GP or heart failure nurse the same day</li>
<li>Up 2 kg with breathlessness or new ankle swelling - same-day medical review</li>
</ul>

<p>Your team may give you a written "fluid action plan" with personal cut-offs and instructions about adjusting your diuretic.</p>

<h2>Fluid and salt</h2>

<p><strong>Fluid:</strong> Most people with heart failure should aim for 1.5 to 2 litres of fluid per day. This includes everything liquid - tea, coffee, soup, milk on cereal, ice blocks. In hot weather or with vomiting/diarrhoea, the limit is sometimes loosened. Ask your team for your personal target.</p>

<p><strong>Salt:</strong> Aim for under 5 g (about a teaspoon) per day total. Most salt comes from packaged foods - bread, processed meats, sauces, takeaways - rather than the salt shaker. Read labels: aim for less than 400 mg sodium per 100 g. Cooking from scratch, even simply, is the easiest way.</p>

<p>Avoid salt substitutes that are high in potassium (NoSalt, LoSalt) - they can interact with heart failure medicines.</p>

<h2>Exercise</h2>

<p>Regular activity strengthens the heart and improves quality of life. The aim is gentle, regular, and sustainable - not intense.</p>

<ul>
<li>Walking, swimming, gentle cycling, gardening</li>
<li>Build up gradually - even 10 minutes a day to start is worthwhile</li>
<li>Aim for 150 minutes per week of moderate activity (when you can talk but not sing)</li>
<li>Stop and rest if breathless, dizzy, or chest pain</li>
</ul>

<p>Cardiac rehabilitation is a free, supervised programme that takes the guesswork out of starting. Ask for a referral.</p>

<h2>The medicines - a few practical notes</h2>

<p><strong>Take them every day, even when you feel well.</strong> The medicines are working precisely because you feel well.</p>

<p><strong>Sit up before standing</strong> if you take a beta blocker or ACE inhibitor and feel light-headed. Get out of bed slowly.</p>

<p><strong>Keep up your potassium foods</strong> (banana, orange, kumara, fish) if you take a loop diuretic - and have your bloods checked when asked.</p>

<p><strong>Avoid anti-inflammatories</strong> (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen) - they cause fluid retention and worsen kidney function. Paracetamol is fine.</p>

<p><strong>Vaccinations:</strong> annual flu, COVID-19 boosters as recommended, and pneumococcal vaccine. These reduce admission risk.</p>

<h2>Mood and sleep</h2>

<p>Depression and anxiety are common and worsen outcomes. Tell your GP if you feel persistently low.</p>

<p>Sleeping on extra pillows, or waking short of breath, are warning signs - tell your team.</p>

<p>Sleep apnoea (loud snoring, daytime sleepiness, witnessed pauses) is common and treatable. Ask about a sleep study.</p>

<h2>Sex and intimacy</h2>

<p>Most people with stable heart failure can have sex safely. If you can climb two flights of stairs without trouble, sex is unlikely to strain the heart.</p>

<p>Common concerns: tiredness, breathlessness, side effects of medicines (some lower libido or affect erections). Talk to your GP - simple changes often help.</p>

<p>Important: do not take erectile dysfunction medicines (sildenafil, tadalafil) within 24 hours of GTN spray.</p>

<h2>Travel</h2>

<p>Most travel is fine when stable. Ask your GP for a medical letter listing your medicines. Carry these in hand luggage. On long flights, stand and walk every couple of hours.</p>

<p>If you use oxygen, contact the airline at least two weeks ahead.</p>

<h2>When to call</h2>

<p>Call your GP or heart failure nurse the same day for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden weight gain (above)</li>
<li>New ankle swelling spreading up the leg</li>
<li>Increasing breathlessness with normal activities</li>
<li>Waking at night gasping for breath</li>
<li>New cough that brings up frothy or pink-tinged sputum</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe breathlessness at rest</li>
<li>Chest pain</li>
<li>Fainting</li>
<li>Confusion or feeling very unwell</li>
</ul>
