<p>Stress is the body's response to demand - it is normal, useful in short bursts, and built into how we are wired. The problem is not stress itself but stress that does not switch off: financial pressure, caregiving, work that never finishes, grief, illness, broken sleep, an unsafe relationship. When the system stays "on" for weeks or months, it starts to show up as poor sleep, fatigue, headaches, gut symptoms, irritability, low mood, and reduced concentration. The strategies in this handout are not about being calm in the face of bad circumstances. They are about giving your body and mind the recovery time the wiring expects.</p>

<h2>Stress, anxiety, depression - what is the line?</h2>

<p>Stress is a response to a real external pressure that usually eases when the pressure does. Anxiety is a state of worry that persists, often without an obvious external trigger, and tends to come with physical symptoms (racing heart, dread, restlessness). Depression is a more pervasive state of low mood, loss of interest, and reduced energy lasting two weeks or more. The boundaries are blurry, and stress can tip into either. The practical question is whether something is recoverable when the trigger passes (more likely stress) or whether it has taken on a life of its own (more likely anxiety or depression). If in doubt, see your GP.</p>

<h2>What helps - the strategies with the strongest evidence</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sleep.</strong> Short or broken sleep is the single biggest amplifier of stress. Protect a consistent sleep window before doing anything else - see <a href="/lifestyle-prevention/sleep/">Sleep</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Movement.</strong> Regular physical activity reduces stress, anxiety, and depressive symptoms with effect sizes comparable to many medications<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>. The dose is moderate exercise on most days; intensity matters less than consistency.</li>
  <li><strong>Social contact.</strong> Loneliness is itself a chronic stressor. A short call or walk with someone you trust does measurable things to your nervous system. See <a href="/handouts/social-connection/">Social connection</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Mindfulness and breathing practice.</strong> Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) and similar structured programmes have moderate evidence for reducing stress and anxiety<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>. Even 5 to 10 minutes of slow nasal breathing - in for 4, out for 6 - genuinely shifts the autonomic nervous system in the moment.</li>
  <li><strong>Time outdoors.</strong> Even short walks in green or blue space (parks, beach, ngahere) lower cortisol and improve mood; the effect does not require an alpine summit.</li>
  <li><strong>Limits on stimulants and depressants.</strong> Caffeine after midday wrecks sleep for many people; alcohol fragments sleep and worsens mood the next day. Both are worth experimenting with.</li>
  <li><strong>Saying no, and saying it earlier.</strong> Most overload is downstream of overcommitment. Ask: what would I say no to next week if I had to drop one thing?</li>
  <li><strong>Time off screens.</strong> Especially work email and social media. Pre-sleep is the high-yield window.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does not help, despite popular belief</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Doomscrolling</strong> - news cycling without action increases stress without giving you any control to act on it</li>
  <li><strong>Drinking to relax</strong> - alcohol is sedating in the moment but raises baseline anxiety the next day</li>
  <li><strong>Pushing through</strong> when sleep, food, and connection are slipping - the deficit compounds</li>
  <li><strong>One-off "wellness" activity</strong> - regular smaller doses of recovery beat occasional spa weekends</li>
</ul>

<h2>Workplace stress and burnout</h2>

<p>Burnout has three components: emotional exhaustion, cynicism toward work, and reduced sense of effectiveness. Genuine burnout responds poorly to time-management apps and well to changes in workload, role, or boss. If you can, raise it with your manager or your EAP (most large NZ employers fund free counselling - your HR team can tell you how to access it). If the workplace is the problem, sometimes the answer is the workplace.</p>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms have lasted more than 2 to 4 weeks and are not lifting</li>
  <li>You are sleeping badly most nights, or sleeping a lot but feeling unrefreshed</li>
  <li>You have lost interest in things that usually feel good</li>
  <li>You are using alcohol, food, or screens to manage in a way you would rather not</li>
  <li>Your mood, anxiety, or coping is affecting your work, your tamariki, or your relationships</li>
  <li>You are having thoughts of harming yourself or are not safe</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are in crisis or having thoughts of suicide, call <strong>1737</strong> (free, 24/7, phone or text), <strong>Lifeline 0800 543 354</strong>, or go to your nearest emergency department.</p>

<p>Your GP can refer to short-term funded talking therapy through several NZ pathways (Access and Choice, brief intervention, Te Whatu Ora primary mental health), and many practices have a stop-and-listen with a HIP (health improvement practitioner) or health coach available the same day.</p>
