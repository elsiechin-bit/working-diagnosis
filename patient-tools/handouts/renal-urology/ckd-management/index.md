## Chronic Kidney Disease – Management

Management of chronic kidney disease focuses on slowing progression and reducing cardiovascular risk through tight blood pressure control, optimal glycaemic management, and cardioprotective medications. Early intervention in CKD G3-G5 prevents or delays progression to kidney failure requiring dialysis.


## Blood pressure management

**Target blood pressure**
Target is <120/90 mmHg for all CKD patients (KDIGO 2021 guidelines). Intensive BP control (SBP 120–129 mmHg) slows CKD progression and reduces cardiovascular events.

**ACE inhibitor or ARB (first-line)**
ACE inhibitors (ramipril 2.5–5 mg daily, lisinopril 10–20 mg daily) or ARBs (losartan 50–100 mg daily, valsartan 80–160 mg daily) are standard first-line. Both reduce glomerular hypertension, decrease proteinuria, and slow eGFR decline independent of systemic BP reduction. Start low dose and titrate to full dose over 1–2 weeks. Monitor serum creatinine and potassium 1–2 weeks after initiation; mild eGFR drop (5–10%) is expected and does not indicate harm. Hyperkalaemia is common; monitor potassium frequently in advanced CKD.

Do not combine ACE/ARB with direct renin inhibitor (aliskiren). Dual blockade (ACE + ARB) is not recommended. Use with caution or avoid if potassium persistently >5.5 mmol/L or eGFR falls acutely >20%.

**Add-on antihypertensives**
Diuretic (amlodipine 5 mg daily or hydrochlorothiazide 12.5 mg daily) if BP inadequate on ACE/ARB alone. Diuretics also reduce oedema if present. Calcium channel blocker (amlodipine preferred in renal disease). Beta-blocker (atenolol, carvedilol) for additional benefit if CVD or heart failure present. Alpha-blocker not recommended as first-line in CKD.

## Glycaemic management in diabetes

**HbA1c target**
Target HbA1c 7% (53 mmol/mol) for most CKD + diabetes patients. Relax target to 8% (64 mmol/mol) in advanced CKD (G4-G5) to reduce hypoglycaemia risk and mortality. Very tight control (<7%) does not provide additional benefit and increases hypoglycaemia risk.

**SGLT2 inhibitor**
SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin 10 mg daily, dapagliflozin 10 mg daily) slow CKD progression and reduce cardiovascular events. Benefits accrue independent of glucose lowering; use in all CKD G2-G5 with T2DM (eGFR ≥20 acceptable). Monitor for genital mycotic infection and euglycemic DKA (rare). Contraindicated in CKD G5 pre-dialysis (eGFR <15).

**GLP-1 receptor agonist**
GLP-1RA (semaglutide, dulaglutide, liraglutide) for CKD + T2DM and albuminuria. Slows CKD progression and reduces cardiovascular events. GLP-1RA cause weight loss and improve cardiovascular outcomes; use if obesity or CVD present. Monitor renal function; generally safe in CKD G3-G5.

**Insulin**
Insulin is required if T1DM or T2DM uncontrolled on oral agents. Dose reduction needed as eGFR declines to avoid hypoglycaemia. Insulin clearance decreases in advanced CKD.

**Avoid medication-induced hypoglycaemia**
Reduce sulfonylurea or meglitinide dose in CKD G4-G5 due to reduced drug clearance. Glucose target slightly higher (7.5–8.5%) is safer in dialysis patients.

## Lipid management

**Statin (moderate to high intensity)**
Recommended for all CKD patients age 50–75 or those with diabetes or known CVD. Atorvastatin 20–40 mg daily or rosuvastatin 10–20 mg daily. Statins reduce cardiovascular events and slow CKD progression moderately. Monitor LDL; target LDL <1.8 mmol/L if known CVD or high-risk CKD.

**Ezetimibe**
May be added if LDL remains elevated despite statin.

## Proteinuria reduction

**Non-pharmacological measures**
Dietary protein restriction to 0.6–0.8 g/kg/day reduces proteinuria and may slow progression. Low sodium diet (<2.3 g sodium daily) enhances ACE/ARB effect. Weight loss if obese.

**Pharmacological**
ACE/ARB reduces albuminuria by 30–50% and is cornerstone. SGLT2 inhibitor provides additional reduction. Combination ACE + ARB is not recommended (no additional benefit, increased risk).

## Anaemia management

**Diagnosis and investigation**
Anaemia (Hb <130 g/L men, <120 g/L women) is common in CKD. Investigate iron status: check ferritin, TSAT, transferrin saturation. Goal: ferritin 100–500 µg/L, TSAT 20–50%.

**Iron supplementation**
Iron deficiency is common; oral iron (ferrous sulfate 200 mg TDS) if TSAT <20%. Intravenous iron (ferric carboxymaltose) preferred in dialysis patients for better absorption. Monitor for gastrointestinal side effects.

**Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESA)**
If Hb <100 g/L and eGFR <30 (or on dialysis) despite adequate iron repletion, consider ESA (subcutaneous erythropoietin or darbepoetin). Target Hb 100–120 g/L (avoid >120 g/L due to cardiovascular risk). ESA is expensive; reserve for symptomatic anaemia with documented iron repletion.

**Monitor response**
Assess iron stores, B12, folate, and reticulocyte count if Hb not rising as expected.

## Mineral and bone disease management

**Phosphate management**
Target serum phosphate <1.5 mmol/L in CKD G4-G5. Dietary phosphate restriction (processed foods, cola, dairy). Phosphate binder if dietary measures insufficient: calcium carbonate 1–2 g TDS with meals (caution: increases calcium load, risk of hypercalcaemia and vascular calcification); non-calcium binder (sevelamer, lanthanum) preferred in advanced CKD. Monitor serum calcium (avoid hypercalcaemia).

**Parathyroid hormone (PTH) management**
Secondary hyperparathyroidism (elevated PTH with phosphate retention) is common in CKD G4-G5. If PTH elevated despite phosphate control and normal calcium, consider calcitriol (active vitamin D, 0.25–0.5 µg TDS) or calcimimetic (cinacalcet 30–180 mg daily) to suppress PTH. Monitor calcium and phosphate carefully.

**Vitamin D supplementation**
Ergocalciferol (vitamin D2) supplementation may be considered if 25-OH vitamin D <20 ng/mL, but does not prevent secondary hyperparathyroidism. Calcitriol (active vitamin D) is preferred for suppressing PTH in advanced CKD.

## Cardiovascular risk reduction

**Antiplatelet therapy**
Aspirin 75–100 mg daily for secondary prevention (prior CVD). Primary prevention with aspirin is not recommended in CKD G3-G5 unless very high CVD risk. NSAIDs are contraindicated (increase AKI risk and hyperkalaemia).

**Lipid and BP management**
Already covered above; these are cornerstones of CVD risk reduction.

## Medications to avoid in CKD

**Contraindicated or use with caution**
NSAIDs: contraindicated in CKD G4-G5 or CKD + albuminuria. Increase hyperkalemia, AKI, and progression risk.
Metformin: use caution if eGFR 30–45 (dose adjustment); contraindicated if eGFR <30 or acute illness.
ACE/ARB: caution if serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L or eGFR declining >20%.
Loop diuretics: use only if fluid overload; avoid volume depletion.
Trimethoprim and pentamidine: increase potassium; avoid in CKD.
Potassium supplements: contraindicated in CKD with hyperkalaemia.

**Dose adjustments**
Many drugs require dose reduction in advanced CKD (eGFR <30). Check eGFR-based dosing for: antibiotics (gentamicin, vancomycin), anticoagulants (dabigatran, apixaban), antiretrovirals, and others. Consult drug formulas or BNF for specific adjustments.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**Frequency**
CKD G3a-G3b: 6-monthly renal function, electrolytes, phosphate, calcium.
CKD G4: 3-monthly renal function, electrolytes, phosphate, calcium, PTH, anaemia check.
CKD G5: monthly renal function, electrolytes, phosphate, calcium, PTH; coordinate dialysis planning.

**Trajectory assessment**
Plot eGFR trend to estimate time to kidney failure (CKD G5). If eGFR decline is linear, simple slope calculation helps predict dialysis start date.

**Renal replacement therapy planning**
When eGFR approaches 15, refer for RRT planning: discuss dialysis modality (peritoneal vs haemodialysis), transplant evaluation, conservative care, or shared decision-making.

## Patient education

Explain importance of BP control, adherence to medications, dietary modifications (sodium, potassium, phosphate), and regular monitoring. Provide dietary counselling referral if available. Discuss future RRT options early to allow informed choice.

## Complications in advanced CKD

**Acute decompensation**
Fluid overload with pulmonary oedema: diuretics, fluid restriction.
Hyperkalaemia: restrict potassium, stop ACE/ARB if acute, consider cation-exchange resin (sodium polystyrene) or IV calcium gluconate if ECG changes.
Metabolic acidosis: sodium bicarbonate 500 mg TDS if HCO3 <18 mmol/L.

**Hypertensive crisis**
If BP >160/100 with symptoms: admit for controlled reduction (avoid sudden drops which may cause acute renal worsening).

**Acute kidney injury superimposed on CKD**
AKI is more likely in advanced CKD; hold ACE/ARB during acute illness if risk of prerenal failure. Reintroduce once stable.

## Field notes

ACE/ARB are the cornerstone of CKD management, not for BP reduction alone but for proteinuria reduction and progression slowing. Initial eGFR drop of 5–10% is expected and reflects haemodynamic change, not harm—do not stop. SGLT2 inhibitors are increasingly essential second agents for CKD + diabetes or albuminuria. Tight BP control (<120/90) is critical; expect need for 2–3 agents. Most CKD G3 patients remain stable on medical management for years; reassure appropriately. Advanced CKD (G4-G5) requires close monitoring and early preparation for RRT; do not delay referral. Hyperkalemia is the major barrier to ACE/ARB use; address with dietary restriction, diuretics, or consider adding patiromer if needed to keep potassium <5.5.
