## Pyelonephritis

Acute pyelonephritis is bacterial infection of the kidney parenchyma and collecting system, usually resulting from ascending infection via the urethra and bladder. Incidence is 15–20 per 10,000 women annually; 1–2% of men. Presentation ranges from mild febrile illness responsive to oral antibiotics in community to severe sepsis requiring ICU admission. Early recognition and appropriate antibiotic therapy prevent abscess formation, sepsis, and progression to acute kidney injury.


## Definition and epidemiology

Acute pyelonephritis is bacterial infection of the kidney parenchyma and collecting system. Diagnosis requires fever ≥38°C or clinical symptoms of systemic infection (rigors, malaise) plus either loin pain/costovertebral angle tenderness or urinalysis findings (pyuria, bacteriuria) with positive urine culture. Incidence in women is 15–20 per 10,000 annually; higher in pregnancy and those with urinary tract abnormality. Men have lower incidence (1–2 per 10,000) but higher risk of complications (abscess, sepsis). Risk factors include female sex, pregnancy, recurrent lower UTI, incomplete bladder emptying, neurogenic bladder, urinary obstruction (stone, tumour, stricture), vesicoureteral reflux, and diabetes.

## Pathophysiology

Acute pyelonephritis results from ascending infection via the urethra and bladder; haematogenous spread is rare. Uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) accounts for 80% of community-acquired cases. Bacterial virulence factors (P fimbriae, type 1 fimbriae, alpha-haemolysin, lipopolysaccharide) promote adherence to uroepithelium and invasion. Incomplete bladder emptying, vesicoureteral reflux, and urinary obstruction increase bacterial colonization and ascension to kidneys. Acute inflammatory response (infiltration of neutrophils and macrophages) causes tubular inflammation, oedema, and impaired renal function. Severe infection causes abscess formation (collection of pus within renal parenchyma) or perinephric abscess (infection tracking outside renal capsule). Sepsis develops if bacterial toxins trigger systemic inflammatory response, leading to hypotension, multi-organ dysfunction, and shock.

## Clinical presentation

**Mild-moderate pyelonephritis**
Fever (≥38°C), rigors. Loin or costovertebral angle pain (often unilateral). Dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency. Nausea, vomiting. Tachycardia; blood pressure normal. Examination reveals loin tenderness or costovertebral angle tenderness on percussion. Urinalysis shows pyuria (>5 WBC per hpf), bacteriuria, nitrites.

**Severe pyelonephritis or urosepsis**
High fever (>39°C), rigors. Severe loin pain. Marked tachycardia (>120 bpm), tachypnoea (>20/min), hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg). Altered mental status (confusion, delirium). Nausea, vomiting with dehydration. Indicates sepsis requiring urgent hospital admission and ICU assessment.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Onset and duration of fever. Loin pain character and location. Dysuria, urinary frequency. Preceding lower UTI symptoms. Prior pyelonephritis or recurrent UTI history. Risk factors: pregnancy status, urinary obstruction history, diabetes, catheterization, recent urological instrumentation.

**Examination**
Vital signs: fever, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate assess sepsis severity. Costovertebral angle tenderness on percussion (fist percussion over loin). Abdominal examination for loin tenderness, guarding (suggests peritoneal involvement). Signs of sepsis: tachycardia >120, hypotension, altered mental status.

**Investigations**
Urine culture and sensitivity (gold standard; ≥10⁵ CFU/mL diagnostic; lower counts acceptable if symptomatic). Urinalysis: leukocytes, nitrites, bacteriuria. FBC: elevated WBC (left shift common). Renal function (serum creatinine, urea, electrolytes): assess for acute kidney injury (prerenal from dehydration, intrinsic from inflammation). Liver function tests: mildly elevated transaminases common.

Renal ultrasound or non-contrast CT abdomen/pelvis: indicated if fever persists >48 hours despite antibiotics, immunocompromised patient, or obstruction suspected. Assesses kidney size, echogenicity (increased suggests oedema), hydronephrosis (obstruction), perinephric fluid (abscess), or focal collections.

Blood cultures: indicated in systemically unwell or septic patients; positive in 10–20% of pyelonephritis cases.

DMSA nuclear medicine scan: reserved for recurrent pyelonephritis to assess for renal scarring; not acute phase investigation.

## Severity assessment

SIRS criteria (systemic inflammatory response syndrome): fever >38°C or <36°C, heart rate >90 bpm, respiratory rate >20/min, WBC >12K or <4K. ≥2 of 4 criteria suggests systemic infection.

SOFA score (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment): integrates organ dysfunction markers; SOFA ≥2 indicates sepsis.

Sepsis definition (2016): systemic infection with organ dysfunction (SOFA ≥2). Septic shock: sepsis with refractory hypotension requiring vasopressors.

## Management

**Mild-moderate pyelonephritis (non-septic)**
Oral antibiotic: trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole DS 160/800 mg BD for 7–14 days (first-line) or fluoroquinolone (norfloxacin 400 mg BD, ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD) for 7–14 days if TMP-SMX resistance or allergy. Alternative: cephalexin 500 mg QID for 7–14 days if beta-lactam allergy (cephalosporin safe if no severe penicillin reaction).

Supportive care: hydration (oral fluids if tolerating; IV fluids if vomiting). Analgesia: paracetamol or NSAIDs (ibuprofen 400 mg TDS). Fasting if vomiting; advance diet as tolerated.

Monitoring: vital signs, urine output, repeat FBC and renal function at 48–72 hours if not improving.

**Severe pyelonephritis or sepsis**
Admit immediately to hospital. IV fluids: aggressive resuscitation with 0.9% saline or balanced crystalloid, targeting urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hour. IV antibiotic: broad-spectrum pending culture results. Options: cephalosporin (cefotaxime 2 g TDS IV) + gentamicin (3–5 mg/kg once daily IV); or piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g TDS IV. Switch to narrower spectrum upon culture results.

ICU admission if septic shock (hypotension refractory to fluids requiring vasopressors), multi-organ dysfunction, or altered mental status.

Foley catheter for accurate urine output monitoring. Central line if haemodynamically unstable.

Repeat imaging (ultrasound or CT) if fever persists >48–72 hours to exclude abscess or obstruction.

**Obstruction with infection (urological emergency)**
Urgent decompression required: percutaneous nephrostomy or ureteric stent placement. Defer antibiotic therapy in septic obstruction until after drainage to improve antibiotic delivery. Urology referral emergent.

**Pregnancy**
Pyelonephritis in pregnancy requires hospital admission. Same IV antibiotic regimen; cephalosporin and gentamicin are safe. Risk of preterm labour and maternal sepsis is high. Fetal monitoring if ≥24 weeks.

## Complications

**Renal abscess**
Collection of pus within renal parenchyma. Presents as persistent fever despite antibiotics. Diagnosis by CT (enhanced fluid collection). Treatment: percutaneous drainage (CT or ultrasound-guided) in addition to IV antibiotics.

**Perinephric abscess**
Infection extending beyond renal capsule. More serious than renal abscess; high sepsis risk. Requires percutaneous drainage urgently.

**Urosepsis**
Systemic inflammatory response to bacterial infection. Presents as hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status. Requires ICU admission, aggressive resuscitation, and broad-spectrum antibiotics.

**Acute kidney injury**
Prerenal (from dehydration): reversed with IV fluids. Intrinsic: from inflammation; usually recovers with antibiotic therapy but may persist if severe. Post-renal: from obstruction; reversed after decompression.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**For uncomplicated pyelonephritis treated outpatient:**
Follow-up at 48–72 hours to confirm clinical improvement (fever resolved, loin pain improving, appetite normalizing). Switch to oral antibiotic if improving. Repeat renal function and FBC if abnormal at baseline. Complete 7–14 day antibiotic course.

**For hospitalized pyelonephritis:**
Daily vital signs, urine output, renal function, and FBC during acute phase. Fever curve: most respond within 48–72 hours. If fever persists, repeat imaging (ultrasound/CT) to assess for abscess.

**Post-discharge:**
Reassess at 1–2 weeks if fever persistent or slow to resolve. Urine culture repeat not routinely indicated if symptoms resolved (absence of fever indicates successful treatment). Counsel on prevention (adequate hydration, post-coital voiding, prompt treatment of lower UTI).

**Recurrent pyelonephritis:**
Urological referral for investigation of anatomical abnormality (reflux, obstruction, stone).

## Prevention and patient education

Prevention measures identical to lower UTI: adequate hydration, post-coital voiding, avoidance of spermicide. Prompt treatment of lower UTI symptoms reduces progression to pyelonephritis. Explain importance of completing full antibiotic course even if symptoms resolve (risks relapse and antibiotic resistance).

## Field notes

Pyelonephritis is serious infection; fever with loin pain and dysuria should be treated urgently. Outpatient oral antibiotic is acceptable for mild-moderate, non-septic pyelonephritis in reliable patients with normal renal function and no obstruction. Hospitalize if sepsis, renal impairment, pregnancy, immunocompromised, or obstruction. Fever persisting >48 hours despite appropriate antibiotics warrants imaging to exclude abscess or obstruction. Gentamicin (as part of dual IV therapy) is used in severe cases due to synergy with beta-lactam; single agent therapy with piperacillin-tazobactam is equally effective. Obstruction with infection is urological emergency; drainage takes priority over antibiotic therapy in septic obstruction. DMSA scarring is risk in recurrent pyelonephritis, particularly in children; less relevant in adults but justifies investigation of recurrence. Pregnancy with pyelonephritis carries high preterm labour risk; hospital admission and close monitoring essential.
