<p>Advance care planning (ACP) is a process. It is the set of conversations and documents that record what matters to you, what kind of medical care you would and would not want, and who you would like to speak for you if you cannot speak for yourself. It is not just for people who are dying. The single best time to do ACP is when you are well, because that is the conversation that will be referred back to if you are ever very unwell.</p>

<h2>Why this matters</h2>

<p>If you become so unwell that you cannot speak for yourself, decisions still have to be made. Without an ACP, those decisions fall to your family and your medical team trying to guess what you would have wanted. Families often disagree, doctors err on the side of more treatment by default, and the person at the centre of it all has no voice. With an ACP, your values and preferences become the starting point. Studies in NZ and internationally show that ACP increases the chance that the care you actually receive lines up with what you would have asked for, and reduces family distress in bereavement.[1]</p>

<h2>The conversation</h2>

<p>Advance care planning is fundamentally a series of conversations, and the documents that come out of them are secondary. The conversation is best had with the people who would be involved if you became unwell:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Your partner or whoever lives with you</li>
  <li>Your adult children, if you have them</li>
  <li>The person you would name as your enduring power of attorney</li>
  <li>Your GP</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not need to do all of this in one sitting. Many people find it helpful to think through some questions on their own first, then bring them to a quieter moment with family.</p>

<h2>Questions worth thinking about</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What does a good day look like for me right now?</li>
  <li>What activities or relationships make my life feel worthwhile?</li>
  <li>What would be unacceptable to me - what level of disability, dependence, or symptom burden would I rather not be kept alive in?</li>
  <li>If I became unable to speak for myself, who would I want to make medical decisions for me?</li>
  <li>What are my views on hospital admission, intensive care, CPR, tube feeding, antibiotics in the last weeks of life?</li>
  <li>Where would I prefer to be if I were dying - home, hospice, hospital, rest home?</li>
  <li>What religious or cultural practices matter at the end of life?</li>
</ul>

<h2>The documents</h2>

<h3>Advance directive (or "advance care plan")</h3>
<p>A written record of your views, values, and preferences, often using a structured template. The Health Quality &amp; Safety Commission NZ runs the national ACP programme and provides a free planning guide. An advance directive is not legally binding in NZ in the same way as in some other countries, but it is given strong weight by medical teams in deciding what treatment is in your best interest. Make sure your GP has a copy in your medical records.</p>

<h3>Enduring power of attorney for personal care and welfare (EPOA-PCW)</h3>
<p>A legal document that names one or two trusted people to make health and welfare decisions for you if you lose mental capacity. Set up before any cognitive decline. Best done with a lawyer or a trustee company; it is not the same as the financial EPOA. Without an EPOA, your family may need to apply to the Family Court if a dispute arises.</p>

<h3>Resuscitation order ("Allow Natural Death" or NFR)</h3>
<p>A specific medical order, usually completed in hospital or rest home, that records whether CPR should be attempted if your heart stops. The conversation is most useful before you are very unwell. The presumption in NZ is to attempt CPR unless documented otherwise. CPR has lower success rates than most people expect, especially in advanced age or serious illness.[2]</p>

<h3>Goals of care document</h3>
<p>Many DHBs use a "Goals of Care" or "Te Ara Whakapiri" document on hospital admission, summarising the planned ceiling of treatment for that admission. This is not the same as an advance directive but works alongside one.</p>

<h2>Common myths</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>"It means I don't get treatment."</strong> No. Most ACPs are about HOW to be treated, not WHETHER. You can ask for full active treatment in some scenarios and comfort-only in others.</li>
  <li><strong>"It is only for old people."</strong> No. The most distressing real-world cases are usually younger adults whose families have to guess. Anyone over 18 can do this.</li>
  <li><strong>"Once written, it is set in stone."</strong> No. You can revise it any time. Many people review every five years or after a major health event.</li>
  <li><strong>"My GP knows what I want."</strong> Possibly, but they cannot act on that without it being documented in your file.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical steps</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Download the NZ "My Advance Care Plan" guide from the Health Quality &amp; Safety Commission. It is free.</li>
  <li>Have one or two conversations with people who matter to you.</li>
  <li>Write the plan up. It does not need to be a long document; even a single page is useful.</li>
  <li>Give a copy to your GP, your EPOA, and your closest family. Keep an electronic copy.</li>
  <li>If relevant, see a lawyer to set up an EPOA-PCW.</li>
  <li>Review every few years and after any significant change in health.</li>
</ul>
