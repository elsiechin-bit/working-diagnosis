Bowel cancer is one of the most common cancers in New Zealand, with around 3,000 new diagnoses each year. When found early, it has a 90%+ cure rate. Found late, survival drops sharply. The free National Bowel Screening Programme uses a simple home test to find bowel cancer at its earliest stage. Around 70% of bowel cancers found through screening are at the easily-curable stages.&sup1;

<h2>How it works</h2>

<p>Every two years, eligible New Zealanders are sent a free home test kit (called a faecal immunochemical test, or FIT). The test detects tiny amounts of blood in the stool that you cannot see. Most positive tests turn out to be from haemorrhoids or polyps - but some find early cancers.</p>

<h2>Who is eligible?</h2>

<p>From 2024 onwards, the programme is being phased to:</p>

<ul>
<li>All NZ residents aged 60-74 (everyone)</li>
<li>Maori and Pacific peoples from age 50-74 (lower starting age because of higher rates and earlier presentation)</li>
</ul>

<p>Eligibility is being progressively widened. Check at <a href="https://www.timetoscreen.nz/bowel-screening">timetoscreen.nz</a> or call 0800 924 432.</p>

<h2>What the kit contains</h2>

<ul>
<li>A small test tube with a stick attached to the lid</li>
<li>A "catch sheet" or instructions for collecting a sample</li>
<li>A pre-paid return envelope</li>
<li>Instructions in your language - 14 languages available</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to do the test</h2>

<ol>
<li>Read the instructions in the kit</li>
<li>Use the catch sheet (or aluminium foil over the toilet) to collect a small stool sample</li>
<li>Use the stick to scrape the stool surface in several places</li>
<li>Put the stick back in the test tube and close firmly</li>
<li>Write your name and date of birth on the tube</li>
<li>Place the tube in the labelled envelope</li>
<li>Post within 1 day if possible (or store in fridge - not freezer - for up to 7 days)</li>
</ol>

<p>The whole process takes less than 5 minutes.</p>

<h2>When not to do the test</h2>

<ul>
<li>If you have visible bleeding from the bottom currently - see your GP first</li>
<li>If you have severe diarrhoea or vomiting - wait until well</li>
<li>If you are menstruating - wait 3 days after the period</li>
<li>If you have known bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis, recent surgery) under specialist care</li>
<li>If you have had a colonoscopy in the past 12 months</li>
</ul>

<h2>What the result means</h2>

<p>You receive a letter within 2-3 weeks.</p>

<p><strong>Negative result</strong> (no blood detected): you will be re-invited in 2 years. A negative test means cancer is unlikely now, but does not guarantee no cancer ever - so see your GP if you develop symptoms (bleeding, change in bowel habit, weight loss).</p>

<p><strong>Positive result</strong> (blood detected): you will be referred for a colonoscopy. Most positive tests do NOT find cancer - usually the cause is haemorrhoids, polyps, or inflammation. About 1 in 12 positive tests finds a cancer; about half find polyps that, if not removed, could become cancer.</p>

<h2>Colonoscopy after a positive screen</h2>

<p>Free colonoscopy is provided through the screening programme.</p>

<p>What to expect:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bowel preparation the day before - a strong laxative drink to clear the colon. Spend the day at home near a toilet</li>
<li>Procedure under sedation, takes 30-60 minutes</li>
<li>A flexible camera examines the entire colon</li>
<li>Polyps are removed during the procedure</li>
<li>Biopsies of any abnormal areas</li>
<li>Recovery in the unit for 30-60 minutes after</li>
<li>Need someone to drive you home and stay with you for 24 hours</li>
</ul>

<p>Risks are small (about 1 in 1000 of bleeding or perforation needing intervention) but real. The benefits, when polyps or cancers are found, are substantial.</p>

<h2>Why screening is worthwhile</h2>

<ul>
<li>Detects bowel cancer at curable stages</li>
<li>Removes precancerous polyps before they become cancer</li>
<li>Reduces bowel cancer deaths by around 25-30% in screened populations&sup2;</li>
<li>Catches the silent cancers that have not yet caused symptoms</li>
</ul>

<p>Most early bowel cancers cause no symptoms - by the time symptoms develop, the cancer is often more advanced.</p>

<h2>Common reasons people put it off</h2>

<p><strong>"It's gross."</strong> The test is fast, clean, and uses a tiny stick.</p>

<p><strong>"I feel fine."</strong> That is the point. Early bowel cancer feels fine.</p>

<p><strong>"I'm too busy."</strong> 5 minutes once every 2 years.</p>

<p><strong>"I'm afraid of the result."</strong> Most are negative. If positive, the test has just done its job - early detection is the best outcome.</p>

<p><strong>"I had one done last time."</strong> Bowel cancer takes years to develop. Repeat testing is what works.</p>

<p>Maori and Pacific participation rates are lower than NZ European - extra effort to spread the word in whanau and communities saves lives.</p>

<h2>If you are too young or do not qualify</h2>

<p>Risk factors for earlier bowel cancer:</p>

<ul>
<li>Family history of bowel cancer or polyps - especially under age 50</li>
<li>Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis)</li>
<li>Lynch syndrome or familial adenomatous polyposis</li>
</ul>

<p>If any apply, see your GP - earlier or more frequent colonoscopy may be recommended.</p>

<h2>Symptoms - whatever your age</h2>

<p>See your GP regardless of screening status if you have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bleeding from the bottom or blood in the stool</li>
<li>Change in bowel habit lasting more than 3 weeks</li>
<li>Unexplained weight loss</li>
<li>Tiredness with iron deficiency</li>
<li>Persistent abdominal pain</li>
<li>Family history concerns</li>
</ul>
