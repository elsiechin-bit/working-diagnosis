<p>New Zealand has a publicly funded health system. Hospital care, emergency treatment, and most community health services are free or heavily subsidised for eligible residents. General practice (your GP) is also subsidised but usually involves a co-payment. Understanding how the system is structured helps you make better use of it.</p>

<h2>Who is eligible</h2>

<p>You are entitled to publicly funded health services if you are a NZ citizen or permanent resident, or hold certain work or student visas. If you are unsure whether you qualify, the Ministry of Health eligibility checker at <a href="https://www.tewhatuora.govt.nz">tewhatuora.govt.nz</a> has current guidance. Overseas visitors are generally not eligible for subsidised services and should have travel insurance.</p>

<h2>Your GP: the starting point</h2>

<p>General practice (family medicine) is the foundation of primary care in NZ. GPs handle the majority of everyday health needs, coordinate ongoing conditions, and are the usual gateway to specialist and hospital services.</p>

<p>You enrol with a general practice through a <strong>Primary Health Organisation (PHO)</strong> - the funding body that links GPs to the public system. Enrolling with a PHO unlocks subsidised fees. If you are not enrolled, you will pay a higher unsubsidised rate. Enrolment is free and takes a few minutes at your GP reception.</p>

<p>GPs can charge whatever they choose above the government subsidy, so fees vary between practices. Typical enrolled adult fees range from $15 to $60+ per visit, depending on the practice and your circumstances.</p>

<h2>Reducing your GP fees</h2>

<p>Several mechanisms can lower what you pay at your GP:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Enrolment with a PHO</strong> - the baseline subsidy that applies to all enrolled patients. Always enrol if you have not already.</li>
  <li><strong>Community Services Card (CSC)</strong> - available to lower-income individuals and households. It increases the government subsidy your GP receives, which most practices pass on as a reduced fee. Apply through Work and Income NZ (0800 559 009 or <a href="https://www.workandincome.govt.nz">workandincome.govt.nz</a>).</li>
  <li><strong>Very Low Cost Access (VLCA) practices</strong> - some practices serve high-need communities and are required to keep fees below a set threshold (currently around $19.50 for adults and free for children). Your local PHO can tell you which practices in your area are VLCA-funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Free visits for children under 14</strong> - GP visits are free for children aged under 14 at most enrolled practices.</li>
  <li><strong>High Use Health Card (HUHC)</strong> - if you visit a GP 12 or more times in a year, you may qualify for a High Use Health Card, which increases your subsidy further. Ask your GP practice about eligibility.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Prescriptions</h2>

<p>Medicines approved by <strong>Pharmac</strong> (the government's pharmaceutical purchasing agency) are funded. For each funded medicine, there is a co-payment of <strong>$5 per item</strong>, with a maximum of $100 per year (20 items). After you reach the annual cap, further funded prescriptions are free for the rest of that year - keep your receipts and ask your pharmacist to track this.</p>

<p>Prescription charges are free for:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Children under 14</li>
  <li>People holding a Community Services Card (for themselves and their dependants)</li>
</ul>

<p>Not all medicines are funded by Pharmac. If your GP prescribes something that is not on the funded schedule, you will pay the full cost. Your GP or pharmacist can tell you whether an alternative funded medicine is available.</p>

<h2>Referrals and specialist care</h2>

<p>Access to most publicly funded specialist services requires a referral from your GP. Your GP sends a referral letter, and the hospital or specialist service decides whether you meet the threshold for publicly funded treatment. NZ uses a <strong>clinical priority scoring</strong> system - patients whose need exceeds a certain score are accepted onto the waiting list; those below the threshold are returned to GP care with advice.</p>

<p>If you are accepted, you will receive a First Specialist Assessment (FSA) appointment. From there, further treatment, surgery, or follow-up may be arranged through the public system or, if clinically urgent, fast-tracked.</p>

<p>Waiting times vary significantly by region and specialty. If waiting is a hardship or your condition changes, let your GP know - they can review the referral or escalate if needed.</p>

<h2>Hospital and emergency care</h2>

<p>Admission to a public hospital - whether for surgery, an acute illness, or an inpatient stay - is <strong>free</strong> for eligible NZ residents. There are no co-payments for hospital care.</p>

<p>Emergency departments (EDs) are also free for eligible residents. That said, EDs are designed for genuine emergencies - serious injury, chest pain, stroke symptoms, severe breathing difficulty, significant trauma. For urgent but non-life-threatening problems (infections, sprains, minor wounds, illness that cannot wait for a GP appointment), <strong>urgent care or after-hours clinics</strong> are faster, cheaper, and more appropriate.</p>

<h2>Accident cover through ACC</h2>

<p>The Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC) is a uniquely NZ scheme that provides no-fault cover for injuries caused by accidents. This includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>GP and specialist consultations related to the injury</li>
  <li>Physiotherapy and other rehabilitation</li>
  <li>Surgery if required</li>
  <li>Weekly compensation if the injury prevents you from working</li>
</ul>

<p>ACC covers <em>injuries</em>, not illnesses. A broken ankle from a fall is covered; a knee replacement for arthritis is not. Your GP lodges the ACC claim on your behalf - you do not need to do this yourself. If ACC declines a claim, you have the right to dispute the decision.</p>

<h2>After-hours care</h2>

<p>When your GP is closed:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Healthline</strong> (0800 611 116) - free 24/7 nurse advice line. A registered nurse can assess symptoms, advise on urgency, and direct you to the right care. Use this before driving to an ED if you are unsure how serious something is.</li>
  <li><strong>Urgent care clinics</strong> - operate evenings and weekends. Fees apply (usually $50-$100+ for adults) but are lower than an ED presentation for non-emergencies. Many are enrolled with PHOs and can reduce costs with a CSC.</li>
  <li><strong>Emergency department (ED)</strong> - for genuine emergencies only. If in doubt between urgent care and ED, call Healthline first.</li>
  <li><strong>111</strong> - for life-threatening emergencies: severe chest pain, difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, major bleeding, stroke symptoms (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 111).</li>
</ul>

<p>Ambulance callouts through St John or Wellington Free Ambulance are not free in all situations. A co-payment may apply. If you are a member of St John, your membership covers this.</p>

<h2>Mental health services</h2>

<p>Your GP is the starting point for most mental health needs. GPs can prescribe, provide brief counselling, and refer to secondary services.</p>

<p>Publicly funded mental health and addiction services are tiered by severity:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Primary care</strong> - GP-based management for mild to moderate conditions. Some practices have access to subsidised counselling or psychology services.</li>
  <li><strong>Community Mental Health Teams</strong> - for moderate to severe conditions requiring specialist input. Accessed via GP referral.</li>
  <li><strong>Inpatient services</strong> - for acute psychiatric crises. Accessed through ED or crisis assessment teams.</li>
</ul>

<p>Free or subsidised counselling is available through some PHOs and funded programmes. Ask your GP what is available locally - provision varies by region. <strong>1737</strong> (free call or text, any time) connects you with a trained counsellor for immediate support.</p>

<h2>What the public system does not fully cover</h2>

<p>Some health areas sit largely outside public funding:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dental care</strong> - publicly funded for children (through the Community Oral Health Service, available to school-age children and adolescents up to age 18). Adults pay privately. Some DHB services exist for specific high-need groups, but routine adult dental is not funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Optometry and glasses</strong> - eye examinations and glasses are not publicly funded for adults. Children have access to some funded services through school health checks.</li>
  <li><strong>Elective surgery waiting times</strong> - if you do not meet the clinical priority threshold for publicly funded surgery, you may be managed in primary care or choose to pay privately.</li>
  <li><strong>Some medicines</strong> - medicines not listed on the Pharmac schedule are not subsidised and can be expensive. In some cases, your GP can apply for Special Authority funding for non-funded medicines.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Health New Zealand - Te Whatu Ora</h2>

<p>Since 2022, New Zealand's 20 District Health Boards were replaced by a single national body, <strong>Health New Zealand - Te Whatu Ora</strong>. This body now plans, funds, and delivers most public health services nationally, with regional commissioning. Your day-to-day experience with your GP and local hospital is largely unchanged, but Health NZ is the organisation responsible for the overall system.</p>

<h2>Questions worth asking at your next appointment</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Am I enrolled with a PHO? (If not: how do I enrol?)</li>
  <li>Do I qualify for a Community Services Card or High Use Health Card?</li>
  <li>Is this prescription funded by Pharmac, or will I pay full price?</li>
  <li>What are my options if the wait for specialist care is long?</li>
  <li>Is there a VLCA-funded practice closer to me?</li>
</ul>
