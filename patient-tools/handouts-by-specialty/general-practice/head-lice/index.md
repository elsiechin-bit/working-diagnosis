<p>Head lice are common, especially in school-aged children, and not a sign of poor hygiene. They are annoying but harmless and easy to treat with the right approach. This handout explains how to identify head lice and what actually works.</p>

<h2>What are head lice</h2>

<p>Head lice are small insects about the size of a sesame seed that live on the scalp and feed on blood. They spread by direct head-to-head contact. The main sign of infestation is itching from the lice bites. Lice cannot jump or fly - they only spread by contact.</p>

<h2>Signs of head lice</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Itching, especially at the nape of the neck or behind the ears</li>
  <li>Visible lice or nits (empty egg cases) on the hair and scalp</li>
  <li>Nits appear as tiny cream or white specks stuck to hair shafts, often near the scalp</li>
  <li>Sometimes scratching leads to small sores on the scalp</li>
  <li>Not all itchy scalps are lice - other causes include dry scalp or dermatitis</li>
</ul>

<h2>Checking for head lice</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Use a fine-tooth comb.</strong> Wet the hair and comb through slowly, starting at the roots. Wipe the comb on a white tissue or paper to see any lice clearly.</li>
  <li><strong>Check the scalp carefully.</strong> Look behind the ears, at the nape of the neck, and along the hair line where lice prefer to hide.</li>
  <li><strong>Look for nits and lice.</strong> Lice are darker and move; nits are stationary and attached to hair shafts.</li>
  <li><strong>Comb when wet.</strong> Wet hair is easier to comb and slows lice movement.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment approaches</h2>

<p><strong>Physical removal is most effective.</strong> Wet combing with a fine-tooth comb applied to wet hair every 3 - 4 days for 2 weeks removes lice and nits effectively. This requires patience but works without chemicals.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Wet combing method:</strong> Wet hair with conditioner, comb through slowly and methodically from roots to ends. Wipe the comb on tissue and check for lice. Repeat every 3 - 4 days for 14 days to catch lice that hatch from nits.</li>
  <li><strong>Chemical treatments:</strong> Anti-lice lotions or shampoos (permethrin or pyrethrins) are available over-the-counter. Follow instructions carefully as effectiveness varies. Repeat treatment after 7 days to kill lice that hatch from nits. Always check with pharmacist if your child has sensitive skin.</li>
  <li><strong>Combination approach:</strong> Some parents combine a single application of anti-lice lotion with regular wet combing for 2 weeks.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What does not work</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Regular shampooing - does not remove lice</li>
  <li>Tea tree oil or other "natural" remedies - no reliable evidence</li>
  <li>Cutting hair short - lice do not disappear; they simply have less to cling to</li>
  <li>Shaving the head - extreme and usually unnecessary</li>
</ul>

<h2>Managing contacts and preventing spread</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Notify the school or childcare.</strong> So other parents can check their children.</li>
  <li><strong>Check family members.</strong> Use a fine-tooth comb to check all family members' heads.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat contacts if infested.</strong> Only treat people who actually have lice, not preventatively.</li>
  <li><strong>Minimal other measures needed:</strong> Lice do not survive long off the scalp, so washing items is not usually necessary. Avoid sharing combs, towels, and pillows temporarily.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are unsure whether lice are present</li>
  <li>Your child has a skin reaction to treatment</li>
  <li>Treatment is not working after two weeks</li>
  <li>Your child is scratching severely and the scalp is infected</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your pharmacist or GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>How do I use the anti-lice treatment safely?</li>
  <li>Which treatment method is best for my child?</li>
  <li>How often should I comb and check?</li>
  <li>Are there any side effects from treatment?</li>
</ul>
