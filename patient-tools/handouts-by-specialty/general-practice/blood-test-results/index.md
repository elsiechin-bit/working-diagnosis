<p>Blood tests are common in general practice, used to check your health, diagnose conditions, and monitor treatment. The results can seem confusing with lots of abbreviations and numbers. This handout explains common blood tests and what the results mean.</p>

<h2>Basic blood test information</h2>

<p>Your blood test results will show:
  The test name (for example, "Full Blood Count" or FBC)
  Your result (a number)
  The normal range (the range that is considered healthy)
  Units (like millimetres of mercury or mmol/L)
  Sometimes a flag indicating whether the result is normal, high, or low</p>

<p>A result outside the normal range does not always mean something is wrong - context matters. Your GP will interpret results in light of your symptoms, medicines, and overall health.</p>

<h2>Full Blood Count (FBC)</h2>

<p>This counts different types of cells in your blood.</p>

<p><strong>Red Blood Cells (RBC) and Haemoglobin.</strong> Low levels cause anaemia - tiredness, shortness of breath, pale skin. Causes include iron deficiency, B12 or folate deficiency, chronic disease, or blood loss. High levels are rare but can indicate dehydration or lung disease.</p>

<p><strong>White Blood Cells (WBC).</strong> These fight infection. Low levels suggest bone marrow problems, certain medicines, or HIV. High levels indicate infection, inflammation, leukaemia, or stress response.</p>

<p><strong>Platelets.</strong> These help blood clot. Low levels increase bleeding risk (bruising, nosebleeds). High levels can increase clotting risk.</p>

<h2>Kidney and liver function tests</h2>

<p><strong>Creatinine and estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR).</strong> These measure kidney function. Low eGFR (below 60) suggests kidney disease. Your doctor may adjust medicine doses if kidneys are weak.</p>

<p><strong>Urea.</strong> This is a waste product filtered by kidneys. High levels suggest kidney problems or dehydration.</p>

<p><strong>Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT).</strong> These indicate liver cell damage from hepatitis, fatty liver, alcohol, or medicines. Mildly raised levels are common and often not serious, but high levels need investigation.</p>

<p><strong>Bilirubin.</strong> This is processed by the liver. High levels cause jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes) and suggest liver problems or bile duct blockage.</p>

<h2>Blood glucose and diabetes screening</h2>

<p><strong>Fasting glucose.</strong> This is measured after not eating overnight. Normal is below 5.5 mmol/L. 5.5 - 7.0 suggests prediabetes. Above 7.0 on repeat testing suggests diabetes.</p>

<p><strong>HbA1c.</strong> This measures average blood glucose over 3 months. Below 5.7% is normal. 5.7 - 6.4% suggests prediabetes. Above 6.5% indicates diabetes. This test is used to diagnose diabetes and monitor control in people already diagnosed.</p>

<h2>Cholesterol tests</h2>

<p><strong>Total cholesterol.</strong> Ideally below 5.2 mmol/L. Higher levels increase heart disease risk.</p>

<p><strong>LDL cholesterol ("bad" cholesterol).</strong> This deposits in arteries. Lower is better. Target depends on your risk of heart disease.</p>

<p><strong>HDL cholesterol ("good" cholesterol).</strong> This removes cholesterol from arteries. Higher is better. Below 1.0 mmol/L in men or 1.3 in women increases heart disease risk.</p>

<p><strong>Triglycerides.</strong> These are a type of fat. High levels (above 2.3 mmol/L) increase heart disease risk and can cause pancreatitis.</p>

<h2>Inflammation markers</h2>

<p><strong>C-Reactive Protein (CRP) and Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR).</strong> These measure inflammation. Raised levels indicate infection, inflammation, or autoimmune disease. They are useful for monitoring conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.</p>

<h2>Thyroid function</h2>

<p><strong>TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone).</strong> High TSH suggests an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism) - causing tiredness, weight gain, cold intolerance. Low TSH suggests overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism) - causing weight loss, tremor, anxiety.</p>

<p><strong>Free T4 (thyroxine).</strong> This is the active thyroid hormone. Low levels confirm hypothyroidism. High levels confirm hyperthyroidism.</p>

<h2>Bone health markers</h2>

<p><strong>Calcium and phosphate.</strong> These are important for bone health. Low calcium suggests vitamin D deficiency or kidney problems. High calcium can indicate bone disease or cancer and needs investigation.</p>

<p><strong>Alkaline phosphatase.</strong> Raised levels suggest bone disease or liver problems.</p>

<h2>Iron tests</h2>

<p><strong>Iron (serum iron), Ferritin, and Transferrin saturation.</strong> Low ferritin suggests iron deficiency. High ferritin suggests iron overload (haemochromatosis or repeated transfusions). Ferritin is also raised in inflammation, so context matters.</p>

<h2>Vitamin B12 and folate</h2>

<p>Low levels cause anaemia and nerve damage. Vegetarians and vegans may have low B12. Folate deficiency is common in pregnancy. Both are easily treated with supplements.</p>

<h2>When to be concerned</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Very high or very low results (marked with a flag)</li>
  <li>Results significantly outside the normal range</li>
  <li>Abnormal results that are new or worsening from previous tests</li>
  <li>Multiple abnormal results together</li>
</ul>

<p>However, isolated mildly abnormal results are often not serious. Your doctor will decide whether to investigate further or repeat testing.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Are my results normal?</li>
  <li>What do the abnormal results mean?</li>
  <li>Do I need further testing?</li>
  <li>Do I need treatment based on these results?</li>
  <li>When should my results be rechecked?</li>
  <li>Should I change anything - diet, exercise, medicines?</li>
  <li>What do I need to do before my next test?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things that affect blood test results</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Fasting.</strong> Some tests require overnight fasting; eating or drinking beforehand can affect results.</li>
  <li><strong>Time of day.</strong> Some values vary throughout the day - your doctor may schedule testing at a specific time.</li>
  <li><strong>Medicines.</strong> Many medicines affect test results - tell your doctor about everything you are taking.</li>
  <li><strong>Exercise.</strong> Strenuous exercise before blood tests can affect results.</li>
  <li><strong>Dehydration.</strong> This concentrates blood and can raise some values.</li>
  <li><strong>Stress and anxiety.</strong> These can affect glucose and some other values.</li>
</ul>
