New Zealand has some of the highest ultraviolet (UV) levels of any country. Our peak summer UV is around 40% higher than in matching latitudes in the northern hemisphere, partly because of cleaner air and the seasonal thinning of ozone over the Southern Ocean.&sup1; The result: sunburn, premature skin ageing, and skin cancer are common. The good news is that sun damage is largely preventable.

<h2>The UV index - your daily guide</h2>

<p>The UV index measures how strong the sun is. From October to March in NZ, the UV regularly reaches "very high" (8-10) or "extreme" (11+). Sun protection is recommended whenever the UV index is 3 or above.</p>

<p>You can check the daily UV forecast at <a href="https://www.uv2day.niwa.co.nz">uv2day.niwa.co.nz</a> or in the MetService app. Remember:</p>

<ul>
<li>UV is highest between 10 am and 4 pm in summer (and 11 am to 4 pm with daylight saving)</li>
<li>Cloud cover blocks only some UV - you can still burn on overcast days</li>
<li>UV reflects off water, sand, snow, and concrete</li>
<li>UV passes through car windows (side windows especially)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Slip, Slop, Slap, Wrap</h2>

<p>The Cancer Society's slogan is short and works:</p>

<p><strong>Slip</strong> on a shirt and slip into shade.</p>

<p><strong>Slop</strong> on broad-spectrum SPF 30+ sunscreen.</p>

<p><strong>Slap</strong> on a wide-brimmed or legionnaire hat.</p>

<p><strong>Wrap</strong> on close-fitting wrap-around sunglasses.</p>

<h2>Sunscreen - using it properly</h2>

<p>Most people use far too little. To get the SPF on the bottle, you need:</p>

<ul>
<li>About a teaspoon for the face and neck</li>
<li>A teaspoon for each arm</li>
<li>A teaspoon for each leg</li>
<li>A teaspoon for the front of the body</li>
<li>A teaspoon for the back</li>
<li>Around 35 mL (about a shot glass) for an adult in togs</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Apply 20 minutes before going outside</strong> so it has time to bind to skin.</p>

<p><strong>Re-apply every 2 hours</strong>, and immediately after swimming, towelling, or heavy sweating - even if the bottle says "water-resistant".</p>

<p><strong>Choose broad-spectrum SPF 30 or higher</strong> (50+ for high UV days or fair skin). Look for "UVA" with a circle around it on Australian/NZ products.</p>

<p>Sunscreen does not give you 100% protection. Even SPF 50 lets through about 2% of UV.</p>

<h2>Clothing and hats</h2>

<p>Clothing is the most reliable protection. Look for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tightly-woven fabric (hold up to the light - the less you can see through, the better)</li>
<li>Long sleeves and longer shorts when out for hours</li>
<li>UPF-rated swimwear and rashies for kids and water</li>
<li>Wide-brim hats (at least 7.5 cm brim all the way round)</li>
<li>Legionnaire hats for kids (with a tail at the back)</li>
</ul>

<p>Sunglasses should be wrap-around or close-fitting and meet AS/NZS 1067 standards (look for the "Eye Protection Factor 10" or similar marking).</p>

<h2>Children</h2>

<p>Most lifetime UV exposure happens before age 18. Just one blistering sunburn in childhood doubles melanoma risk.&sup2;</p>

<ul>
<li>Babies under 6 months should be kept out of direct sun. Use clothing, hats, and shade rather than sunscreen on this age</li>
<li>From 6 months: sunscreen plus the other measures</li>
<li>Most ECEs and primary schools enforce "no hat, no play" in summer term</li>
<li>Reapply sunscreen at lunchtime (a small amount in their lunchbox or at the school office)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Vitamin D - getting the balance right</h2>

<p>Vitamin D is made in the skin from UV. In summer in NZ, a few minutes of incidental exposure (walking to the car, taking out the rubbish) is plenty. In winter, especially south of Wellington, you may need supplements - ask your GP if you have risk factors (older age, dark skin, fully covered, housebound, certain medications).</p>

<p>You do not need to sunbathe for vitamin D. The skin damage outweighs the benefit.</p>

<h2>Tanning beds</h2>

<p>Commercial sunbeds are illegal in New Zealand from 2017 for under-18s, and the Cancer Society advises against their use at any age. There is no safe level of tanning bed UV.</p>

<h2>Self-checking</h2>

<p>Once a year, do a head-to-toe skin check. Use a mirror or ask a partner to check your back, scalp, and behind your ears. See your GP for any new, changing, or different-looking spot. See our <a href="/handouts/skin-cancer-abcde/">ABCDE handout</a> for what to look for.</p>
