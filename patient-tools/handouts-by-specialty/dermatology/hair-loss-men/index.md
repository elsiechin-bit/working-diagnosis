<p>Most men will lose at least some hair over their lifetime. Around half of men show noticeable male-pattern hair loss by age 50, and around 80 percent by age 80.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> It is not a disease and it does not signal anything ominous about your health. It is genetic and hormonal. The treatments that genuinely work are short - there are essentially three of them, all available in New Zealand. The marketplace of shampoos, supplements, laser combs, peptides, and microneedling kits sold for hair loss is large, profitable, and largely without good evidence.</p>

<h2>The common pattern</h2>

<p>Male-pattern hair loss (androgenetic alopecia) is gradual thinning that follows a recognisable pattern: receding at the temples and forehead, thinning at the crown, with the back and sides usually preserved. It is driven by a sensitivity of hair follicles to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), a hormone derived from testosterone. Once a follicle has fully miniaturised, the hair from it does not return.</p>

<p>This sensitivity is inherited. Looking at the men on both sides of your family is the best predictor of how your own pattern will progress.</p>

<h2>Other causes worth knowing about</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Telogen effluvium.</strong> A diffuse shedding two to three months after a major physical or emotional stress (illness, surgery, major life event, big weight loss). Usually reverses on its own over six to twelve months.</li>
  <li><strong>Alopecia areata.</strong> Sudden, well-defined patches of hair loss with otherwise normal skin. An autoimmune condition. Often regrows but may recur.</li>
  <li><strong>Scarring alopecias.</strong> Permanent loss with skin changes - redness, scaling, itch. Less common but needs early dermatology referral.</li>
  <li><strong>Iron deficiency, thyroid disease, low vitamin D.</strong> Worth checking, especially if shedding is recent or diffuse, but routine "hair loss bloods" rarely change management in clear male-pattern loss.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What works</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Topical minoxidil 5%.</strong> Available over the counter in NZ pharmacies. Apply twice daily to the scalp. About 60 percent of men see some thickening over six to twelve months.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> Works while you use it; the effect is lost over a few months if you stop. Side effects are uncommon (scalp irritation, mild facial hair if it migrates).</li>
  <li><strong>Oral finasteride 1mg daily.</strong> Prescription only in NZ, unfunded by PHARMAC for hair loss, costs around $30 to $60 a month from a pharmacy. Blocks the conversion of testosterone to DHT. Slows further loss in around 90 percent of men and produces visible regrowth in around two-thirds.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> Sexual side effects (reduced libido, erectile difficulties, lowered ejaculate volume) occur in about 1 to 2 percent of men in randomised trials and usually resolve when the medicine is stopped.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Hair transplantation.</strong> Surgical relocation of follicles from the back of the head (where they are not DHT-sensitive) to the thinning areas. Effective and durable, but costly (typically several thousand dollars in NZ) and only worth doing once the pattern of loss is stable.</li>
</ul>

<p>Combining minoxidil and finasteride works better than either alone for most men. Most men who get a good result are using one or both consistently for at least six to twelve months before deciding whether they help.</p>

<h2>What does not have good evidence</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Caffeine, biotin, and "DHT-blocking" shampoos</li>
  <li>Multivitamins and hair supplements (unless you have a documented deficiency)</li>
  <li>Laser combs and most home red-light devices (small, low-quality studies)</li>
  <li>Peptide serums, exosome treatments, and most "stem cell" hair therapies</li>
  <li>Microneedling on its own (modest evidence as an addition to minoxidil, not a replacement)</li>
  <li>Hair fibre powders - cosmetic camouflage only, not treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>None of these will harm you. The harm is mostly to your wallet.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sudden or patchy hair loss, rather than gradual thinning</li>
  <li>Hair loss with skin changes (redness, scaling, scarring, itch)</li>
  <li>Diffuse shedding accompanied by fatigue, weight change, cold intolerance, or heavy periods in a partner who notices their own loss too</li>
  <li>Wanting to start finasteride or minoxidil and discuss whether they are right for you</li>
  <li>Considering hair transplantation - your GP can help you choose a reputable provider and time it well</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Does my pattern look like ordinary male-pattern loss, or could there be another cause?</li>
  <li>Would minoxidil, finasteride, or both be more suitable for me?</li>
  <li>What side effects should I watch for, and how long until I know if it is working?</li>
</ul>
