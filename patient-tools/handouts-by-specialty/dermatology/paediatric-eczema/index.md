<p>Eczema (atopic dermatitis) affects around 1 in 5 NZ children at some point.[1] It is caused by a combination of barrier dysfunction in the skin, a tendency to immune over-reaction, and triggers from the environment. Most children outgrow it. The treatment is well-established and effective when used properly. The most common reason eczema is not controlled is undertreatment - usually because parents have been told to be cautious with steroid creams and have therefore used too little, too late, for too short a time. This handout sets the record straight.</p>

<h2>What eczema looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Itchy, red, scaly patches</li>
  <li>In babies: cheeks, scalp, behind ears, outside of arms and legs</li>
  <li>In toddlers and older children: insides of elbows, behind knees, wrists, ankles, neck</li>
  <li>Skin can be dry, lichenified (thick), weeping or crusted in flares</li>
  <li>Often worse at night - sleep disrupted by itching</li>
  <li>Tends to flare and settle over weeks</li>
</ul>

<h2>The two-pillar approach</h2>

<p>Eczema management has two parts. Both matter; one without the other does not work.</p>

<h3>Pillar 1: emollient (moisturiser) every day, every part of the skin, forever</h3>

<p>Eczema skin loses water 2-3 times faster than normal skin. Moisturiser locks water in, reduces itch, prevents flares, and reduces the need for steroid cream long-term.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Apply at least twice a day.</strong> More often if practical.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply to ALL skin, not just affected patches.</strong> The "good" skin is also barrier-impaired.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply within 3 minutes of bathing</strong> while the skin is damp - traps water in.</li>
  <li><strong>Use a lot.</strong> A baby with widespread eczema gets through 250-500g a week. An adult with severe eczema might use 500g a week. If a tub lasts a month, you are not using enough.</li>
  <li><strong>Stroke on in the direction of hair growth</strong>, do not rub in vigorously.</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ-funded options:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Aqueous cream</strong> - widely available; works as a wash, less good as a leave-on moisturiser</li>
  <li><strong>Wool fat (urea-based) creams</strong> like Sorbolene</li>
  <li><strong>Cetomacrogol-based creams</strong> like Healtheries Cetaphil moisturising cream</li>
  <li><strong>Hydrous emulsifying ointment</strong> - thicker, very protective for very dry skin</li>
  <li><strong>Specialised emollients</strong> like QV Cream, QV Bath Oil, Cetaphil, Dermeze - many partially funded</li>
</ul>

<p>Ointments are more effective than creams which are more effective than lotions, but oilier formulations are also less popular. Use what your child will tolerate - the best moisturiser is the one that gets used.</p>

<h3>Pillar 2: topical steroid for flares</h3>

<p>This is the part where most parents have been given confusing advice. Topical steroids are essential, effective, and safe when used correctly. Used incorrectly (sporadically, in too-low strengths, for too short) they fail to control flares and the eczema worsens.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Use at the FIRST sign of redness or itching</strong>, not after the flare has been raging for a week</li>
  <li><strong>Apply once or twice daily</strong> to the affected areas only</li>
  <li><strong>Continue for at least 7 days after the skin has settled</strong> - sub-clinical inflammation persists; stopping too early is the main reason for rebound</li>
  <li><strong>Apply emollient as well</strong> - usually 30 minutes apart, or one before bath / one after</li>
  <li><strong>Use the right strength for the site:</strong>
    <ul>
      <li>Face, neck, groin, eyelids: hydrocortisone 1% (mild) for short courses</li>
      <li>Body and limbs: betamethasone valerate 0.1% (moderate-strong)</li>
      <li>Hands, feet, lichenified skin: methylprednisolone aceponate (Advantan) or stronger if specialist-guided</li>
    </ul>
  </li>
</ul>

<h3>The fingertip unit</h3>

<p>The amount of steroid cream squeezed from a 5mm-nozzle tube along the length of the last segment of an adult finger. Roughly 0.5g. Covers an area the size of two adult palms. Worth knowing because it is a more reliable measure than "a thin smear".</p>

<h2>Steroid concerns - the honest answer</h2>

<p>Topical steroids used as above do not cause:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Skin thinning that is meaningful or visible in normal use</li>
  <li>Adrenal suppression in normal doses on intact skin</li>
  <li>Long-term harm to bones or growth</li>
  <li>"Steroid addiction" or rebound when used and stopped appropriately</li>
</ul>

<p>"Topical steroid withdrawal" is a real phenomenon associated with prolonged inappropriate use of high-potency steroids on the face and other sensitive sites. It is not a reason to under-treat ordinary childhood eczema.</p>

<h2>Bathing</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Daily bath or shower in lukewarm water, 5-10 minutes</li>
  <li>Avoid hot water (worsens itch)</li>
  <li>Use bath emollients (QV Bath Oil, hydrous emulsifying ointment as wash) instead of soap</li>
  <li>Pat dry, apply moisturiser within 3 minutes</li>
</ul>

<h2>Wet wraps for severe flares</h2>

<p>For widespread or stubborn flares, wet wrapping over emollient and steroid can dramatically settle the skin in 2-3 days. Get a Plunket or paediatric nurse to demonstrate the first time. Worth knowing the technique exists.</p>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<p>Most childhood eczema is constitutional - the skin will flare without obvious triggers. But common contributors:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Heat, sweating, woollen or synthetic fabrics</li>
  <li>Soap, bubble bath, scented products</li>
  <li>Pet fur, dust, pollens</li>
  <li>Saliva (drool around the mouth in babies)</li>
  <li>Stress, illness, teething</li>
  <li>Food allergies in a small minority of children, particularly with severe eczema in the first year - but routine food elimination diets do not help most children and can cause nutritional issues</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Eczema not controlled on regular emollient + topical steroid for 2-3 weeks</li>
  <li>Suspected infection - weeping, yellow crusts, painful, child unwell with fever (impetigo, eczema herpeticum)</li>
  <li>Eczema affecting eating, sleeping, school, or family life</li>
  <li>Wanting to discuss food allergy testing</li>
  <li>Eczema with growth issues or recurrent infection - rare immune issues to consider</li>
  <li>To get a written eczema action plan</li>
</ul>

<h2>What might come next if your GP is involved</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Higher-strength topical steroids</li>
  <li>Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) - useful for face and as steroid-sparing</li>
  <li>Phototherapy for older children</li>
  <li>Specialist referral for biologics in severe disease - dupilumab is now funded in NZ for moderate-severe eczema in children with Special Authority</li>
</ul>
