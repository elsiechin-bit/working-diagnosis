Molluscum contagiosum is a very common, harmless skin infection caused by a poxvirus. It is most often seen in children aged 1-10. The spots can look alarming - shiny, dome-shaped, sometimes with a tiny dimple in the centre - but they are not dangerous, do not scar, and almost always clear by themselves. The main thing parents need is reassurance and patience.

<h2>What to look for</h2>

<ul>
<li>Small (1-5 mm) round, shiny, flesh-coloured or pearly bumps</li>
<li>Often a tiny central dimple (umbilication) - the diagnostic sign</li>
<li>Usually painless</li>
<li>Sometimes itchy or surrounded by a patch of eczema</li>
<li>Occur in clusters, often in skin folds (armpits, behind knees, groin) or on trunk and limbs</li>
<li>Most children have between 5 and 30; sometimes more, sometimes just one or two</li>
</ul>

<p>In adults, molluscum on genital skin is treated as a sexually transmitted infection - see a sexual health clinic.</p>

<h2>How it spreads</h2>

<ul>
<li>Skin-to-skin contact - including in childcare, swimming pool changing rooms, family beds</li>
<li>Shared towels, sponges, bath toys</li>
<li>Picking or scratching can spread spots to nearby skin (auto-inoculation)</li>
</ul>

<p>The virus is mildly contagious. Children with molluscum can attend school and daycare. There is no need to keep them off swimming, but if there are visible spots it is helpful to cover with a waterproof plaster or rash guard.</p>

<h2>Natural course</h2>

<p>Without any treatment, molluscum:</p>

<ul>
<li>Usually clears within 12-18 months in healthy children, sometimes up to 2-4 years</li>
<li>Each individual spot lasts about 6-12 weeks</li>
<li>New spots often keep appearing while old ones resolve</li>
<li>Often becomes red, crusty, or pus-filled briefly just before resolving (the "BOTE" sign - "beginning of the end") - this is the immune system clearing it, not infection</li>
<li>Heals without a scar in most children</li>
</ul>

<p>Once cleared, immunity is usually long-lasting. Re-infection is uncommon.</p>

<h2>Should we treat?</h2>

<p>Most paediatric molluscum is left alone. Reasons to consider treatment:</p>

<ul>
<li>Spots in visible places causing teasing</li>
<li>Many spots persisting for over 18-24 months</li>
<li>Eczema flaring around the molluscum (treat the eczema with steroid cream and emollients)</li>
<li>Older children unhappy with appearance</li>
<li>Genital spots in adults</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatments do not work brilliantly and can be uncomfortable - the trade-off is real. For most families, watchful waiting is best.</p>

<h2>Treatment options</h2>

<p><strong>Squeezing or piercing.</strong> Some parents are taught to squeeze out the central core after a warm bath. Effective but uncomfortable; risk of bleeding and minor scarring. Not for the faint-hearted child.</p>

<p><strong>Cantharidin (in clinic).</strong> A blistering agent applied by GP or dermatologist; well-tolerated by most children.</p>

<p><strong>Cryotherapy.</strong> Liquid nitrogen freezing - effective but painful.</p>

<p><strong>Curettage.</strong> Scraping individual spots out under local anaesthetic cream; quick but can scar.</p>

<p><strong>Imiquimod, potassium hydroxide, salicylic acid.</strong> Mixed evidence; sometimes used.</p>

<p><strong>Berdazimer gel</strong> (newer, not widely available in NZ).</p>

<p>None of these is consistently better than waiting it out, so the choice depends on how bothered the family is.</p>

<h2>What about the eczema patch around them?</h2>

<p>About 1 in 5 children develop an itchy eczematous patch around their molluscum. Treat with emollients and a brief course of mild topical steroid (e.g. hydrocortisone 1%) - this does not spread the molluscum and helps the child stop scratching.</p>

<h2>Tips for home</h2>

<ul>
<li>Avoid sharing towels and bath water between siblings during active spots</li>
<li>Cut nails short to reduce scratching damage</li>
<li>Keep skin moisturised - dry skin is more likely to develop new spots</li>
<li>Cover spots in visible locations with a plaster or clothing if going to swimming or contact sport</li>
<li>Reassure children that the spots are not dangerous and will go away</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>You are not sure of the diagnosis</li>
<li>The spots become very inflamed, painful, or covered in yellow crust (could be secondary bacterial infection)</li>
<li>Spots on or near the eyelid - rarely cause conjunctivitis</li>
<li>Many widespread spots in someone immunocompromised</li>
<li>Adult molluscum on the genitals (sexual health clinic)</li>
</ul>
