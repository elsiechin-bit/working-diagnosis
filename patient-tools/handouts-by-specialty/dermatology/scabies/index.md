Scabies is an itchy skin condition caused by tiny mites that burrow into the top layer of skin. It is contagious, sometimes very itchy, and very treatable - but treatment fails in some families because parts of the plan are skipped. This handout walks through the full programme.

<h2>What scabies looks like</h2>

<ul>
<li>Intense itching, classically worse at night</li>
<li>Small red bumps, sometimes in pairs, scattered or in clusters</li>
<li>Tiny silvery burrows (1-10 mm long) in the skin - look between fingers and toes, on wrists, around the navel</li>
<li>Red-brown crusty patches in skin folds</li>
<li>In babies and young children: spots on the palms, soles, scalp, and face (less common in adults)</li>
<li>In men: itchy bumps on the penis and scrotum</li>
<li>In women: itchy bumps around nipples and lower abdomen</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms usually start 4-6 weeks after first infection - the immune system needs time to react. With re-infection, itch can begin within 1-3 days.</p>

<h2>How it spreads</h2>

<ul>
<li>Prolonged skin-to-skin contact - hugging, sharing a bed, holding babies on a hip</li>
<li>Within households, schools, rest homes, prisons</li>
<li>Less commonly: shared clothes, bedding, towels (mites survive 24-72 hours off skin)</li>
<li>Sex - so genital scabies is treated as both a parasitic and sexually transmitted concern</li>
</ul>

<p>Brief contacts (handshakes, public transport seats) almost never spread scabies.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Usually clinical, based on the pattern and history. Your GP may use a magnifying lens to look for burrows. In doubtful cases, a skin scraping looked at under a microscope can show the mite or eggs.</p>

<h2>Treatment - the family approach</h2>

<p>This is critical: <strong>treat everyone in the household and all close contacts at the same time</strong>, including those without itch. Mites can be present without symptoms.</p>

<p>The standard treatment in NZ is <strong>permethrin 5% cream</strong> (Lyclear, A-Scabies):</p>

<ol>
<li>Apply in the evening, after a bath or shower (skin should be cool and dry, not hot)</li>
<li>Apply to <strong>every inch</strong> of skin from chin to soles of the feet - including:
  <ul>
  <li>Behind the ears</li>
  <li>Between fingers and toes (use a cotton bud)</li>
  <li>Under fingernails and toenails (cut nails first)</li>
  <li>Around the navel</li>
  <li>Genital and anal areas</li>
  <li>Buttock cleft</li>
  </ul>
</li>
<li>For infants and the elderly, also include the scalp and face (avoiding the eyes)</li>
<li>Leave on for at least 8 hours (overnight is easiest), then wash off</li>
<li>Repeat the whole application 7 days later</li>
</ol>

<p>One tube treats one adult; a family of 4 needs 4-5 tubes. Permethrin is sold over the counter but is cheap on prescription.</p>

<p><strong>Oral ivermectin</strong> is an alternative for resistant cases or institutional outbreaks. Two doses, one week apart. Prescribed by GP - not first-line in most NZ cases but useful when topical treatment is impractical (large families, rest home outbreaks, crusted scabies).</p>

<h2>The clean-up</h2>

<p>On the morning after treatment, in parallel with the cream:</p>

<ul>
<li>Hot wash (60°C+) all clothes, towels, sheets, pillowcases used in the past 4 days</li>
<li>Tumble dry on hot or iron - heat kills mites</li>
<li>Items that cannot be washed: seal in a plastic bag for 7 days. Mites die without skin contact</li>
<li>Vacuum mattresses, sofas, car seats, and the floor</li>
<li>Soft toys: bag for 7 days or hot wash</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not need to spray the house with insecticide, treat pets, or replace furniture.</p>

<h2>Itching after treatment</h2>

<p>The itch can persist for 2-6 weeks even after successful treatment - this is the immune response settling, not active infection. To manage:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cool baths and emollients (Cetaphil, QV, aqueous cream)</li>
<li>A mild topical steroid (hydrocortisone 1%) for itchy patches</li>
<li>Sedating antihistamine at bedtime (chlorpheniramine, promethazine) for sleep disruption</li>
</ul>

<p>If itching is worsening, new burrows appear, or it is still bothersome at 6 weeks, see your GP - re-treatment may be needed.</p>

<h2>Crusted (Norwegian) scabies</h2>

<p>A severe form with thick scaly crusts and millions of mites. Highly contagious. More common in elderly, immunosuppressed, or those with severe disability. Always needs combined topical and oral ivermectin and is usually managed with specialist input.</p>

<h2>Contacts</h2>

<p>Treat:</p>

<ul>
<li>Everyone living in the same house</li>
<li>Sexual partners from the past 8 weeks</li>
<li>Anyone you have shared a bed or had close prolonged skin contact with</li>
<li>Childcare and rest home contacts may need treating in outbreaks - the public health team will guide</li>
</ul>

<h2>School and work</h2>

<p>Children can return to school 24 hours after starting treatment. Adults can return to work the same day, provided treatment is complete.</p>
