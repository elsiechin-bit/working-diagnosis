Psoriasis is a chronic, immune-driven skin condition that affects around 2-3% of New Zealanders. It causes thick, scaly patches that can itch, burn, or crack. There is no cure, but a lot can be done to keep it under control. Many people achieve clear or near-clear skin with modern treatment.

<h2>What it looks like</h2>

<p>Most common: <strong>chronic plaque psoriasis</strong>. Well-defined red or pink patches with silvery scale, typically on:</p>

<ul>
<li>Elbows and knees</li>
<li>Lower back and trunk</li>
<li>Scalp (often the first site)</li>
<li>Behind the ears</li>
<li>Nails - pitting, thickening, separation from the nail bed</li>
</ul>

<p>Less common forms:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Guttate psoriasis</strong> - small drop-like spots, often after a streptococcal sore throat in young people</li>
<li><strong>Inverse psoriasis</strong> - smooth red patches in skin folds (groin, under breasts, armpits)</li>
<li><strong>Pustular psoriasis</strong> - white pustules on red skin, can be localised or rarely whole-body</li>
<li><strong>Erythrodermic psoriasis</strong> - widespread bright red skin with peeling - urgent, see your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>It is not just skin</h2>

<p>Psoriasis is a systemic inflammatory condition. Around 1 in 3 people develop <strong>psoriatic arthritis</strong> - joint pain and stiffness, often in fingers and toes. Tell your GP if you have new joint pain, swelling, or morning stiffness.</p>

<p>Psoriasis also raises the risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, depression, and inflammatory bowel disease.&sup1; Regular blood pressure, cholesterol, and diabetes screening are part of good psoriasis care.</p>

<h2>Triggers</h2>

<p>Common triggers for flares:</p>

<ul>
<li>Stress</li>
<li>Skin injury (cuts, scratches, sunburn) - new patches can appear in damaged areas (Koebner phenomenon)</li>
<li>Infections, especially streptococcal throat infections</li>
<li>Some medicines - lithium, beta blockers, antimalarials, sudden withdrawal of oral steroids</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Heavy alcohol use</li>
<li>Cold, dry weather</li>
</ul>

<p>Identifying personal triggers takes time. Keep a flare diary if it is not obvious.</p>

<h2>Day-to-day skin care</h2>

<ul>
<li>Moisturise daily, ideally twice. Thick emollients (Cetaphil, QV cream, white soft paraffin) work better than thin lotions</li>
<li>Avoid soap and hot baths - use soap-free wash or aqueous cream</li>
<li>Pat dry, do not rub</li>
<li>Sunlight in moderation helps most people - 15-30 minutes of midday sun several times a week is often enough. Always avoid burning</li>
<li>Manage scalp psoriasis with coal tar shampoo (T-Gel, Polytar) or salicylic acid preparations</li>
</ul>

<h2>Topical treatments</h2>

<p>For mild to moderate psoriasis, topical creams work well for most people:</p>

<p><strong>Topical steroids</strong> - moderate to potent strength for body, mild for face and folds. See our <a href="/handouts/topical-steroid-creams/">steroid creams handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Vitamin D analogues</strong> (calcipotriol, often combined with steroid as Daivobet/Daivonex). Slower-acting but good for maintenance.</p>

<p><strong>Coal tar</strong> - older but effective; messy and smells.</p>

<p><strong>Salicylic acid</strong> - thins scale to let other treatments penetrate.</p>

<p>Apply consistently and to thickness - psoriasis needs more product than eczema.</p>

<h2>Phototherapy</h2>

<p>Narrowband UVB phototherapy in a hospital or specialist clinic is highly effective for moderate psoriasis. Sessions are 2-3 times per week for 8-12 weeks. Available through public hospitals (referred by GP) and some private clinics.</p>

<h2>Systemic and biologic treatment</h2>

<p>For moderate to severe psoriasis or psoriatic arthritis, oral or injected medicines:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Methotrexate</strong> - weekly tablet, has been used for decades</li>
<li><strong>Ciclosporin</strong> - short-term use for flares</li>
<li><strong>Acitretin</strong> - oral retinoid</li>
<li><strong>Biologic injections</strong> (adalimumab, secukinumab, ixekizumab, risankizumab, others) - target specific immune signals; can clear severe psoriasis very effectively. PHARMAC funds these for moderate-severe psoriasis with strict criteria. Referral to dermatology required</li>
</ul>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking - the strongest modifiable trigger</li>
<li>Limit alcohol - heavy drinking flares psoriasis and worsens response to treatment</li>
<li>Lose weight if overweight - even modest loss improves psoriasis severity and treatment response</li>
<li>Manage stress - mindfulness, exercise, counselling all help</li>
<li>Treat strep throat infections promptly</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mental health</h2>

<p>Psoriasis carries a substantial emotional burden. Visible patches affect work, relationships, and mood. Depression and anxiety rates are roughly double the general population.&sup2; Tell your GP if you are struggling - support, counselling, and treatment of the psoriasis itself all help.</p>
