Topical steroids are the mainstay of treatment for eczema and many other inflammatory skin conditions. They have been used for over 60 years and, when used properly, are safe and very effective. Many people use too little, too late, and stop too soon - which is why eczema flares hang around. This handout explains how to get the best from them.

<h2>How they work</h2>

<p>Topical steroids reduce inflammation. They calm down the redness, itch, and scaling. They do not cure the underlying tendency to eczema, but they reset the skin so other measures (moisturisers, trigger management) can keep it well.</p>

<h2>The four strengths</h2>

<p>Steroids are grouped by potency:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mild:</strong> hydrocortisone 1%. Available over-the-counter. Used on face, eyelids, groin, and infants</li>
<li><strong>Moderate:</strong> clobetasone, betamethasone valerate 0.05%, triamcinolone. Used on body</li>
<li><strong>Potent:</strong> betamethasone valerate 0.1%, methylprednisolone aceponate (Advantan), mometasone (Elocon). Used for stubborn body eczema, scalp, hands, feet</li>
<li><strong>Very potent:</strong> clobetasol propionate (Dermol). Used short-term for very stubborn areas</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP picks the strength based on the area of body, severity, and age. Stronger does not always mean better - matching the steroid to the site is key.</p>

<h2>How much to use - the fingertip unit</h2>

<p>One <strong>fingertip unit (FTU)</strong> is the amount of cream squeezed from a normal tube tip onto the end of an adult finger, from fingertip to the first crease. One FTU treats an area of skin equal to two adult palms.</p>

<p>Examples:</p>

<ul>
<li>Face and neck: about 2.5 FTU</li>
<li>One whole arm: 3 FTU</li>
<li>One whole leg: 6 FTU</li>
<li>Trunk front: 7 FTU</li>
<li>Trunk back: 7 FTU</li>
<li>One hand (both sides): 1 FTU</li>
</ul>

<p>For children, use proportionally less - your GP can show you. Most people apply too little; a thin film that just glistens is what you want.</p>

<h2>How to apply</h2>

<ol>
<li>Apply once or twice a day to the inflamed (red, itchy, rough) skin only - not all over</li>
<li>A thin film, gently rubbed in</li>
<li>Best applied after a bath or shower while skin is slightly damp</li>
<li>Wait 15-20 minutes before applying moisturiser, or apply moisturiser first then steroid</li>
<li>Wash hands afterwards (unless treating hands)</li>
</ol>

<p>Continue until the skin feels smooth and the itch settles - this is usually 7-14 days. Stopping early is the most common reason flares come back. Some people benefit from "weekend therapy" - applying steroid two days a week to the spots that keep flaring.</p>

<h2>Common worries (most are not borne out by evidence)</h2>

<p><strong>Skin thinning.</strong> Yes, this can happen with prolonged use of strong steroids on thin skin (face, eyelids, groin), or daily use for many months. With sensible use guided by your GP, it is uncommon. Skin thickening from chronic eczema is far more disfiguring.</p>

<p><strong>Absorption into the bloodstream.</strong> Tiny amounts get through, but levels are clinically insignificant unless you use very large quantities of strong steroid daily for months. Even infants can use mild and moderate steroids safely.</p>

<p><strong>Topical steroid withdrawal.</strong> Rare and almost only seen after very prolonged misuse of potent steroids on the face. Not a concern for typical, GP-guided use.</p>

<p><strong>Eczema getting worse if I stop.</strong> Eczema is a chronic relapsing condition. Flares come back because of the underlying tendency, not because the steroid was used.</p>

<h2>Where to be cautious</h2>

<p><strong>Face and eyelids:</strong> use mild steroid only, for short courses (1-2 weeks). Stronger steroids can thin the skin or worsen rosacea/perioral dermatitis.</p>

<p><strong>Groin and armpits:</strong> mild only, short course. The skin folds increase absorption.</p>

<p><strong>Children under 1:</strong> mild steroid (hydrocortisone 1%). Use moderate only if the GP advises.</p>

<p><strong>Infections:</strong> do not apply to obviously infected skin (yellow crust, oozing pus, fever). See your GP - antibiotics may be needed first or alongside.</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>Most flares settle within 7-10 days of regular treatment. If not, see your GP. Possibilities include: not enough steroid, secondary infection, the wrong diagnosis (e.g. tinea), or a need for stronger treatment.</p>

<h2>Maintenance</h2>

<p>Once eczema is settled, the work continues:</p>

<ul>
<li>Moisturise twice daily even when skin looks normal (the single best thing to prevent flares)</li>
<li>Avoid known triggers (soap, hot water, wool, fragrances)</li>
<li>Use steroid early at the first sign of a flare - do not wait until it is severe</li>
<li>Keep the prescription handy; do not run out</li>
</ul>
