Warts are small rough growths caused by human papillomavirus (HPV). They are extremely common, especially in school-aged children. Most warts are harmless, and around two-thirds disappear on their own within two years. If they bother you, several treatments can help.

<h2>Types you might see</h2>

<p><strong>Common warts</strong> - rough, raised lumps with a "cauliflower" surface, usually on hands, fingers, or knees.</p>

<p><strong>Plantar warts (verrucae)</strong> - on the soles of the feet, often flattened by pressure with tiny black dots (clotted capillaries) inside. Can be painful when standing.</p>

<p><strong>Flat warts</strong> - smaller, smoother, flesh-coloured. Often appear in groups on face, arms, or legs.</p>

<p><strong>Filiform warts</strong> - thread-like projections, often on face, eyelids, or neck.</p>

<p><strong>Genital warts</strong> - require a different approach (see your GP or sexual health clinic). The HPV vaccine prevents most of these.</p>

<h2>How they spread</h2>

<p>Through skin-to-skin contact and contaminated surfaces (gym mats, swimming pool floors, shared towels). The virus enters through tiny breaks in the skin. Spreading to other parts of your own body is also common, especially with picking or shaving.</p>

<h2>The "do nothing" option</h2>

<p>Most warts disappear by themselves, helped by your immune system. In children, two-thirds clear within two years.&sup1; If a wart is small, painless, and not in a visible place, leaving it alone is reasonable.</p>

<h2>Self-treatment options</h2>

<p>Several work, none works for everyone, and most need patience.</p>

<p><strong>Salicylic acid</strong> (Wartoff, Duofilm, Compound W). The best-evidenced home treatment.</p>

<ul>
<li>Soak the wart in warm water for 5-10 minutes</li>
<li>Gently file the surface with a pumice or emery board (kept just for warts)</li>
<li>Apply the solution directly to the wart - protect surrounding skin with petroleum jelly</li>
<li>Cover with a plaster overnight</li>
<li>Repeat daily for 8-12 weeks - persistence is key</li>
</ul>

<p>Cure rate around 50-70% with consistent use over 2-3 months.</p>

<p><strong>Cryotherapy products</strong> (Wartner, Compound W Freeze) - apply once a week for 3-4 weeks. Less powerful than clinic-strength liquid nitrogen but useful for hardier warts.</p>

<p><strong>Duct tape</strong> - covering the wart for several days, then filing, has been studied with mixed results. Cheap, harmless, worth trying alongside salicylic acid.</p>

<h2>GP-administered treatments</h2>

<p>If self-treatment fails, your GP may offer:</p>

<p><strong>Cryotherapy</strong> (liquid nitrogen) - sprayed or applied with a cotton bud. Painful for a few seconds, sometimes blisters afterwards. Repeated every 2-3 weeks. Works for about 70% of warts after 3-4 sessions.</p>

<p><strong>Cantharidin</strong>, <strong>imiquimod</strong>, <strong>podophyllotoxin</strong>, <strong>5-fluorouracil cream</strong> - sometimes used for stubborn cases.</p>

<p><strong>Curettage and cautery</strong> - scraping the wart out under local anaesthetic. Useful for one or two stubborn warts; can leave a scar.</p>

<p><strong>Specialist treatments</strong> (laser, intralesional bleomycin, immunotherapy) - reserved for resistant warts.</p>

<h2>Verrucae (plantar warts)</h2>

<p>The same approaches as common warts, but verrucae are tougher because of the pressure on the sole. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Soak and file regularly - dead tissue blocks treatment penetration</li>
<li>Salicylic acid plasters specially designed for verrucae work well</li>
<li>Cushion painful verrucae with a doughnut-shaped pad</li>
<li>Wear flip-flops in public showers to avoid spread to others</li>
<li>Some sport schools require covered verrucae for swimming</li>
</ul>

<h2>What not to do</h2>

<ul>
<li>Do not pick or scratch - this spreads the virus to other parts of your skin</li>
<li>Do not share emery boards, pumice stones, towels, or socks</li>
<li>Do not shave over warts - it spreads them along the shave line</li>
<li>Do not apply over-the-counter wart products to face, genital, or anal areas without GP advice</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wart on the face that you are unhappy with</li>
<li>Painful verruca interfering with walking</li>
<li>Wart that does not improve after 12 weeks of home treatment</li>
<li>Wart that bleeds easily, changes colour, or grows quickly (rarely, skin cancers can mimic warts)</li>
<li>Many warts, or warts in someone immunosuppressed</li>
<li>Genital or anal warts</li>
</ul>

<h2>Prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Avoid walking barefoot in public showers, pools, and gym changing rooms</li>
<li>Keep feet dry; sweating encourages warts</li>
<li>Cover any cuts or scrapes</li>
<li>Use the HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9) - free in NZ for everyone aged 9-26. Prevents the high-risk genital HPV types and the wart-causing types 6 and 11</li>
</ul>
