Hair loss is common and rarely a sign of serious disease, but it can be very distressing. The first step is working out what type you have, because the treatments differ. Most people have one of three patterns: pattern hair loss, telogen effluvium (shedding), or alopecia areata. This handout covers all three and several others.

<h2>How hair grows</h2>

<p>Each follicle cycles between growth (anagen, 2-7 years), transition (catagen, weeks), and rest (telogen, 2-4 months). About 90% of hairs are growing at any time. Losing 50-150 hairs a day is normal - you simply do not notice them on most days.</p>

<h2>Pattern hair loss (androgenetic alopecia)</h2>

<p>The most common cause in both men and women. Caused by genetic sensitivity of follicles to androgens.</p>

<p><strong>In men:</strong> receding temples and thinning crown, eventually meeting on top. Usually starts in 20s-30s but can be earlier or later.</p>

<p><strong>In women:</strong> diffuse thinning across the top of the scalp, with the front hairline preserved. The parting widens. Usually starts after 40 but can be earlier.</p>

<p>Treatments:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Minoxidil</strong> (Regaine, Hair-A-Gain) - 2% or 5% lotion or foam. Apply twice daily. Available without prescription. Slows progression and partly reverses thinning in 50-70% of users. Effects are gradual (4-6 months to see) and stop if you stop using it</li>
<li><strong>Oral finasteride</strong> (1 mg, men only) - blocks the hormone that miniaturises follicles. Effective in around 80% of men. Side effects (sexual side effects, mood) are uncommon but should be discussed with your GP</li>
<li><strong>Oral minoxidil</strong> (low dose, 0.25-1 mg daily) - increasingly used for both men and women. Side effects include facial hair growth and ankle swelling</li>
<li><strong>Spironolactone</strong> - off-label for women with pattern hair loss, especially with PCOS or high androgens</li>
<li><strong>Hair transplant</strong> - private, expensive, but effective for stable pattern loss</li>
<li><strong>Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections</strong> - some evidence; multiple sessions needed; private</li>
</ul>

<p>Cosmetic options (volumising shampoos, fibres like Toppik, scarves, hair pieces, wigs) are valid choices and can transform appearance without medication.</p>

<h2>Telogen effluvium (shedding)</h2>

<p>Sudden increase in shedding - clumps in the brush or shower, hair on pillows, ponytail thinning. Caused by an upstream stressor that pushes more follicles into the resting phase.</p>

<p>Common triggers (typically 2-4 months before the shed):</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe illness or fever (including COVID-19)</li>
<li>Surgery</li>
<li>Major emotional stress or bereavement</li>
<li>Postpartum (3-4 months after birth)</li>
<li>Significant weight loss or restrictive diet</li>
<li>Iron deficiency</li>
<li>Thyroid disease</li>
<li>Medicines (some antidepressants, beta blockers, anticonvulsants, retinoids)</li>
</ul>

<p>The good news: telogen effluvium is self-limiting. Hair grows back over 6-12 months once the trigger has passed. Your GP will check iron studies (ferritin), thyroid, and review medications. Eat well, manage stress, and be patient - new short hairs around the hairline are a sign of regrowth.</p>

<h2>Alopecia areata</h2>

<p>An autoimmune condition that causes round bald patches, usually 2-5 cm across, with smooth pink skin and no scaling. Most patches regrow within months. Occasionally progresses to total scalp loss (alopecia totalis) or whole-body loss (alopecia universalis).</p>

<p>Treatments:</p>

<ul>
<li>Watchful waiting - many small patches regrow on their own within 6-12 months</li>
<li>Topical or injected corticosteroids - first line for limited disease</li>
<li>Topical minoxidil</li>
<li>Topical anthralin or contact immunotherapy (specialist clinics)</li>
<li>Oral JAK inhibitors (baricitinib, ritlecitinib) - very effective for severe alopecia areata; specialist prescribing, mostly private in NZ</li>
</ul>

<p>Alopecia areata is sometimes associated with thyroid disease, vitiligo, or other autoimmune conditions - your GP may screen.</p>

<h2>Other causes</h2>

<p><strong>Traction alopecia</strong> - from tight hairstyles (braids, ponytails, buns, extensions). Reverses if caught early; permanent if pulled for years.</p>

<p><strong>Trichotillomania</strong> - hair pulling. Patches with broken hairs of varying lengths. Treated with cognitive behavioural therapy.</p>

<p><strong>Scarring alopecias</strong> (lichen planopilaris, frontal fibrosing alopecia, central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia, discoid lupus) - inflammation destroys follicles permanently. Early diagnosis matters - prompt referral to dermatology can stop progression.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea capitis</strong> - fungal scalp infection in children. See our <a href="/handouts/tinea/">tinea handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Chemotherapy-related</strong> - extensive but usually fully reversible.</p>

<h2>What your GP will check</h2>

<ul>
<li>Pattern of loss (where, how it spreads)</li>
<li>Onset and rate</li>
<li>Family history</li>
<li>General health, recent illnesses, weight changes</li>
<li>Medications and supplements</li>
<li>Stress, sleep, diet</li>
<li>Periods and pregnancy history (women)</li>
<li>Skin and nail signs (autoimmune clues)</li>
</ul>

<p>Tests often include: full blood count, ferritin, thyroid function, vitamin D. Other tests if specific causes are suspected.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<ul>
<li>Eat enough protein (60-90 g per day for an average adult) - hair is essentially keratin protein</li>
<li>Avoid crash dieting</li>
<li>Treat iron deficiency to ferritin above 50-70 ng/mL</li>
<li>Manage thyroid disease</li>
<li>Wash with gentle shampoo - avoid harsh treatments while shedding</li>
<li>Use heat tools and bleach sparingly</li>
<li>Loosen hairstyles - traction is preventable damage</li>
</ul>

<h2>The mental side</h2>

<p>Hair loss matters. Anxiety, low mood, and reduced self-esteem are common. Tell your GP if it is wearing you down - support, counselling, and effective treatment all help. Online communities (Alopecia NZ) connect you with others living with hair loss.</p>
