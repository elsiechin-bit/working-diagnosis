Most moles never become cancer. The challenge is knowing which changes matter. This handout helps you decide when to come back to your GP and when a spot can be safely watched.

<h2>The big rule: change matters more than appearance</h2>

<p>A mole that has looked the same for 30 years is almost always fine, even if it is bumpy, dark, or large. A mole that has changed in the last few months deserves attention - even a small spot. The single most important warning sign for melanoma is <strong>change</strong>.</p>

<h2>Changes that warrant a GP visit</h2>

<ul>
<li>Getting larger (over weeks to months)</li>
<li>Changing colour - new black, blue, red, white, or skin-coloured areas in a previously uniform spot</li>
<li>New shape - asymmetry developing, ragged or notched edges</li>
<li>Itching, tingling, or pain</li>
<li>Bleeding, oozing, or crusting that returns</li>
<li>Lump arising within or alongside an existing flat mole</li>
<li>Pigment spreading beyond the edge into surrounding skin</li>
<li>A new spot in someone over 30</li>
</ul>

<p>A useful prompt: "Does this look the same as it did six months ago?" If not, see your GP.</p>

<h2>Changes that are usually OK to watch</h2>

<ul>
<li>Mild fading or darkening with sun exposure (most moles do this)</li>
<li>A small amount of even thickening over years</li>
<li>Coarse hairs growing through a mole - this is normal and reassuring</li>
<li>Mole catching on clothing or shaving and bleeding briefly - if it heals normally and goes back to its usual look, this is mechanical, not cancer</li>
</ul>

<p>If in doubt, see your GP. Reassurance is free and quick.</p>

<h2>Photo monitoring at home</h2>

<p>For moles you are watching, take a photograph:</p>

<ul>
<li>Use good light</li>
<li>Include a small ruler or coin in the frame for size</li>
<li>Take a close-up plus a wider context shot showing the spot's location</li>
<li>Repeat every 2-3 months and compare</li>
<li>Save photos in a dedicated album labelled by date and body site</li>
</ul>

<p>Phone-based mole-tracking apps exist; they help with logging but should not replace clinical assessment.</p>

<h2>How long to watch before deciding</h2>

<p>If a flat mole has been mildly changing over weeks, a 2-4 week period of monitoring with photos is reasonable - but only if the change is subtle and you have no other concerning features. Anything new, fast-growing, bleeding, or symptomatic should be reviewed promptly.</p>

<p>Nodular melanoma (a fast-growing lump) does not give you the luxury of months of watching. New raised growth that gets bigger week-to-week is a same-week GP visit.</p>

<h2>If your GP says "watch and review"</h2>

<p>This is a common and reasonable plan for spots that are slightly atypical but not obviously suspicious. Make sure:</p>

<ul>
<li>You have a clear timeframe ("come back in 3 months")</li>
<li>The location and appearance are documented (photo or dermoscopic image)</li>
<li>You know what changes would warrant earlier review</li>
<li>An appointment is booked - "come back if you are worried" is easy to forget</li>
</ul>

<h2>What gets removed</h2>

<p>Suspicious spots are excised - not just biopsied off the top - so the lab can measure depth properly. The procedure is under local anaesthetic, takes 15-30 minutes, and leaves a stitched line. Results take about a week. Most spots removed turn out to be benign, but the certainty is worth the small scar.</p>

<h2>The "junior" mole rule</h2>

<p>Most melanomas appear de novo (in previously normal skin) rather than from existing moles. So a brand-new spot in an adult is just as concerning as a changing old mole.</p>

<h2>Special places to check</h2>

<p>Areas that get missed:</p>

<ul>
<li>Scalp - part the hair regularly, especially if thinning</li>
<li>Behind ears</li>
<li>Soles of feet and between toes</li>
<li>Under finger and toenails - any new dark streak, especially if widening or extending into the surrounding skin (Hutchinson sign)</li>
<li>Genital area, perineum, between buttocks</li>
<li>The back - hardest to see; ask a partner</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you have many moles</h2>

<p>People with 50+ moles, atypical moles, or a personal/family history of melanoma may benefit from regular surveillance and mole-mapping. Discuss with your GP. Some areas have access to dermatology clinics for high-risk patients; private mole-mapping services are widely available.</p>
