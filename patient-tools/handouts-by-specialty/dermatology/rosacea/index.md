Rosacea is a common chronic facial skin condition. It typically affects fair-skinned adults aged 30-60. The hallmark is persistent redness across the cheeks, nose, chin, and forehead, sometimes with small bumps that look like acne. Rosacea cannot be cured, but most people can keep it well-controlled with the right approach.

<h2>How rosacea looks</h2>

<p>Four overlapping patterns (most people have features of more than one):</p>

<p><strong>1. Erythematotelangiectatic.</strong> Persistent redness with visible small blood vessels (telangiectasia) on the cheeks and nose. Easy flushing.</p>

<p><strong>2. Papulopustular.</strong> Red bumps and small pus-filled spots on a background of redness. No blackheads (this is what distinguishes it from acne).</p>

<p><strong>3. Phymatous.</strong> Thickening of the skin, especially on the nose (rhinophyma). More common in men.</p>

<p><strong>4. Ocular rosacea.</strong> Red, gritty, watery eyes; styes; blepharitis. Often missed.</p>

<h2>What rosacea is not</h2>

<p>Rosacea is sometimes confused with acne, lupus, or simple sun damage. It is not caused by poor hygiene, alcoholism, or aggressive cleaning. People who blush easily are at higher risk; those of Celtic or northern European descent are most affected.</p>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<p>Most people identify a personal pattern over time. Frequent triggers:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sun exposure</li>
<li>Heat - hot drinks, spicy food, hot showers, saunas</li>
<li>Cold wind</li>
<li>Alcohol, especially red wine</li>
<li>Stress and embarrassment</li>
<li>Strenuous exercise</li>
<li>Hot, humid weather</li>
<li>Topical irritants - alcohol-based toners, fragranced cosmetics, harsh exfoliants</li>
<li>Some skin care ingredients - menthol, witch hazel, peppermint, eucalyptus</li>
<li>Topical steroids on the face (long-term use can cause "steroid rosacea")</li>
</ul>

<p>Keep a 2-week trigger diary if patterns are not obvious.</p>

<h2>Daily skin care</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wash with lukewarm water and a gentle non-soap cleanser - twice daily, no harder</li>
<li>Pat dry, do not rub</li>
<li>Apply a bland moisturiser daily (Cetaphil, QV, La Roche-Posay Toleriane all work for many)</li>
<li>SPF 30+ every morning, year-round - mineral (zinc/titanium) sunscreens are usually best tolerated</li>
<li>Avoid alcohol-based products, fragrances, "active" ingredients (retinol, AHAs/BHAs) unless guided</li>
<li>Make-up: green-tinted primer can neutralise redness; mineral foundation is well tolerated</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medical treatments</h2>

<p>Treatment depends on which features are most troublesome.</p>

<p><strong>For redness:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Brimonidine gel (Mirvaso) - reduces redness for several hours; can rebound</li>
<li>Vascular laser or IPL - reduces visible vessels and overall flush. Mostly private, several sessions usually needed</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>For bumps and pustules:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Metronidazole 0.75% gel/cream - apply twice daily for 8-12 weeks</li>
<li>Ivermectin 1% cream (Soolantra) - apply nightly; effective for bumpy rosacea</li>
<li>Azelaic acid 15% gel/cream - alternative or add-on</li>
<li>Oral antibiotics - low-dose doxycycline (40-100 mg daily) for 6-12 weeks for moderate to severe disease</li>
<li>Oral isotretinoin - for severe or refractory rosacea, specialist-supervised</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>For ocular rosacea:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Daily warm compresses to the eyelids</li>
<li>Lid hygiene with diluted baby shampoo or proprietary lid wipes</li>
<li>Artificial tears for dryness</li>
<li>Doxycycline if severe; ophthalmology referral for sight-threatening disease</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>For phymatous rosacea (nose thickening):</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Early oral isotretinoin can prevent progression</li>
<li>Established rhinophyma is treated surgically (laser, electrosurgery, dermabrasion) - dermatology or plastics referral</li>
</ul>

<h2>Steroid creams - avoid on the face</h2>

<p>Steroid creams initially settle rosacea but cause it to flare worse on stopping. They can also cause perioral dermatitis. Do not use on the face for rosacea unless your dermatologist says so for a specific short course.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<ul>
<li>Identify and avoid your personal triggers</li>
<li>Keep skin care simple - the more products, the more chance of irritation</li>
<li>Daily SPF and a hat - sun is a major driver</li>
<li>Eat slowly to avoid temperature flush; cool down spicy or hot foods</li>
<li>Manage stress - rosacea worsens with chronic stress</li>
<li>Some people find a low-histamine or low-FODMAP diet helps; experiment if interested</li>
</ul>

<h2>The mental side</h2>

<p>Visible facial redness affects confidence and mood. Rates of anxiety, depression, and social avoidance are higher in rosacea than the general population.&sup1; Tell your GP if it is wearing you down. Effective skin treatment, plus support if needed, helps.</p>
