<p>A panic attack is the body's emergency alarm going off when there is no actual emergency. It feels like dying. It is not. Panic attacks are common (around 1 in 4 people will have at least one in their lifetime), they are not dangerous, and they are very treatable. This handout walks through what is happening, what to do tonight, and the longer-term steps that get the alarm to stop misfiring.</p>

<h2>What is actually happening</h2>

<p>Your body has an automatic alarm system - the fight-or-flight response - that is meant to protect you from real threats. In a panic attack, that system fires when there is no threat. The body responds as if a tiger has appeared:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Adrenaline floods the bloodstream</li>
  <li>Heart rate jumps</li>
  <li>Breathing speeds up</li>
  <li>Muscles tense</li>
  <li>Blood diverts from extremities to large muscles (causing tingling, cold hands)</li>
  <li>Digestion slows (nausea, butterflies)</li>
</ul>

<p>The brain interprets all this physical activity as evidence that something is genuinely wrong - "if I feel this bad, I must be dying" - which produces more adrenaline, which intensifies the symptoms. That feedback loop is the panic attack.</p>

<h2>What a panic attack feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Heart racing or pounding</li>
  <li>Chest tightness or pain</li>
  <li>Shortness of breath, can't take a satisfying breath</li>
  <li>Tingling in hands, face, or feet</li>
  <li>Feeling unreal, dizzy, light-headed</li>
  <li>Feeling like you are going to die, lose control, or "go crazy"</li>
  <li>Hot or cold flushes, sweating</li>
  <li>Nausea, churning stomach</li>
  <li>Trembling, shakiness</li>
</ul>

<p>It peaks within 10 minutes and almost always settles within 20-30 minutes. It cannot last forever. The body cannot sustain that level of arousal indefinitely.</p>

<h2>What is NOT happening</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are not having a heart attack. (If chest pain is part of an unfamiliar pattern, especially in someone over 40 or with cardiac risk factors, see a doctor - but for someone with a history of panic, this is panic.)</li>
  <li>You are not going to faint. Panic raises blood pressure; fainting requires a drop. Lightheaded does not mean "about to faint".</li>
  <li>You are not going to suffocate. The shortness of breath is hyperventilation, not lack of oxygen. Your blood oxygen is fine.</li>
  <li>You are not losing your mind. Feeling unreal is a known panic symptom called depersonalisation; it is unpleasant but not dangerous.</li>
  <li>You are not going to die. No one has died from a panic attack itself.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do in the moment</h2>

<h3>Slow your breathing</h3>
<p>Hyperventilation drives most panic symptoms. Slowing the breath out is the single most effective intervention.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds</li>
  <li>Breathe out through pursed lips (like blowing out a candle slowly) for 6-8 seconds</li>
  <li>Pause for 1-2 seconds</li>
  <li>Repeat for 2-3 minutes</li>
</ul>

<p>The longer out-breath activates the rest-and-digest system that counters fight-or-flight. If counting feels too hard, the goal is just: out-breath longer than in-breath.</p>

<h3>Ground yourself in the room</h3>
<p>The 5-4-3-2-1 technique. Slow yourself by naming, out loud or in your head:</p>

<ul>
  <li>5 things you can see</li>
  <li>4 things you can touch</li>
  <li>3 things you can hear</li>
  <li>2 things you can smell</li>
  <li>1 thing you can taste</li>
</ul>

<p>It works because it pulls attention from the racing internal alarm to the external real world.</p>

<h3>Cold</h3>
<p>Splash your face with cold water, hold an ice cube, take a cold drink, put your wrists under cold tap. Triggers the dive reflex which slows the heart rate.</p>

<h3>Tell yourself the truth</h3>

<ul>
  <li>"This is a panic attack."</li>
  <li>"It will peak in 5-10 minutes and pass."</li>
  <li>"I am not in actual danger."</li>
  <li>"My body is doing something it does not need to do right now. I will be okay."</li>
</ul>

<p>The aim is not to make the attack go away by force - that often makes it worse. The aim is to ride it out, knowing it will pass.</p>

<h2>What does NOT help (despite popular advice)</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Trying to control your breathing forcefully</strong> - often becomes more controlled hyperventilation. Slow exhale is the goal, not deep breathing in.</li>
  <li><strong>Drinking alcohol</strong> - reduces panic in the moment but makes anxiety worse over hours and days</li>
  <li><strong>Using a benzodiazepine "as needed"</strong> at the first sign of an attack - reinforces the belief that the attack is dangerous and requires medication. Useful as a short bridge in some specific situations, but rarely the long-term answer.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoiding situations where panic happened before</strong> - feels like it helps. Actually shrinks your life and makes future panic more likely. Avoidance is the engine of panic disorder.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What about future panic attacks?</h2>

<p>One panic attack is unsettling. Recurrent attacks plus worry about having more is "panic disorder", which affects around 1 in 100 NZ adults at any one time and is one of the most treatable mental health conditions there is.</p>

<h3>Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT)</h3>
<p>The first-line treatment. Around 70-80% of people with panic disorder are significantly better after a course (8-12 sessions).[1] Two parts:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cognitive work</strong> - identifying and updating beliefs that drive panic ("if I feel my heart, I am having a heart attack")</li>
  <li><strong>Interoceptive exposure</strong> - deliberately producing the bodily sensations that trigger panic (running on the spot, breathing through a straw, spinning) so the brain learns the sensations are not dangerous</li>
</ul>

<p>Available free in NZ via:</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/">Just A Thought</a> - has a free panic disorder course online</li>
  <li>Brief intervention counselling at most NZ GP practices</li>
  <li>Private therapists - around NZ$120-200 per session</li>
</ul>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>SSRIs (e.g. sertraline, escitalopram) reduce frequency and severity of attacks, usually within 4-6 weeks. Considered alongside or instead of therapy. Talk to your GP.</p>

<p>Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, diazepam) work fast but are rarely used long-term in panic disorder because of dependence risk and because they prevent the natural extinction of fear that recovery requires.</p>

<h3>Lifestyle</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Regular exercise - reduces baseline anxiety</li>
  <li>Cut caffeine - especially if attacks happen after coffee</li>
  <li>Sleep - poor sleep raises panic risk</li>
  <li>Reduce alcohol - it worsens anxiety overnight</li>
  <li>Practice the breathing technique daily for 5 minutes when you are NOT panicking - so it is rehearsed when you need it</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You have had more than one panic attack and are worried about future ones</li>
  <li>You are starting to avoid places or situations because of panic</li>
  <li>You are using alcohol or drugs to cope</li>
  <li>Panic is interfering with work, study, relationships</li>
  <li>The first attack and you are unsure what it was</li>
  <li>Chest pain that does not fit your usual panic pattern, or in someone over 40 with risk factors - rule out cardiac cause first</li>
</ul>
