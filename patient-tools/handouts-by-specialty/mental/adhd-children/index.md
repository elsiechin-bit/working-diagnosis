<p>Around 5-7% of NZ children meet criteria for ADHD.[1] It is the most common neurodevelopmental condition in childhood. Diagnosis and treatment in NZ are reasonably well-established, though waitlists in the public system can be long. This handout walks through what ADHD looks like in children, how to get them assessed, what treatment involves, and the things that matter most for long-term outcomes.</p>

<h2>What ADHD is in children</h2>

<p>A neurodevelopmental condition with three core features that have to be present from before age 12, persistent for at least 6 months, and causing real impairment in two or more settings (home, school, sports, social):</p>

<h3>Inattention</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Trouble sustaining attention in tasks or play</li>
  <li>Does not appear to listen when spoken to directly</li>
  <li>Does not follow through on instructions, leaves tasks unfinished</li>
  <li>Trouble organising activities and possessions</li>
  <li>Avoids tasks requiring sustained mental effort</li>
  <li>Loses things needed for tasks (school items, jacket, phone)</li>
  <li>Easily distracted</li>
  <li>Forgetful in daily activities</li>
</ul>

<h3>Hyperactivity</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Fidgets, squirms, cannot sit still</li>
  <li>Leaves seat in classroom when expected to remain</li>
  <li>Runs or climbs in inappropriate situations</li>
  <li>Cannot play quietly</li>
  <li>"Always on the go" or "driven by a motor"</li>
  <li>Talks excessively</li>
</ul>

<h3>Impulsivity</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Blurts out answers before questions complete</li>
  <li>Cannot wait their turn</li>
  <li>Interrupts or intrudes on others</li>
  <li>Acts without thinking about consequences</li>
</ul>

<p>Three presentations are recognised:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Predominantly inattentive (the "daydreamer" pattern; more common in girls)</li>
  <li>Predominantly hyperactive-impulsive</li>
  <li>Combined</li>
</ul>

<h2>What ADHD is NOT</h2>

<ul>
  <li>"Just being a kid" - all children have moments of all of these. ADHD is when they are persistent, significant, and impairing</li>
  <li>"Bad parenting" - parenting affects how ADHD is expressed but does not cause it. ADHD is highly heritable.</li>
  <li>"Sugar" or "food colouring" - small effects in some children, not the cause</li>
  <li>"Too much screen time" - associated, not causative; though excessive screens worsen attention in any child</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things that look like ADHD but are not</h2>

<p>Particularly important to consider before assessment:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Anxiety</strong> - very common in school-aged children, often misdiagnosed as inattention</li>
  <li><strong>Trauma</strong> - dysregulation, hypervigilance, attention problems</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep problems</strong> - including obstructive sleep apnoea (often associated with enlarged tonsils/adenoids), restless sleep, late bedtimes</li>
  <li><strong>Hearing or vision problems</strong> - undetected hearing loss looks like inattention</li>
  <li><strong>Specific learning difficulties</strong> - dyslexia, dyscalculia. Child appears not to listen, actually cannot keep up</li>
  <li><strong>Autism spectrum</strong> - overlaps significantly with ADHD; many children meet criteria for both</li>
  <li><strong>Iron deficiency</strong> - real but underrecognised contributor</li>
  <li><strong>Tic disorders</strong> - some tics look like fidgeting</li>
  <li><strong>Boredom from being academically advanced</strong> - the child is bored, not inattentive</li>
</ul>

<p>Good assessment screens for these. The order matters: it is reasonable to address sleep, anxiety, and hearing first, then reassess.</p>

<h2>The assessment process in NZ</h2>

<h3>Step 1: school and family observation</h3>
<p>Most diagnoses start with feedback from a teacher, family, or sports coach. The pattern usually involves multiple settings, not just home or just school.</p>

<h3>Step 2: see your GP</h3>
<p>Your GP will:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Take a history from you and from your child</li>
  <li>Check hearing, vision, growth</li>
  <li>Review for sleep, anxiety, low mood, family stress</li>
  <li>Sometimes blood tests for iron, thyroid</li>
  <li>Often arrange Conners or SDQ rating scales completed by parents and teachers</li>
  <li>Refer for paediatric assessment</li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 3: paediatric assessment</h3>
<p>In NZ, ADHD diagnosis in children is usually made by a developmental or general paediatrician (sometimes a child psychiatrist). Available via:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Public paediatric services</strong> - free, GP referral required. Wait times typically 6-18 months. Some regions have specific neurodevelopmental services.</li>
  <li><strong>Private paediatrician</strong> - around NZ$500-800 for first assessment plus ongoing visits.</li>
</ul>

<p>The assessment is comprehensive: developmental history, family history, school information, structured rating scales, observation, screening for co-existing conditions (autism, learning difficulties, anxiety, depression, sleep).</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<h3>Children under 6</h3>
<p>Behavioural intervention first - parent training programmes (Triple P, Incredible Years, NZ-specific Whanau Ora frameworks). Medication is rarely first-line under 6 except in severe cases.</p>

<h3>Children 6 and over</h3>
<p>The strongest evidence supports combined behavioural and medication approach.[2]</p>

<h4>Medication</h4>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Methylphenidate</strong> (Rubifen, Concerta, Ritalin LA) - first-line, funded in NZ. Long-acting forms preferred for school-day cover.</li>
  <li><strong>Dexamphetamine</strong> - second-line. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Lisdexamfetamine</strong> (Vyvanse) - long-acting, funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Atomoxetine</strong> (Strattera) - non-stimulant, funded with Special Authority. Useful where stimulants poorly tolerated or contraindicated, or where co-existing anxiety is prominent.</li>
  <li><strong>Guanfacine</strong> - non-stimulant, funded. Useful especially where tics, sleep problems, or aggression coexist.</li>
</ul>

<p>Stimulants in children require Special Authority and initial prescription by a paediatrician. Common side effects: appetite reduction (monitor weight and growth), sleep disturbance, mood changes, occasional tics. Most are dose-related and manageable. Cardiovascular history is reviewed before starting; ECG sometimes performed.</p>

<h4>Behavioural and educational support</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Parent training</li>
  <li>School-based supports - Individual Education Plan (IEP), Resource Teacher of Learning and Behaviour (RTLB)</li>
  <li>Occupational therapy for sensory or motor coordination issues</li>
  <li>Specific support for any co-existing learning difficulty</li>
  <li>Cognitive behavioural therapy if anxiety or depression coexist</li>
</ul>

<h2>What makes for good outcomes</h2>

<p>Long-term studies suggest that children with ADHD do well in adulthood when:</p>

<ul>
  <li>The diagnosis is made early enough that academic confidence is preserved</li>
  <li>They have at least one trusted adult outside the family (teacher, coach, mentor)</li>
  <li>Strengths are explicitly named and developed alongside difficulties</li>
  <li>Co-existing conditions are also addressed (anxiety, learning difficulties, sleep)</li>
  <li>Medication is used when needed and reviewed regularly</li>
  <li>Family relationships are protected from being defined by the diagnosis</li>
</ul>

<p>Children whose ADHD is recognised but who are otherwise treated as themselves, supported in their interests, and protected from the message that they are "lazy" or "naughty" generally do well.</p>

<h2>Specific situations</h2>

<h3>Girls</h3>
<p>Often diagnosed late. Inattentive type is more common in girls and easy to miss because they are quiet rather than disruptive. Often labelled as "lazy", "in their own world", or "anxious" before the underlying ADHD is recognised. If you have a daughter who fits the description, push for assessment.</p>

<h3>Adolescents</h3>
<p>The teenage years are when a lot of previously-managed ADHD becomes clearer, often as academic demands increase or executive function becomes more central. Adolescents are at higher risk of substance use, mental health problems, accidents, and risky driving - treatment reduces these.</p>

<h3>Co-existing autism</h3>
<p>Around 30-50% of children with ADHD have features of autism. The combination is its own thing and benefits from clinicians who understand both.</p>
