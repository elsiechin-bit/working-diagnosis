<p>OCD - obsessive-compulsive disorder - is one of the most stigmatised, misunderstood, and treatable mental health conditions. It is not the same as being tidy, organised, or particular. It is a serious condition that can hijack hours of every day and produce real suffering, and it responds well to a specific kind of therapy. This handout walks through what OCD actually is, what it is not, and what works.</p>

<h2>What OCD actually is</h2>

<p>OCD is defined by two components:</p>

<h3>Obsessions</h3>
<p>Unwanted, intrusive thoughts, images, or urges that cause significant anxiety or distress. The person typically tries to suppress them or neutralise them with another thought or action.</p>

<p>Common themes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Contamination (germs, dirt, illness)</li>
  <li>Harm (fears of hurting someone, including loved ones)</li>
  <li>Sexual or relationship intrusions</li>
  <li>Religious or moral scrupulosity</li>
  <li>Symmetry, order, "just right" feelings</li>
  <li>Forbidden or taboo thoughts</li>
  <li>Health-related (a specific subset is health anxiety with OCD features)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Compulsions</h3>
<p>Repetitive behaviours or mental acts the person feels driven to perform in response to the obsession, aimed at preventing some dreaded event or reducing distress. Not actually connected to what they are meant to prevent, or clearly excessive.</p>

<p>Common compulsions:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Washing, cleaning</li>
  <li>Checking (locks, appliances, work, body)</li>
  <li>Counting, repeating, ordering</li>
  <li>Mental rituals (silent prayers, replacing "bad" thoughts with "good")</li>
  <li>Reassurance seeking</li>
  <li>Avoidance of triggers</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why "just stop" does not work</h2>

<p>The OCD cycle is self-sustaining:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Intrusive thought arises (everyone has these; people without OCD shrug them off)</li>
  <li>The thought generates intense distress because it feels meaningful, dangerous, or shameful</li>
  <li>The compulsion provides short-term relief from the distress</li>
  <li>The brain "learns" that the compulsion was necessary</li>
  <li>The next time the thought arises, the compulsion comes faster and feels more necessary</li>
</ol>

<p>Trying to suppress the thought makes it more frequent (the white-bear effect). Trying to reason with it does not work because OCD is not really a reasoning problem. The compulsions feel like the only way to manage the distress, but they are what keep the cycle going.</p>

<h2>Common OCD types worth knowing</h2>

<h3>Contamination OCD</h3>
<p>Fear of germs, dirt, illness, contagion. Compulsions: washing, avoidance, cleaning. The most stereotyped form, but only one of many.</p>

<h3>Checking OCD</h3>
<p>Fear of harm coming to self or others through a missed action. Compulsions: re-checking locks, taps, appliances, work. Often hours per day.</p>

<h3>"Pure O"</h3>
<p>OCD with primarily mental compulsions and few visible behaviours. The person looks fine externally but is exhausted by hours of internal rituals. Particularly under-recognised.</p>

<h3>Harm OCD</h3>
<p>Intrusive thoughts about harming someone the person loves - a baby, partner, parent. Highly distressing, almost never lead to actual harm. The person typically avoids being alone with the loved one and seeks reassurance constantly.</p>

<h3>Relationship OCD (ROCD)</h3>
<p>Persistent doubts about whether the person loves their partner enough, whether their partner is "the one", whether the relationship is "real". Constant analysis and reassurance-seeking.</p>

<h3>Scrupulosity</h3>
<p>Religious or moral OCD - persistent doubt about whether one has sinned, blasphemed, behaved correctly. Common compulsions: praying, confessing, replacing "bad" thoughts.</p>

<h3>Just Right / Symmetry</h3>
<p>Need for things to feel "just right" - not driven by belief that something bad will happen, more by the discomfort of incompleteness. Common in OCD with co-existing autism.</p>

<h2>What is NOT OCD</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Liking things tidy or organised</li>
  <li>Being detail-oriented or particular at work</li>
  <li>Having superstitious thoughts occasionally</li>
  <li>Routine checking that fits the situation (locking the door before bed)</li>
  <li>Trichotillomania (hair-pulling) and skin-picking are related but different conditions</li>
</ul>

<p>The clinical line is impairment - OCD takes up at least an hour a day, causes real distress, and gets in the way of life. Without those, it is not OCD.</p>

<h2>Treatment - the specific kind that works</h2>

<h3>Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)</h3>
<p>The first-line and most evidence-based treatment for OCD.[1] Around 60-70% of patients with OCD see significant improvement.</p>

<p>ERP involves:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Exposure</strong> - deliberately and repeatedly engaging with the trigger that produces the obsession (touching the dirty thing, leaving the door unchecked, allowing the intrusive thought)</li>
  <li><strong>Response prevention</strong> - NOT performing the compulsion afterwards. Sitting with the distress until it falls on its own.</li>
</ul>

<p>The brain learns: the feared outcome did not happen, AND the distress passes on its own without the compulsion. Over weeks the OCD loses its grip.</p>

<p>ERP is uncomfortable. It works. It is best done with a trained therapist - generic CBT without ERP is significantly less effective for OCD.</p>

<p>Available in NZ via:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Specialist private psychologists with OCD training</li>
  <li>Some DHB community mental health services</li>
  <li><a href="https://www.justathought.co.nz/courses/ocd">Just A Thought - free OCD course</a></li>
</ul>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>SSRIs - sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram, paroxetine, fluvoxamine. Often at higher doses than for depression (e.g. sertraline up to 200mg). Effects build over 8-12 weeks. Around 50-60% of patients respond meaningfully.</p>

<p>For treatment-resistant OCD, clomipramine (a tricyclic with strong serotonergic effect) is sometimes used. Atypical antipsychotic augmentation in severe cases.</p>

<h3>Combined</h3>
<p>ERP plus SSRI is more effective than either alone for moderate to severe OCD.</p>

<h2>What does NOT work well</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Generic talking therapy.</strong> Talking about the obsessions can actually make them stronger. ERP is the specific tool.</li>
  <li><strong>Reassurance-seeking.</strong> A core feature of OCD is the relief from being told "it's fine". The relief is short and reinforces the cycle. Loved ones often need coaching to stop providing reassurance.</li>
  <li><strong>Just trying to think positive thoughts.</strong> Suppression strengthens obsessions.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoidance of triggers.</strong> Shrinks life and worsens OCD.</li>
</ul>

<h2>For loved ones</h2>

<p>Living with someone with OCD is exhausting. The pattern that helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Recognise the difference between the person and the OCD</li>
  <li>Stop providing reassurance about OCD-driven fears (this is hard but core to recovery)</li>
  <li>Do not participate in compulsions on their behalf (cleaning, checking, answering reassurance questions)</li>
  <li>Support them in pursuing ERP</li>
  <li>Look after yourself - OCD support family groups exist</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Repetitive thoughts or behaviours taking more than an hour a day</li>
  <li>Significant distress around specific themes</li>
  <li>Avoidance of situations because of intrusive thoughts</li>
  <li>Reassurance-seeking or compulsive checking that worries you or your family</li>
  <li>Co-existing depression, anxiety, eating problems</li>
  <li>Children with new ritualistic behaviour, especially after a streptococcal infection - PANDAS is a recognised syndrome that needs different treatment</li>
</ul>
