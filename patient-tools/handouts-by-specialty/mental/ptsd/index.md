<p>Post-traumatic stress disorder is the persistent psychological response to events that overwhelmed the brain's normal capacity to process them. Around 6% of NZ adults experience PTSD at some point.[1] It is not a sign of weakness or character flaw. It is what the nervous system can do when faced with events it was not built to integrate. The good news is that PTSD is treatable - sometimes dramatically so - with specific trauma-focused therapies. This handout walks through what to recognise, why early help matters, and how to access trauma-informed care in NZ.</p>

<h2>What counts as trauma</h2>

<p>Trauma can come from a single event or from prolonged or repeated exposure. Common precipitants:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Serious accident or injury (especially with sense of life threat)</li>
  <li>Sexual assault or sexual abuse</li>
  <li>Physical assault</li>
  <li>Witnessing a death or serious injury</li>
  <li>Sudden loss of a close person</li>
  <li>Combat exposure</li>
  <li>Natural disaster</li>
  <li>Childhood abuse or neglect (often labelled "complex PTSD" when prolonged in childhood)</li>
  <li>Medical trauma (ICU stay, life-threatening illness, traumatic birth)</li>
  <li>Repeated exposure to others' trauma (vicarious trauma in healthcare, emergency services, journalism)</li>
</ul>

<p>What makes an event traumatic is not the objective severity but the brain's response - whether it overwhelmed normal coping. Two people in the same incident can have very different outcomes.</p>

<h2>The four symptom clusters</h2>

<p>PTSD diagnosis requires symptoms in all four areas, lasting more than a month, and causing significant distress or impairment.</p>

<h3>1. Intrusion</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Unwanted, distressing memories of the event</li>
  <li>Nightmares - often direct replays or thematically related</li>
  <li>Flashbacks - feeling as if the event is happening again, with full sensory and emotional re-experience</li>
  <li>Intense distress at reminders</li>
  <li>Physical reactions to reminders (sweating, racing heart, nausea)</li>
</ul>

<h3>2. Avoidance</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Avoidance of memories, thoughts, feelings related to the event</li>
  <li>Avoidance of external reminders - people, places, activities, objects, situations</li>
</ul>

<h3>3. Negative changes in thinking and mood</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Inability to remember important parts of the event</li>
  <li>Persistent negative beliefs about self, others, or the world ("I am broken", "no one can be trusted")</li>
  <li>Persistent distorted blame of self or others</li>
  <li>Persistent negative emotional state (fear, horror, anger, guilt, shame)</li>
  <li>Loss of interest in activities</li>
  <li>Detachment from others, feeling cut off</li>
  <li>Inability to feel positive emotions</li>
</ul>

<h3>4. Changes in arousal and reactivity</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Irritability, angry outbursts</li>
  <li>Reckless or self-destructive behaviour</li>
  <li>Hypervigilance - constantly scanning for threat</li>
  <li>Exaggerated startle response</li>
  <li>Concentration problems</li>
  <li>Sleep disturbance</li>
</ul>

<h2>Acute stress vs PTSD</h2>

<p>In the first few weeks after a traumatic event, intense distress is normal. The brain is processing. Symptoms often look like PTSD but typically settle. The diagnosis of PTSD requires symptoms persisting beyond a month, with significant ongoing impact.</p>

<p>If symptoms are severe in the first month, brief trauma-focused intervention (a few sessions of CBT) reduces the chance of progression to chronic PTSD.</p>

<h2>Complex PTSD (CPTSD)</h2>

<p>Increasingly recognised. Develops after prolonged or repeated trauma, often in childhood (abuse, neglect, sustained domestic violence, prolonged war exposure). In addition to standard PTSD features, CPTSD includes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Disturbances in self-organisation - persistent negative self-concept, feelings of worthlessness, shame</li>
  <li>Disturbances in relationships - persistent difficulties with intimacy and trust</li>
  <li>Affect dysregulation - difficulty managing emotions</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment for CPTSD is similar but typically longer and includes more focus on relationship and identity work.</p>

<h2>Why early help matters</h2>

<p>Untreated PTSD often persists for years. Treatment in the first 1-3 years has higher success rates and prevents the secondary problems that accumulate over time (depression, alcohol use, relationship breakdown, chronic pain, employment loss).</p>

<h2>Treatment - what actually works</h2>

<h3>Trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT)</h3>
<p>The most evidence-based treatment. Around 70% of patients have significantly reduced symptoms after a course (12-16 sessions).[2] Two main forms:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)</strong> - focus on identifying and updating "stuck points" in thinking about the trauma</li>
  <li><strong>Prolonged Exposure (PE)</strong> - gradual revisiting of the trauma narrative in a safe context, plus exposure to avoided situations</li>
</ul>

<h3>EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)</h3>
<p>An alternative trauma-focused therapy that uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, taps, sounds) while the person revisits the trauma. Effectiveness is comparable to TF-CBT, often shorter course. NZ has a growing number of EMDR-trained therapists.</p>

<h3>Medication</h3>
<p>SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine, fluoxetine) are first-line. Reduce symptoms by 30-40%. Often combined with therapy. Discuss with your GP.</p>

<p>Prazosin can help with PTSD-related nightmares specifically.</p>

<p>Benzodiazepines are NOT recommended in PTSD - they prevent the natural processing of trauma memories and can worsen long-term outcomes.</p>

<h2>What does NOT work well</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Critical incident debriefing immediately after trauma - older approach, evidence does not support it, may cause harm</li>
  <li>Generic talking therapy without trauma focus</li>
  <li>Trying to "just move on" or push through</li>
  <li>Self-medication with alcohol or cannabis - feels like it helps, worsens long-term</li>
  <li>Avoidance - reinforces fear network</li>
</ul>

<h2>Specific situations</h2>

<h3>Veterans and emergency services</h3>
<p>Specific NZ services - Veterans' Affairs supports, NZ Police trauma services, Fire Service peer support. Often free.</p>

<h3>Sexual assault</h3>
<p>NZ has free specialist services - Rape Prevention Education, HELP, Wellington Rape Crisis. Crisis support and ongoing therapy. ACC funds therapy for sexual abuse - the criteria are reasonable and the assessment can be done by your GP, counsellor, or specialist service.</p>

<h3>Childbirth trauma</h3>
<p>Increasingly recognised. PADA (Perinatal Anxiety and Depression Aotearoa) and maternal mental health services can help. EMDR is often particularly effective.</p>

<h3>Family violence</h3>
<p>Women's Refuge, Shine, Are You OK 0800 456 450. Police 111 if in immediate danger. Specialist trauma therapists post-leaving.</p>

<h3>Refugee trauma</h3>
<p>Refugees As Survivors (RAS) NZ provides specialised trauma support, with culturally appropriate therapists and interpreters.</p>

<h2>ACC and trauma therapy</h2>

<p>If your trauma was caused by a sensitive claim (sexual abuse or assault) or a physical injury (workplace, motor vehicle, criminal assault, treatment injury), ACC funds therapy. The criteria are reasonable and the application can be supported by your GP, counsellor, or specialist agency. Worth knowing: ACC sensitive claims pay for ongoing therapy, often weekly, sometimes for years if needed.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms in the four clusters above lasting more than a month after a traumatic event</li>
  <li>Increasing avoidance or withdrawal</li>
  <li>Sleep disrupted by nightmares</li>
  <li>Using alcohol or drugs to cope</li>
  <li>Impact on work, relationships, parenting</li>
  <li>Thoughts of self-harm</li>
  <li>You suspect childhood trauma is showing up now in adult life</li>
</ul>
