<p>Fever is one of the most common reasons parents bring children to a GP. The good news is that most fevers in healthy children are caused by self-limiting viral infections that the immune system clears in a few days. The fever itself is not the danger. What matters is how your child looks and behaves, and whether you spot the small minority of fevers that need urgent attention.</p>

<h2>What fever actually is</h2>

<p>A temperature of 38°C or higher. Fever is a normal immune response, not a disease. It helps the body fight infection. The height of the temperature does not reliably predict how serious the underlying illness is - a child with a temperature of 40°C from a viral illness can be much less unwell than a child with 38.2°C from sepsis.</p>

<h2>Measuring temperature</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Under 4 weeks:</strong> any rectal thermometer reading of 38°C or above is significant; see urgent care</li>
  <li><strong>4 weeks to 5 years:</strong> ear (tympanic) or under-arm (axillary) thermometers are practical and accurate enough; forehead infrared thermometers vary in accuracy</li>
  <li><strong>Over 5 years:</strong> ear or oral thermometers</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not need to take temperature obsessively. Once an hour during a feverish day, more often if your child is deteriorating, otherwise just check by feel.</p>

<h2>When to seek care urgently (any age)</h2>

<p>Phone Healthline 0800 611 116 (free, 24/7) or go to ED / call 111 if your child has any of these:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Looks very unwell</strong> - floppy, drowsy, glazed, hard to wake, not making eye contact, not responding to you the way they normally do</li>
  <li><strong>A non-blanching rash</strong> - small purple-red spots that do not fade when you press a glass against them. Possible meningococcal sepsis.</li>
  <li><strong>Difficulty breathing</strong> - working hard to breathe, ribs sucking in, grunting, blue around lips</li>
  <li><strong>Persistent vomiting</strong> with inability to keep fluids down</li>
  <li><strong>Seizure or fit</strong> (febrile convulsion - see separate section)</li>
  <li><strong>Severe headache with stiff neck or sensitivity to light</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Cold hands and feet, mottled skin, prolonged capillary refill</strong> (press a fingernail, count how long the colour returns - over 2 seconds is concerning)</li>
  <li><strong>Not weeing</strong> - dry nappies for more than 8-12 hours, no tears when crying, sunken fontanelle in babies</li>
  <li><strong>Persistent crying you cannot console</strong> or, conversely, an unusually quiet child</li>
</ul>

<h2>Special considerations by age</h2>

<h3>Under 4 weeks old</h3>
<p>Any temperature of 38°C or above is a medical emergency. Newborns can deteriorate quickly. Take to ED.</p>

<h3>4 weeks to 3 months</h3>
<p>Fever needs same-day GP or ED assessment. Babies in this age range cannot show illness reliably - even a small fever can hide a serious infection.</p>

<h3>3 months to 6 months</h3>
<p>Fever above 39°C, or fever lasting more than 24 hours, or any worry about how the baby is, needs same-day assessment.</p>

<h3>Over 6 months</h3>
<p>Most fevers can be managed at home initially. Use the warning signs above to decide when to seek care.</p>

<h2>What to do at home</h2>

<h3>Comfort and fluids</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Light clothing, light bedding. Do not bundle them up.</li>
  <li>Room temperature comfortable, not hot.</li>
  <li>Offer fluids often - small sips frequently. Breast milk, formula, water, oral rehydration solution.</li>
  <li>Do not force food. Appetite returns when they feel better.</li>
  <li>Comfort and reassurance. A child who can sleep on a parent is a child who is reasonably well.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Paracetamol and ibuprofen</h3>
<p>Treat for comfort, not for the temperature number. If your child is happy enough, you do not need to medicate.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Paracetamol</strong> (Pamol, Panadol) - 15mg/kg every 4-6 hours, maximum 4 doses in 24 hours. Available in liquid for infants and children.</li>
  <li><strong>Ibuprofen</strong> (Nurofen, Fenpaed) - 5-10mg/kg every 6-8 hours, with food. Avoid in dehydrated children. Avoid under 3 months.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not give aspirin</strong> to a child under 16 - small risk of Reye's syndrome.</li>
  <li>You can alternate paracetamol and ibuprofen if one is not enough on its own. Track doses on paper to avoid double-dosing.</li>
</ul>

<p>Sponging with cool water, cold baths, or fans are not helpful and can make children more uncomfortable. The aim is comfort, not making the temperature go down.</p>

<h2>Febrile convulsion</h2>

<p>Some young children (6 months to 5 years) have a brief seizure when their temperature rises rapidly. Witnessing one is terrifying but the seizure itself is usually harmless and self-limiting. See the separate febrile convulsion handout.</p>

<p>Do call 111 the first time it happens, especially if it lasts longer than 5 minutes, the child does not return to normal afterwards, or it occurs more than once in the same illness.</p>

<h2>The illness courses to expect</h2>

<p>Most children have 6-8 viral illnesses a year, especially in the first years of childcare or school. A typical pattern:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Day 1-2: fever and feeling unwell</li>
  <li>Day 3-4: fever settling, but coughs and runny noses are at their worst</li>
  <li>Day 5-7: gradual recovery</li>
</ul>

<p>If fever lasts more than 3-4 days without an obvious source, or returns after a clear period, see your GP. This pattern can suggest secondary bacterial infection (ear, sinus, pneumonia) or another diagnosis.</p>

<h2>What is probably <em>not</em> a problem</h2>

<ul>
  <li>A high temperature in a child who is otherwise interactive, drinking, and uncomfortable but not floppy</li>
  <li>Fever after vaccinations - mild and short-lived, usually within 24-48 hours</li>
  <li>Fever with a clear viral source (runny nose, sore throat, cough, mild rash)</li>
  <li>Fever that lasts up to 3 days in an otherwise reasonable child</li>
</ul>
