<p>Driving is more than transport. For many older New Zealanders it is independence, identity, and the practical means of getting to family, the doctor, and the supermarket. Stopping driving is one of the harder transitions of later life. This handout is for the driver, the family, and the GP. It covers what the law requires, what the science shows, and what to do when the conversation about stopping needs to happen.</p>

<h2>What the law requires in NZ</h2>

<p>Two things are worth knowing.</p>

<p>First, every NZ driver aged 75 and over needs a medical assessment to renew their licence. The licence is renewed at 75, then at 80, then every two years from 80 onwards. The medical certificate is filled out by your GP using the NZTA medical aspects of fitness to drive form.</p>

<p>Second, drivers of any age have a legal obligation to inform NZTA if they have a medical condition that affects their driving safety. Doctors also have a professional obligation to notify NZTA if a patient is unsafe to drive and unwilling to stop voluntarily. This is not a punishment. It is a public safety mechanism.</p>

<h2>Conditions that matter</h2>

<p>The conditions most often relevant to driving in older adults:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dementia and cognitive impairment</strong> - mild cognitive impairment usually compatible with driving with surveillance; mild dementia often compatible for a time with on-road assessment; moderate dementia is usually incompatible</li>
  <li><strong>Vision</strong> - cataracts, age-related macular degeneration, glaucoma. Annual optometry review is sensible</li>
  <li><strong>Hearing loss</strong> - more important than people think, especially for emergency vehicle awareness</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular events</strong> - rules apply after a heart attack, after a stent, after a stroke or TIA, with significant heart failure, with serious arrhythmias</li>
  <li><strong>Diabetes</strong> - particularly if on insulin or sulfonylureas because of hypoglycaemia risk</li>
  <li><strong>Epilepsy or seizures</strong> - mandatory non-driving period after a seizure (typically 12 months for private licence)</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep disorders</strong> - obstructive sleep apnoea with daytime sleepiness, narcolepsy</li>
  <li><strong>Parkinson's disease</strong> - depending on motor symptoms and dyskinesia</li>
  <li><strong>Medications</strong> - sleeping tablets, sedating antihistamines, opioids, anticonvulsants, some antidepressants, alcohol</li>
</ul>

<p>Specific NZTA fitness-to-drive standards exist for each. The detail is in the NZTA Medical Aspects of Fitness to Drive guide, which your GP uses for assessments.</p>

<h2>Warning signs of unsafe driving</h2>

<p>Worth noticing, in yourself or someone you love:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Getting lost on familiar routes</li>
  <li>Multiple "near misses" or new dings and scrapes on the car</li>
  <li>Difficulty staying in lane, drifting</li>
  <li>Slow reactions or being startled by other vehicles</li>
  <li>Stopping at green lights or going through red lights</li>
  <li>Difficulty judging gaps in traffic or distance to other cars</li>
  <li>Other drivers honking at you more often</li>
  <li>Friends or family declining to be a passenger or quietly arranging alternative transport</li>
  <li>Trouble turning to check blind spots</li>
  <li>Increasing anxiety or fatigue after driving</li>
</ul>

<h2>The on-road assessment</h2>

<p>If your GP has concerns about your driving but cannot make a clear recommendation from the consultation alone, they will refer for an occupational therapist driving assessment. This is a structured 60 to 90 minute drive with a specialist OT, usually with both written/cognitive testing and an on-road component in the assessor's dual-control vehicle. Assessments are funded through ACC if the issue is injury-related and through DHBs in some cases; otherwise paid privately (around NZ$500-800).</p>

<p>Possible outcomes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pass - continue driving</li>
  <li>Pass with conditions - daytime only, local area only, automatic vehicle only</li>
  <li>Fail - the OT recommends NZTA suspend the licence</li>
</ul>

<p>The OT report is sent to NZTA and your GP. NZTA makes the final licence decision.</p>

<h2>Having the conversation</h2>

<p>If you are the family member, the conversation is hard but essential. Some things that help:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Talk early, before the situation is acute</li>
  <li>Talk in private, not after a near miss when emotions are high</li>
  <li>Lead with concern for them and others, not control</li>
  <li>Acknowledge what driving means - independence, identity, freedom</li>
  <li>Have alternatives ready (see below) - "stop driving" without a plan to still get places will be resisted</li>
  <li>If your GP shares your concern, ask the GP to bring it up too. A doctor's voice often lands differently</li>
  <li>Frame it as a transition rather than a single event - daytime only first, then local only, then a driving assessment, then alternatives</li>
</ul>

<h2>Alternatives to driving</h2>

<p>The NZ system has more options than people realise:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Total Mobility</strong> - subsidised taxi vouchers for people unable to use public transport. Apply through your local council or Age Concern. Up to 50% off taxi fares to a capped amount per ride.</li>
  <li><strong>SuperGold Card</strong> - free off-peak public transport across NZ for over-65s (varies by region; check Auckland Transport, Metlink, etc.)</li>
  <li><strong>Driving Miss Daisy</strong>, <strong>Freedom Companion Drivers</strong>, and similar paid services - companion driving with door-to-door pickup</li>
  <li><strong>Community shuttles</strong> - many areas have free or low-cost shuttle services for medical appointments and shopping; ask your local Citizens Advice Bureau</li>
  <li><strong>Family rotas</strong> - shared driving among siblings or extended family, planned in advance</li>
  <li><strong>Online shopping with delivery</strong> - all major NZ supermarkets deliver</li>
</ul>

<h2>Practical tips for staying safe while you still drive</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Drive in daylight where you can; reaction time is slower at night and glare is harder to manage</li>
  <li>Avoid peak hour traffic if possible</li>
  <li>Plan your route; avoid unfamiliar destinations under time pressure</li>
  <li>Keep windscreens, mirrors, and headlights clean</li>
  <li>Get your car serviced regularly</li>
  <li>Check vision annually</li>
  <li>Avoid driving for several hours after taking sleeping tablets, strong painkillers, or new medicines that might cause drowsiness</li>
  <li>Never drive after even one alcoholic drink in older age</li>
  <li>Take regular breaks on long trips - older drivers fatigue faster</li>
</ul>
