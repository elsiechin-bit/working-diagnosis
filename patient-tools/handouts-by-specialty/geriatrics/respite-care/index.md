<p>Respite care is temporary care for a person who needs support due to age, illness, or disability - arranged specifically to give their usual caregiver a break. It is one of the most important (and underused) parts of the NZ aged and disability care system. Caregiver burnout is real, and using respite is not giving up: it is how families sustain care over years rather than months.</p>

<h2>Who respite care is for</h2>

<p>Respite can be arranged for anyone who depends on a family member or informal caregiver for day-to-day support - most commonly:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Older adults with dementia, frailty, or complex medical needs</li>
  <li>People with physical or intellectual disabilities</li>
  <li>People recovering from stroke or serious illness who need temporary support while family carers rest</li>
</ul>

<p>The caregiver does not need to be in crisis to access respite. It is appropriate to plan for it before exhaustion sets in.</p>

<h2>Types of respite care</h2>

<h3>In-home respite</h3>
<p>A trained support worker or caregiver comes to the person's home for a set number of hours. The person stays in familiar surroundings. This works well for shorter breaks and when the person is resistant to going to a facility.</p>

<h3>Residential respite</h3>
<p>A short stay - typically days to a few weeks - at a rest home, care facility, or disability residential service. The person is cared for around the clock while the family carer has a proper break. Most rest homes that offer permanent care also have respite beds, though availability varies.</p>

<h3>Day programmes</h3>
<p>The person attends a structured day programme several days per week, returning home in the evening. This is particularly useful for people with dementia who benefit from social stimulation and routine, while their carer works or rests during the day. Age Concern and some dementia-specific organisations run day programmes in most centres.</p>

<h3>Carer Support (informal arrangements)</h3>
<p>The Carer Support subsidy allows families to pay a trusted person - a friend, neighbour, or other family member not living in the same household - to provide short-term relief care. This is more flexible than formal services and can suit rural areas or families with existing informal networks.</p>

<h2>How respite care is funded</h2>

<p>Funding pathways differ depending on the person's age and whether their needs relate to age, disability, or an accident injury.</p>

<h3>For older people (aged 65 and over)</h3>

<p>The gateway is the <strong>Needs Assessment and Service Coordination (NASC)</strong> service. NASC is funded by Health New Zealand and operates regionally. A NASC assessor visits the person at home and uses a standardised tool called <strong>InterRAI</strong> to assess their needs, functional ability, and the demands on their caregiver.</p>

<p>Based on the assessment, NASC allocates a funded support package which may include:</p>
<ul>
  <li>A set number of <strong>Carer Support subsidy days</strong> per year - funded breaks that can be taken as residential respite, day care, or informal carer relief</li>
  <li><strong>Home support hours</strong> to assist with daily tasks (not the same as carer relief, but reduces overall carer burden)</li>
  <li><strong>Funded residential respite</strong> - short stays in a rest home at government-funded rates, without the asset testing that applies to permanent admission</li>
</ul>

<p>The number of subsidised respite days varies by assessed need. It is worth asking NASC explicitly about respite entitlement and Carer Support - these do not always appear in the initial package without prompting.</p>

<h3>For people with disabilities (under 65)</h3>

<p><strong>Whaikaha - the Ministry of Disabled People</strong> funds disability supports, including respite, for eligible people under 65. Access is again through a local NASC service, using a disability-specific needs assessment rather than InterRAI.</p>

<p>Funding options include:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Carer Support subsidy</strong> (same scheme as for older adults)</li>
  <li><strong>Individualised Funding (IF)</strong> - a direct funding model where the person or their family manages a budget to purchase their own supports, including respite</li>
  <li><strong>Residential respite</strong> via contracted disability support providers</li>
</ul>

<h3>ACC-funded respite</h3>

<p>If the person needing care has an injury covered by ACC, ACC can fund respite and home support as part of the rehabilitation package. This is separate from the NASC pathway and is managed through the person's ACC case manager.</p>

<h3>Veterans' Affairs</h3>

<p>Eligible war veterans may access additional respite funding through Veterans' Affairs New Zealand, on top of standard entitlements.</p>

<h2>How to access NASC</h2>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Get a referral.</strong> Your GP can refer directly to your local NASC. Family members can also self-refer by contacting NASC themselves. Your GP practice or local hospital social worker can give you the correct contact for your region.</li>
  <li><strong>Assessment visit.</strong> A NASC assessor visits the home, usually within a few weeks (though wait times vary by region and can be longer in busier areas). They assess both the person and the impact on the caregiver. It is useful for the main caregiver to be present during this visit.</li>
  <li><strong>Support package confirmed.</strong> NASC determines what funded services are available and coordinates with providers to set them up. You can request a review if needs change.</li>
</ol>

<p>Refer early - NASC waitlists exist, and it is better to have funding in place before a crisis forces the issue.</p>

<h2>Choosing a residential respite facility</h2>

<p>Rest homes and care facilities in NZ must be certified by Health New Zealand under the <strong>Health and Disability Services Standards</strong>. Certification audits are publicly available and worth reviewing before choosing a facility.</p>

<p>NZ care facilities are categorised by the level of care they are certified to provide:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest home level</strong> - for people who need help with daily living but not 24-hour nursing</li>
  <li><strong>Hospital level</strong> - for people with higher medical or nursing needs</li>
  <li><strong>Dementia specialist care</strong> - secure units with staff trained in dementia care, for people who are at risk of wandering or have significant behavioural symptoms</li>
  <li><strong>Psychogeriatric care</strong> - for people with complex mental health needs in later life</li>
</ul>

<p>When visiting a facility, reasonable questions include: what is the staffing ratio overnight? How are behavioural symptoms managed? What activities are available? How do they handle medical deterioration during a short stay?</p>

<h2>Private respite (without NASC funding)</h2>

<p>Families can arrange and pay privately for respite without going through NASC. Private residential respite at a rest home typically costs $200-$400+ per day. In-home private respite through a support agency varies by region. This is an option when access to NASC is delayed or when families need more flexibility than the funded allocation allows.</p>

<h2>When to plan ahead</h2>

<p>Respite is easier to arrange in advance than in a crisis. A few situations worth planning for:</p>
<ul>
  <li>The carer is having planned surgery or a hospital stay</li>
  <li>The carer needs a holiday</li>
  <li>The carer's own health is declining</li>
  <li>Family members are visiting from overseas and want to give the usual carer a genuine break</li>
  <li>The demands of care are increasing and home-based care will not be sustainable indefinitely</li>
</ul>

<p>Discussing respite with your GP is appropriate even before a formal NASC assessment - your GP can help you understand what is available locally and initiate the referral.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask at your next appointment</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Can you refer us to the local NASC service?</li>
  <li>What Carer Support days might we be entitled to?</li>
  <li>Are there day programmes locally that would suit our situation?</li>
  <li>What happens if I (the carer) need to go into hospital unexpectedly - is there an emergency respite pathway?</li>
  <li>Is there a social worker attached to this practice who can help us navigate the options?</li>
</ul>
