<p>If you take five or more regular medicines, you are taking what doctors call "polypharmacy". It is not automatically a bad thing. Many people need every tablet they are on. But each medicine you add increases the risk of side effects, interactions, and falls, and the more medicines you take, the more important it is to review whether each one is still earning its place. Deprescribing is the formal name for stopping or reducing medicines that are no longer helping. It is a normal part of good medical care.</p>

<h2>Why we review</h2>

<p>What was right for you at 60 may not be right at 80. Three things change as we age:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Your kidneys and liver process medicines more slowly.</strong> The same dose can build up and cause side effects.</li>
  <li><strong>Your body is more sensitive to certain effects.</strong> Sleeping tablets, blood pressure medicines, and pain relief all hit harder.</li>
  <li><strong>The reason you started a medicine may have changed.</strong> The blood pressure target you had at 65 may not be the right one at 85.</li>
</ul>

<p>Polypharmacy is also the strongest predictor of being admitted to hospital because of an adverse drug reaction. Around one in ten admissions in older NZ adults is medicine-related, and a meaningful proportion are preventable.[1]</p>

<h2>Medicines we look at carefully</h2>

<h3>Sleeping tablets and anti-anxiety tablets</h3>
<p>Zopiclone, lorazepam, diazepam, temazepam. Effective short-term but long-term use is associated with falls, fractures, daytime drowsiness, memory problems, and dependence. We try to taper slowly rather than stop suddenly.</p>

<h3>Anti-inflammatory pain relief</h3>
<p>Ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen. Effective for pain but raise the risk of stomach bleeds, kidney damage, and heart problems in older adults. Often replaced with paracetamol, topical creams, or a short course rather than chronic use.</p>

<h3>Anticholinergic medicines</h3>
<p>Some bladder tablets (oxybutynin), some antihistamines (chlorpheniramine, promethazine), some antidepressants (amitriptyline), and some Parkinson's tablets. They can cause confusion, dry mouth, constipation, and falls. The "anticholinergic burden" of all your medicines added together matters more than any single tablet.[2]</p>

<h3>Statins very late in life</h3>
<p>Statins reduce heart attack and stroke risk over years. The benefit takes time to show; in someone with limited life expectancy or significant frailty, that benefit may not be reached. This is a discussion, not an automatic stop.</p>

<h3>Blood pressure medicines</h3>
<p>Targets in older adults are often more relaxed than in younger adults, particularly if you are getting dizzy on standing. Your GP may lower the dose or stop a medicine if your blood pressure is now low for you.</p>

<h3>Proton pump inhibitors</h3>
<p>Omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole. Excellent for short-term reflux or ulcer protection. Long-term use is associated with low magnesium, B12 deficiency, and a small increase in pneumonia and fracture risk. Many people on long-term PPIs can be tapered.</p>

<h3>Anticoagulants and antiplatelets</h3>
<p>Warfarin, dabigatran, rivaroxaban, aspirin, clopidogrel. The benefits (preventing strokes and clots) are real, but bleeding risk rises with age and falls. The decision to continue is individual and benefits from periodic review, not automatic continuation.</p>

<h2>What deprescribing actually looks like</h2>

<p>Deprescribing is not stopping medicines abruptly. It is a planned, supervised process that follows three principles:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>One change at a time.</strong> Stopping or reducing more than one medicine in the same week makes it impossible to tell what caused any side effect or improvement.</li>
  <li><strong>Slowly when needed.</strong> Many medicines need a taper over weeks. This includes sleeping tablets, antidepressants, opioids, beta-blockers, and proton pump inhibitors.</li>
  <li><strong>With a follow-up plan.</strong> Either an appointment with your GP, a phone check-in, or a clear instruction to come back if a specific symptom returns.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How you can help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Bring all your medicines (including over-the-counter, herbal, and supplements) to your appointment - actual bottles, not a list. This catches duplicates and ones nobody knew you were taking.</li>
  <li>Ask why you take each one and how you would know if it was working.</li>
  <li>Tell your GP if a medicine has any side effect, even a small one.</li>
  <li>Ask before stopping or starting anything yourself, including supplements.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions worth asking your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is each of these medicines actually doing for me?</li>
  <li>Are any of them treating a side effect of another medicine?</li>
  <li>Have my goals or my health changed enough that any of them could be stopped?</li>
  <li>What would happen if I stopped this one?</li>
  <li>How would I know if any new symptom is from a medicine?</li>
</ul>
