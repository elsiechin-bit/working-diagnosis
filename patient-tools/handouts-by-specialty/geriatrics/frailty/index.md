<p>Frailty is a clinical syndrome, not just "looking frail". It describes a measurable loss of biological reserve - the capacity that lets healthy older adults bounce back from a chest infection, a fall, or surgery. Roughly 25% of New Zealanders aged 75 and over meet criteria for frailty.[1] Recognising it matters because it changes the calculus of almost every medical decision: who benefits from a screening test, who can tolerate a major operation, what blood pressure target makes sense, when to deprescribe.</p>

<h2>The five-component picture</h2>

<p>The most-used research definition of frailty (Fried's criteria) requires three or more of the following:[2]</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Unintentional weight loss</strong> of more than 4.5kg or 5% of body weight in the previous year</li>
  <li><strong>Self-reported exhaustion</strong> on most days</li>
  <li><strong>Weakness</strong> measured by a low handgrip strength</li>
  <li><strong>Slow walking speed</strong> - typically less than 0.8 metres per second over a 4-metre flat walk</li>
  <li><strong>Low physical activity</strong> - less than the recommended weekly activity for age</li>
</ul>

<p>One or two of those is "pre-frail" - a warning sign that intervention can still reverse. Three or more is "frail". The trajectory from robust to frail to severely frail is not a fixed track; it can be stabilised and sometimes reversed.</p>

<h2>Why does it matter?</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Frail older adults are <strong>roughly twice as likely to be admitted to hospital</strong> in the next year</li>
  <li>They have <strong>longer hospital stays and higher rates of discharge to a rest home</strong> rather than home</li>
  <li>They are more likely to <strong>experience harm from medicines</strong>, especially blood pressure tablets, sleeping tablets, and pain relief</li>
  <li>They take <strong>longer to recover from any illness or injury</strong> and are more likely to lose function permanently after each episode</li>
  <li>They benefit less from screening interventions like cancer screening (because the time horizon to benefit is longer than likely life expectancy)</li>
</ul>

<p>This is not about writing people off. The opposite. Identifying frailty changes what we offer: more tailored, more proactive, more focused on what matters to the person.</p>

<h2>What helps - the evidence-based interventions</h2>

<h3>Resistance exercise</h3>
<p>The single most effective intervention. Strength training two or three times a week reduces frailty progression and improves mortality and disability outcomes.[3] In NZ, the Otago Exercise Programme, Steady As You Go, and Live Stronger for Longer all incorporate progressive resistance training. Body weight, resistance bands, or light weights all work; what matters is that the muscles feel worked.</p>

<h3>Protein and nutrition</h3>
<p>Older adults need more protein per kilo of body weight than younger adults to maintain muscle mass - around 1.0 to 1.2 grams per kilo per day. Practical examples: 100g chicken, an egg, a yoghurt, a glass of milk, a tin of fish each provide 10-20g of protein. Spread protein across all three meals; the body uses it more efficiently than dumping it into one meal. Vitamin D supplementation in deficient older adults reduces falls.</p>

<h3>Treating contributors</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pain</strong> - undertreated pain is a major driver of inactivity</li>
  <li><strong>Depression and anxiety</strong> - common, undertreated, reduce activity and intake</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep apnoea</strong> - causes daytime exhaustion mistaken for "just getting old"</li>
  <li><strong>Anaemia</strong> - iron, B12, folate, and chronic disease all contribute</li>
  <li><strong>Hypothyroidism</strong> - easy to miss in older adults</li>
  <li><strong>Visual and hearing loss</strong> - reduce engagement with the world and accelerate decline</li>
</ul>

<h3>Social engagement</h3>
<p>Loneliness is associated with faster frailty progression and earlier mortality, independent of physical activity. The mechanisms are not fully understood, but the effect size is comparable to smoking. Group classes, day programmes, religious communities, volunteer roles, family contact - all of these matter.</p>

<h3>Medication review</h3>
<p>The medicines that helped at 65 may be harming at 85. See the polypharmacy handout. The classes that come up most are sleeping tablets, anticholinergics, anti-inflammatories, and overly tight blood pressure control.</p>

<h3>Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment</h3>
<p>For people who are clearly frail or recently bouncing in and out of hospital, a comprehensive geriatric assessment by a geriatrician (sometimes via Older Persons' Health services in your DHB) is associated with better outcomes than usual care.[4] Ask your GP about referral.</p>

<h2>What does not help</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Bed rest in hospital. Even short hospital stays cause loss of muscle mass that takes weeks to recover. Get out of bed. Walk if you can. Eat sitting up.</li>
  <li>Restrictive diets. Most frail older adults need <em>more</em> calories and protein, not fewer. Low-fat low-salt low-sugar diets that work in middle age can cause weight loss in old age.</li>
  <li>Sleeping tablets prescribed long-term. They worsen falls and confusion.</li>
  <li>Aggressive blood pressure or blood sugar targets in late life. The harms outweigh the benefits when life expectancy is shorter.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things to discuss with your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Have I had an unintentional weight loss in the last year? Should we work out why?</li>
  <li>Am I on any medicines that might be making frailty worse?</li>
  <li>Would I benefit from a structured strength and balance programme like the Otago Exercise Programme?</li>
  <li>Am I eating enough protein? Should I see a dietitian?</li>
  <li>Could anything I am taking be tapered or stopped?</li>
  <li>Should I have a Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment?</li>
  <li>What are my goals for the next 5 years and how should care line up with them?</li>
</ul>
