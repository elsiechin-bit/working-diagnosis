<p>Roughly one in three New Zealanders aged 65 or over falls at least once a year, and the rate roughly doubles after 80.[1] Most falls do not cause injury, but a hip fracture in someone older than 80 still carries a one-year mortality of around 20-30%.[2] Falls are not an inevitable part of getting older. Most are predictable, and many are preventable.</p>

<h2>Why falls happen</h2>

<p>A fall is almost never one thing. It is usually three or four small things that add up: a bit of weakness, a bit of unsteadiness, a slightly slippery rug, the wrong glasses prescription, a new tablet, a step you used to clear and now do not. The good news is that interventions that target several factors at once tend to work much better than those that target only one.</p>

<h2>The home audit</h2>

<p>Walk through every room and look for these. ACC will fund a community falls assessor in some areas; ask your GP or look up your local DHB.</p>

<h3>Floors and walkways</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Loose rugs and mats - either tape them down or remove them</li>
  <li>Cords running across walkways - tape down or reroute</li>
  <li>Clutter in hallways and on stairs - clear walking paths</li>
  <li>Uneven thresholds between rooms - mark or eliminate</li>
</ul>

<h3>Lighting</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Bright lights at the top and bottom of stairs</li>
  <li>A nightlight or motion-sensor light between bed and toilet</li>
  <li>Light switches at the entrance to every room you use after dark</li>
  <li>Higher-wattage bulbs in the kitchen and bathroom</li>
</ul>

<h3>Bathroom</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Grab rails by the toilet and inside the shower or bath</li>
  <li>Non-slip mat in the shower and on the bathroom floor</li>
  <li>A shower seat if standing in the shower feels unsteady</li>
  <li>Raised toilet seat if getting up from a low toilet is hard</li>
</ul>

<h3>Stairs</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Handrails on both sides where possible</li>
  <li>Contrasting tape or paint on the edge of each step</li>
  <li>Step lights or a clear light source overhead</li>
</ul>

<h3>Outside</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Even paths, no moss, no loose pavers</li>
  <li>Outdoor lighting on at the front door</li>
  <li>A handrail at any external step</li>
</ul>

<h2>Strength and balance: what actually works</h2>

<p>The single most effective intervention for falls is a structured strength and balance programme. The Otago Exercise Programme, developed in NZ, is the most studied and the most widely funded here.[3] It is a set of 17 exercises plus a walking plan, done at home with periodic visits from a trained provider. Trials show roughly a 35% reduction in falls and fall-related injuries in people who complete it.[3]</p>

<p>You do not need to do anything fancy. A regular tai chi class works. Steady on Your Feet and Live Stronger for Longer are both NZ programmes designed around the same evidence. Aim for two to three sessions a week; you should feel slightly out of breath and your legs should feel worked the next day.</p>

<h2>Vision and hearing</h2>

<p>Get your eyes checked annually. New bifocals are a known fall risk in the first three months because the lower lens distorts steps; some people switch to single-vision distance glasses for walking outdoors. Untreated cataracts roughly double fall risk and surgery is funded in NZ once visual acuity drops below threshold. Hearing also matters: hearing loss is associated with three-fold higher fall risk, partly because of the role of the inner ear in balance.</p>

<h2>Medicines that increase fall risk</h2>

<p>Ask your GP or pharmacist to review your medicines specifically with falls in mind. The classes that matter most are:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Sleeping tablets and benzodiazepines (zopiclone, lorazepam, diazepam, temazepam)</li>
  <li>Older antidepressants and some SSRIs</li>
  <li>Antipsychotics</li>
  <li>Strong painkillers (opioids)</li>
  <li>Blood pressure medicines if your blood pressure drops on standing</li>
  <li>Anticholinergic medicines (some bladder, some hay fever, some Parkinson's)</li>
</ul>

<p>This does not mean stopping any of them on your own. It means a conversation with your GP about whether any can be reduced or swapped.</p>

<h2>Vitamin D, calcium, and bones</h2>

<p>Vitamin D supplementation reduces falls in people who are deficient and does not help those who are not, so blood test first if your GP is unsure. Calcium from food (dairy, leafy greens, tinned fish with bones) is preferred over supplements. If you have had a fragility fracture or low bone density on a DEXA scan, ask about bone-strengthening medicines.</p>

<h2>Footwear</h2>

<p>Shoes with a low heel, a firm sole, and laces or velcro that hold the foot. Avoid loose slippers, high-cushioning trainers (they reduce sensory feedback), and walking around in socks on smooth floors.</p>

<h2>If you have already fallen</h2>

<p>Tell your GP. Even a fall without injury is a strong predictor of the next one. Your GP can arrange a structured falls assessment, which usually includes blood pressure lying and standing, vision, balance and gait testing, and a medication review. If you fell because you blacked out, that needs separate investigation.</p>

<h2>If you cannot get up after a fall</h2>

<p>Stay calm. If you have a personal medical alarm, use it. If you have a phone in reach, call 111. If neither, try to roll onto your front, get to your hands and knees, and crawl to a stable piece of furniture (a bed or sofa) to push yourself up. Do not let pride keep you on the floor; lying still on a hard floor for hours causes muscle damage and pressure injuries.</p>

<p>If you live alone and have fallen, talk to your GP about a personal medical alarm. ACC and St John both run alarm services in NZ.</p>
