<p>Most adults will spend roughly a third of their lives asleep. When sleep goes well it is invisible. When it goes wrong, it tends to leak into everything else: mood, concentration, appetite, blood pressure, the patience you have for the people you love. This handout is a starting point. It is not medical advice for your specific situation, but it should help you make sense of what is normal, what is not, and what to try before booking a GP appointment.</p>

<h2>How sleep is meant to work</h2>

<p>Sleep is not a single state. Across a typical night, your brain cycles through several stages, each lasting roughly 90 minutes. Lighter stages near the start of the night, deeper restorative stages a few hours in, and longer dream sleep (REM) toward morning. Most adults have four to six of these cycles per night.</p>

<p>Two things govern when you feel sleepy:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sleep pressure.</strong> The longer you have been awake, the stronger the drive to sleep. A chemical called adenosine builds up while you are awake and clears while you sleep. Caffeine works mostly by blocking the adenosine receptor, which is why coffee delays the feeling of being tired.</li>
  <li><strong>Your body clock (circadian rhythm).</strong> A roughly 24-hour cycle that decides when you naturally want to sleep, when you feel alert, when your body temperature dips, and when hormones like melatonin and cortisol rise and fall. Light, especially bright morning light, is the strongest signal that sets this clock.</li>
</ul>

<p>The two systems normally line up: sleep pressure is high and the body clock says "night" at the same time. When they fall out of sync (shift work, jet lag, late-night screens, irregular bedtimes), sleep gets harder.</p>

<h2>How much sleep do I actually need?</h2>

<p>For most adults, between seven and nine hours a night. Children need more. People over 65 often sleep slightly less and find their sleep becomes lighter and more fragmented, which is normal rather than a disease.</p>

<p>Sleep need is genuinely variable. A small minority of adults function well on six hours; an even smaller minority truly need ten or more. The honest test is not the number of hours but how you feel: alert through the day without needing caffeine to function, an even mood, and the ability to fall asleep within 15 to 30 minutes when you go to bed.</p>

<h2>Common sleep problems</h2>

<h3>Trouble falling asleep or staying asleep (insomnia)</h3>

<p>Insomnia means trouble falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early at least three nights a week for at least three months, and it bothers you during the day. Short bouts of poor sleep around stressful events are different and usually settle. Long-running insomnia tends to persist on its own and benefits from active treatment rather than waiting it out.</p>

<p>The single most effective treatment for chronic insomnia is a structured course of cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I). It works better than sleeping tablets in the medium term and the effects last after you stop. More on this below.</p>

<h3>Snoring and obstructive sleep apnoea</h3>

<p>Snoring on its own is common and usually harmless. The thing to watch for is <strong>witnessed pauses in breathing</strong>, choking or gasping arousals, or waking unrefreshed despite enough hours in bed. That pattern is suggestive of obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), where the airway briefly collapses repeatedly through the night.</p>

<p>Untreated OSA contributes to daytime sleepiness, high blood pressure, atrial fibrillation, motor vehicle crashes, and worse outcomes after some operations. It is treatable, usually with a CPAP machine, sometimes with a dental device or weight reduction. If your partner reports apnoea events or you fall asleep at the wheel, please see your GP.</p>

<h3>Restless legs syndrome (RLS)</h3>

<p>An uncomfortable, sometimes irresistible urge to move the legs, worse in the evening or at rest, partially relieved by movement. It is not "just being fidgety". Iron deficiency is a common reversible cause, so a blood test is worth doing. Caffeine, alcohol, and some antidepressants make it worse.</p>

<h3>Shift work and jet lag</h3>

<p>If your work schedule rotates or you cross time zones, your body clock and your sleep pressure fall out of sync. Strategies that help include consistent anchor sleep, strategic light exposure, naps before night shifts, and a set wind-down routine. Most people underestimate how long it takes to adapt: roughly one day per time zone for jet lag.</p>

<h3>Sleep changes with age</h3>

<p>Older adults often go to bed earlier, wake earlier, and sleep more lightly with more night-time awakenings. This is not insomnia and does not always need treatment. What does need attention is excessive daytime sleepiness, dramatic changes in sleep pattern over weeks, or sleep that is being disrupted by pain, breathing problems, anxiety, or medicines.</p>

<h2>What to try first</h2>

<p>If you are sleeping badly, the steps below help most people most of the time. Give them at least two to three weeks of consistent effort before deciding whether they have helped. None of these are quick fixes, but together they shift the odds.</p>

<h3>Keep a steady wake time</h3>

<p>Going to bed at the same time matters less than waking at the same time. A consistent wake time, including weekends, is the single most powerful signal for your body clock. Catching up on weekends tends to make Monday harder.</p>

<h3>Get morning light</h3>

<p>Bright light within the first hour of waking sets your circadian clock for the day. Outdoor light is much stronger than indoor lighting (even on a cloudy NZ day). Ten to twenty minutes outside in the morning is usually enough.</p>

<h3>Watch caffeine, alcohol, and nicotine</h3>

<p>Caffeine has a half-life of around five hours, longer in some people. A 3pm coffee may still be working at midnight. Aim to stop caffeine by mid-afternoon if sleep is suffering. Alcohol may help you fall asleep but fragments the second half of the night and worsens sleep apnoea. Nicotine is a stimulant.</p>

<h3>Reserve the bed for sleep</h3>

<p>If you cannot fall asleep within 20 to 30 minutes, get out of bed, do something quiet and unstimulating in dim light, and only return when sleepy. This sounds counter-intuitive but it is core to CBT-I. The aim is to break the association between bed and frustration.</p>

<h3>Wind down for an hour</h3>

<p>The brain does not switch from work mode to sleep mode instantly. Build a 30 to 60 minute wind-down ritual: dim lights, finish meals, put work and screens away, do something gentle and consistent. Reading, a warm shower, slow stretching, journalling - whatever signals to your body that the day is done.</p>

<h3>Bedroom basics</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Cool: 18 to 20 degrees works for most people.</li>
  <li>Dark: blockout curtains or an eye mask if streetlights are an issue.</li>
  <li>Quiet: ear plugs or a white noise app if needed.</li>
  <li>Bed for sleep and intimacy only. Working from bed is one of the strongest predictors of insomnia.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Move your body during the day</h3>

<p>Regular physical activity is one of the most reliable ways to deepen sleep. Even a 20-minute walk most days helps. Vigorous exercise within an hour of bedtime keeps some people awake; for most people earlier in the day is better.</p>

<h3>If your mind races at night</h3>

<p>A "worry window" earlier in the evening can help: 15 minutes with a notebook, write down what is on your mind and what (if anything) you can do about it tomorrow. Putting concerns on paper outside of bed often reduces their grip at 2am.</p>

<h2>When the basics are not enough</h2>

<h3>Cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I)</h3>

<p>CBT-I is the first-line treatment for chronic insomnia. It is usually four to eight sessions, in person or online, and it teaches a structured set of techniques: stimulus control (the bed-for-sleep rule above), sleep restriction (deliberately compressing time in bed to rebuild sleep pressure), cognitive work on unhelpful thoughts about sleep, and relaxation methods.</p>

<p>It outperforms sleeping tablets in studies and the effects persist after the course ends. The catch is that it requires effort and short-term tolerance of feeling more tired before it gets better. <a href="https://www.sleepio.com">Sleepio</a> is one widely-validated digital CBT-I programme used in the UK NHS; it is available in NZ on subscription. Some NZ psychologists offer in-person CBT-I; ask your GP for referral options in your area.</p>

<h3>Treating what is keeping you awake</h3>

<p>Insomnia often rides on the back of something else. Anxiety, depression, chronic pain, untreated reflux, an enlarged prostate, restless legs, sleep apnoea, perimenopausal hot flushes, certain medicines (some antidepressants, steroids, beta-blockers, decongestants). Naming the underlying issue and treating it is often the most direct route to better sleep.</p>

<h2>Sleeping tablets: the honest version</h2>

<p>Hypnotic tablets such as zopiclone, temazepam, and benzodiazepines work in the short term but have meaningful trade-offs: tolerance develops within weeks, dependence and withdrawal are real, falls and fractures rise (especially in older adults), and a small but consistent literature links long-term use to cognitive decline and earlier mortality. NZ guidance favours short courses (one to two weeks) for acute situational insomnia, not chronic use.</p>

<p>Melatonin is a hormone your body already makes. Slow-release melatonin (Circadin) is funded in NZ for adults aged 55 and over with primary insomnia, and is sometimes used off-label for jet lag and shift work. Over-the-counter melatonin is now available in NZ pharmacies for adults over 55. The effect size is modest; the safety profile is good.</p>

<p>Some antidepressants (low-dose mirtazapine, doxepin, amitriptyline) and antihistamines are sometimes used off-label for sleep. They have their own side-effect profiles and are best discussed with your GP rather than self-prescribed.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your partner has noticed you stop breathing, choke, or gasp during sleep.</li>
  <li>You are falling asleep during the day in situations you should be alert in: driving, working, in conversation.</li>
  <li>Insomnia has lasted more than three months despite trying the basics.</li>
  <li>Sleep is being disrupted by pain, low mood, anxiety, hot flushes, or breathing problems that need their own treatment.</li>
  <li>You suspect restless legs syndrome, especially with new symptoms or a family history.</li>
  <li>You have been on sleeping tablets for more than a few weeks and want a plan to come off them safely.</li>
  <li>Sleep behaviours that suggest a parasomnia: acting out dreams, sleepwalking, repeated vivid nightmares.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is probably <em>not</em> a problem</h2>

<ul>
  <li>One or two bad nights here and there. Almost everyone has them.</li>
  <li>Waking briefly once or twice a night and falling back asleep within minutes. This is normal.</li>
  <li>Needing slightly less sleep than you used to as you get older.</li>
  <li>Vivid dreams that you remember. Dream recall varies and rarely indicates anything wrong.</li>
</ul>
