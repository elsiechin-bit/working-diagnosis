<p>Sleep is the topic that can make a confident parent crumble at 3am. There is so much advice out there - some of it useful, some of it making things worse. This handout focuses on what is actually known: safe sleep, what is developmentally normal, what tends to help, and the few situations where you should ask your GP, midwife, or Plunket nurse.</p>

<h2>Safe sleep - the non-negotiables</h2>

<p>The single most important section. Sudden Unexpected Death in Infancy (SUDI) is rare but devastating, and most cases happen during sleep. Following the safe-sleep basics dramatically reduces risk:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>On the back, every sleep, every time.</strong> Not on the side, not on the front.</li>
  <li><strong>Face up, face clear, face uncovered.</strong> No bedding over the face.</li>
  <li><strong>Smoke-free environment</strong> - before birth, after birth, in pregnancy, around the home.</li>
  <li><strong>Their own sleep space.</strong> Cot, bassinet, pepi-pod, wahakura. Firm, flat mattress. No pillow under 12 months. No loose bedding, bumpers, soft toys, or sheepskins.</li>
  <li><strong>Same room as parents</strong> for the first 6 months at least. Not the same bed in unsafe circumstances (alcohol, smoking, very tired parents, sofa sleeping). Shared sleeping in safe circumstances is acceptable for breastfeeding families - see Plunket guidance.</li>
  <li><strong>Breastfeeding</strong> reduces SUDI risk and helps establish sleep biology.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is normal by age</h2>

<h3>Newborn (0-3 months)</h3>
<ul>
  <li>14-17 hours of sleep across 24 hours, in chunks of 2-4 hours</li>
  <li>No day-night rhythm yet - they are not "broken", their internal clock is still developing</li>
  <li>Frequent feeding around the clock is normal and expected</li>
  <li>Some babies "cluster feed" in the evenings - common, not a sign of inadequate milk supply</li>
</ul>

<h3>3-6 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>14-15 hours of sleep, mostly at night with 3-4 daytime naps</li>
  <li>Day-night rhythm starts to emerge</li>
  <li>Some babies sleep 5-6 hour stretches at night; many do not yet</li>
  <li>The 4-month "sleep regression" is real - sleep cycles are maturing, often disrupting what was working</li>
</ul>

<h3>6-12 months</h3>
<ul>
  <li>13-14 hours total, usually with 2 daytime naps</li>
  <li>Most babies wake 1-3 times overnight; some sleep through</li>
  <li>Solid food does not magically improve night sleep, despite advice you will hear</li>
  <li>Separation anxiety often kicks in around 8-9 months and disrupts sleep</li>
</ul>

<h3>12-24 months (toddler)</h3>
<ul>
  <li>11-14 hours total, usually with 1 daytime nap</li>
  <li>Bedtime resistance and night waking peaks - normal developmentally</li>
  <li>Transitions: cot to bed, dropping the daytime nap, big-kid pyjamas - these can disturb sleep for a few weeks</li>
</ul>

<h3>2-5 years</h3>
<ul>
  <li>10-13 hours total, often with a 1-2 hour afternoon nap that drops by age 3-4</li>
  <li>Bedtime can be a battle of wills</li>
  <li>Nightmares appear around age 3</li>
  <li>Night terrors - dramatic, the child seems awake but is not - usually resolve on their own</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Routine</h3>
<p>The same sequence at the same time every evening. A typical infant routine: bath, feed, pyjamas, book, song, into the cot drowsy but awake. The routine matters more than the time. The body learns: this sequence means sleep.</p>

<h3>Wind-down</h3>
<p>30 minutes of low light, low stimulation, no screens. Quiet music, a story, a cuddle, dim the lights. The brain needs warning that sleep is coming.</p>

<h3>Sleep environment</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Dark room (blackout curtains for early sleepers)</li>
  <li>Cool (16-19 degrees comfortable)</li>
  <li>Quiet, or with white noise if your home is loud</li>
  <li>Familiar, predictable - same cot, same lovey from around 6 months</li>
</ul>

<h3>Drowsy but awake</h3>
<p>From around 3-4 months, the aim is to put the baby down in the cot when they are tired but still awake, so they learn to fall asleep in their sleep place. This is not always possible (and not always necessary in early months), but the more they associate cot = falling asleep, the less they need to be re-fed or re-rocked at every wake.</p>

<h3>Daytime light, exercise, time outdoors</h3>
<p>Helps set the body clock. Especially for toddlers and older children.</p>

<h2>The hard bits</h2>

<h3>Night feeding</h3>
<p>Most babies need night feeds for the first few months. Most are physically capable of dropping night feeds by 6-9 months but many continue out of habit. There is no rush. If night feeds are working for you, keep going. If they are exhausting and the baby is otherwise well-fed during the day, gradual reduction usually works.</p>

<h3>Sleep training</h3>
<p>There are several approaches - graduated extinction ("controlled crying"), bedtime fading, the chair method, the no-cry methods. Evidence supports most approaches as effective for infants over 6 months when families are ready. None work for every family. Speak to a Plunket nurse, GP, or sleep consultant if you want guidance. Avoid sleep training before 4-6 months.</p>

<h3>The 4-month regression</h3>
<p>Sleep that was working stops working. The baby's sleep cycles are maturing. Usually settles within 2-4 weeks. Hold the routine, hold the safe-sleep basics, get help if needed.</p>

<h3>Toddler bedtime resistance</h3>
<p>Universal between 18 months and 3 years. The pattern that helps:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Predictable routine</li>
  <li>Choices within limits ("the blue book or the red book?")</li>
  <li>Clear, calm "this is the last one" - and stick to it</li>
  <li>Consistent return to bed without lengthy negotiation</li>
  <li>Reward good bedtimes the next morning</li>
</ul>

<h3>Night waking in toddlers</h3>
<p>Most night waking after age 1 is settled by removing the thing that has become a sleep crutch (feeding to sleep, parent in the room, being rocked). This takes consistency over 1-3 weeks.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP, Plunket, or midwife</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sleep is a major source of family stress</strong> - parental wellbeing matters too</li>
  <li><strong>Snoring, mouth breathing, pauses in breathing during sleep</strong> - consider obstructive sleep apnoea, often related to enlarged tonsils or adenoids</li>
  <li><strong>Sudden change in sleep pattern</strong> in a previously good sleeper - rule out illness, ear infection, reflux</li>
  <li><strong>Reflux making sleep painful</strong> - common, often improves around 6 months</li>
  <li><strong>Persistent unsettled crying day and night</strong> in a young infant - can be normal but worth a check</li>
  <li><strong>Postnatal depression or anxiety</strong> - profoundly affects sleep, treatable</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep training advice</strong> - structured help often works much faster than DIY</li>
</ul>
