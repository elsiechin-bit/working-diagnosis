<p>Stopping smoking is the single most powerful thing most smokers can do for their health. The benefits start within hours - blood pressure and carbon monoxide drop the same day, and within a year the heart attack risk has roughly halved. Within 10 to 15 years, lung cancer risk approaches that of a never-smoker. Most people who successfully quit have made several attempts before the one that stuck, so a previous attempt that did not work is not failure - it is data. Daily smoking among NZ adults has dropped from around 25% in the 1990s to under 10% today<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>, and the NZ Smokefree Aotearoa 2025 plan continues to push the rate down toward 5%.</p>

<h2>What works best</h2>

<p>The combinations that get the highest quit rates in good clinical trials are: behavioural support PLUS medication, started at the same time. On their own, willpower-only attempts succeed in only a small minority. Pairing a coach (Quitline, your GP, a stop-smoking nurse) with the right medication roughly doubles your chance of being smoke-free a year later.</p>

<h3>Behavioural and coaching support</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Quitline (0800 778 778)</strong> is free, NZ-based, and offers phone, text, and online support. The texts are surprisingly effective on their own and pair well with medication.</li>
  <li><strong>Aukati Kai Paipa</strong> and <strong>Pacific Quit Smoking Services</strong> offer kaupapa-Maori and Pacific-led support nationally - ask your GP or call Quitline for the closest service.</li>
  <li><strong>Your GP or practice nurse</strong> can prescribe medication and book follow-up around your quit date. A single 10-minute conversation about quitting at a regular GP visit is enough to lift quit rates measurably.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT)</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Combination NRT</strong> - a 24-hour patch PLUS a fast-acting product (gum, lozenge, mouth spray, or inhalator) - is more effective than any single NRT product alone, with quit rates around a third higher in pooled trial data<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>. Use the patch for the steady background dose and the fast-acting product for cravings.</li>
  <li><strong>Funded in NZ</strong> with a Quit Card or prescription. Quitline can issue a Quit Card by phone; your GP or practice nurse can prescribe.</li>
  <li><strong>Use the right strength.</strong> If you smoke 10 or more a day, start on the 21 mg patch, not the 14 mg.</li>
  <li><strong>Use it for long enough.</strong> Most people stop NRT too early. 8 to 12 weeks is the standard course; longer is fine if you need it.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Varenicline and bupropion</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Varenicline</strong> (Champix) is the most effective single medication available, more effective than NRT alone in head-to-head trials. Funded in NZ on Special Authority criteria; your GP can apply. Take with food, start 1 week before your quit date, plan around the (often-mild) nausea.</li>
  <li><strong>Bupropion</strong> (Zyban) is an alternative; less effective than varenicline but useful when varenicline is unsuitable.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Vaping as a cessation tool</h3>

<p>For adults who have not been able to quit with NRT or medication, switching from cigarettes to vaping is a legitimate cessation option in NZ guidance. The recent Cochrane review found vaping with nicotine helps more people stop smoking than NRT alone. The plan: switch fully (not dual use), use a strong-enough device, and build in a step-down on nicotine concentration once you are stable. See the separate <a href="/handouts/vaping/">Vaping handout</a> for detail.</p>

<h2>If you slip</h2>

<p>A "lapse" - one cigarette, one day - is not a "relapse" unless you let it become one. Most successful quitters slip at least once. The useful response is curiosity, not catastrophe: what was the trigger, what would help next time, do you need to step the medication up or change something about the support? Your GP and Quitline can adjust the plan rather than scrap it.</p>

<h2>Practical tips around the quit date</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pick a date</strong> within the next 2 weeks; tell people; remove tobacco, lighters, ashtrays from the house and car.</li>
  <li><strong>Start medication a few days before</strong> the date if it is varenicline, or on the day if it is NRT.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan for the high-risk moments:</strong> first coffee, after meals, alcohol, breaks at work, driving. A fast-acting NRT product within reach helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Move your routine.</strong> Different chair, different mug, different walk-route - breaks the cue-craving link.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep some tools for the long tail:</strong> cravings get less frequent week by week but can still hit at 6 to 12 months. Holding onto a few lozenges is sensible, not weakness.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You want to quit and would like a Quit Card, NRT prescription, or varenicline</li>
  <li>You have tried before and want to try a different combination</li>
  <li>You have a chronic illness made worse by smoking (COPD, heart disease, diabetes) - cessation support is part of standard treatment</li>
  <li>You are pregnant or breastfeeding - support and NRT are both safer than continuing to smoke</li>
  <li>You have ever had seizures, an eating disorder, severe mental illness, or take medications that interact with bupropion or varenicline</li>
</ul>
