<p>Food labels in New Zealand provide nutrition information to help you make informed choices. Learning to read and interpret nutrition panels helps you compare products and understand what you are eating.</p>

<h2>How to read the nutrition information panel</h2>

<p>The nutrition information panel on the back of packages shows quantities per 100 grams and per serving. Key information includes energy (kilojoules), protein, total fat, saturated fat, carbohydrates, sugars, sodium, and fibre. Comparing per 100 grams lets you compare different package sizes fairly.</p>

<h2>Understanding key nutrients</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Energy.</strong> Measured in kilojoules (kJ). Compare to understand calorie density. High-energy foods are not bad - nuts are high-energy but nutritious.</li>
  <li><strong>Protein.</strong> Measured in grams. Aim for adequate protein across your day (about 50 grams for women, 65 grams for men).</li>
  <li><strong>Total fat.</strong> Not all fat is bad. Distinguish between saturated and unsaturated fats when choosing.</li>
  <li><strong>Saturated fat.</strong> Limit to less than 10% of energy daily. High saturated fat is linked to heart disease. Look for products lower in saturated fat.</li>
  <li><strong>Carbohydrates.</strong> Include sugars and fibre. Not all carbohydrates are equal - whole grains and fibre are healthier than refined carbohydrates.</li>
  <li><strong>Sugars.</strong> Listed separately from other carbohydrates. Aim to limit to less than 25 grams daily. Check sugars in seemingly healthy foods like yoghurt and breakfast cereals.</li>
  <li><strong>Sodium.</strong> Limit to less than 2300 milligrams daily. Products with less than 120 mg per 100 grams are low in sodium.</li>
  <li><strong>Fibre.</strong> Aim for 25 - 30 grams daily. Fibre supports digestive health and satiety.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Front-of-pack labelling in New Zealand</h2>

<p>Some NZ products display the Health Star Rating (1 to 5 stars) or nutrient content claims on the front. Five stars indicates a healthier choice; one star indicates less healthy. This is a quick way to compare products in the same category.</p>

<h2>Health claims and their meanings</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Low fat.</strong> Less than 5 grams fat per 100 grams (solids) or 2.5 grams per 100 ml (liquids).</li>
  <li><strong>Low sodium.</strong> Less than 120 milligrams per 100 grams.</li>
  <li><strong>Good source of fibre.</strong> At least 3 grams fibre per serving.</li>
  <li><strong>Sugar free.</strong> Less than 0.5 grams sugar per 100 grams, but may contain sweeteners. Do not assume sugar-free products are calorie-free.</li>
  <li><strong>No added sugar.</strong> Manufacturer has not added sugar, but the product may naturally contain sugars (fruit, milk).</li>
</ul>

<h2>Ingredients list - what to look for</h2>

<p>Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. Check the first few ingredients - they make up most of the product. Be aware of hidden sugars (listed as glucose, honey, agave, or many other names). Highly processed foods have long ingredient lists. Simpler is usually better.</p>

<h2>Practical tips for label reading</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Compare per 100 grams.</strong> This allows fair comparison regardless of package size.</li>
  <li><strong>Check serving size.</strong> A product might look low in sugar per serving because the serving is very small.</li>
  <li><strong>Look for whole grains.</strong> "Wholemeal" or "wholegrain" early in the ingredient list indicates a healthier choice than refined grains.</li>
  <li><strong>Be cautious of health claims.</strong> A product labeled "low fat" might be high in sugar. Read the full panel.</li>
  <li><strong>Watch for ultra-processed foods.</strong> Many additives and long ingredient lists suggest ultra-processing.</li>
  <li><strong>Compare products in the same category.</strong> Comparing two breakfast cereals using the Health Star Rating helps choose the better option.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Special labelling - allergens and dietary requirements</h2>

<p>Allergen information must be clearly stated if the product contains common allergens (peanuts, tree nuts, milk, eggs, sesame). Vegan, vegetarian, and gluten-free labelling help those with dietary requirements.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask yourself when reading labels</h2>

<ul>
  <li>How much sugar is in this product?</li>
  <li>Is this a good source of fibre?</li>
  <li>How much sodium does it contain?</li>
  <li>Are there better options in this product category?</li>
  <li>How long is the ingredient list?</li>
  <li>What is the serving size, and how many servings are in the package?</li>
</ul>
