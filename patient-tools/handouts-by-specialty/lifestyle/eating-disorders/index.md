<p>Eating disorders are serious mental illnesses, not lifestyle choices, diets, or willpower problems. They affect roughly 1 in 20 New Zealanders at some point in their lives, across all ages, ethnicities, body sizes, and genders.[1] They have the highest mortality rate of any mental illness when untreated. The good news is they are treatable. Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes. This handout is a starting point: what to recognise, when to seek help, and how the NZ system is organised.</p>

<h2>The myths that get in the way</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>"Only thin people have eating disorders."</strong> Most people with eating disorders are not underweight. Bulimia, binge eating disorder, and atypical anorexia all occur in people of normal or above-average weight.</li>
  <li><strong>"Only girls and women."</strong> Around a third of NZ eating disorder presentations are male. Less recognised, often diagnosed later.</li>
  <li><strong>"It is a teenage thing."</strong> Onset can be at any age. Mid-life onset is increasingly recognised, often around perimenopause or major life transitions.</li>
  <li><strong>"They are doing it to themselves."</strong> Eating disorders involve real disturbances in brain function, hunger signals, body perception, and emotional regulation. They are not a choice.</li>
  <li><strong>"Just eat / just stop eating that much."</strong> If it were that simple, we would not be talking about an illness.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The four main types</h2>

<h3>Anorexia nervosa</h3>
<p>Restriction of food intake leading to significantly low body weight, intense fear of weight gain, distorted perception of body shape and size. Subtypes:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Restricting type - severe restriction without binge or purge behaviours</li>
  <li>Binge-eating/purging type - episodes of binge eating or compensatory behaviours</li>
</ul>

<p>Lifetime prevalence around 1-2% of women, 0.3% of men. Most life-threatening of the eating disorders due to physical complications (cardiac arrhythmias, electrolyte disturbances, refeeding syndrome).</p>

<h3>Bulimia nervosa</h3>
<p>Recurrent binge eating - eating an objectively large amount in a discrete period with sense of loss of control - followed by compensatory behaviours: vomiting, laxative misuse, fasting, or excessive exercise. Body weight is usually normal or above. Recurrent binge-purge cycles cause electrolyte disturbances, dental erosion, GI complications.</p>

<h3>Binge eating disorder (BED)</h3>
<p>Recurrent binge eating without compensatory behaviours, with marked distress about the binges. The most common eating disorder. Often in higher BMI ranges. Frequently misdiagnosed as "just" being overweight.</p>

<h3>Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder (ARFID)</h3>
<p>Eating little or only specific foods, not driven by body image. Sensory aversion, fear of negative consequences (choking, vomiting), low interest in eating. More commonly recognised in children but occurs at any age. Often co-occurs with autism.</p>

<h3>Other</h3>
<p>"Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder" (OSFED) covers significant disordered eating that does not fit one of the above categories. Includes atypical anorexia (all features of anorexia but weight in normal range) and orthorexia (obsessive focus on "clean" eating). These are clinically significant and need treatment.</p>

<h2>Warning signs</h2>

<h3>Behavioural</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Skipping meals, eating very little, eating in isolation</li>
  <li>Eating in highly restricted patterns, lots of rules</li>
  <li>Counting calories obsessively, weighing food</li>
  <li>Binge eating (eating an objectively large amount, often in secret, with distress)</li>
  <li>Going to the bathroom immediately after meals</li>
  <li>Excessive exercise driven by guilt, especially after eating</li>
  <li>Avoiding social situations involving food</li>
  <li>Sudden adoption of restrictive diet (vegetarian, vegan, gluten-free) often as cover</li>
  <li>Hoarding or hiding food</li>
  <li>Unusual preoccupation with food, recipes, cooking for others without eating</li>
  <li>Frequent weight checking or body checking (mirror, measurements)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Physical</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Significant weight loss or weight fluctuation</li>
  <li>Loss of menstrual periods</li>
  <li>Always cold, often wearing layers</li>
  <li>Hair thinning or loss</li>
  <li>Lanugo (fine hair growth on face and body)</li>
  <li>Dry skin, brittle nails</li>
  <li>Tooth erosion, swollen salivary glands (in vomiting)</li>
  <li>Knuckle calluses (Russell's sign, in vomiting)</li>
  <li>Fatigue, dizziness, fainting</li>
  <li>Slow heart rate, low blood pressure</li>
  <li>Constipation</li>
</ul>

<h3>Emotional and cognitive</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Intense preoccupation with weight, shape, food</li>
  <li>Mood worse around meals or after them</li>
  <li>Anxiety about eating in front of others</li>
  <li>Distorted body image - seeing oneself larger than one is</li>
  <li>Self-worth tied to weight, shape, eating</li>
  <li>Guilt or shame after eating</li>
  <li>Withdrawal from friends and activities</li>
  <li>Depression, anxiety</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why early help matters</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Recovery rates are significantly higher with treatment within the first 1-3 years of illness onset</li>
  <li>Physical complications accumulate with time - bone density loss, cardiac problems, fertility issues</li>
  <li>Patterns become more entrenched the longer they continue</li>
  <li>Brain changes from sustained malnutrition are partially reversible early but may persist if untreated long-term</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to access help in NZ</h2>

<h3>Step 1: GP</h3>
<p>The right starting point. Your GP will:</p>
<ul>
  <li>Take a history without judgement</li>
  <li>Check physical observations - weight, height, BMI, blood pressure, heart rate, hydration</li>
  <li>Order blood tests - electrolytes, kidney and liver function, full blood count, iron, B12, thyroid</li>
  <li>Sometimes ECG</li>
  <li>Refer to specialist services</li>
</ul>

<p>If you cannot face going alone, take a friend or family member. If your GP does not seem to take it seriously, advocate for referral or seek a different GP. Eating disorders are sometimes still missed in primary care.</p>

<h3>Step 2: specialist services</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Regional Eating Disorder Services (RES)</strong> - DHB-funded specialist services in main centres. Free. Multidisciplinary teams (psychiatrist, psychologist, dietitian, family therapist). Refer via GP. Take referrals from anywhere in their region.</li>
  <li><strong>Hospital admission</strong> - for medical instability or severe malnutrition. Usually short-term to stabilise, then back to outpatient or specialist eating disorder ward.</li>
  <li><strong>Private therapy</strong> - psychologists with eating disorder specialty. NZ$120-200 per session. The NZ Association of Counsellors and Eating Disorders Association of NZ have searchable directories.</li>
  <li><strong>Family-based treatment (FBT)</strong> for adolescents - evidence-based first-line for under-18s, often available through public services.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Step 3: ongoing support</h3>

<ul>
  <li>Maudsley FBT for younger patients</li>
  <li>Cognitive Behavioural Therapy - Enhanced (CBT-E) for adults</li>
  <li>Dietitian with eating disorder experience</li>
  <li>Peer support and family support groups</li>
  <li>Medication for co-existing depression, anxiety, or to support refeeding</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you are supporting someone</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Avoid commenting on weight, shape, or food choices</li>
  <li>Avoid making eating into a confrontation</li>
  <li>Express concern for the person, not for the eating - "I am worried about how you are doing"</li>
  <li>Encourage early professional help</li>
  <li>Family-based therapy actively involves you</li>
  <li>Look after yourself - supporting someone with an eating disorder is exhausting</li>
  <li>Get support yourself - EDANZ runs family support</li>
</ul>
