<p>This handout takes a deliberately non-diet view. Weight is one signal, but it is not the most useful one. Two people of the same weight can have very different metabolic health depending on how they move, what they eat, how they sleep, how stressed they are, and what their genes hand them. The behaviours that improve metabolic health - more movement, more whole food, better sleep, less alcohol - help regardless of whether the scale changes. Around 1 in 3 NZ adults sit in the overweight category and around 1 in 3 in the obese category by current BMI cut-offs<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>, with rates considerably higher among Maori and Pacific peoples - a pattern driven mostly by housing, food access, work, and intergenerational structural factors, not by individual willpower.</p>

<h2>Why the scale is a poor compass on its own</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>BMI is a population tool</strong>, not a diagnostic test. It misses the difference between muscle and fat, ignores where fat sits, and was derived from European populations - it tends to underestimate metabolic risk in some Asian groups and overestimate in some Pacific groups.</li>
  <li><strong>Weight fluctuates</strong> with hydration, hormones, and bowel content by 1 to 2 kg day-to-day. Daily weighing is mostly noise.</li>
  <li><strong>"Metabolically healthy" at a higher weight</strong> is real - if blood pressure, blood glucose, lipids, fitness, and waist circumference are good, the higher number on the scale matters less than people are sometimes told.</li>
  <li><strong>"Metabolically unhealthy" at a normal weight</strong> is also real - sometimes called "thin outside, fat inside" (TOFI). Sedentary behaviour, ultra-processed food, and poor sleep can give you a metabolic profile that looks like obesity at a healthy BMI.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What metabolic health actually means</h2>

<p>The markers your GP and clinic team will be paying attention to alongside (or instead of) weight are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Waist circumference</strong>, taken at the navel - a better marker of visceral fat than BMI</li>
  <li><strong>Blood pressure</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Fasting blood glucose or HbA1c</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Lipid profile</strong> (cholesterol fractions and triglycerides)</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiorespiratory fitness</strong> - even a basic measure like how easily you climb stairs predicts long-term outcomes well</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep, mood, and stress</strong> - all directly affect insulin sensitivity, appetite, and cardiovascular risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why most diets fail in the long run</h2>

<p>Restrictive diets work in the short term and fail in the long term, with the majority of weight regained within 2 to 5 years and a substantial minority ending heavier than they started<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>. The failure is biological, not moral. The body defends a weight setpoint with hormonal and metabolic responses (raised hunger hormones, lowered satiety hormones, slower resting metabolism) that get stronger the more weight is lost. Repeated cycles of dieting and regain (weight cycling) appear to do their own metabolic damage and are linked to disordered eating, anxiety, and depression.</p>

<h2>What does help, regardless of the scale</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Move more, sit less.</strong> The biggest health gains come from moving from "doing very little" to "doing some" - 150 minutes a week of moderate activity, plus muscle-strengthening 2 days a week. See the <a href="/handouts/getting-active/">Getting active handout</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat more whole food.</strong> Vegetables, fruit, legumes, whole grains, fish, nuts. The Heart Foundation NZ "eating for a healthy heart" pattern is solid and easy to follow.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce ultra-processed food.</strong> The strongest evidence is around drinks - sugary drinks, energy drinks, and frequent alcohol drive metabolic risk hard.</li>
  <li><strong>Prioritise sleep.</strong> Short sleep (under 6 hours) reliably worsens insulin resistance and appetite regulation; see <a href="/lifestyle-prevention/sleep/">Sleep</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress.</strong> Chronic stress raises cortisol and pushes appetite toward energy-dense food. Movement, social contact, and mindfulness all help.</li>
  <li><strong>Build relationships with food, not rules around it.</strong> Eat at regular intervals, allow all foods, eat with people where you can. Most evidence-based eating advice is unfussy.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you do want to focus on weight</h2>

<p>If after that you and your GP still want to target weight specifically - because of joint pain, sleep apnoea, diabetes, or fertility, for instance - the realistic options in NZ are: structured behavioural programmes (Green Prescription, dietitian referral), GLP-1 receptor agonist medications (semaglutide, liraglutide - currently mostly self-funded for weight in NZ outside specific funded indications, expensive), and bariatric surgery via DHB or private referral for severe obesity. Honest framing matters here: 5 to 10% sustained weight loss does meaningfully improve diabetes, blood pressure, and joint pain, but more than that is hard to achieve and harder to keep. See the separate <a href="/handouts/weight-loss-medications/">Weight-loss medications handout</a> for medication detail.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Annual cardiovascular risk check from age 30 (Maori, Pacific, South Asian) or 45 (everyone else)</li>
  <li>Worry about diabetes, blood pressure, sleep apnoea, fatty liver, or PCOS</li>
  <li>Joint pain that is limiting movement</li>
  <li>Disordered eating - bingeing, purging, severe restriction, weight as a near-constant focus. Eating disorders happen at every body size and are very treatable</li>
  <li>You feel weight-shamed by health care - tell us. We do not always get this right and we want to</li>
</ul>
