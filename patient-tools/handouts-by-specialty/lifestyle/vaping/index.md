<p>Vaping (using e-cigarettes) involves heating a liquid - usually containing nicotine, propylene glycol, glycerine, and flavourings - to make a vapour you inhale. Vapes have been widely available in Aotearoa for about a decade, and have rapidly become the most common way young people are first exposed to nicotine. The honest summary of the evidence is this: vaping is much less harmful than smoking - around 95% less harmful by the most-cited estimate<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> - but it is not harmless, and we do not yet know its long-term effects.</p>

<h2>The NZ context</h2>

<p>The position taken by Health New Zealand (Te Whatu Ora) and the Ministry of Health is two-sided. For adults who smoke, vaping is officially supported as a cessation tool - it is one of the more effective options available and was a central plank of the Smokefree Aotearoa 2025 plan. For young people and never-smokers, vaping is actively discouraged: NZ has had some of the highest youth vaping rates in the world, and most policy changes since 2023 (restrictions on disposables, flavour limits, retail proximity rules) have been aimed at reducing youth uptake.</p>

<h2>What we know about the harms</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Nicotine is highly addictive.</strong> Modern vape devices deliver nicotine quickly and at high concentrations, sometimes higher than a typical cigarette. Dependence can develop within weeks, particularly in adolescents whose brains are still developing.</li>
  <li><strong>Throat, mouth, and airway irritation.</strong> Cough, dry mouth, sore throat, and a feeling of tightness or wheeziness are commonly reported. Most settle on stopping.</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular effects of nicotine.</strong> Vaping raises heart rate and blood pressure. People with heart disease or high blood pressure should treat that as a real concern.</li>
  <li><strong>Risk to children at home.</strong> Liquid nicotine is a poison; even a few millilitres swallowed by a small child can cause serious illness. Store devices and refills out of reach of tamariki, and keep the National Poisons Centre number to hand.</li>
  <li><strong>EVALI.</strong> A serious lung injury (e-cigarette or vaping use-associated lung injury) was reported mainly in the USA in 2019, linked overwhelmingly to illicit THC vape liquids containing vitamin E acetate, not to regulated nicotine vapes. Cases in NZ have been very rare, but it remains a reason to avoid black-market or modified products.</li>
  <li><strong>Pregnancy.</strong> Nicotine in any form crosses the placenta and affects foetal development. Vaping in pregnancy is not safe, though it is less harmful than continuing to smoke.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What we do not yet know</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Long-term respiratory effects over decades of use</li>
  <li>Cancer risk - many of the carcinogens in cigarette smoke are absent or much lower in vape aerosol, but long follow-up data does not yet exist</li>
  <li>Effects on the developing adolescent brain - early research suggests effects on attention, memory, and mood, but the picture is incomplete</li>
  <li>Cardiovascular outcomes over 20 to 30 years of use</li>
</ul>

<p>This is not a reason to panic, but it is a reason to be cautious - especially if you have never smoked, are young, or are pregnant.</p>

<h2>If you are using vaping to quit smoking</h2>

<p>For adult smokers, switching from cigarettes to vaping reduces exposure to most of the harmful chemicals in tobacco smoke. UK and NZ guidance both treat vaping as a legitimate cessation tool. The most effective approach is to switch fully (rather than continuing to do both), use a device strong enough to satisfy your nicotine need, and have a plan to step down nicotine strength over time. Quitline can help you build that plan, and the same nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) options that work for smoking - patches, gum, lozenges - can also help bring vaping under control later.</p>

<h2>If you are vaping but never smoked</h2>

<p>The risk-benefit calculation here is different. You are not displacing the much greater harm of cigarettes - you are simply taking on a new set of risks, including nicotine dependence and the long-term unknowns above. Most adults who started vaping in this category say they wish they had not. The earlier you stop, the better.</p>

<h2>Quitting vaping</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Quitline (0800 778 778)</strong> is free, supports both smoking and vaping cessation, and can text or call you at agreed times. NZ-specific, evidence-based, and works.</li>
  <li><strong>Nicotine replacement therapy</strong> (patches, gum, lozenges) is funded with a Community Services Card. Combination NRT - a patch plus a fast-acting product like gum or lozenges - is more effective than either alone, with quit rates around a third higher than single-product NRT in pooled trial data<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Step down the nicotine strength gradually</strong> in your vape liquid before stopping the device altogether. Going from 50 mg/mL (the NZ legal maximum for nicotine-salt vape liquids<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup>) straight to 0 mg/mL is rarely successful.</li>
  <li><strong>Identify your triggers</strong> (times of day, places, emotions) and plan a substitute behaviour. Most cravings pass within a few minutes.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell people you are quitting.</strong> Social support roughly doubles success rates.</li>
</ul>

<h2>For parents and whanau</h2>

<p>If you are worried about a young person vaping, it usually helps to lead with curiosity rather than confrontation. Ask what they enjoy about it, what their friends are doing, and whether they have ever tried to stop. Many tamariki want to quit but feel hooked. Quitline supports under-18s, and your GP can talk through NRT options for adolescents where appropriate.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are vaping and are pregnant or planning pregnancy</li>
  <li>You have a heart condition, asthma, or COPD and feel vaping is making it worse</li>
  <li>You have a persistent cough, breathlessness, or chest pain that started after taking up vaping</li>
  <li>You have tried to quit on your own and want help with NRT, varenicline, or other medication</li>
  <li>A child has accessed a vape device or liquid - call the National Poisons Centre on 0800 764 766 immediately</li>
</ul>
