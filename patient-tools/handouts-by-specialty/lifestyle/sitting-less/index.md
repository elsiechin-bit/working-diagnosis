<p>Sitting and exercise are different things. The evidence over the past decade has made it clear that you can meet the physical activity guidelines, exercise hard at the gym for an hour, and still increase your risk of heart disease and early death if the rest of your day is spent sitting. The technical phrase is "active couch potato": well-exercised, but sedentary the other 23 hours. The good news is that long sitting is among the easiest health behaviours to shift - the changes are small, low-effort, and require nothing you do not already do.</p>

<h2>What the evidence shows</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Higher daily sitting time is independently linked to higher mortality and cardiovascular disease</strong>, with the risk rising sharply once total sitting passes around 8 hours a day, particularly when daily moderate physical activity is low<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Doing 30 to 40 minutes of moderate activity a day largely offsets the mortality risk</strong> of sitting up to 8 hours, but does not fully erase it for very long sitting (10+ hours).</li>
  <li><strong>Breaking sitting up matters as well as total time.</strong> Standing or walking for a couple of minutes every 30 minutes appears to reduce post-meal blood glucose spikes and improves cardiometabolic markers in trial settings.</li>
  <li><strong>Type of sitting matters less than people think.</strong> Sitting at a desk, in a car, on the sofa, or at the dinner table all count. Television viewing has a slightly stronger association with adverse outcomes in observational data, possibly because it pairs with snacking and late-night arousal.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What this looks like in Aotearoa</h2>

<p>NZ adults sit an average of around 8 to 9 hours a day, with desk-based workers typically clocking more once commute, evening, and weekend sitting are added up<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>. Tamariki and rangatahi are not exempt - school sitting plus screen time after school can run close to 10 hours, which is part of the reason "sit less, move more" is now a deliberate strand of NZ children's activity guidelines.</p>

<h2>How to break up sitting at work</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>The 30-minute rule.</strong> Stand up for 1 to 2 minutes every half hour. A simple watch or phone alarm is enough; no app required.</li>
  <li><strong>Walking meetings</strong> for 1:1s, particularly for ideas or planning conversations - works in person and on a phone call.</li>
  <li><strong>Standing for phone calls</strong> by default, particularly short calls.</li>
  <li><strong>Move the printer, kettle, or rubbish bin</strong> a little further from your desk. Tiny daily friction adds up.</li>
  <li><strong>If you have a sit-stand desk</strong>, alternate every 45 to 60 minutes; standing all day is its own problem (sore lower back, varicose vein flare).</li>
  <li><strong>Take lunch away from your desk</strong>, even a short walk outside is plenty.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to break up sitting at home</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Move during ad breaks or between episodes</strong> - kettle on, dishes, a few squats, a quick walk to the letterbox.</li>
  <li><strong>Stretch on the floor</strong> rather than the sofa for half an hour in the evening - same passive viewing, very different posture and metabolic profile.</li>
  <li><strong>Do household admin standing</strong> - paying bills, sorting mail, online shopping at a kitchen bench rather than slumped on the couch.</li>
  <li><strong>Build movement into care routines</strong> - walks with tamariki, stairs at a kura pickup, gardening on weekends.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you cannot stand or walk much</h2>

<p>For people with joint pain, balance problems, or mobility limits, "sit less" still applies but the form changes. Seated movement matters: ankle pumps, calf raises in a chair, marching in place, gentle resistance band exercises, and avoiding more than an hour without a posture change. ACC funds physiotherapy assessment if pain is what is keeping you sitting; community exercise classes for older adults (Steady As You Go, council-run classes) are another good entry point.</p>

<h2>If you sit a lot for work</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Movement adds up across the week, not the day.</strong> A long sitting day balanced by an active morning is fine. Try not to stack sedentary days on top of each other.</li>
  <li><strong>Active commute where you can</strong> - even 10 minutes of walking each way changes your daily total dramatically.</li>
  <li><strong>Plan one walking break each working day</strong> as a non-negotiable. Treat it like a meeting.</li>
  <li><strong>Exercise still matters.</strong> Sitting less does not replace the activity guidelines - it complements them.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Lower back pain, hip pain, or sciatica that is not improving with movement</li>
  <li>Calf pain, leg swelling, or DVT symptoms after long-haul travel or surgery</li>
  <li>Persistent shortness of breath on stairs or moderate activity</li>
  <li>Cardiovascular risk check is overdue (age 30 for Maori, Pacific, South Asian; 45 for everyone else)</li>
  <li>Diabetes is poorly controlled and you sit most of the day - movement is part of the treatment plan</li>
</ul>
