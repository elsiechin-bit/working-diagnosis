<p>Regular movement is one of the most well-studied things you can do for your health. It lowers the risk of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, depression, dementia, falls, and a long list of cancers. The good news: most of the gain comes from getting started, not from getting fast - the biggest jump in life expectancy and disease risk happens between doing nothing and doing a moderate amount, well before anyone needs to be running marathons. Around half of NZ adults are not currently meeting the activity guidelines<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup>, so if movement has slipped, you have plenty of company.</p>

<h2>What the guidelines actually say</h2>

<p>The current NZ guidelines, aligned with WHO 2020<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup>, are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Adults (18 to 64):</strong> at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity each week, OR 75 minutes of vigorous activity, OR a mix of the two; PLUS muscle-strengthening activity on at least 2 days a week</li>
  <li><strong>Older adults (65+):</strong> the same as adults, with the addition of balance and falls-prevention exercises (such as tai chi, Steady As You Go, or Otago) on 3 or more days a week</li>
  <li><strong>Tamariki and rangatahi (5 to 17):</strong> at least 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity every day, with vigorous activity and muscle/bone-strengthening activity included on at least 3 days a week</li>
  <li><strong>Sit less, move more:</strong> break up long periods of sitting where you can - this matters at any age</li>
</ul>

<h2>Moderate or vigorous? The talk test</h2>

<p>You do not need a heart-rate monitor. <strong>Moderate</strong> activity is brisk enough that you can talk in sentences but not sing - a brisk walk, gardening, mowing the lawn, swimming gently, kapa haka, easy cycling, carrying groceries up a hill. <strong>Vigorous</strong> activity is hard enough that you can only get a few words out at a time - jogging, faster cycling, swimming laps, a tough waiata practice, touch rugby, climbing stairs at speed.</p>

<h2>What counts as strength training</h2>

<p>Strength training does not have to mean a gym. Anything that loads your muscles enough to feel hard within about 8 to 15 repetitions counts, and two short sessions a week is the dose:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bodyweight:</strong> squats, push-ups (against a wall, bench, or floor), lunges, planks</li>
  <li><strong>Garden and household:</strong> digging, carrying full buckets, lifting firewood, vacuuming hard</li>
  <li><strong>Resistance bands or simple dumbbells</strong> at home</li>
  <li><strong>Group classes</strong>: pump, pilates, harder yoga styles, council or community gym programmes (often low-cost)</li>
</ul>

<p>Strength training is the part most people skip and is the single most useful thing for older adults to add - it preserves muscle, supports bone density, and reduces falls.</p>

<h2>Fitting it in when life is full</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Start where you are.</strong> Adding 10 minutes a day of brisk walking is genuinely worthwhile. The biggest health gain comes from going from zero to something.</li>
  <li><strong>Stack movement onto existing habits.</strong> Walk while you take a phone call, get off the bus a stop early, take stairs, walk the school run.</li>
  <li><strong>Pick something you actually enjoy.</strong> The best exercise is the one you keep doing - kapa haka, social touch, tramping, gardening, dancing all count.</li>
  <li><strong>Use the weather, do not fight it.</strong> Cold, wet days are short walks, stair laps, or a YouTube workout; long, light evenings are when you stretch the longer outings.</li>
  <li><strong>If you have an injury</strong>, ACC funds physiotherapy and your GP can refer you. Modify rather than stop.</li>
</ul>

<h2>If you have a long-term condition</h2>

<p>For nearly every chronic condition - heart disease, type 2 diabetes, COPD, depression, osteoarthritis, chronic pain - the right amount of regular movement is part of the treatment, not a risk. Green Prescription, a free NZ service, can connect you with a coach who tailors a programme to your situation. Ask your GP for a referral.</p>

<h2>When to check in with your doctor first</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You have known heart disease, uncontrolled high blood pressure, or have had a recent cardiac event</li>
  <li>You get chest pain, severe breathlessness, dizziness, or fainting on exertion</li>
  <li>You have severe joint pain or a recent injury you have not had assessed</li>
  <li>You have diabetes on insulin or a sulphonylurea and want to start a new vigorous routine - exercise can drop your blood glucose</li>
  <li>You are pregnant and unsure what intensity is safe</li>
  <li>You have not been active for a long time and are over 50, or have several risk factors for heart disease</li>
</ul>
