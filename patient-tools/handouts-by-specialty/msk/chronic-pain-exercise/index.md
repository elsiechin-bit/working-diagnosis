<p>Chronic pain (pain lasting more than 3 months) often leads to reduced activity due to fear or previous flare-ups. However, movement and exercise are among the most effective treatments for reducing chronic pain and improving function. This handout explains why activity helps and practical steps to restart exercise safely.</p>

<h2>Why activity seems to hurt but actually helps</h2>

<p>Pain from overuse is different from pain from damage. In chronic pain, your nervous system has become sensitised - it perceives threat where there may be little actual danger. Staying inactive leads to deconditioning, weakness, and stiffness, which paradoxically increases pain. Gradual, consistent activity helps "retrain" your nervous system to perceive pain as less threatening and rebuilds physical capacity.</p>

<h2>The fear-avoidance trap</h2>

<p>Pain can create a cycle: pain leads to fear of activity, which leads to avoidance, which leads to deconditioning and weakness, which increases pain. Breaking this cycle requires gradually returning to activity despite discomfort. This is different from ignoring severe pain - you are progressing within a safe range.</p>

<h2>Starting exercise safely with chronic pain</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Start low, go slow.</strong> Begin with activities so gentle they seem almost insignificant (a 5-minute walk, gentle stretching). This builds confidence and proves to your nervous system that activity is safe.</li>
  <li><strong>Consistency matters more than intensity.</strong> A 10-minute daily walk is more effective than an occasional intense session. Regular activity helps your nervous system adapt.</li>
  <li><strong>Pace yourself.</strong> Aim for steady, moderate activity rather than boom-bust cycles (doing lots, then avoiding). Keep daily activity relatively consistent.</li>
  <li><strong>Choose activities you tolerate.</strong> Walking, swimming, cycling, tai chi, yoga, or dancing are often well tolerated. Pick something you might enjoy.</li>
  <li><strong>Gradually increase.</strong> After 2 - 4 weeks of consistent activity, slowly increase duration or intensity by 10 - 20%. Progress is measured in weeks, not days.</li>
  <li><strong>Include strength training.</strong> Once you can do 20 - 30 minutes of aerobic activity comfortably, add light resistance training (bodyweight, bands, or light weights) twice weekly. Strength helps reduce pain long-term.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not avoid activity due to expected pain.</strong> Some discomfort during activity is normal and expected; it does not mean you are doing damage. Pain afterwards that is worse than pain during activity suggests you did too much.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Managing flares and setbacks</h2>

<p>Flares are part of chronic pain and are not failures. If you have a flare, reduce activity temporarily but do not stop - stay gently active. Do not catastrophise the flare or use it as an excuse to go back to complete inactivity. Continue your exercise program at a reduced level until pain improves, then gradually progress again.</p>

<h2>Pain and movement expectations</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pain during activity is not necessarily harmful.</strong> In chronic pain, some discomfort during or shortly after activity is expected and does not mean damage is occurring.</li>
  <li><strong>Pain lasting hours or worsening the next day suggests you did too much.</strong> Adjust your activity level and progress more slowly.</li>
  <li><strong>Improvement is gradual.</strong> Pain reduction typically takes weeks to months. Function often improves before pain reduces.</li>
  <li><strong>Patience is essential.</strong> Resuming activity after months or years of avoidance takes time. Expect progress, not overnight recovery.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Psychological approaches that help</h2>

<p>Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and pain neuroscience education help retrain how you think about pain and reduce fear-avoidance. Mindfulness and acceptance-based approaches help tolerate discomfort while staying active. Your GP can refer you to these therapies.</p>

<h2>Medicines and pain relief</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief (paracetamol, NSAIDs) can help initially, but relying on pain relief to avoid activity is not effective long-term. Use medicines strategically - for example, taking pain relief before exercise if needed to participate, but not using them as a reason to avoid movement.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You are unsure what level of activity is safe for your condition</li>
  <li>Your pain is preventing all activity despite trying graded approaches</li>
  <li>You develop new symptoms (weakness, numbness, swelling) alongside pain</li>
  <li>You feel depressed or anxious about returning to activity</li>
  <li>You want referral to physiotherapy or pain psychology services</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is causing my chronic pain, and is there any structural damage?</li>
  <li>What level of activity is safe for me to start with?</li>
  <li>Can you refer me to physiotherapy or a pain specialist?</li>
  <li>Is cognitive behavioural therapy available?</li>
  <li>What pain relief options might help while I rebuild activity?</li>
</ul>
