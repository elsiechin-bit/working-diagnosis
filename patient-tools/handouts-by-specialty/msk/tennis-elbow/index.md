<p>Tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) and golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis) are common overuse injuries affecting the tendons on either side of your elbow. Despite their names, they are usually caused by repetitive gripping, lifting, or twisting activities rather than just sports. Most cases improve significantly with conservative treatment.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<p><strong>Tennis elbow (outside of elbow):</strong> Pain on the outer side of your elbow, often worsened by gripping, lifting, or activities involving wrist extension (like holding a coffee cup or shaking hands).</p>

<p><strong>Golfer's elbow (inside of elbow):</strong> Pain on the inner side of your elbow, often worsened by gripping, throwing, or activities involving wrist flexion (like lifting or throwing a ball).</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pain that worsens with certain arm movements</li>
  <li>Weakness in grip strength</li>
  <li>Pain that typically develops gradually over weeks</li>
  <li>Difficulty with work or leisure activities requiring gripping</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Tennis and golfer's elbow are caused by overuse of forearm muscles and tendons. Repetitive gripping, lifting, or throwing movements cause tiny tears and inflammation in the tendons where they attach to the elbow bone. Gradual onset suggests overuse, while sudden severe pain may indicate a partial tear.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest the painful activity.</strong> Avoid gripping, lifting, or movements that trigger pain for 2 - 4 weeks. This allows inflammation to settle.</li>
  <li><strong>Use ice.</strong> Apply ice packs for 15 minutes, several times daily, especially after activities that provoke pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Wear an elbow strap or brace.</strong> A brace that compresses the muscle belly (just below the elbow) reduces stress on the tendon attachment and provides significant pain relief for many people.</li>
  <li><strong>Use pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen helps manage pain. Ibuprofen may be particularly helpful in the early inflammatory phase.</li>
  <li><strong>Stretch gently.</strong> Once acute pain settles, gentle stretching of your forearm muscles helps prevent recurrence. Stretch slowly and hold for 30 seconds, several times daily.</li>
  <li><strong>Strengthen gradually.</strong> Once pain is improving, gentle strengthening exercises (like wrist curls with light weights or resistance bands) rebuild muscle strength and prevent recurrence.</li>
  <li><strong>Modify your technique.</strong> If caused by work or sport, address the movement that triggered it - adjust your grip, use better equipment, or change your technique.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief is usually sufficient. Topical anti-inflammatory creams applied to the elbow may help. Steroid injections are sometimes used but evidence suggests they provide only short-term relief; exercises and strengthening offer better long-term outcomes.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is severe or sudden</li>
  <li>Symptoms are not improving after 6 weeks despite conservative treatment</li>
  <li>You develop significant weakness in your grip or arm</li>
  <li>Pain is affecting your work or quality of life</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most cases resolve within 6 to 12 months with conservative treatment. The key is early intervention with rest, ice, and bracing, followed by gradual return to activity with proper technique and strength maintenance.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Is imaging (ultrasound) needed?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist for exercise advice?</li>
  <li>What activities should I avoid?</li>
  <li>When should I consider other treatments?</li>
</ul>
