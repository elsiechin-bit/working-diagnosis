<p>Plantar heel pain is one of the most common foot complaints and usually comes from plantar fasciitis - inflammation of the tissue running along the sole of your foot. While it can be painful and frustrating, most people recover well with conservative treatment. This handout explains what helps.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sharp pain in the heel, often worst first thing in the morning or after resting</li>
  <li>Pain that improves slightly with walking but worsens again with prolonged standing or activity</li>
  <li>Tenderness along the arch of the foot</li>
  <li>Pain that typically worsens gradually over weeks or months</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Plantar fasciitis occurs when the plantar fascia (tough tissue connecting your heel to your toes) becomes irritated and inflamed. Contributing factors include high-impact activities, being overweight, tight calf muscles, and wearing unsupportive footwear. It is not usually caused by a single injury but rather by cumulative stress on the fascia.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stretch your calf muscles.</strong> Tight calf muscles increase strain on the plantar fascia. Stretch your calves (leaning into a wall, lunges) for 30 seconds, several times daily, especially before getting out of bed in the morning.</li>
  <li><strong>Wear supportive shoes.</strong> Choose footwear with good arch support and cushioning. Avoid flat shoes, thongs, or unsupportive sneakers. Shoes with a small heel (1 - 1.5 cm) also help by reducing tension on the fascia.</li>
  <li><strong>Use a heel cup or insole.</strong> Silicone heel cups or arch support insoles (available at pharmacies) reduce stress on the plantar fascia and provide relief for many people.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply ice or massage with a frozen bottle.</strong> Rolling your foot on a frozen water bottle for 5 - 10 minutes, several times daily, provides pain relief and reduces inflammation.</li>
  <li><strong>Use pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen helps manage pain so you can stay active. Ibuprofen may be particularly helpful in the early inflammatory stage.</li>
  <li><strong>Limit high-impact activities temporarily.</strong> Reduce running and jumping while symptomatic. Walking and swimming are usually tolerated better.</li>
  <li><strong>Lose weight if overweight.</strong> Weight loss directly reduces stress on your heel.</li>
  <li><strong>Night splints help some people.</strong> A night splint holds your foot stretched during sleep, allowing the fascia to heal. Many people find this very helpful.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is severe or worsening despite self-care after 6 weeks</li>
  <li>You have swelling, redness, or heat in your heel</li>
  <li>You develop sudden severe pain</li>
  <li>Pain is limiting your ability to walk or function</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medical treatments if conservative care is not enough</h2>

<p>If the above measures are not working after 6 - 8 weeks, your GP may consider steroid injections into the heel or extracorporeal shock wave therapy. These are typically considered only after conservative treatment has been tried thoroughly.</p>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Most people recover fully within 3 to 12 months with conservative treatment. The key is early intervention with stretching, proper footwear, and activity modification. Persistent symptoms usually respond to patience and consistency with the measures above.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Should I see a podiatrist for custom insoles?</li>
  <li>Is imaging needed?</li>
  <li>When should I be concerned this is not improving?</li>
  <li>Are there other treatment options if conservative care is not working?</li>
</ul>
