<p>Knee osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common type of arthritis, affecting the cartilage that cushions your knee joint. While there is no cure, most people manage their symptoms well and stay active with the right approach. This handout explains what osteoarthritis is and what actually helps.</p>

<h2>What it is</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Gradual wear and tear of the smooth cartilage inside the knee joint</li>
  <li>Can develop at any age but becomes more common from age 50 onwards</li>
  <li>Risk factors include previous injury, being overweight, and repetitive strain</li>
  <li>Symptoms usually develop slowly over months or years</li>
</ul>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain or aching in the knee, especially with activity or at the end of the day</li>
  <li>Stiffness, particularly after resting or on waking in the morning</li>
  <li>Swelling or fluid around the knee (though this is not always present)</li>
  <li>Creaking or grinding sensation when moving the knee</li>
  <li>Feeling of instability or your knee "giving way"</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stay active.</strong> Movement is one of the most important treatments. Gentle exercise strengthens muscles around your knee, reduces pain, and preserves function. Walking, swimming, or water aerobics are excellent low-impact options. Aim for at least 150 minutes of activity per week.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage your weight.</strong> If you are overweight, losing even 5 - 10% of your body weight reduces stress on your knees and can significantly improve symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Use simple pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen taken regularly can help you stay active. Ibuprofen may be particularly helpful as it reduces inflammation, but check with your GP if you have stomach or kidney concerns.</li>
  <li><strong>Apply ice after activity.</strong> Icing for 15 - 20 minutes after activity may reduce pain and swelling, especially if your knee feels hot or puffy.</li>
  <li><strong>Wear supportive footwear.</strong> Good shoes with cushioning and arch support reduce stress on your knee joints. Avoid high heels for prolonged periods.</li>
  <li><strong>Consider a knee support.</strong> Some people find elastic knee sleeves or compression garments helpful, particularly during activity.</li>
  <li><strong>Physiotherapy strengthens your muscles.</strong> A physiotherapist can teach you specific exercises to strengthen the muscles around your knee, which is one of the most effective treatments for osteoarthritis.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines</h2>

<p>Over-the-counter pain relief (paracetamol or ibuprofen) is usually the first step. If these are not enough, your GP may discuss topical creams (applied directly to the skin) or stronger options. Steroid injections into the knee can provide temporary relief for some people. Your GP will advise whether these are appropriate for you.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Pain is not improving with self-care and simple pain relief</li>
  <li>Your knee is significantly swollen, hot, or red</li>
  <li>You have fever or feel unwell</li>
  <li>Your knee repeatedly "gives way" or feels unstable</li>
  <li>Pain is limiting your ability to do things you want to do</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Osteoarthritis usually progresses slowly, and many people manage symptoms well for years or decades. Joint replacement surgery is available if conservative treatments no longer provide adequate relief, but it is reserved for cases where significant disability persists despite all other options.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What exercises are safe for my knee?</li>
  <li>Would physiotherapy help me?</li>
  <li>Do I need imaging (X-ray or scan)?</li>
  <li>Are there other options if my symptoms worsen?</li>
</ul>
