<p>Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) occurs when the median nerve in the wrist is compressed, causing numbness and tingling in the hand. While painful and bothersome, it is very treatable with conservative measures, and surgery is reserved for severe cases.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Numbness or tingling in the thumb, index, middle, and half of the ring finger</li>
  <li>Symptoms often worse at night or early morning</li>
  <li>Pain or aching in the hand, forearm, or even upper arm</li>
  <li>Weakness in grip strength</li>
  <li>Shaking out the hand often provides temporary relief</li>
  <li>In severe cases, muscle wasting at the base of the thumb</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>The carpal tunnel is a narrow passage in the wrist through which the median nerve passes. When tissues in the tunnel swell or tighten, the nerve becomes compressed. Contributing factors include repetitive hand use, pregnancy, diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, hypothyroidism, and age. Often no single cause is identified.</p>

<h2>Conservative treatment (first-line)</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Rest the wrist.</strong> Avoid repetitive gripping or forceful hand movements. Modify activities that trigger symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Wear a wrist splint.</strong> A night splint keeps the wrist in neutral position during sleep, reducing nerve compression overnight. Many people get significant relief from splinting alone.</li>
  <li><strong>Ice and anti-inflammatories.</strong> Ice packs and ibuprofen may reduce swelling and inflammation in the carpal tunnel.</li>
  <li><strong>Modify workplace ergonomics.</strong> Keep your wrist straight while typing, use wrist support, and take regular breaks from repetitive tasks.</li>
  <li><strong>Stretch your wrist and forearm.</strong> Gentle wrist and forearm stretches improve flexibility and may reduce symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid pressure on the wrist.</strong> Use a padded wrist rest while working to reduce direct pressure on the carpal tunnel.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medical treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Steroid injections:</strong> Injecting corticosteroid into the carpal tunnel can reduce inflammation and provide relief, though effects are often temporary (weeks to months).</p>

<p><strong>Surgery:</strong> Carpal tunnel release surgery cuts the band of tissue (flexor retinaculum) that compresses the nerve, increasing space in the tunnel. Surgery is considered when conservative treatment fails, symptoms are severe, or there is evidence of nerve damage. Most people improve significantly with surgery, though complete resolution may take months.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You develop numbness or tingling in your hand</li>
  <li>Symptoms are affecting your work or sleep</li>
  <li>You have weakness in your hand or grip</li>
  <li>Conservative measures are not helping</li>
  <li>You want to discuss treatment options</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>CTS is usually diagnosed by history and examination. Your doctor may arrange nerve conduction studies (EMG/NCS) to confirm the diagnosis and assess severity, especially if surgery is being considered. X-rays are not routinely needed.</p>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<p>Mild CTS often improves with conservative measures, especially splinting and activity modification. Moderate to severe CTS may require surgery for lasting relief. After surgery, most people improve significantly, though recovery takes weeks to months.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I definitely have carpal tunnel syndrome?</li>
  <li>How severe is my nerve compression?</li>
  <li>Should I wear a splint?</li>
  <li>Do I need nerve conduction studies?</li>
  <li>When would surgery be considered?</li>
  <li>Can I modify my work to improve symptoms?</li>
</ul>
