<p>Statins are among the most-studied medicines in history and one of the most argued-about online. Around 800,000 New Zealanders take one. They reliably reduce heart attacks, strokes, and deaths in people at meaningful cardiovascular risk. They also have side effects, including some that are real, some that are exaggerated, and some that get blamed on statins despite better evidence pointing elsewhere. This handout walks through what statins actually do, who genuinely benefits, the side effect picture, and the conversation worth having with your GP.</p>

<h2>What statins actually do</h2>

<p>Statins (atorvastatin, simvastatin, rosuvastatin, pravastatin) reduce LDL cholesterol by around 30-55% depending on the statin and dose. The lower LDL slows or reverses the build-up of plaque in artery walls. Less plaque means lower risk of heart attack and stroke.</p>

<p>The benefit is largest in people who already have cardiovascular disease (after a heart attack, stroke, or with known angina or peripheral vascular disease). It is also significant in people at moderate-to-high cardiovascular risk who have not had an event yet. The benefit is smaller in people at low risk - which is why statins are not given to everyone.</p>

<h2>The numbers</h2>

<p>The most useful framing for any preventative medicine is "absolute risk reduction" - how much your actual chance of an event drops.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>After a heart attack</strong> - taking a statin for 5 years reduces your chance of a second heart attack or stroke from around 25% to 18%, and your chance of dying from any cause from around 11% to 9%[1]</li>
  <li><strong>High cardiovascular risk (5-year risk above 15%)</strong> - statins reduce events by around 25-30%; over 10 years, this is meaningful</li>
  <li><strong>Moderate risk (5-year risk 5-15%)</strong> - more modest absolute benefit; the conversation is more individual</li>
  <li><strong>Low risk</strong> - small absolute benefit; lifestyle usually preferred first</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP can calculate your 5-year cardiovascular risk score using NZ-specific equations (PREDICT). The score combines your age, sex, ethnicity, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, diabetes, and other factors.</p>

<h2>The actual side effects</h2>

<h3>Real and reasonably common</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Muscle aches</strong> - around 5-10% of people have some muscle ache that responds to dose reduction or switching statins. Genuinely painful muscles in 1-2%. Severe muscle damage (rhabdomyolysis) is very rare - around 1-2 per 10,000 person-years.[2]</li>
  <li><strong>Mild liver enzyme rise</strong> - common but rarely meaningful. Severe liver injury is very rare.</li>
  <li><strong>Slightly increased risk of new diabetes</strong> - about 1 extra case per 1000 people per year on statin. The benefit on heart attack and stroke outweighs this in most people who would be prescribed a statin.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep changes</strong> in some people, especially with simvastatin at higher doses</li>
</ul>

<h3>Things often blamed on statins where the evidence is weaker</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Memory and cognition</strong> - large trials show no consistent effect; some individuals report subjective change. The dementia association in observational studies actually goes the other way.</li>
  <li><strong>Energy levels</strong> - debated</li>
  <li><strong>"Brain fog"</strong> - reported anecdotally; not well-supported in trials</li>
</ul>

<p>The "nocebo effect" with statins is well-documented. People warned about side effects develop side effects more often than people not warned, even on placebo. This is not "all in your head" - it is a real cognitive-physical interaction. It does mean that some side effect attribution is mistaken.</p>

<h2>If you have side effects</h2>

<p>Worth a structured conversation with your GP. Options:</p>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Stop and rechallenge.</strong> Stop the statin. If the symptom resolves, restart. If symptom returns, it is more likely the statin.</li>
  <li><strong>Switch.</strong> Different statins have different side effect profiles. Many people who cannot tolerate one tolerate another.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce the dose.</strong> A lower dose works better than no statin. Even three days a week of rosuvastatin gives meaningful effect.</li>
  <li><strong>Add ezetimibe.</strong> Different mechanism, lowers LDL by another 15-20%, often well-tolerated.</li>
  <li><strong>Consider a PCSK9 inhibitor.</strong> Injectable, very effective, NZ-funded with Special Authority for high-risk patients who cannot tolerate statins or need extra reduction.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Lifestyle alongside (not instead of)</h2>

<p>Diet, exercise, weight, smoking cessation all have cardiovascular benefit. None replaces a statin in someone with established cardiovascular disease, but the combination is more powerful than either. The Heart Foundation Mediterranean-style guidance is the most-evidenced eating pattern for cardiovascular risk.</p>

<h2>Common questions worth asking your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is my 5-year cardiovascular risk?</li>
  <li>What does taking this statin actually reduce that risk to?</li>
  <li>What is my LDL on the medication, and what is the target?</li>
  <li>Should I have a baseline liver enzyme and creatine kinase test? (Yes if not done.)</li>
  <li>If side effects appear, what do we try next?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Drug interactions worth knowing</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Grapefruit juice</strong> - increases the level of some statins (especially simvastatin). Either avoid or use a different statin.</li>
  <li><strong>Some antibiotics</strong> - clarithromycin, erythromycin can interact</li>
  <li><strong>Antifungals</strong> - itraconazole, ketoconazole</li>
  <li><strong>Some HIV medicines</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Amiodarone</strong> (heart rhythm medication)</li>
</ul>

<p>Always check with your GP or pharmacist before starting a new medicine while on a statin.</p>

<h2>Specific situations</h2>

<h3>Pregnancy and breastfeeding</h3>
<p>Statins are not used in pregnancy. Stop before trying to conceive.</p>

<h3>Older adults</h3>
<p>The conversation shifts after age 75 - benefits remain in those with established disease, but in primary prevention the calculation changes. Worth discussing with your GP rather than automatically continuing or stopping.</p>

<h3>Liver disease</h3>
<p>Most statins are safe in mild to moderate liver disease, including non-alcoholic fatty liver. Severe liver disease is a contraindication.</p>

<h3>Kidney disease</h3>
<p>Statins remain effective and important. Atorvastatin and rosuvastatin doses may be adjusted in advanced kidney disease.</p>
