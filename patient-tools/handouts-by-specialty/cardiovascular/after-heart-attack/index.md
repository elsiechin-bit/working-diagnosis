A heart attack (myocardial infarction) is frightening, but most people recover well and return to a full life. The first three months matter most. The medicines you start in hospital and the changes you make at home together cut the risk of another event by roughly half.

<h2>The first few weeks</h2>

<p>You will usually be discharged within a few days, often with a stent in place. Expect to feel tired for two to four weeks. Light activity (short walks, gentle housework) is encouraged from day one. Avoid heavy lifting and strenuous exercise for about a month, or until your cardiologist or GP says otherwise.</p>

<p>Driving: in New Zealand, private drivers must not drive for two weeks after an uncomplicated heart attack with a stent, or four weeks if no stent was placed. Commercial drivers (heavy vehicles, taxi, bus) need longer and a medical clearance.</p>

<p>Sex: usually safe to resume after two to four weeks if you can climb two flights of stairs without chest pain.</p>

<p>Returning to work: most office workers return at four to six weeks, manual workers at six to twelve weeks.</p>

<h2>Cardiac rehabilitation</h2>

<p>You will be referred to a cardiac rehabilitation programme, usually starting four to six weeks after discharge. These free programmes (run through DHBs and Heart Foundation) combine supervised exercise, education, and peer support. People who attend cardiac rehab have around 25% lower risk of dying in the next year compared with those who do not.&sup1;</p>

<h2>The medicines that protect you</h2>

<p>Most people leave hospital on five medicines. Each one targets a different part of the disease.</p>

<p><strong>Aspirin (75 to 100 mg daily, lifelong):</strong> stops platelets from sticking together inside arteries.</p>

<p><strong>A second antiplatelet</strong> (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel): taken alongside aspirin for 12 months after a heart attack, then usually stopped. The combination is called dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT).</p>

<p><strong>A statin (high-dose):</strong> atorvastatin 40-80 mg or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg. The aim is LDL cholesterol below 1.4 mmol/L. See our <a href="/handouts/statins/">statins handout</a> for more.</p>

<p><strong>A beta blocker</strong> (metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol, or carvedilol): slows the heart and protects damaged muscle. Usually continued for at least 12 months, often longer.</p>

<p><strong>An ACE inhibitor or ARB</strong> (cilazapril, candesartan): protects the heart from remodelling and lowers blood pressure.</p>

<p>Do not stop any of these without speaking to your doctor. Stopping the second antiplatelet early is particularly risky and can cause a stent to clot off.</p>

<h2>Warning signs to act on</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> if you have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Chest pain or pressure lasting more than 10 to 15 minutes, especially if not relieved by GTN spray</li>
<li>Severe breathlessness at rest</li>
<li>Sudden dizziness or fainting</li>
<li>Pain spreading to arm, jaw, neck, or back</li>
</ul>

<p>See your GP within a few days for: gradually increasing breathlessness, swelling of ankles, or new palpitations.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle that matters most</h2>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> The single biggest thing you can do. Quitting after a heart attack roughly halves the risk of another. Quitline 0800 778 778 is free and very effective.</p>

<p><strong>Move your body.</strong> Aim for 150 minutes per week of moderate exercise (brisk walking counts). Start gently, build up over weeks.</p>

<p><strong>Eat a Mediterranean-style diet.</strong> More vegetables, fruit, fish, olive oil, nuts, legumes. Less red meat, processed food, and sugary drinks.</p>

<p><strong>Limit alcohol.</strong> No more than 10 standard drinks per week, with at least two alcohol-free days.</p>

<p><strong>Mind your mood.</strong> Depression after a heart attack is common (up to 1 in 3 people)&sup2; and worsens outcomes. Tell your GP if you feel persistently low or hopeless.</p>

<h2>Follow-up</h2>

<p>You will usually have:</p>

<ul>
<li>GP review at 1-2 weeks after discharge</li>
<li>Cardiology follow-up at 6-12 weeks</li>
<li>Lipid check at 4-6 weeks to confirm statin is working</li>
<li>Annual reviews thereafter</li>
</ul>
