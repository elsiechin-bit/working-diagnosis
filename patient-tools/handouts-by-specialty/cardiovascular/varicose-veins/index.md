Varicose veins are enlarged, twisted veins, most often on the legs. They are extremely common, particularly with age and after pregnancy. For most people they are a cosmetic concern, but in some they cause symptoms or skin problems that need treatment.

<h2>Why they happen</h2>

<p>Leg veins push blood back up to the heart against gravity. Tiny one-way valves in the veins keep blood moving upward. When valves weaken, blood pools in surface veins, stretching them out.</p>

<p>Risk factors:</p>

<ul>
<li>Family history (the strongest predictor)</li>
<li>Age</li>
<li>Female sex</li>
<li>Pregnancy (especially with multiple pregnancies)</li>
<li>Obesity</li>
<li>Long periods standing (teaching, hospitality, hairdressing)</li>
<li>Previous DVT</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Many varicose veins are painless. When symptoms occur, they include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Aching or heaviness in the legs, worse by the end of the day</li>
<li>Throbbing or burning</li>
<li>Mild swelling around the ankles</li>
<li>Itching over the veins</li>
<li>Restless legs</li>
<li>Cramping at night</li>
</ul>

<p>Skin changes (darkening or thickening over the lower leg, eczema, or ulcers) suggest more advanced venous disease and warrant referral.</p>

<h2>Self-care - the foundation</h2>

<p>This is what helps most people most of the time:</p>

<p><strong>Move regularly.</strong> Calf muscles pump blood upward. Walking, swimming, and cycling all help. Avoid prolonged standing or sitting still - get up at least every 30 minutes.</p>

<p><strong>Elevate the legs.</strong> When resting, prop your legs above heart level for 15-20 minutes a few times a day.</p>

<p><strong>Compression stockings.</strong> Graduated compression stockings (class 1 or 2) reduce aching and swelling. Buy them from a pharmacy or surgical supply shop. Put them on first thing in the morning before legs swell. Most people who use them daily report fewer symptoms.</p>

<p><strong>Manage weight.</strong> Even modest weight loss reduces symptoms.</p>

<p><strong>Skin care.</strong> Moisturise the lower leg. Avoid scratching - the skin over varicose veins can be fragile.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<p>Soon, if you have:</p>

<ul>
<li>An ulcer or sore over a varicose vein that is not healing</li>
<li>Skin discoloration spreading over the lower leg</li>
<li>Bleeding from a vein (apply firm pressure and elevate; seek same-day care)</li>
<li>A hard, red, tender lump along the line of a vein (suggests superficial thrombophlebitis)</li>
<li>Sudden one-sided leg swelling (DVT can mimic or co-exist with varicose veins)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Procedures</h2>

<p>For symptoms that persist despite self-care, or for skin complications, several treatments exist. In New Zealand, most are private (insurance or self-pay), as the public system funds them mainly for ulcers or severe disease. Costs vary widely.</p>

<p><strong>Endovenous ablation</strong> (laser or radiofrequency) - the most common modern treatment. A thin catheter is passed up the vein and heat seals it from the inside. Done as a day procedure under local anaesthetic.</p>

<p><strong>Foam sclerotherapy</strong> - a foam is injected into the vein, causing it to scar and close. Useful for smaller veins or as a top-up.</p>

<p><strong>Surgical stripping</strong> - the older operation, done under general anaesthetic. Less common now but still used.</p>

<p><strong>Compression therapy with skin care</strong> - the cornerstone of treating venous ulcers, often with regular district nurse visits.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Many varicose veins appear or worsen in pregnancy and improve over the months after birth. Compression stockings (specifically maternity weight) help symptoms. Avoid invasive treatment until after pregnancy.</p>

<h2>Recurrence</h2>

<p>Varicose veins can return even after successful treatment - sometimes the same veins, sometimes new ones. Continued self-care and follow-up matters.</p>
