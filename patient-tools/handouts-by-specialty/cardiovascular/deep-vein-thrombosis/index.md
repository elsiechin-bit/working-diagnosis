A deep vein thrombosis, or DVT, is a blood clot in a deep vein, usually in the leg. The main risk is that part of the clot can break off and travel to the lungs (pulmonary embolism), which is dangerous. With prompt diagnosis and blood thinners, most DVTs are treated as outpatients and people recover well.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Suspect a DVT if you have, in one leg only:</p>

<ul>
<li>Calf or thigh pain or aching</li>
<li>Swelling that does not settle overnight</li>
<li>Warmth and redness</li>
<li>A heavy or full feeling</li>
<li>Visible surface veins (less common)</li>
</ul>

<p>Most DVTs affect one leg. Pain is often worse on standing or walking. Some DVTs cause minimal symptoms.</p>

<h2>When to act urgently - call 111</h2>

<p>If a DVT travels to the lungs, it is a pulmonary embolism (PE). Symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden breathlessness</li>
<li>Sharp chest pain, often worse with deep breaths</li>
<li>Coughing up blood</li>
<li>Fast heart rate</li>
<li>Feeling faint or collapsing</li>
</ul>

<p>This is a medical emergency.</p>

<h2>Why DVTs happen</h2>

<p>Risk factors include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Recent surgery, especially hip, knee, abdominal, or pelvic</li>
<li>Long-haul travel (over 4 hours, especially over 8)</li>
<li>Pregnancy and the 6 weeks after birth</li>
<li>Combined oral contraceptive pill or HRT</li>
<li>Cancer (active or recent)</li>
<li>Reduced mobility - hospital stay, leg in plaster, long illness</li>
<li>Obesity</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Inherited clotting tendencies (factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation)</li>
<li>Previous DVT or PE</li>
</ul>

<p>Often there is no single cause, just a combination.</p>

<h2>How DVT is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Your GP or ED will use the Wells score (a checklist of features) plus a blood test (D-dimer) to decide who needs a scan. Most people with possible DVT will have an ultrasound (a leg vein scan), often the same day. Ultrasound is painless and the definitive test.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Anticoagulation (blood thinners) - usually a direct oral anticoagulant like rivaroxaban or apixaban. Most people are managed at home. Hospital admission is only needed if very unwell, very large clot, or pulmonary embolism with strain on the heart.</p>

<p><strong>Duration:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>3 months if the clot was provoked by a clear temporary cause (e.g. surgery, plaster cast)</li>
<li>At least 3-6 months, often longer or lifelong, if no clear provoking cause</li>
<li>Lifelong if active cancer or recurrent clots</li>
</ul>

<p>See our <a href="/handouts/anticoagulants/">anticoagulants handout</a> for tips on living with blood thinners.</p>

<h2>Compression stockings</h2>

<p>Graduated compression stockings can reduce post-DVT swelling. Buy them from a pharmacy or specialist supplier. They should be measured to fit. Wear during the day for at least the first few months.</p>

<h2>Recovery</h2>

<p>Most pain and swelling improves over 2-6 weeks. Walk - it is good for circulation and helps healing. Elevate the leg when resting.</p>

<p>About 1 in 4 people develop "post-thrombotic syndrome" - chronic swelling, heaviness, sometimes skin changes. Compression stockings reduce this risk.</p>

<h2>Preventing another</h2>

<p>If you have had one DVT, you have a higher risk of another. Sensible measures:</p>

<ul>
<li>Move regularly on long flights or car trips - stand, walk, stretch every 1-2 hours</li>
<li>Stay well hydrated when travelling</li>
<li>Wear flight stockings if past DVT or other risk factors</li>
<li>Tell any new doctor, surgeon, or dentist about your history</li>
<li>If you take the combined contraceptive pill, discuss with your GP - usually changed to a non-oestrogen method</li>
<li>Consider preventive blood thinners around any future surgery (your surgeon will plan this)</li>
</ul>
