Heart failure means the heart pumps less efficiently than it should. The name sounds frightening, but with modern treatment most people live well for many years. The "failure" describes how the heart performs, not that it is about to stop.

<h2>What heart failure is - and is not</h2>

<p>A healthy heart pumps blood around the body each time it beats. In heart failure the pump is weakened (the muscle squeezes less strongly) or stiffened (it does not fill properly). Either way, blood backs up - usually into the lungs (causing breathlessness) or into the legs (causing swelling).</p>

<p>Heart failure is a chronic condition, like diabetes or high blood pressure. It is not the same as a heart attack, although a heart attack is one cause.</p>

<h2>Common symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Breathlessness, especially with exertion or when lying flat</li>
<li>Waking at night needing to sit up to breathe</li>
<li>Swelling of the ankles, legs, or sometimes abdomen</li>
<li>Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance</li>
<li>Sleeping on more pillows than you used to</li>
<li>Sudden weight gain (fluid) - 1 kg or more in two days</li>
<li>Loss of appetite or feeling full quickly</li>
</ul>

<h2>What caused it?</h2>

<p>Common causes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Coronary artery disease (narrowed heart arteries) - the most common cause</li>
<li>High blood pressure (long-standing)</li>
<li>Previous heart attack</li>
<li>Heart valve problems</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation</li>
<li>Cardiomyopathy (a primary muscle problem, sometimes inherited)</li>
<li>Diabetes</li>
<li>Heavy alcohol use, certain chemotherapy drugs, viral infections</li>
</ul>

<p>Often there is more than one cause.</p>

<h2>Two main types</h2>

<p><strong>Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF):</strong> the heart muscle is weakened. The "ejection fraction" is below 40%. This is the more familiar form and has the most treatments proven to extend life.</p>

<p><strong>Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF):</strong> the heart muscle squeezes normally but is too stiff to fill well. More common in older people, women, and those with high blood pressure or diabetes. Treatments are improving.</p>

<h2>Tests to expect</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Blood test (BNP or NT-proBNP):</strong> elevated when the heart is under strain</li>
<li><strong>ECG:</strong> looks for old damage and rhythm problems</li>
<li><strong>Echocardiogram:</strong> ultrasound of the heart - the key test</li>
<li><strong>Chest X-ray:</strong> shows fluid on the lungs and heart size</li>
<li><strong>Routine bloods:</strong> kidney function, iron, thyroid, diabetes</li>
</ul>

<h2>The medicines that change outcomes</h2>

<p>Modern heart failure treatment (especially for HFrEF) uses four medicine groups - the "four pillars". Each works differently. Together they cut the risk of dying or being admitted to hospital by around 60%.&sup1;</p>

<p><strong>1. ACE inhibitor or ARB (or sacubitril/valsartan):</strong> cilazapril, candesartan, or Entresto. Lowers blood pressure and protects heart muscle.</p>

<p><strong>2. Beta blocker:</strong> bisoprolol, carvedilol, or metoprolol succinate. Slows the heart and reduces workload. Started low and titrated up.</p>

<p><strong>3. MRA (mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist):</strong> spironolactone or eplerenone. Reduces fibrosis. Needs blood tests for potassium.</p>

<p><strong>4. SGLT2 inhibitor:</strong> empagliflozin or dapagliflozin. Originally diabetes drugs, now proven to help heart failure even without diabetes. Funded in NZ for HFrEF.</p>

<p>Plus, as needed: a loop diuretic (furosemide) to remove excess fluid.</p>

<p>The aim is to reach the highest dose you tolerate. This may take several months of GP visits and dose adjustments. Be patient - the gain is in the long run.</p>

<h2>Devices and procedures</h2>

<p>Some patients are offered:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Implantable cardioverter defibrillator (ICD):</strong> a small device that shocks dangerous rhythms</li>
<li><strong>Cardiac resynchronisation therapy (CRT):</strong> a special pacemaker for hearts where the chambers do not contract together</li>
<li><strong>Coronary intervention</strong> if blockages are present</li>
<li><strong>Valve repair or replacement</strong> if valves are the underlying cause</li>
</ul>

<h2>Daily life</h2>

<p>See our <a href="/handouts/heart-failure-daily-management/">daily management handout</a> for weight monitoring, fluid and salt, exercise, and warning signs.</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>Heart failure is highly variable. Many people feel much better within months of starting medicines and stay stable for years. Others have a more progressive course. Cardiac rehabilitation, regular GP review, and prompt action when symptoms change all improve outcomes.</p>
