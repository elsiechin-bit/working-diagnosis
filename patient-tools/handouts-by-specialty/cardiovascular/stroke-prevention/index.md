<p>Stroke is a major cause of disability and death, but much of the risk is modifiable. Managing blood pressure, controlling heart rhythm problems, and maintaining healthy lifestyle habits significantly reduce your chance of stroke. This handout explains your risk factors and what helps prevent stroke.</p>

<h2>What increases your stroke risk</h2>

<ul>
  <li>High blood pressure - the single largest modifiable risk factor</li>
  <li>Atrial fibrillation (irregular heart rhythm) - increases risk sevenfold</li>
  <li>Diabetes - increases risk twofold to threefold</li>
  <li>High cholesterol - contributes to plaque formation in arteries</li>
  <li>Smoking - significantly increases risk in both active and passive smokers</li>
  <li>Heavy alcohol consumption - increases risk of both ischemic and haemorrhagic stroke</li>
  <li>Obesity and sedentary lifestyle</li>
  <li>Previous stroke or TIA (transient ischaemic attack)</li>
  <li>Age - risk increases with age, especially over 65</li>
  <li>Family history of stroke</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps - controlling modifiable risk factors</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Manage blood pressure aggressively.</strong> Target is usually below 140/90 mmHg, lower if you have diabetes or previous stroke. Most people benefit from medicines; do not rely on lifestyle alone if your BP is elevated. Checking at home helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Treat atrial fibrillation.</strong> If you have irregular heartbeat, you likely need anticoagulation (warfarin or DOAC - direct oral anticoagulant) to prevent clots forming in the heart that can travel to the brain. Never ignore atrial fibrillation.</li>
  <li><strong>Control diabetes tightly.</strong> Keep your HbA1c below 7% (or as your doctor recommends). Good diabetes control reduces stroke risk.</li>
  <li><strong>Lower cholesterol if needed.</strong> Statins reduce stroke risk, especially if you have previous stroke, diabetes, or significant plaque. Take as prescribed.</li>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking dramatically increases stroke risk. Quitting reduces risk within months. Ask your doctor for support - there are effective medicines and counselling.</li>
  <li><strong>Limit alcohol.</strong> More than 2 standard drinks daily increases risk. Aim for moderate consumption or abstinence if you have stroke risk factors.</li>
  <li><strong>Stay physically active.</strong> Aim for 30 minutes moderate activity most days. This helps blood pressure, weight, cholesterol, and diabetes control.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain healthy weight.</strong> Overweight and obesity increase stroke risk through multiple mechanisms. Weight loss helps blood pressure and diabetes control.</li>
  <li><strong>Eat a Mediterranean-style diet.</strong> Emphasis on vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, fish, and olive oil. This diet reduces stroke risk.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress and mood.</strong> Depression and chronic stress increase stroke risk. Counselling and stress-reduction techniques help.</li>
</ul>

<h2>After a TIA or previous stroke</h2>

<p>If you have had a stroke or TIA, your risk of another is high. You need antiplatelet treatment (usually aspirin or clopidogrel) or anticoagulation, aggressive blood pressure control, statin therapy, and lifestyle modifications. Do not delay starting these medicines. The first weeks and months after a TIA or stroke are critical for preventing recurrence.</p>

<h2>Recognising stroke symptoms - time is brain</h2>

<p>Stroke is a medical emergency. Call 111 immediately if you have sudden onset of facial weakness, arm weakness, speech difficulty, or loss of vision. Time from symptom onset is critical - clot-busting medicines work within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>To check your stroke risk if you have risk factors</li>
  <li>To monitor blood pressure if you have hypertension</li>
  <li>If you have been diagnosed with atrial fibrillation</li>
  <li>If you have had a TIA or stroke</li>
  <li>If you need help stopping smoking</li>
  <li>To discuss cholesterol levels and statin therapy</li>
  <li>If you develop warning symptoms (transient weakness, vision loss, speech difficulty)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is my personal stroke risk?</li>
  <li>Should I be on blood pressure medication, and what is my target?</li>
  <li>Do I need a statin for cholesterol?</li>
  <li>If I have atrial fibrillation, do I need anticoagulation?</li>
  <li>What lifestyle changes will most reduce my risk?</li>
  <li>Should I monitor my blood pressure at home?</li>
  <li>What symptoms should I watch for that need emergency care?</li>
</ul>
