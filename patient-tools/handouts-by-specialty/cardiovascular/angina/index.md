Angina is chest pain caused by narrowed heart arteries. It is a warning that part of your heart muscle is not getting enough oxygen, usually during exertion. With good treatment, most people with angina lead full lives.

<h2>What angina feels like</h2>

<p>Classic angina is:</p>

<ul>
<li>A heaviness, tightness, or pressure in the centre of the chest</li>
<li>Sometimes spreading to the jaw, neck, shoulder, or left arm</li>
<li>Brought on by exertion, cold air, heavy meals, or strong emotion</li>
<li>Relieved within a few minutes by rest or by glyceryl trinitrate (GTN) spray</li>
</ul>

<p>Less typical patterns are common in women, older people, and those with diabetes - breathlessness, fatigue, indigestion, or jaw pain alone.</p>

<h2>Stable vs unstable angina</h2>

<p><strong>Stable angina</strong> happens predictably with effort and settles with rest. The pattern is consistent over weeks or months.</p>

<p><strong>Unstable angina</strong> is new pain, pain at rest, or pain that is suddenly more frequent, severe, or easily provoked. This is a medical emergency - call <strong>111</strong>.</p>

<h2>Using your GTN spray</h2>

<p>GTN (glyceryl trinitrate) is a fast-acting medicine that relaxes blood vessels. Carry it everywhere.</p>

<p>If you get angina pain:</p>

<ol>
<li>Stop and sit down (do not stand - it can cause dizziness)</li>
<li>Spray once under the tongue</li>
<li>Wait 5 minutes</li>
<li>If pain is still present, spray again and call <strong>111</strong></li>
<li>If pain is still present after another 5 minutes, take a third spray while waiting for the ambulance</li>
</ol>

<p>You can also use GTN <em>before</em> activities you know will trigger pain (e.g. before climbing a steep hill).</p>

<p>Side effects: headache (very common, eases with use), flushing, light-headedness. Sit down before spraying.</p>

<h2>The medicines that prevent angina</h2>

<p><strong>Aspirin 100 mg daily</strong> - prevents clots forming on plaques.</p>

<p><strong>Statin (high-dose)</strong> - lowers cholesterol and stabilises plaques. See our <a href="/handouts/statins/">statins handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Beta blocker</strong> (metoprolol, bisoprolol, atenolol) - slows the heart so it needs less oxygen. First-line angina prevention.</p>

<p><strong>Calcium channel blocker</strong> (amlodipine, diltiazem) - relaxes arteries. Used if beta blocker not tolerated, or added on.</p>

<p><strong>Long-acting nitrate</strong> (isosorbide mononitrate) - second-line, taken once daily. You need an 8-hour nitrate-free window each day or it stops working.</p>

<p><strong>ACE inhibitor</strong> - often added if you also have high blood pressure or diabetes.</p>

<h2>When you might need a procedure</h2>

<p>If pain remains poorly controlled despite medicines, or if a stress test or angiogram shows severe narrowing, your cardiologist may recommend:</p>

<p><strong>Angioplasty with stent</strong> - a thin tube is passed up an artery to open the narrowed segment and leave a small mesh stent in place.</p>

<p><strong>Coronary artery bypass graft (CABG)</strong> - open-heart surgery using arteries or veins from elsewhere in the body to bypass blocked sections. Used when several arteries are involved.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Single most important change.</p>

<p><strong>Exercise within your limits.</strong> Regular aerobic activity (walking, swimming, cycling) improves angina threshold over time. Stop and rest if pain comes on.</p>

<p><strong>Eat well.</strong> Mediterranean-style diet - vegetables, fruit, fish, olive oil, nuts.</p>

<p><strong>Manage stress.</strong> Strong emotion can trigger angina. Mindfulness, counselling, or simply slowing down may help.</p>

<p><strong>Avoid known triggers.</strong> Big meals followed by exertion, cold-wind walks, sudden heavy lifting.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - call 111</h2>

<ul>
<li>Chest pain that does not settle after 2-3 GTN sprays</li>
<li>Pain at rest, or waking you from sleep</li>
<li>Pain becoming more frequent or severe than usual</li>
<li>Pain with breathlessness, sweating, or feeling very unwell</li>
</ul>
