Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is a long-term condition where the airways and air sacs of the lungs are damaged, making it harder to breathe. In NZ around 1 in 7 adults over 40 has COPD, often undiagnosed.&sup1; The biggest cause is smoking, but not the only one. The damage cannot be reversed, but treatment can substantially slow progression and improve daily life.

<h2>What COPD is</h2>

<p>COPD covers two overlapping problems:</p>

<p><strong>Chronic bronchitis</strong> - inflamed, mucus-producing airways. The classic feature is cough with phlegm on most days for months at a time.</p>

<p><strong>Emphysema</strong> - the small air sacs (alveoli) lose their elasticity and merge into larger, less efficient spaces. Result: trapped air, breathlessness, reduced oxygen uptake.</p>

<p>Most people have features of both.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Breathlessness, at first only with exertion, later with everyday activities</li>
<li>Long-standing cough, often producing phlegm</li>
<li>Frequent winter chest infections</li>
<li>Wheeze</li>
<li>Tight chest</li>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Weight loss in advanced disease</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms develop gradually over years. Many people put early breathlessness down to ageing or being out of shape.</p>

<h2>Causes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Smoking</strong> - the cause in around 80-90% of NZ cases</li>
<li><strong>Long-term workplace exposure</strong> to dust, fumes, smoke (farming, construction, welding, baking)</li>
<li><strong>Indoor air pollution</strong> - wood burners, poorly ventilated homes</li>
<li><strong>Severe childhood respiratory infections</strong></li>
<li><strong>Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency</strong> - a rare inherited condition that causes emphysema in non-smokers and young adults</li>
</ul>

<h2>How COPD is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Spirometry is the key test. You blow as hard and as long as you can into a machine. COPD is diagnosed when the ratio FEV1/FVC is below 0.7 after a bronchodilator. Stage is set by FEV1 (compared to predicted):</p>

<ul>
<li>Mild (Stage 1): FEV1 above 80%</li>
<li>Moderate (Stage 2): FEV1 50-79%</li>
<li>Severe (Stage 3): FEV1 30-49%</li>
<li>Very severe (Stage 4): FEV1 below 30%</li>
</ul>

<p>The newer GOLD ABE classification combines symptoms and exacerbation history to guide treatment. Your GP works to that.</p>

<h2>Other tests</h2>

<ul>
<li>Chest X-ray</li>
<li>Pulse oximetry (oxygen levels)</li>
<li>Full blood count, sputum if needed</li>
<li>CT scan in some cases</li>
<li>Alpha-1 antitrypsin level if onset under age 45 or non-smoker</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> The single most important step. Quitting at any stage slows decline. Quitline 0800 778 778 is free.</p>

<p><strong>Inhalers.</strong> Common combinations:</p>

<ul>
<li>Long-acting bronchodilators - LAMA (tiotropium), LABA (salmeterol, formoterol)</li>
<li>Combined LAMA-LABA (Anoro, Spiolto, Ultibro) - first-line for most</li>
<li>Inhaled corticosteroid added (Trelegy, Trimbow, Breo) for those with frequent flares or asthma overlap</li>
<li>Reliever (salbutamol or short-acting LAMA) for symptom flares</li>
</ul>

<p>Use a spacer with metered-dose inhalers. See our <a href="/handouts/asthma-inhaler-spacer/">inhaler technique handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Pulmonary rehabilitation.</strong> A structured 6-12 week programme of supervised exercise and education. The single most under-used intervention in COPD - it improves walking distance, breathlessness, and quality of life as much or more than medications.&sup2; Free in most DHB regions. Ask your GP for a referral.</p>

<p><strong>Vaccinations.</strong> Annual flu, COVID-19 boosters as recommended, pneumococcal vaccine, and Shingrix if eligible.</p>

<p><strong>Mucolytics</strong> (acetylcysteine) for some people with thick sticky sputum.</p>

<p><strong>Long-term oxygen therapy</strong> for people with severely low oxygen at rest. Improves survival in this group.</p>

<p><strong>Surgical options</strong> (lung volume reduction, transplant) - rare, for selected patients.</p>

<h2>Living with COPD</h2>

<ul>
<li>Move daily. Even gentle walks help</li>
<li>Pace activities; sit on a stool to do tasks like showering</li>
<li>Eat well; weight loss can be a problem in advanced disease</li>
<li>Manage anxiety - breathlessness drives anxiety, anxiety drives breathlessness. CBT and relaxation techniques help</li>
<li>Plan for breathlessness - have inhalers in pockets, know your action plan</li>
<li>Get the flu vaccine each year before winter</li>
<li>Keep a stash of standby antibiotics and prednisone if your GP has prescribed them</li>
</ul>

<h2>Exacerbations (flare-ups)</h2>

<p>Most COPD flares are triggered by viral or bacterial chest infections. Signs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Increased breathlessness</li>
<li>More cough or phlegm</li>
<li>Phlegm changing colour (yellow, green, brown)</li>
<li>Tight chest, wheeze</li>
<li>Tiredness</li>
</ul>

<p>Mild flares often respond to increased inhaler use. Moderate flares need oral prednisone (30 mg daily for 5 days) and sometimes antibiotics. Severe flares need hospital. See our <a href="/handouts/copd-management/">COPD management handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Warning signs - same-day or 111</h2>

<p>Call 111 for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe breathlessness, unable to talk in sentences</li>
<li>Lips, fingertips blue</li>
<li>Drowsy or confused</li>
<li>Coughing up large amounts of blood</li>
<li>Severe chest pain</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>COPD typically progresses slowly. People who quit smoking, stay active, and follow their treatment plan often remain stable for many years. Average life expectancy is reduced but varies hugely - many people live 15-20 years after diagnosis with good care.</p>
