<p>Bronchiolitis is the most common reason a baby is hospitalised in NZ winter. It is a viral infection of the small airways in the lungs, usually caused by respiratory syncytial virus (RSV). It mostly affects babies under 12 months, and the under-6-month group are most likely to need hospital support. Most babies get through it at home with attentive feeding and careful watching, but recognising the small minority who need oxygen or feeding support is what this handout is about.</p>

<h2>What is happening</h2>

<p>The virus inflames and clogs the smallest airways in the lungs (bronchioles). This makes air harder to move in and out. Breathing becomes faster, more laboured, and noisier. Feeding becomes harder because the baby cannot suck, swallow, and breathe at the same time as easily. The work of breathing increases the calorie need at the same time as the intake drops.</p>

<h2>What to expect over the illness</h2>

<p>The classic course is two weeks. Worst around days 3-5.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Days 1-2:</strong> like a cold - runny nose, mild cough, low-grade fever</li>
  <li><strong>Days 3-5:</strong> peak severity - cough worse, wheezy or crackly noise from chest, fast breathing, working hard, feeding difficult</li>
  <li><strong>Days 5-10:</strong> gradual improvement - cough still there but less work</li>
  <li><strong>Days 10-14:</strong> mostly resolved, occasional cough</li>
</ul>

<p>The cough can persist for 3-4 weeks. The wheezy noise usually settles by day 7-10.</p>

<h2>Watch and feed</h2>

<p>The two parental skills that matter most:</p>

<h3>Feeding</h3>
<p>The single most important thing is keeping fluids in. A baby who is feeding around 50-75% of usual is usually doing OK. A baby who has stopped feeding, or who can only manage a few sucks before pulling off breathless, is one to take to a doctor today.</p>

<ul>
  <li>Smaller, more frequent feeds work better than usual-volume feeds</li>
  <li>Saline drops in the nose 5-10 minutes before each feed clear the airway</li>
  <li>Feeds may need to be paced - pause to let them catch breath</li>
  <li>Wet nappies are the marker - 4-6 wet nappies per 24 hours is roughly OK</li>
</ul>

<h3>Watching the breathing</h3>

<p>Count breaths over a full minute when the baby is settled and not crying. Normal breathing rates:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Newborn-3 months: 30-60 breaths per minute</li>
  <li>3-12 months: 25-40 breaths per minute</li>
  <li>1-3 years: 20-30 breaths per minute</li>
</ul>

<p>Look at how hard they are working as much as the rate:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Mild:</strong> slightly fast breathing, mild ribs visible, alert, feeding OK</li>
  <li><strong>Moderate:</strong> noticeably fast breathing, ribs and chest sucking in (recession), nostril flaring, head bobbing, feeding less than usual</li>
  <li><strong>Severe:</strong> very fast or very slow, severe recession, grunting, pale or blue, exhausted, not feeding</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP today</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Working harder to breathe than yesterday</li>
  <li>Feeding less than half usual</li>
  <li>Fewer than 4 wet nappies in 24 hours</li>
  <li>Fever in a baby under 3 months</li>
  <li>Increasingly tired, sleepier than usual</li>
  <li>Pre-existing condition (heart, lung, prematurity, immune problems)</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to call 111 or go to ED</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Working very hard to breathe - severe recession, grunting, head bobbing</li>
  <li>Pale, mottled, blue around lips, blue extremities</li>
  <li>Pauses in breathing (apnoea) - common in young infants with bronchiolitis, even more so in those born prematurely</li>
  <li>Floppy, drowsy, hard to wake</li>
  <li>Not feeding at all, or vomiting all feeds</li>
  <li>You feel they are deteriorating - parent intuition is reliable here</li>
</ul>

<p>Babies under 3 months with any signs of bronchiolitis warrant a same-day check. Premature babies, babies with congenital heart disease, and babies with chronic lung conditions are at higher risk and need lower threshold for review.</p>

<h2>What treatment looks like</h2>

<p>For most babies, treatment is supportive - fluids, careful watching, paracetamol for discomfort if needed. Antibiotics, salbutamol inhalers, and oral steroids are NOT helpful in routine bronchiolitis.[1] Several trials have looked. The evidence shows they do not change the course.</p>

<p>If hospital admission is needed, the main supports are:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Oxygen via nasal prongs</strong> if the baby's oxygen levels are low</li>
  <li><strong>Tube feeding via a nasogastric tube</strong> if the baby is too breathless to feed safely</li>
  <li><strong>High-flow nasal oxygen</strong> for moderate cases - delivered via heated humidified prongs</li>
  <li><strong>Intensive care</strong> with non-invasive or invasive ventilation in severe cases - rare</li>
</ul>

<p>Most admissions are 2-4 days. Worst breathing usually peaks on hospital day 1-2, then improvement.</p>

<h2>The 2024 change in NZ - RSV monoclonal antibody</h2>

<p>From 2024, NZ funds nirsevimab (an RSV monoclonal antibody) for high-risk infants under 12 months going into their first RSV season - particularly premature babies, babies with chronic lung disease, congenital heart disease, or other vulnerabilities. It is a single intramuscular injection that provides passive protection through the season. Funding criteria are evolving; ask your GP, paediatrician, or Plunket nurse if your baby is eligible. A maternal RSV vaccine is also available for pregnant women in late pregnancy in some settings.</p>

<h2>What about palivizumab?</h2>

<p>Palivizumab (Synagis) is the older RSV monoclonal antibody, requiring monthly injections through the season. Funded for very high-risk infants in NZ - extreme prematurity, complex heart disease. Largely being replaced by nirsevimab now.</p>

<h2>Things to know</h2>

<h3>Bronchiolitis vs asthma</h3>
<p>Children under 12 months wheezing with a cold almost always have bronchiolitis, not asthma. Asthma is rarely diagnosed under 2 (some preschool wheeze is asthma but the diagnosis is harder). The presence of family history of atopy (asthma, eczema, hay fever) and the child's own atopic features matter for assessing future risk.</p>

<h3>Will my baby get asthma?</h3>
<p>Around 30-40% of children who have had bronchiolitis go on to wheeze again with future colds in the toddler years. Most "grow out of it" by school age. A subset develop asthma. The link is debated - bronchiolitis may flag a child predisposed to wheezy disease, rather than causing asthma.</p>

<h3>Can I prevent it?</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Handwashing, especially older siblings handling the baby</li>
  <li>Smoke-free home (including no smoking in cars)</li>
  <li>Breastfeeding reduces severity</li>
  <li>RSV antibody for eligible infants (see above)</li>
  <li>Limit large gatherings of children during peak RSV season for very young infants</li>
</ul>
