Long COVID describes symptoms that continue for at least three months after a COVID-19 infection and are not explained by another diagnosis. Around 5-10% of people who catch COVID-19 develop persistent symptoms.&sup1; The science is moving fast, and there is no single cure, but a structured approach to pacing, sleep, autonomic symptoms, and gradual rebuilding helps most people improve over time.

<h2>What it can look like</h2>

<p>Long COVID is not one condition. Common symptom clusters include:</p>

<p><strong>Fatigue and post-exertional malaise.</strong> Tiredness that is far out of proportion to activity. Crashing for days after relatively minor exertion. The single most common pattern.</p>

<p><strong>Brain fog.</strong> Difficulty concentrating, finding words, multitasking, remembering things. Often comes and goes.</p>

<p><strong>Breathlessness.</strong> Out of breath with stairs, walking, or sometimes at rest.</p>

<p><strong>Heart racing on standing (POTS-like).</strong> Pounding heart, light-headedness, dizziness when going from lying or sitting to standing.</p>

<p><strong>Headaches.</strong> Often new pattern, sometimes daily.</p>

<p><strong>Sleep disturbance.</strong> Trouble falling or staying asleep; unrefreshing sleep.</p>

<p><strong>Loss of smell or taste.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Muscle and joint aches.</strong></p>

<p><strong>Mood symptoms - anxiety, low mood</strong> - often a mix of biological effects and the psychological burden of being unwell.</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<p>Most people improve over 6-12 months, sometimes longer. Recovery is often non-linear - good weeks followed by setbacks. Recognising and accepting this pattern helps.</p>

<h2>Pacing - the cornerstone</h2>

<p>The single biggest mistake in long COVID recovery is pushing too hard on a good day, then crashing for days. The strategy is called <strong>pacing</strong>:</p>

<ol>
<li>Identify your current sustainable activity level - what you can do day after day without crashing</li>
<li>Stay below this level, even when you feel better</li>
<li>Build up <strong>gradually</strong> - 5-10% a week if stable</li>
<li>Plan rest <em>before</em> you need it - schedule short rests through the day</li>
<li>Track activity and symptoms (a simple diary or app) so patterns become visible</li>
</ol>

<p>The "envelope of activity" expands slowly as recovery progresses. Pushing through the envelope sets you back; staying within it lets it grow.</p>

<h2>Post-exertional malaise (PEM)</h2>

<p>If your symptoms get worse 12-72 hours after activity (mental, physical, or emotional), this is PEM. It is a crucial symptom because it changes the rehab approach.</p>

<p>If you have PEM:</p>

<ul>
<li>Avoid traditional graded exercise programmes that push through symptoms</li>
<li>Use heart-rate-based pacing - keep your HR below a threshold (often 50-60% of max for moderate cases)</li>
<li>Build up activity at half the speed you would expect</li>
<li>Heat, cold, alcohol, and illness can all trigger PEM - factor these in</li>
</ul>

<h2>Sleep</h2>

<p>Sleep is regenerative. Many people with long COVID sleep poorly. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Consistent bedtime and wake-up time, even at weekends</li>
<li>Avoid daytime naps over 30 minutes (or after 3 pm)</li>
<li>Quiet, cool, dark bedroom; phone out of the room</li>
<li>Cut caffeine after midday and alcohol within 3 hours of sleep</li>
<li>If insomnia is severe, see your GP - cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the most effective long-term treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>POTS-like symptoms</h2>

<p>If you feel light-headed or your heart races when standing, you may have postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) or related autonomic dysfunction.</p>

<p>What helps:</p>

<ul>
<li>Increase salt and fluid - 2.5-3 L per day water plus more salt with meals (unless you have high blood pressure or heart failure)</li>
<li>Compression stockings (knee-high or thigh-high)</li>
<li>Stand up slowly; sit down briefly first</li>
<li>Recumbent exercise (rowing machine, recumbent bike) is better tolerated than upright initially</li>
<li>Some people benefit from medications (ivabradine, propranolol, fludrocortisone) - discuss with your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>Brain fog</h2>

<ul>
<li>Cognitive pacing - mental effort uses energy too. Plan demanding tasks for the time of day you feel sharpest, and break them into chunks</li>
<li>Reduce multitasking</li>
<li>Use lists, calendars, and reminders</li>
<li>Speech and language therapy or occupational therapy can help in significant cases</li>
<li>Treat sleep, mood, and pacing first - all of these worsen cognition</li>
</ul>

<h2>Breathlessness</h2>

<ul>
<li>Breathing exercises - slow nose-in, mouth-out breathing; 4-7-8 technique</li>
<li>Pursed-lip breathing during exertion</li>
<li>Diaphragmatic breathing (some people develop a shallow upper-chest pattern after COVID and benefit from retraining)</li>
<li>If breathlessness is severe or new, see your GP - other causes (PE, anaemia, lung damage, heart problems) need to be excluded first</li>
</ul>

<h2>Mental health</h2>

<p>Anxiety and low mood are extremely common in long COVID, both as a direct effect of the illness and as a response to chronic disability. Tell your GP. Talking therapy (CBT, ACT), medications when appropriate, and peer support can all help. The goal is not "thinking your way out" of long COVID but rather supporting wellbeing while the body heals.</p>

<h2>What does NOT help (or is unproven)</h2>

<p>Be wary of expensive private treatments claiming to "cure" long COVID. Several have been promoted - intravenous infusions, hyperbaric oxygen at unregulated centres, supplement stacks - with little evidence. If a clinic charges thousands of dollars and promises a quick fix, it is unlikely to deliver. Discuss any major treatment with your GP first.</p>

<h2>What to ask your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>Have we ruled out other causes? (anaemia, thyroid, vitamin D, B12, sleep apnoea, depression)</li>
<li>Do I need cardiac or respiratory tests?</li>
<li>Can I be referred to a long COVID clinic or rehabilitation programme? (Available in some DHB areas)</li>
<li>Is there a physio or OT who understands long COVID locally?</li>
<li>What support is available for return to work?</li>
</ul>

<h2>Returning to work</h2>

<p>Phased return is almost always better than full return. Think:</p>

<ul>
<li>Reduced hours initially - 2-4 hours a day, building up</li>
<li>Working from home when possible</li>
<li>Cognitive pacing built into the schedule</li>
<li>Open conversation with employer; many will accommodate when asked</li>
<li>Income protection / ACC - long COVID is sometimes covered if work-related; discuss with your GP</li>
</ul>

<h2>Future infections</h2>

<p>Each COVID infection carries some risk of long COVID. Up-to-date vaccination, well-fitting masks in higher-risk situations, and good ventilation reduce the risk. People with previous long COVID are likely at higher risk of recurrence with reinfection.</p>
