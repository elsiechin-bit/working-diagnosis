COVID-19 remains a common illness in New Zealand. Most people now have a milder course thanks to immunity from vaccination and prior infection, but it can still be serious for some groups. The official advice changes from time to time - check Health NZ for the latest. This handout covers the essentials of home care and when to seek help.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Fever or chills</li>
<li>New or worsening cough</li>
<li>Sore throat</li>
<li>Runny or blocked nose</li>
<li>Muscle aches</li>
<li>Headache</li>
<li>Fatigue</li>
<li>Loss or change of taste or smell (less common with newer variants)</li>
<li>Nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea</li>
<li>Shortness of breath</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms can be similar to flu or other respiratory viruses - testing tells the difference.</p>

<h2>Testing</h2>

<p>Rapid antigen tests (RATs) are widely available, free for many groups in NZ, and reliable when symptoms are present. PCR tests are sometimes used in vulnerable patients or those needing antiviral treatment - your GP will arrange this.</p>

<p>If you have symptoms but the first RAT is negative, repeat after 24-48 hours - they can take 1-2 days from symptom onset to turn positive.</p>

<h2>Isolation advice</h2>

<p>The current Health NZ recommendation is to stay home for at least 5 days from the day symptoms started or your test was positive (whichever was earlier), and until 24 hours after symptoms have improved.</p>

<p>Even after isolation ends:</p>

<ul>
<li>Wear a mask in indoor public spaces for several more days</li>
<li>Avoid visiting older relatives or immunosuppressed contacts for 10 days</li>
<li>Avoid visiting hospitals and aged care for 10 days</li>
</ul>

<p>If your job involves close contact with vulnerable people (healthcare, aged care), follow your workplace policy - often longer absence is required.</p>

<h2>Telling close contacts</h2>

<p>Let people you have spent time with know if you test positive, especially:</p>

<ul>
<li>Older relatives</li>
<li>People with chronic conditions or weakened immune systems</li>
<li>Pregnant contacts</li>
</ul>

<p>They may be eligible for early antiviral treatment if they catch it.</p>

<h2>Home care</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Rest.</strong> Sleep as much as you need</li>
<li><strong>Fluids.</strong> Plenty of water, soup, juice</li>
<li><strong>Paracetamol or ibuprofen</strong> for fever, headache, body aches</li>
<li><strong>Steam or saline rinses</strong> for blocked nose</li>
<li><strong>Honey</strong> (over age 1) for cough</li>
<li><strong>Salt-water gargles</strong> for sore throat</li>
<li><strong>Stop or reduce smoking</strong></li>
<li><strong>Stay isolated</strong> from household members where possible - own bedroom and bathroom if you can, mask if shared spaces</li>
</ul>

<h2>Antiviral treatment</h2>

<p>Antiviral medicines (Paxlovid - nirmatrelvir/ritonavir, or molnupiravir) reduce hospital admission and severe illness in people at higher risk. They must be started within 5 days of symptom onset. They are funded for:</p>

<ul>
<li>People aged 65+ (60+ for Maori and Pacific)</li>
<li>People with chronic conditions (diabetes, severe asthma/COPD, kidney disease, heart disease, immunosuppression)</li>
<li>Pregnant women in some cases</li>
<li>Severely immunocompromised at any age</li>
</ul>

<p>If you may qualify, contact your GP, pharmacist, or Healthline 0800 358 5453 as soon as you test positive.</p>

<p>Paxlovid interacts with many common medicines (statins, some blood pressure drugs, blood thinners, anti-rejection drugs). Your prescriber will check.</p>

<h2>When to seek help</h2>

<p>Call your GP or Healthline 0800 611 116 same-day for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Increasing breathlessness</li>
<li>Worsening symptoms despite 5-7 days</li>
<li>Difficulty keeping fluids down</li>
<li>Reduced urine output</li>
<li>Worsening cough or chest pain</li>
<li>You are at high risk and want antiviral assessment</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe breathlessness, unable to speak in sentences</li>
<li>Lips or fingertips blue</li>
<li>Confusion or drowsiness</li>
<li>Chest pain</li>
<li>Collapsing or fainting</li>
<li>Severe headache or stiff neck</li>
</ul>

<h2>Looking after yourself in recovery</h2>

<p>Most people feel reasonably well within 1-2 weeks. Cough and tiredness can persist for several weeks longer. Tips:</p>

<ul>
<li>Build up activity gradually - 10-20% a week</li>
<li>Listen to your body; rest if needed</li>
<li>Sleep well</li>
<li>Eat normally</li>
<li>Avoid pushing back to full work or sport too quickly - some research suggests rapid return increases risk of long COVID and recurrence</li>
</ul>

<p>If symptoms persist beyond 12 weeks, see your GP - this may be long COVID. See our <a href="/handouts/long-covid/">long COVID handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Children</h2>

<p>Most children recover well from COVID-19. Watch for:</p>

<ul>
<li>High fever lasting more than a few days</li>
<li>Fast or laboured breathing</li>
<li>Reduced fluid intake or wet nappies</li>
<li>Drowsiness, persistent vomiting</li>
<li>Rash, swollen hands/feet, red eyes (rare condition called PIMS-TS - urgent review)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Vaccination</h2>

<p>Up-to-date vaccination remains the most effective protection from severe disease, especially for older people and the immunocompromised. Boosters are recommended every 6-12 months for eligible groups. Free in NZ. See <a href="https://www.health.govt.nz">health.govt.nz</a> or call 0800 28 29 26 for current recommendations.</p>
