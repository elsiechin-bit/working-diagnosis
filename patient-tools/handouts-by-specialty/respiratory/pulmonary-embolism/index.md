A pulmonary embolism (PE) happens when a blood clot, usually from a deep vein in the leg, travels to the lungs and blocks an artery. It is a serious condition - if untreated, large PEs can be fatal. With prompt diagnosis and blood thinners, most people recover well.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Classic features:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden breathlessness, even at rest</li>
<li>Sharp chest pain, often worse on deep breath</li>
<li>Coughing up blood (haemoptysis)</li>
<li>Fast heart rate or palpitations</li>
<li>Light-headedness or fainting</li>
<li>Anxiety or feeling of doom</li>
</ul>

<p>Smaller PEs may cause only mild breathlessness on exertion or a vague chest tightness. Bigger PEs cause sudden severe symptoms and circulatory collapse.</p>

<p>Often there are also signs of a leg clot (DVT):</p>

<ul>
<li>One-sided leg swelling</li>
<li>Calf pain or aching</li>
<li>Warmth and redness in the leg</li>
</ul>

<p>See our <a href="/handouts/deep-vein-thrombosis/">DVT handout</a>.</p>

<h2>What to do - urgent</h2>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> immediately for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sudden severe breathlessness</li>
<li>Chest pain with breathlessness</li>
<li>Coughing up blood</li>
<li>Fainting or near-fainting</li>
<li>Lips or fingertips turning blue</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are at risk of clots and notice gradual worsening breathlessness or a one-sided swollen leg, see your GP same-day.</p>

<h2>Risk factors</h2>

<p>Most PEs happen in people with one or more risk factors:</p>

<ul>
<li>Recent surgery, especially hip, knee, or major abdominal surgery</li>
<li>Long-haul flights or car journeys (over 4-8 hours)</li>
<li>Pregnancy and the 6 weeks after birth</li>
<li>Combined oral contraceptive pill or hormone replacement therapy</li>
<li>Cancer (active or recent)</li>
<li>Reduced mobility - hospital stay, leg in plaster, severe illness</li>
<li>Obesity</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Inherited clotting disorders (factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation)</li>
<li>Previous DVT or PE</li>
<li>Older age</li>
</ul>

<p>Sometimes PE happens without a clear risk factor.</p>

<h2>How PE is diagnosed</h2>

<p>If your doctor suspects PE, they will usually arrange:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>D-dimer blood test</strong> - if low and clinical risk is low, PE is excluded without further imaging</li>
<li><strong>CT pulmonary angiogram (CTPA)</strong> - the definitive scan</li>
<li><strong>VQ scan</strong> - alternative if iodine contrast is contraindicated, or in pregnancy</li>
<li><strong>Leg ultrasound</strong> if DVT signs present</li>
</ul>

<p>An ECG and chest X-ray are usually done as well, mostly to rule out other causes.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Anticoagulation (blood thinners).</strong> The mainstay of treatment. Most people in NZ now start a direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC) - apixaban or rivaroxaban - which can be started at home. Dabigatran needs an initial 5 days of injectable anticoagulant first.</p>

<p>Length of treatment:</p>

<ul>
<li>3 months for PE provoked by a clear temporary cause (surgery, plaster cast)</li>
<li>At least 3-6 months, often longer or lifelong, for unprovoked PE</li>
<li>Long-term for active cancer, recurrent PE, or hereditary clotting disorders</li>
</ul>

<p>See our <a href="/handouts/anticoagulants/">anticoagulants handout</a> for tips on living with blood thinners.</p>

<p><strong>Hospital admission</strong> is reserved for severe PE - those with low blood pressure, low oxygen, or signs of strain on the heart. They may need clot-dissolving treatment (thrombolysis) or surgical clot removal.</p>

<p><strong>IVC filter</strong> - rarely placed, usually only when anticoagulation is not possible.</p>

<h2>Recovery</h2>

<ul>
<li>Most people feel better within days of starting blood thinners</li>
<li>Breathlessness on exertion can take weeks to months to fully resolve</li>
<li>About 1 in 3 people develop ongoing breathlessness, called post-PE syndrome - usually mild</li>
<li>1-4% develop chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) - rare but treatable specialist condition</li>
<li>Anxiety after PE is common - tell your GP if it persists</li>
</ul>

<p>Activity:</p>

<ul>
<li>Walk daily as soon as you can - improves circulation</li>
<li>Build up exercise gradually over weeks</li>
<li>Most people return to normal exercise within 4-6 weeks</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing another</h2>

<p>If you have had a PE, your future risk is higher. Important measures:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tell every clinician (GP, surgeon, dentist) about your history</li>
<li>If on the combined contraceptive pill, switch to a non-oestrogen method</li>
<li>Move regularly on long flights or trips - stand or walk every 1-2 hours, do calf exercises in seat</li>
<li>Stay well hydrated when travelling</li>
<li>Wear flight stockings if your GP recommends</li>
<li>Lose weight if overweight</li>
<li>Stop smoking</li>
<li>Discuss prophylactic anticoagulation with your surgeon for any future surgery</li>
</ul>

<h2>Investigation for cause</h2>

<p>After a PE, your GP may consider:</p>

<ul>
<li>Cancer screening if PE was unprovoked (especially over 50) - age-appropriate examination, blood tests, sometimes imaging</li>
<li>Thrombophilia (clotting disorder) testing - usually only in select cases (young, unusual location, family history)</li>
<li>Antiphospholipid syndrome screening</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Pregnant women have a higher PE risk, especially in the third trimester and immediately after delivery. Treatment uses heparin injections - DOACs and warfarin are not suitable in pregnancy. Specialist input from obstetrics and haematology.</p>
