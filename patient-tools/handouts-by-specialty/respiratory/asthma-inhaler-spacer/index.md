Inhaler technique is the easiest thing to get wrong and one of the easiest things to fix. Studies show around 70-90% of patients use their inhaler less than perfectly.&sup1; The result: medicine ends up on the back of the throat instead of in the lungs. The good news: a spacer and good technique can double the dose that actually reaches your airways.

<h2>The two main types of inhaler</h2>

<p><strong>Metered-dose inhaler (MDI, "puffer").</strong> Pressurised canister, sprays out a fast cloud. Always best with a spacer.</p>

<p><strong>Dry-powder inhaler (DPI).</strong> Examples: Turbuhaler, Accuhaler, Ellipta, Easyhaler. You breathe in deeply and quickly to pull powder out of the device. No spacer needed.</p>

<h2>Using an MDI with a spacer (best technique)</h2>

<ol>
<li>Shake the inhaler well</li>
<li>Take the cap off</li>
<li>Connect the inhaler to the back of the spacer</li>
<li>Sit or stand upright</li>
<li>Breathe out gently and fully</li>
<li>Put the spacer mouthpiece between your teeth and seal your lips around it (or use the mask if a young child)</li>
<li>Press the inhaler ONCE to release a puff</li>
<li>Breathe in slowly and deeply through the mouth - 4-5 seconds</li>
<li>Hold your breath for 5-10 seconds if possible</li>
<li>Breathe out gently</li>
<li>If you need a second puff, wait 30 seconds, shake, and repeat from step 5</li>
</ol>

<p>Alternative for young children or anyone who cannot do a single deep breath: tidal breathing - take 4-6 normal breaths through the spacer after each puff.</p>

<h2>Common MDI mistakes</h2>

<ul>
<li>Not shaking the inhaler</li>
<li>Pressing while breathing out (medicine just blows away)</li>
<li>Two puffs at once into the spacer (only one fits efficiently per breath)</li>
<li>Breathing in too quickly (drug hits the back of the throat instead)</li>
<li>Not holding the breath</li>
<li>Pressing several times before breathing in</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why a spacer matters</h2>

<p>Without a spacer, only about 10-20% of the dose from an MDI reaches your lungs - the rest hits the throat or is swallowed. With a spacer, around 30-50% reaches the lungs, side effects are reduced (less thrush from steroid inhalers), and timing of breath does not need to be perfect.</p>

<p>Use a spacer for both reliever (Ventolin) and preventer inhalers. Spacers are funded in NZ when prescribed by your GP. Common brands: Volumatic, AeroChamber, Space Chamber.</p>

<h2>Caring for your spacer</h2>

<ul>
<li>Wash with warm soapy water once a week</li>
<li>Do NOT rinse with clean water afterwards or rub dry - the static charge changes and reduces dose delivery</li>
<li>Air-dry overnight</li>
<li>Replace at least every year, sooner if cracked</li>
</ul>

<h2>Using a Turbuhaler (twist-and-load)</h2>

<ol>
<li>Hold upright</li>
<li>Twist the base anti-clockwise then back clockwise until you hear a click - this loads the dose</li>
<li>Breathe out away from the inhaler (not into it)</li>
<li>Put mouthpiece between teeth, seal lips</li>
<li>Breathe in HARD AND DEEP - much harder than an MDI</li>
<li>Hold breath 5-10 seconds</li>
<li>Replace cap</li>
</ol>

<p>Remember: dry powder inhalers need a strong, fast inhalation. The dose counter shows how many doses are left.</p>

<h2>Using an Accuhaler (slide-and-click)</h2>

<ol>
<li>Hold horizontally with mouthpiece toward you</li>
<li>Slide the cover open with your thumb</li>
<li>Slide the lever back until it clicks - loads dose</li>
<li>Breathe out away from the device</li>
<li>Put mouthpiece in mouth, seal lips</li>
<li>Breathe in HARD AND DEEP</li>
<li>Hold breath 5-10 seconds</li>
<li>Slide cover closed</li>
</ol>

<h2>Using an Ellipta</h2>

<ol>
<li>Slide the cover down until you hear a click - loads dose</li>
<li>Breathe out gently away from the inhaler</li>
<li>Put mouthpiece in mouth, lips sealed</li>
<li>Breathe in long, steady, deep breath</li>
<li>Hold breath 3-4 seconds</li>
<li>Close cover</li>
</ol>

<h2>After taking a steroid inhaler</h2>

<p>Always rinse your mouth and gargle with water (and spit, do not swallow), or brush your teeth. This reduces:</p>

<ul>
<li>Oral thrush</li>
<li>Hoarse voice</li>
<li>Sore throat</li>
</ul>

<h2>Track your doses</h2>

<p>Most modern inhalers have dose counters. Salbutamol (blue Ventolin) MDIs do not have counters, so track by date or use a phone reminder. An "empty" inhaler that still hisses is empty - the propellant lasts longer than the medicine.</p>

<h2>Have someone watch you</h2>

<p>Ask your GP or practice nurse to watch your technique once a year. Even confident users develop bad habits. Asthma educators (free in many regions) can do a full review.</p>

<p>Online videos are useful. The Asthma Foundation NZ has demonstration videos for every inhaler type at <a href="https://www.asthma.org.nz">asthma.org.nz</a>.</p>
