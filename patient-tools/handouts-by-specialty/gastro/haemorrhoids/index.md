Haemorrhoids (piles) are swollen veins around the anus. About half of adults will have them by age 50. They are common, often embarrassing to discuss, and almost always treatable. Most settle with self-care; a small number need procedures.

<h2>Two types</h2>

<p><strong>Internal</strong> - inside the rectum. Painless but can bleed and prolapse (slip outside).</p>

<p><strong>External</strong> - under the skin around the anus. Can be painful, especially if they form a clot (thrombose).</p>

<p>Many people have both.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Bright red blood on the toilet paper, in the bowl, or coating the stool</li>
<li>Itching or irritation around the anus</li>
<li>Discomfort or aching, especially after sitting long periods</li>
<li>Lump felt around the anus</li>
<li>Mucus discharge</li>
<li>A sudden very painful firm lump (thrombosed external haemorrhoid)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What causes them</h2>

<ul>
<li>Straining on the toilet</li>
<li>Constipation or hard stools</li>
<li>Diarrhoea (frequent wiping)</li>
<li>Pregnancy and childbirth</li>
<li>Sitting on the toilet too long (phones, books)</li>
<li>Heavy lifting</li>
<li>Low-fibre diet</li>
<li>Aging - tissues weaken</li>
<li>Family history</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<p>Most haemorrhoids do not need a doctor. See your GP if:</p>

<ul>
<li>First time you notice rectal bleeding</li>
<li>Bleeding does not settle within a week of self-care</li>
<li>Heavy bleeding</li>
<li>Severe pain</li>
<li>Sudden very painful lump</li>
<li>Persistent prolapsed haemorrhoid that does not retract</li>
<li>Change in bowel habit lasting more than 3 weeks</li>
<li>You are over 40 with new bleeding</li>
<li>Family history of bowel cancer</li>
</ul>

<p>The reason: rectal bleeding can also come from polyps, inflammatory bowel disease, or bowel cancer. A GP examination usually reassures, and any uncertainty leads to colonoscopy. See our <a href="/handouts/rectal-bleeding/">rectal bleeding handout</a>.</p>

<h2>Self-care</h2>

<p><strong>Soften your stools.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Eat 25-30 g fibre daily - whole grains, fruit (especially with skin), vegetables, legumes</li>
<li>Drink 1.5-2 litres of water daily</li>
<li>Add a fibre supplement (psyllium husk - Metamucil, Konsyl) if diet alone is not enough</li>
<li>Daily prunes or kiwifruit can help</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Toilet habits.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Go when you feel the urge - do not hold on</li>
<li>Do not strain - if nothing comes after 5 minutes, get up and try again later</li>
<li>Use a small footstool to raise your knees - the squat position relaxes the pelvic floor</li>
<li>No phone or book on the toilet - sitting too long worsens haemorrhoids</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>After-toilet care.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Wipe gently with soft toilet paper or use a wet wipe (alcohol-free, fragrance-free)</li>
<li>Pat dry, do not rub</li>
<li>Some people use a peri bottle or bidet</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Sitz baths.</strong> Sit in 5-10 cm of warm (not hot) water for 10-15 minutes 2-3 times a day. Soothes pain and itch. Pat dry afterwards.</p>

<p><strong>Topical treatments.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Anaesthetic creams (Anusol Plus, Rectinol) - short-term relief of itch and discomfort</li>
<li>Steroid creams (Proctosedyl) - reduce inflammation; use only for short courses (1-2 weeks)</li>
<li>Witch hazel pads (Tucks)</li>
<li>Cold packs for thrombosed external haemorrhoids</li>
</ul>

<p>Most over-the-counter products help symptoms but do not cure haemorrhoids - the lifestyle changes do that.</p>

<h2>Procedures - in clinic</h2>

<p>For persistent or troublesome internal haemorrhoids, your GP or specialist may offer:</p>

<p><strong>Rubber band ligation.</strong> A tiny rubber band is placed at the base of the haemorrhoid, cutting off blood supply. The haemorrhoid drops off in a few days. Most effective non-surgical treatment.</p>

<p><strong>Sclerotherapy.</strong> Injection of a chemical to shrink the haemorrhoid. Older treatment, less commonly used now.</p>

<p><strong>Infrared coagulation.</strong> Heat treatment to coagulate the haemorrhoid tissue.</p>

<p>These are usually outpatient procedures with little downtime.</p>

<h2>Surgery - haemorrhoidectomy</h2>

<p>For very large, thrombosed, or fourth-degree (always-prolapsed) haemorrhoids. Effective but more painful recovery (1-2 weeks of significant pain). Reserved for cases not helped by simpler measures.</p>

<p>Newer alternatives include stapled haemorrhoidopexy and HALO (haemorrhoidal artery ligation operation) - less painful than traditional surgery.</p>

<h2>Thrombosed external haemorrhoid</h2>

<p>A sudden very painful, firm, blue-purple lump. The pain peaks at 48 hours and gradually settles over 1-2 weeks.</p>

<p>Options:</p>

<ul>
<li>Self-care - sitz baths, ice, paracetamol/ibuprofen, topical anaesthetic. Most settle in 7-14 days</li>
<li>Surgical excision under local anaesthetic - faster relief if seen within 48-72 hours of onset</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy and after</h2>

<p>Haemorrhoids are very common in pregnancy and after birth - hormonal changes, pressure from the baby, straining during labour. Most settle within weeks of delivery.</p>

<p>Treatment in pregnancy: high-fibre diet, fluids, sitz baths, topical preparations - check with GP about which products are safe.</p>

<h2>Long-term prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Lifelong high-fibre diet, fluids</li>
<li>Don't strain</li>
<li>Don't sit on the toilet too long</li>
<li>Maintain healthy weight</li>
<li>Stay active</li>
<li>Use a footstool</li>
</ul>
