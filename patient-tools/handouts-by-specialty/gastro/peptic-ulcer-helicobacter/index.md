A peptic ulcer is a break in the lining of the stomach (gastric ulcer) or the first part of the small intestine (duodenal ulcer). About 1 in 10 people will have an ulcer at some point. Most ulcers are caused either by Helicobacter pylori (a bacterium) or by regular use of anti-inflammatories like ibuprofen, diclofenac, or naproxen. Treating the cause heals the ulcer in almost everyone.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Burning or gnawing pain in the upper abdomen</li>
<li>Pain often relieved by food (duodenal) or worsened by food (gastric)</li>
<li>Pain at night, waking you up</li>
<li>Nausea</li>
<li>Loss of appetite</li>
<li>Bloating, fullness</li>
<li>Belching</li>
</ul>

<p>Some ulcers (especially in older adults or those on NSAIDs) cause no pain at all and present with bleeding.</p>

<h2>Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori)</h2>

<p>A bacterium that lives in the stomach. Around 25-30% of NZ adults are infected, more in older adults and Maori, Pacific, and Asian people. Most people with H. pylori never have symptoms. In some, it causes:</p>

<ul>
<li>Peptic ulcers</li>
<li>Gastritis</li>
<li>Indigestion</li>
<li>Increased stomach cancer risk over decades</li>
</ul>

<p>Spread is by close family contact in childhood. Once infected, lifelong unless treated.</p>

<h2>NSAIDs and ulcers</h2>

<p>Non-steroidal anti-inflammatories (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen, aspirin) damage the stomach lining. Risk rises with:</p>

<ul>
<li>Age over 65</li>
<li>Higher dose or longer use</li>
<li>Combination with steroids, blood thinners, or SSRIs</li>
<li>Previous ulcer</li>
<li>H. pylori coexisting</li>
</ul>

<p>Aspirin alone (low dose for heart) carries an ulcer risk that builds over years.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Your GP may arrange:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>H. pylori breath test or stool antigen test</strong> - non-invasive, accurate</li>
<li><strong>Gastroscopy</strong> - the definitive test. A thin tube with a camera down the throat. Lets the gastroenterologist see and biopsy the ulcer</li>
<li><strong>Blood tests</strong> for anaemia (suggests chronic blood loss)</li>
</ul>

<p>Gastroscopy is recommended for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Anyone over 50 with new dyspepsia</li>
<li>Symptoms with red flags (weight loss, vomiting, blood, swallowing difficulty, anaemia)</li>
<li>Persistent symptoms despite treatment</li>
</ul>

<p>Stop H2 blockers (famotidine) for 2 weeks and PPIs (omeprazole) for at least 2 weeks before H. pylori testing - they cause false negatives.</p>

<h2>Red flag symptoms - urgent</h2>

<p>See your GP same day for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Vomiting blood or coffee-ground material</li>
<li>Black, tarry stools (melaena)</li>
<li>Severe abdominal pain</li>
<li>Fainting or feeling very unwell</li>
<li>Difficulty swallowing</li>
<li>Unexplained weight loss</li>
</ul>

<p>Bleeding or perforation are emergencies - call <strong>111</strong>.</p>

<h2>Treatment of H. pylori</h2>

<p>"Triple therapy" is the standard - one PPI plus two antibiotics for 7-14 days. Common NZ regimen:</p>

<ul>
<li>Omeprazole 20 mg twice daily</li>
<li>Amoxicillin 1 g twice daily</li>
<li>Clarithromycin 500 mg twice daily</li>
</ul>

<p>Or a quadruple regimen (PPI + bismuth + tetracycline + metronidazole) where resistance is suspected.</p>

<p>Take all doses, even if symptoms settle. Eradication is confirmed with a breath or stool test 4 weeks after finishing treatment (and at least 2 weeks off any PPI).</p>

<h2>Treatment of NSAID-induced ulcers</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop the NSAID if possible</li>
<li>Start a PPI (omeprazole 20-40 mg daily, pantoprazole 40 mg) for 8 weeks</li>
<li>Confirm healing on repeat gastroscopy if gastric ulcer (small risk of cancer mimicking ulcer)</li>
<li>If NSAID cannot be stopped, take a daily PPI for protection</li>
</ul>

<h2>Lifestyle</h2>

<ul>
<li>Stop smoking - smokers heal slower</li>
<li>Reduce alcohol</li>
<li>Avoid NSAIDs unless essential. Use paracetamol for pain</li>
<li>Avoid foods that worsen your symptoms (varies between individuals - spicy, fatty, citrus for some)</li>
<li>Eat smaller frequent meals if helpful</li>
<li>Avoid late-night eating</li>
</ul>

<p>Stress and spicy food do not cause ulcers - this is an old misconception. They can aggravate symptoms once you have one.</p>

<h2>What to expect during recovery</h2>

<ul>
<li>Pain improves within days of starting PPI</li>
<li>Ulcers heal over 4-8 weeks</li>
<li>Once H. pylori is eradicated, ulcers rarely return</li>
<li>If on long-term NSAIDs, ongoing PPI cover is usually needed</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term PPI use</h2>

<p>PPIs are mostly safe but long-term use has small associated risks:</p>

<ul>
<li>Slightly increased risk of bone fractures</li>
<li>Vitamin B12 deficiency (over years)</li>
<li>Magnesium deficiency</li>
<li>Slightly higher risk of C. difficile and other gut infections</li>
<li>Some studies suggest small increases in pneumonia and dementia risk - debated</li>
</ul>

<p>Do not stop a PPI suddenly if you have been on it long-term - acid often rebounds. Step down gradually with your GP's guidance.</p>

<h2>Stomach cancer surveillance</h2>

<p>People at higher risk of stomach cancer (family history, Maori/Pacific/Asian heritage, certain genetic syndromes) may benefit from H. pylori screening and treatment, even without symptoms. Discuss with your GP.</p>
