Fatty liver disease - now called MASLD (metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease) - is when extra fat builds up in liver cells. It is the most common liver condition in New Zealand, affecting around 1 in 4 adults. Most people have no symptoms. The good news: it is largely reversible with weight loss and the lifestyle changes that also benefit your heart, kidneys, and metabolism.

<h2>The new name</h2>

<p>The condition was previously called NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver disease). The new term MASLD reflects the strong link with metabolic syndrome - excess weight, insulin resistance, raised blood sugar, raised triglycerides, raised blood pressure.</p>

<p>For people with significant alcohol intake plus metabolic features, the term is MetALD.</p>

<h2>The progression</h2>

<p>Most people with fatty liver stay at the simple-fatty-liver stage and never develop trouble. A minority progress through:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Steatosis</strong> - fat in the liver, no inflammation</li>
<li><strong>MASH</strong> (steatohepatitis) - fat plus inflammation; liver enzymes rise</li>
<li><strong>Fibrosis</strong> - scarring</li>
<li><strong>Cirrhosis</strong> - severe scarring; serious</li>
<li><strong>Liver cancer</strong> - rare, mostly in cirrhosis</li>
</ol>

<p>Modern care aims to identify those at higher risk of progression.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Most fatty liver is silent. Symptoms (usually with more advanced disease):</p>

<ul>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Discomfort in the upper right abdomen</li>
<li>Late stage: jaundice, swelling, easy bruising</li>
</ul>

<p>Most fatty liver is found incidentally - on liver function tests, ultrasound for another reason, or screening of metabolic syndrome.</p>

<h2>How it is diagnosed</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Blood tests</strong> - mildly raised ALT and GGT often the first clue. AST/ALT ratio is sometimes useful</li>
<li><strong>Liver ultrasound</strong> - shows "bright" (fatty) liver</li>
<li><strong>FibroScan (transient elastography)</strong> - measures stiffness; the best non-invasive test for fibrosis</li>
<li><strong>FIB-4 score</strong> - a calculated score from age, AST, ALT, platelets - identifies people who need further assessment</li>
<li><strong>Liver biopsy</strong> - rarely needed; reserved for unclear cases</li>
</ul>

<h2>Other liver conditions to exclude</h2>

<p>Your GP will usually screen for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Hepatitis B and C</li>
<li>Iron overload (haemochromatosis)</li>
<li>Autoimmune hepatitis</li>
<li>Wilson's disease (in younger patients)</li>
<li>Excess alcohol intake</li>
<li>Some medicines (methotrexate, amiodarone, tamoxifen)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps - the cornerstones</h2>

<p>The single most powerful intervention: <strong>weight loss</strong>. Even modest weight loss reverses much fatty liver.</p>

<ul>
<li>5% weight loss reduces liver fat substantially</li>
<li>7-10% weight loss can reverse steatohepatitis</li>
<li>10%+ weight loss can reverse fibrosis in many cases</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Other key changes:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Reduce sugary drinks completely - the strongest single dietary change</li>
<li>Cut refined carbs and ultra-processed food</li>
<li>Eat Mediterranean-style - vegetables, legumes, fish, olive oil, nuts</li>
<li>150 minutes of exercise per week, plus 2 sessions of resistance training</li>
<li>Limit alcohol - some experts advise complete avoidance with significant fatty liver</li>
<li>Stop smoking</li>
<li>Treat metabolic syndrome - blood pressure, lipids, glucose</li>
</ul>

<h2>Coffee</h2>

<p>Three to four cups of black coffee a day is associated with lower liver fibrosis and may modestly help. Drink it without sugar, syrup, or sweeteners.</p>

<h2>Medications</h2>

<p>No medicines specifically funded for MASLD in NZ yet, but some treatments help indirectly:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors</strong> (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) - reduce liver fat and HbA1c. Funded for diabetes</li>
<li><strong>GLP-1 agonists</strong> (semaglutide, dulaglutide) - effective for weight loss and metabolic profile; funded for diabetes</li>
<li><strong>Pioglitazone</strong> - reduces liver inflammation; less commonly used due to side effects</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin E</strong> at high dose has some evidence in non-diabetic patients with biopsy-proven steatohepatitis - discuss with specialist</li>
<li><strong>Resmetirom</strong> - new treatment recently approved overseas; not yet funded in NZ</li>
</ul>

<p>Statins are safe in fatty liver and recommended if cardiovascular risk is high - cardiovascular disease is the main cause of death in MASLD.</p>

<h2>Bariatric surgery</h2>

<p>For people with severe obesity, bariatric surgery often dramatically improves or resolves fatty liver. Eligible criteria in NZ usually include BMI over 35 with related conditions, or BMI over 40.</p>

<h2>Surveillance</h2>

<ul>
<li>Annual liver function tests if simple fatty liver</li>
<li>FibroScan or fibrosis score every 1-3 years</li>
<li>If cirrhosis: 6-monthly liver ultrasound and AFP for cancer screening</li>
<li>Hepatitis B vaccination if not immune</li>
</ul>

<h2>Alcohol</h2>

<p>Even moderate alcohol can accelerate liver damage in someone with fatty liver. NZ guidance suggests limiting to under 10 standard drinks per week with at least 2 alcohol-free days. Some hepatologists advise no alcohol with significant MASLD - discuss with your GP.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Most pregnancies are normal. Severe fatty liver of pregnancy (a different condition) is rare and serious. Pre-pregnancy weight optimisation helps maternal and child outcomes.</p>
