Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition where eating gluten (a protein in wheat, rye, and barley) damages the lining of the small intestine. About 1 in 100 New Zealanders has coeliac disease, but most are undiagnosed.&sup1; A strict, lifelong gluten-free diet allows the gut to heal and prevents long-term complications.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Symptoms vary widely. Some people have severe gut symptoms; others have very few or none.</p>

<p><strong>Classic gastrointestinal:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Diarrhoea or pale, bulky, foul-smelling stools</li>
<li>Bloating and wind</li>
<li>Abdominal pain or cramping</li>
<li>Constipation (yes, also)</li>
<li>Weight loss or failure to thrive in children</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Non-gut symptoms - often missed:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Iron deficiency anaemia (sometimes the only sign)</li>
<li>Fatigue</li>
<li>Bone pain or osteoporosis</li>
<li>Mouth ulcers</li>
<li>Dental enamel defects</li>
<li>Itchy blistering rash (dermatitis herpetiformis)</li>
<li>Infertility, recurrent miscarriage</li>
<li>Joint pains</li>
<li>Migraines</li>
<li>Brain fog, anxiety, depression</li>
<li>Peripheral neuropathy</li>
<li>Children: poor growth, delayed puberty</li>
</ul>

<p>Some people have no symptoms at all - "silent coeliac" - found through screening.</p>

<h2>Who should be tested?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Anyone with gut symptoms suggestive of coeliac</li>
<li>First-degree relatives of someone with coeliac (10% risk)</li>
<li>Type 1 diabetes</li>
<li>Autoimmune thyroid disease</li>
<li>Down syndrome, Turner syndrome</li>
<li>Unexplained iron deficiency, low B12, low folate</li>
<li>Unexplained osteoporosis under 50</li>
<li>Recurrent miscarriage or unexplained infertility</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>You must be eating gluten regularly when tested - eliminate gluten before tests and they may be falsely negative.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Blood test for tTG IgA antibodies</strong> (with total IgA to check for IgA deficiency). High specificity</li>
<li><strong>Endoscopy with small bowel biopsy</strong> - the definitive test. Looks for villous atrophy. Done by gastroenterologist</li>
<li><strong>Genetic testing</strong> (HLA-DQ2/DQ8) - useful when antibody tests are inconclusive. Most coeliacs carry these genes, but so do 30% of the population - it does not diagnose, only excludes</li>
</ol>

<h2>Treatment - strict gluten-free diet</h2>

<p>This is the only treatment. Even tiny amounts of gluten (less than a crumb daily) can cause damage in some people. The diet is for life.</p>

<p><strong>Gluten is in:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Wheat (and spelt, kamut, durum, semolina, couscous, bulgur)</li>
<li>Rye</li>
<li>Barley (including malt - so most beer, malt vinegar)</li>
<li>Oats - contaminated with wheat in most processing; certified gluten-free oats are often tolerated</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Hidden sources:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Sauces, soups, gravies, dressings</li>
<li>Stock cubes and seasonings</li>
<li>Sausages and processed meats</li>
<li>Confectionery and licorice</li>
<li>Some medications and supplements</li>
<li>Communion wafers (low-gluten options exist)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Naturally gluten-free:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Rice, corn, potato, quinoa, buckwheat (despite the name), millet, sorghum</li>
<li>All meat, fish, eggs (unprocessed)</li>
<li>All fruits and vegetables</li>
<li>Dairy</li>
<li>Most legumes, nuts, seeds</li>
</ul>

<h2>Reading labels in NZ</h2>

<p>NZ food labelling requires gluten to be listed. Look for:</p>

<ul>
<li>"Gluten free" claim - product contains under 5 mg/kg gluten (the safe threshold)</li>
<li>Ingredients lists - check every time as recipes change</li>
<li>Allergen statements - "contains wheat" or "may contain wheat"</li>
</ul>

<p>The Coeliac NZ "Crossed Grain" symbol indicates products certified gluten-free.</p>

<h2>Cross-contamination</h2>

<p>Gluten can transfer from contaminated surfaces. At home:</p>

<ul>
<li>Separate toaster or use toaster bags</li>
<li>Separate butter, jam, peanut butter, spreads (use clean knife)</li>
<li>Wash boards thoroughly between gluten and gluten-free preparation</li>
<li>Keep gluten-free items separate or above gluten foods in pantry</li>
</ul>

<p>Eating out is harder. Choose restaurants with gluten-free menus and trained staff. Tell waitstaff you have coeliac, not just "gluten intolerance" - they will take it more seriously.</p>

<h2>Follow-up care</h2>

<p>Annual review:</p>

<ul>
<li>Symptoms and quality of life</li>
<li>tTG antibody (should normalise within 6-12 months)</li>
<li>Iron, B12, folate, calcium, vitamin D</li>
<li>Bone density scan at diagnosis and periodically</li>
<li>Liver function</li>
<li>Thyroid function (associated condition)</li>
<li>Diabetes screening (associated)</li>
</ul>

<p>Dietitian referral at diagnosis is highly recommended.</p>

<h2>What if I am still symptomatic on a gluten-free diet?</h2>

<p>Common reasons:</p>

<ul>
<li>Hidden gluten in diet (most common) - dietitian review</li>
<li>Lactose intolerance (often resolves once gut heals)</li>
<li>Coexisting IBS</li>
<li>Microscopic colitis</li>
<li>Refractory coeliac disease (rare)</li>
</ul>

<h2>The cost</h2>

<p>Gluten-free products cost 2-4 times more than wheat equivalents. NZ does not subsidise gluten-free foods. Coeliac NZ membership ($45/year) gives access to a comprehensive product directory.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Untreated coeliac is associated with infertility and miscarriage. On a strict gluten-free diet, fertility and pregnancy outcomes are normal.</p>
