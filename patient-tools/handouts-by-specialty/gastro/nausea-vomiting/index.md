Nausea and vomiting are unpleasant but usually self-limiting symptoms with many possible causes. Most of the time, the body sorts itself out within 24-48 hours. Knowing how to stay hydrated, what to avoid, and when to seek help makes the experience easier and prevents complications like dehydration.

<h2>Common causes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Gastroenteritis</strong> - viral, sometimes bacterial; the most common cause</li>
<li><strong>Migraines</strong></li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy</strong> (especially first trimester)</li>
<li><strong>Motion sickness</strong></li>
<li><strong>Inner ear problems</strong> - vertigo, labyrinthitis</li>
<li><strong>Reflux and indigestion</strong></li>
<li><strong>Medications</strong> - opioids, chemotherapy, antibiotics, NSAIDs</li>
<li><strong>Alcohol or drugs</strong></li>
<li><strong>Anxiety and panic</strong></li>
<li><strong>Food poisoning</strong></li>
<li><strong>Less commonly:</strong> appendicitis, kidney stones, gallstones, bowel obstruction, severe constipation, diabetic ketoacidosis, head injury, increased pressure in the brain, severe heart attack (especially in women and older adults)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do at home - first 4-12 hours</h2>

<p>Let your stomach rest:</p>

<ul>
<li>No food for 1-2 hours</li>
<li>No water either initially - sips often come straight back up</li>
<li>Lie quietly</li>
</ul>

<p>Then start small sips:</p>

<ul>
<li>5-10 ml every 5-10 minutes - water, dilute juice, electrolyte solution (Hydralyte, Pedialyte, Powerade)</li>
<li>Build up gradually as tolerated</li>
<li>Once not throwing up, drink more</li>
</ul>

<p>Reintroduce food slowly when nausea settles - dry toast, crackers, banana, rice, plain pasta. Avoid rich, fatty, or strong-smelling foods initially.</p>

<h2>Avoid</h2>

<ul>
<li>Large amounts of fluid at once</li>
<li>Coffee, alcohol, fizzy drinks initially</li>
<li>Strong food smells</li>
<li>Lying flat after vomiting - prop up on pillows</li>
</ul>

<h2>Anti-nausea medicines</h2>

<p>Available without prescription:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Hyoscine (Sea-Legs)</strong> for motion sickness - take 30-60 minutes before travel</li>
<li><strong>Promethazine (Phenergan)</strong> - useful for motion sickness, mild nausea, and to help sleep through nausea</li>
<li><strong>Cyclizine (Marzine)</strong> - similar uses</li>
<li><strong>Ginger</strong> - tablets, capsules, or fresh - helpful for pregnancy nausea and motion sickness</li>
</ul>

<p>Prescription:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Metoclopramide</strong> - useful for nausea with reflux or migraine. Avoid long use due to neurological side effects</li>
<li><strong>Ondansetron</strong> - very effective; used for chemotherapy and pregnancy. Can constipate. Available on prescription</li>
<li><strong>Domperidone</strong> - useful in some patients. Heart rhythm precaution</li>
</ul>

<p>If vomiting is severe, your GP may give an injection or recommend a wafer or suppository form.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy nausea</h2>

<p>Affects 70-80% of pregnancies, usually peaking around weeks 8-12 and settling by week 16-20. Some women have severe forms (hyperemesis gravidarum) needing hospital treatment.</p>

<p>What helps:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eat small frequent meals - empty stomach worsens nausea</li>
<li>Dry crackers before getting out of bed</li>
<li>Ginger</li>
<li>Pyridoxine (vitamin B6)</li>
<li>Acupressure wristbands</li>
<li>Doxylamine + pyridoxine (Diclectin) - approved in pregnancy</li>
<li>Avoid trigger smells, brushing teeth right after eating</li>
<li>Stay hydrated - even small frequent sips</li>
</ul>

<p>See your GP or midwife if you cannot keep fluids down, are losing weight, or feel very unwell.</p>

<h2>Migraine nausea</h2>

<p>Often as bad as the headache itself. Treat early. Many migraine medications are available with anti-nausea (e.g. paracetamol with metoclopramide). Triptans can help. See our migraine handout if available.</p>

<h2>When to seek same-day help</h2>

<p>See a GP or call Healthline 0800 611 116 for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Vomiting blood or coffee-ground material</li>
<li>Severe abdominal pain</li>
<li>Persistent vomiting beyond 24-48 hours</li>
<li>Unable to keep any fluids down</li>
<li>Signs of dehydration - very dry mouth, no urine for 8+ hours, severe weakness, dizziness on standing</li>
<li>High fever (over 39°C)</li>
<li>Severe headache</li>
<li>Stiff neck or rash with fever</li>
<li>Vomit looks green or yellow-green (bile) in someone with severe pain</li>
<li>Recent head injury</li>
<li>In a baby or small child</li>
<li>Pregnancy with persistent vomiting</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Vomiting with severe chest pain</li>
<li>Vomiting with sudden severe headache</li>
<li>Confusion, drowsiness, or fitting</li>
<li>Severe dehydration in baby or older person</li>
</ul>

<h2>Babies and young children</h2>

<p>Vomiting (and especially with diarrhoea) dehydrates infants quickly. See a doctor or call Healthline if:</p>

<ul>
<li>Under 6 months with any persistent vomiting</li>
<li>Under 1 year with vomiting beyond 6-8 hours</li>
<li>Any child with reduced wet nappies, dry mouth, sunken eyes, drowsiness, refusing fluids</li>
<li>Vomiting after a head injury</li>
<li>Green vomit</li>
<li>Vomiting with severe abdominal pain or distended tummy</li>
<li>Vomiting blood</li>
<li>Severe cough/breathing difficulty alongside</li>
</ul>

<p>For mild gastro in children, use small frequent sips of oral rehydration solution.</p>

<h2>Older adults</h2>

<p>Older people dehydrate quickly and may not feel thirsty. Vomiting in an older person, especially with confusion, off-balance, or new abdominal pain, deserves a low threshold for medical review. Some serious causes (heart attack, sepsis, raised intracranial pressure) can present with vomiting alone.</p>
