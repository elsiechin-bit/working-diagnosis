The pancreas is a gland behind the stomach that makes digestive enzymes and insulin. When it becomes inflamed - pancreatitis - it can cause severe abdominal pain and significant illness. Most cases settle within a week in hospital. The two most common causes (gallstones and alcohol) are both treatable, so finding and removing the cause prevents recurrence.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Severe upper abdominal pain, often spreading to the back</li>
<li>Pain may improve when leaning forward</li>
<li>Nausea and vomiting</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Tender, sometimes distended abdomen</li>
<li>Fast pulse</li>
<li>In severe cases: low blood pressure, shortness of breath, jaundice, confusion</li>
</ul>

<p>The pain of acute pancreatitis is among the most severe in medicine. Almost everyone with pancreatitis is admitted to hospital.</p>

<h2>Causes</h2>

<p><strong>Most common (70-80% of cases):</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Gallstones</li>
<li>Alcohol (especially heavy or binge drinking)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Less common:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>High triglycerides</li>
<li>High calcium</li>
<li>Some medications (azathioprine, valproate, some HIV medications, GLP-1 agonists rarely)</li>
<li>Trauma</li>
<li>Post-ERCP procedure</li>
<li>Viruses (mumps, EBV)</li>
<li>Genetic conditions (PRSS1, SPINK1, CFTR mutations)</li>
<li>Autoimmune pancreatitis</li>
<li>Pancreatic cancer (especially in older adults)</li>
<li>Idiopathic (no cause found in around 10-20%)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>The diagnosis is made by:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe abdominal pain typical of pancreatitis</li>
<li>Lipase (a pancreatic enzyme) at least 3x normal in the blood</li>
<li>Imaging - CT or MRI usually confirms; ultrasound for gallstones</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment in hospital</h2>

<p>Care is supportive:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bowel rest - no food initially</li>
<li>Intravenous fluids</li>
<li>Pain control - usually opioids</li>
<li>Anti-nausea medicines</li>
<li>Reintroduction of food usually within 1-3 days as tolerated</li>
<li>Treatment of cause if known (gallbladder removal, alcohol cessation)</li>
<li>Antibiotics only if infection is present</li>
</ul>

<p>Most uncomplicated cases recover in 5-7 days.</p>

<h2>Severe pancreatitis</h2>

<p>About 1 in 5 cases is severe and needs intensive care:</p>

<ul>
<li>Fluid into chest (pleural effusion) or breathing failure</li>
<li>Kidney failure</li>
<li>Pancreatic necrosis (parts of the gland die off)</li>
<li>Infected necrosis</li>
<li>Pancreatic pseudocysts</li>
</ul>

<p>Severe pancreatitis can be fatal in around 10-15% of those affected. Most who recover return to normal function.</p>

<h2>After discharge</h2>

<p>Common advice for the first 4-6 weeks:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eat small frequent meals</li>
<li>Low-fat diet</li>
<li>Avoid alcohol completely</li>
<li>No smoking</li>
<li>Build up activity gradually</li>
<li>Watch for return of symptoms</li>
</ul>

<p>If gallstones were the cause, gallbladder removal is usually scheduled within 2-6 weeks of recovery (often during the same admission for mild cases).</p>

<p>If alcohol was the cause, alcohol must be stopped permanently. Even a small amount can trigger another attack. Support is essential - see our alcohol-related handouts.</p>

<h2>Chronic pancreatitis</h2>

<p>Repeated attacks can lead to chronic pancreatitis - permanent damage that affects digestion and blood sugar.</p>

<p>Symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Recurrent or persistent abdominal pain</li>
<li>Weight loss</li>
<li>Pale, oily, foul-smelling stools (steatorrhoea) - undigested fat</li>
<li>Diabetes</li>
<li>Vitamin deficiencies</li>
</ul>

<p>Causes are dominated by alcohol but can include genetic factors and recurrent gallstone disease.</p>

<p>Treatment:</p>

<ul>
<li>Stop drinking</li>
<li>Pain management</li>
<li>Pancreatic enzyme replacement (Creon) with meals</li>
<li>Diabetes treatment</li>
<li>Vitamin replacement</li>
<li>Specialist referral - sometimes endoscopic or surgical interventions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pancreatic cancer screening</h2>

<p>People with chronic pancreatitis have higher pancreatic cancer risk, especially over 50 with longstanding disease. Surveillance is sometimes offered. Genetic syndromes (BRCA, hereditary pancreatitis) prompt closer surveillance.</p>

<h2>Lifestyle for prevention</h2>

<ul>
<li>Limit alcohol - under 10 standard drinks per week, ideally less if previous attacks</li>
<li>Maintain healthy weight</li>
<li>Treat high triglycerides if present</li>
<li>Stop smoking - smokers have worse pancreatic outcomes</li>
<li>Treat gallstones if symptomatic</li>
</ul>

<h2>Warning signs - call your GP or 111</h2>

<p>After pancreatitis, return to hospital for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Recurring severe abdominal pain</li>
<li>Persistent vomiting</li>
<li>High fever</li>
<li>Jaundice</li>
<li>New or worsening abdominal swelling</li>
<li>Severe back pain</li>
</ul>
