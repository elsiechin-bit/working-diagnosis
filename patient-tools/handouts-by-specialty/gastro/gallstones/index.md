Gallstones form in the gallbladder, a small pouch under the liver that stores bile. About 1 in 10 NZ adults has gallstones. The majority cause no symptoms and need no treatment. When stones cause symptoms, the usual treatment is to remove the gallbladder. Modern keyhole surgery is straightforward and well tolerated.

<h2>What gallstones are</h2>

<p>Gallstones are solid lumps that form when components of bile (cholesterol, bile salts, calcium) crystallise. Most are made of cholesterol; a smaller proportion are pigment stones.</p>

<h2>Risk factors</h2>

<p>The classic five "F"s remain a useful summary, with newer additions:</p>

<ul>
<li>Female</li>
<li>Forty-plus</li>
<li>Fertile (pregnancy and oestrogen contraceptive raise risk)</li>
<li>Fat (overweight)</li>
<li>Family history</li>
<li>Fast weight loss (after bariatric surgery, very-low-calorie diets)</li>
<li>Diabetes</li>
<li>Liver cirrhosis</li>
<li>Some ethnicities (especially native American, some Pacific groups)</li>
<li>Some medicines (oestrogen, certain cholesterol drugs)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms - "biliary colic"</h2>

<ul>
<li>Severe pain in the upper right abdomen, often radiating to the right shoulder blade</li>
<li>Lasts 30 minutes to several hours</li>
<li>Often after a fatty meal</li>
<li>Constant pain, not crampy (despite the term "colic")</li>
<li>Often with nausea and vomiting</li>
<li>Settles spontaneously</li>
</ul>

<p>Between attacks, you usually feel completely well.</p>

<h2>Complications</h2>

<p><strong>Cholecystitis</strong> - inflammation of the gallbladder. Pain becomes more constant, fever, tender abdomen. Needs antibiotics and usually urgent surgery.</p>

<p><strong>Stone in the bile duct</strong> - causes jaundice (yellow skin and eyes), dark urine, pale stools. Risk of liver damage and cholangitis (infection in the bile duct).</p>

<p><strong>Pancreatitis</strong> - if a stone blocks the pancreatic duct. Severe upper abdominal pain spreading to the back, nausea, vomiting. Hospital admission. See our <a href="/handouts/pancreatitis/">pancreatitis handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Cholangitis</strong> - serious infection in the bile ducts. Fever, jaundice, abdominal pain (Charcot's triad). Emergency.</p>

<p><strong>Gallbladder cancer</strong> - rare but serious. Risk slightly higher in people with longstanding stones, especially porcelain gallbladder.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Ultrasound</strong> - the standard first test. Quick, no radiation, accurate</li>
<li><strong>Blood tests</strong> - liver function, inflammation markers, lipase</li>
<li>Sometimes <strong>MRCP</strong> (MRI of the bile ducts) if a duct stone is suspected</li>
<li><strong>HIDA scan</strong> if function questions</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to act</h2>

<p>Same-day GP or hospital for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pain lasting more than a few hours</li>
<li>Fever with abdominal pain</li>
<li>Jaundice (yellow eyes/skin)</li>
<li>Persistent vomiting</li>
<li>Severe pain spreading to back</li>
<li>Confusion or feeling very unwell</li>
</ul>

<h2>Silent gallstones</h2>

<p>If gallstones are found by chance on a scan and you have no symptoms, the usual recommendation is to leave them alone. Each year, only 2-4% of silent stones cause symptoms. Surgery is not without risk and is reserved for those with symptoms or complications.</p>

<p>Exceptions where surgery may be considered for silent stones:</p>

<ul>
<li>Very large stones (over 3 cm)</li>
<li>Porcelain gallbladder</li>
<li>People with sickle cell disease</li>
<li>Some other specific conditions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment of symptomatic gallstones</h2>

<p><strong>Cholecystectomy</strong> - surgical removal of the gallbladder. Usually keyhole (laparoscopic), under general anaesthetic. Standard treatment for symptomatic stones.</p>

<p>What to expect:</p>

<ul>
<li>4 small incisions on the abdomen</li>
<li>Day surgery in many cases, or 1 night in hospital</li>
<li>Discomfort for 1-2 weeks</li>
<li>Most return to normal activity in 2-3 weeks; heavy lifting at 4-6 weeks</li>
<li>Around 5% need conversion to open surgery (longer recovery)</li>
<li>Small risk (around 1 in 500) of bile duct injury</li>
</ul>

<h2>Life without a gallbladder</h2>

<p>You can live a completely normal life without a gallbladder. Bile flows directly from the liver to the bowel rather than being stored. Most people experience no difference. Some have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Looser stools, especially after fatty meals (usually settles within months)</li>
<li>Mild bloating</li>
<li>Occasional postprandial diarrhoea</li>
</ul>

<p>For the few with persistent diarrhoea, bile-binding agents (cholestyramine) usually help.</p>

<h2>Stones in the bile duct</h2>

<p>If a stone has migrated into the bile duct, it is usually removed via ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography) - a tube passed via the mouth that allows direct access to the bile duct. The gallbladder is then removed surgically.</p>

<h2>Diet</h2>

<p>For people with gallstones not yet operated on:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eat lower-fat meals to reduce attacks</li>
<li>Avoid large meals; eat smaller portions more frequently</li>
<li>Avoid trigger foods - usually fried, fatty, or rich</li>
</ul>

<p>Maintaining a healthy weight reduces both new stones and attacks. Avoid very-rapid weight loss diets, which paradoxically increase stone formation.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Gallstones are common in and after pregnancy. Mild symptoms can be managed conservatively until after delivery. Surgery is sometimes needed in pregnancy (usually second trimester). Discuss with obstetric team.</p>
