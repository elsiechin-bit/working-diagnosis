Rectal bleeding is alarming. The good news: most bleeding turns out to be from haemorrhoids or anal fissures - common, treatable, and not dangerous. But some bleeding is from polyps, inflammatory bowel disease, or bowel cancer. Knowing what to do, and when, is what matters.

<h2>What you might see</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Bright red blood on the paper or in the bowl</strong> - usually from haemorrhoids or fissures</li>
<li><strong>Blood mixed in with the stool</strong> - often from higher up; needs investigation</li>
<li><strong>Dark or maroon-coloured stool</strong> - bleeding from the small bowel or right side of the colon</li>
<li><strong>Black, tarry stool (melaena)</strong> - bleeding from the stomach or upper bowel; medical emergency</li>
<li><strong>Blood in mucus</strong> - can suggest inflammation</li>
</ul>

<p>Sometimes bleeding is not visible - "occult" - and shows up only on a faecal occult blood test (the bowel screening test).</p>

<h2>Common causes</h2>

<p><strong>Haemorrhoids</strong> - bright red, often dripping into bowl, often with itching or lump. See our <a href="/handouts/haemorrhoids/">haemorrhoids handout</a>.</p>

<p><strong>Anal fissure</strong> - small tear in the skin around the anus from a hard or large stool. Sharp pain on defecation, often with bright red blood on paper.</p>

<p><strong>Diverticular disease</strong> - small pouches in the colon wall can bleed; often painless and sudden, more in older adults.</p>

<p><strong>Polyps</strong> - small growths that can bleed; usually painless. Some are precancerous.</p>

<p><strong>Inflammatory bowel disease</strong> - Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis. Often with diarrhoea, urgency, weight loss, abdominal pain.</p>

<p><strong>Bowel cancer</strong> - bleeding may be the only sign. Other symptoms include change in bowel habit, weight loss, anaemia.</p>

<p><strong>Gastroenteritis</strong> with severe inflammation can cause bloody diarrhoea.</p>

<p><strong>Ischaemic colitis</strong> - reduced blood supply to part of the colon, more in older people.</p>

<p><strong>Radiation proctitis</strong> - after pelvic radiotherapy.</p>

<h2>Red flags - urgent</h2>

<p>See your GP same-day, or the emergency department if at night, for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Heavy bleeding</li>
<li>Black, tarry stools</li>
<li>Vomiting blood</li>
<li>Severe abdominal pain</li>
<li>Fainting, dizziness, or feeling very unwell</li>
<li>Pale and short of breath (suggesting significant blood loss)</li>
<li>Blood loss with rapid heart rate or low blood pressure</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> if collapsing, severe ongoing bleeding, or signs of shock.</p>

<h2>See your GP within a week for</h2>

<ul>
<li>First-ever rectal bleeding</li>
<li>Any rectal bleeding over age 50</li>
<li>Blood mixed in the stool (rather than just on the paper)</li>
<li>Persistent bleeding for more than a week</li>
<li>Change in bowel habit lasting over 3 weeks</li>
<li>Weight loss</li>
<li>Tiredness or anaemia (your stools may be darker than normal)</li>
<li>Family history of bowel cancer or polyps</li>
<li>Persistent abdominal pain</li>
</ul>

<h2>What your GP will do</h2>

<ul>
<li>Take a full history</li>
<li>Examine your abdomen</li>
<li>Examine the anus and do a digital rectal examination (gloved finger)</li>
<li>Sometimes proctoscopy (a short tube to inspect the anal canal) in clinic</li>
<li>Order blood tests - full blood count to check for anaemia</li>
<li>Refer for colonoscopy if any concern</li>
</ul>

<h2>Colonoscopy</h2>

<p>The gold standard for investigating bleeding. A flexible camera looks at the whole colon, can biopsy abnormalities, and remove polyps in the same procedure. Usually done under sedation. Preparation involves a strong laxative the day before to clear the bowel.</p>

<p>Recovery is quick; most people return to normal activities the next day.</p>

<h2>NZ bowel screening</h2>

<p>The free National Bowel Screening Programme tests for hidden blood in the stool every 2 years for people aged 60-74 (with a phased extension for Maori and Pacific from age 50). See our <a href="/handouts/nz-bowel-screening/">bowel screening handout</a>. If you receive a kit, do it - it saves lives by detecting cancers and polyps early.</p>

<h2>What you can do at home for likely haemorrhoids/fissure</h2>

<p>While you await assessment or for mild symptoms:</p>

<ul>
<li>Eat 25-30 g of fibre daily</li>
<li>Drink plenty of water</li>
<li>Do not strain</li>
<li>Use a footstool on the toilet</li>
<li>Sitz baths - 10-15 minutes warm water 2-3 times a day</li>
<li>Topical creams (Anusol, Rectinol) for short-term relief</li>
<li>For fissure: paracetamol, sitz baths, and gentle lubrication can help. GTN ointment (prescribed) often heals chronic fissures</li>
</ul>

<h2>Bleeding in pregnancy</h2>

<p>Common, usually from haemorrhoids or fissures from constipation. Tell your midwife or GP, especially if heavy. Most settles after delivery.</p>

<h2>Bleeding in children</h2>

<p>In children, common causes are anal fissures (from constipation) and gastroenteritis. Always discuss with a GP - rectal bleeding in children is uncommon and merits a careful look.</p>
