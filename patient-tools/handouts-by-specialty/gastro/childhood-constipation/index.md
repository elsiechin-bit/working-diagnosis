<p>Constipation in children is common - around 1 in 20 children is affected at some point - and is one of the most distressing conditions for both child and parent.[1] The frustrating thing about it is that the standard advice ("more fibre, more water") is often only a small part of the answer. Childhood constipation is mostly a problem of the rectum becoming stretched and the urge-to-go signal becoming dulled. Treating it well takes weeks to months and a clear plan. This handout walks through it.</p>

<h2>How constipation starts</h2>

<p>It usually begins with one painful or difficult poo - sometimes during weaning, toilet training, after an illness, or after a hospital admission. The child holds on to avoid the next painful experience. Over weeks, more poo accumulates in the rectum, the rectum stretches, the urge signal becomes less reliable, and stool becomes harder. By the time parents notice, there is often a backlog and the child has been holding on for some time.</p>

<p>Signs the cycle is established:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Pooing only every 3-7 days, sometimes with very large stools</li>
  <li>Soiling - small amounts of liquid stool leaking past a backed-up rectum, often mistaken for diarrhoea</li>
  <li>Tummy pain, especially before pooing</li>
  <li>Loss of appetite</li>
  <li>Standing on tiptoes, crossing legs, hiding behind furniture - holding behaviours</li>
  <li>Behaviour change - irritable, reluctant to go anywhere far from a toilet, school refusal in older children</li>
</ul>

<p>Important: soiling is not the child being lazy or naughty. It is overflow around an impacted rectum that they cannot feel.</p>

<h2>The two-stage treatment</h2>

<p>The recipe that works in NZ and internationally has two parts: clear out the backlog (disimpaction), then keep the rectum empty long enough for it to shrink back to normal size and for the urge signal to recalibrate (maintenance). This is not a quick fix. The maintenance phase typically lasts 6 months to 2 years.</p>

<h3>Stage 1: disimpaction</h3>

<p>Macrogol (Movicol, Lax-Sachets, OsmoLax in NZ) is the first-line. It is an osmotic laxative that pulls water into the bowel, softening stool and stimulating movement. Tasteless when mixed in water or juice.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Dose:</strong> high, escalating dose for 5-7 days. The exact regimen depends on age and severity - your GP will guide. A typical disimpaction in a 4-year-old might be 4 sachets a day, scaled up if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Expect:</strong> a lot of poo. Sometimes very runny. Sometimes a temporary worsening of soiling for a few days.</li>
  <li><strong>Continue daily</strong> for up to a week or until the rectum is clearly empty.</li>
</ul>

<p>If macrogol alone is not enough, your GP may add a stimulant laxative (sodium picosulfate, senna, or bisacodyl) for a short period. Enemas and suppositories are rarely needed in children but used in severe cases.</p>

<h3>Stage 2: maintenance</h3>

<p>Once the backlog is cleared, the rectum needs months to return to normal size. The aim is one soft stool every day or every other day. Stopping treatment too early is the most common mistake - the rectum re-stretches and the cycle restarts.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Macrogol daily</strong> at a maintenance dose (lower than disimpaction)</li>
  <li><strong>Continue for at least 6 months</strong>, longer if symptoms have been going on for a long time</li>
  <li><strong>Wean gradually</strong>, only after stools have been consistently soft and regular for 3+ months</li>
  <li>Macrogol is safe long-term. It is not absorbed. There is no rebound and no dependence.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Behavioural support during maintenance</h2>

<p>The medication does the work of softening, but the child also needs to relearn that pooing is safe and routine.</p>

<h3>Toilet sits</h3>
<ul>
  <li>5-10 minutes after each meal (the gastrocolic reflex makes this the easiest time)</li>
  <li>Feet supported - small footstool so the knees are higher than the hips</li>
  <li>Relax, don't strain</li>
  <li>Reward the sit, not the poo. Stickers, a chart, an activity afterwards.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Routine</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Same toilet times each day - after breakfast and after dinner is a common pattern</li>
  <li>Plenty of water through the day</li>
  <li>Reasonable fibre - fruit, vegetables, wholegrain bread, some legumes - but do not push high-fibre when the gut is impacted; it can make discomfort worse before disimpaction is achieved</li>
  <li>Movement - children who are physically active have less constipation</li>
</ul>

<h3>Reward charts</h3>
<p>Effective for children 3 and up. Reward sitting, drinking water, taking medicine - things they can control. Do not reward the poo itself, since they cannot always make it happen.</p>

<h2>What to avoid</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Telling the child off for soiling. They cannot feel it.</li>
  <li>Stopping medication as soon as things improve. Almost guarantees relapse.</li>
  <li>Excessive fibre (bran, psyllium) when the bowel is impacted - can worsen pain</li>
  <li>"Wait and see" - childhood constipation rarely fixes itself once established</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you suspect constipation - earlier is better than later</li>
  <li>Soiling - this is constipation until proven otherwise</li>
  <li>Blood with stools (small streaks of fresh blood are usual with anal fissures from hard stools, but always worth a check)</li>
  <li>Constipation since birth or in the first weeks of life - rule out Hirschsprung's disease</li>
  <li>Severe tummy pain or distension</li>
  <li>Treatment for several weeks with no improvement</li>
  <li>You are running out of patience or your child is becoming distressed - earlier intervention with proper guidance is much better than struggling alone</li>
</ul>

<h2>Special situations</h2>

<h3>Toilet training</h3>
<p>If constipation begins or worsens during toilet training, ease off the training pressure. Treat the constipation first. Resume toilet training when poos are reliably soft and pain-free.</p>

<h3>Coeliac disease</h3>
<p>Less commonly causes constipation than diarrhoea, but worth thinking about in any child with gut symptoms plus poor growth, low iron, or family history.</p>

<h3>School and daycare</h3>
<p>Some children will not poo away from home. Talk to teachers about toilet access, privacy, and quiet times. Many children improve significantly when they have a regular daytime poo at home and are willing to go at school later.</p>
