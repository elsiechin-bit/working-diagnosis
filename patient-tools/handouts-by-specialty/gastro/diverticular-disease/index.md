Diverticula are small pouches that can form in the wall of the colon, usually in the lower (sigmoid) part. They are extremely common - around half of NZ adults over 60 have them, often without knowing.&sup1; Most never cause problems. The condition becomes a problem when pouches inflame (diverticulitis) or bleed.

<h2>Three terms to know</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Diverticulosis</strong> - pouches present, no symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Diverticular disease</strong> - pouches causing some symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Diverticulitis</strong> - inflamed or infected pouches, sometimes with abscess or perforation</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why pouches form</h2>

<p>Long-term pressure inside the colon (often from low-fibre diet, constipation, straining) pushes weak spots in the wall outward. Risk factors:</p>

<ul>
<li>Aging</li>
<li>Low-fibre Western diet</li>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Obesity</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Some genetic predisposition</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p><strong>Diverticulosis</strong> - usually silent.</p>

<p><strong>Symptomatic diverticular disease:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Crampy lower abdominal pain, often left-sided</li>
<li>Bloating and wind</li>
<li>Constipation or alternating bowel habits</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Diverticulitis (acute flare):</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Persistent left lower abdominal pain (sometimes right-sided in some Asian populations)</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Tender abdomen, sometimes with a palpable lump</li>
<li>Change in bowel habit</li>
<li>Nausea, sometimes vomiting</li>
<li>Sometimes bleeding</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Complications:</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Abscess</li>
<li>Perforation (a hole in the bowel) - severe pain and unwell, surgical emergency</li>
<li>Fistula (a tunnel between bowel and bladder, vagina, or skin)</li>
<li>Stricture (narrowing) leading to obstruction</li>
<li>Diverticular bleeding - can be heavy and sudden</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Often picked up incidentally on:</p>

<ul>
<li>Colonoscopy (during bowel screening or other investigation)</li>
<li>CT scan</li>
<li>Sometimes barium enema</li>
</ul>

<p>For a suspected acute attack, your GP may order blood tests, abdominal imaging (CT scan), and admit if severe.</p>

<h2>Day-to-day management of diverticulosis</h2>

<p><strong>Eat more fibre.</strong> 25-30 g per day. Whole grains, vegetables, fruit (with skin), legumes, nuts. Aim to soften stools and reduce pressure.</p>

<p><strong>Drink water.</strong> 1.5-2 litres a day, especially with high fibre.</p>

<p><strong>Stay active.</strong> Reduces flare-ups.</p>

<p><strong>Manage weight.</strong> Excess weight increases risk.</p>

<p><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Reduces complications.</p>

<p><strong>Limit red and processed meat.</strong> Some studies link these with diverticulitis risk.</p>

<p>Old advice was to avoid nuts, seeds, popcorn, and corn. Recent evidence does NOT support this - they do not increase risk and most are healthy fibre sources.&sup2;</p>

<h2>Mild diverticulitis at home</h2>

<p>Some uncomplicated cases can be managed at home if you are well enough:</p>

<ul>
<li>Clear fluids for 24-48 hours</li>
<li>Slow reintroduction of soft, low-residue foods over 2-3 days</li>
<li>Build back to normal fibre over a week</li>
<li>Antibiotics in some cases (recent evidence suggests not all uncomplicated diverticulitis needs antibiotics, but most NZ practice still uses them)</li>
<li>Paracetamol for pain</li>
<li>Avoid NSAIDs - they may worsen complications</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms should improve within 2-3 days. If not, see your GP.</p>

<h2>When to seek urgent care</h2>

<p>Same-day GP or hospital for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Severe abdominal pain</li>
<li>Persistent vomiting</li>
<li>High fever (over 38.5°C)</li>
<li>Inability to keep fluids down</li>
<li>Significant rectal bleeding</li>
<li>Increasing abdominal swelling or rigidity</li>
<li>Signs of shock (very fast pulse, low blood pressure, faintness)</li>
</ul>

<p>Call <strong>111</strong> for severe pain with rigid abdomen and feeling very unwell - perforation is a surgical emergency.</p>

<h2>Hospital treatment</h2>

<p>Severe or complicated diverticulitis usually involves:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bowel rest (no food)</li>
<li>Intravenous fluids</li>
<li>Intravenous antibiotics</li>
<li>Drainage of abscesses (radiology-guided)</li>
<li>Surgery for perforation, stricture, fistula, or recurrent severe attacks</li>
</ul>

<h2>Surgery</h2>

<p>Reserved for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Perforation with peritonitis</li>
<li>Persistent abscess not responding to drainage</li>
<li>Fistula</li>
<li>Stricture causing obstruction</li>
<li>Multiple recurrent severe attacks (occasionally)</li>
<li>Persistent symptoms after recovery</li>
</ul>

<p>Most surgery removes the affected segment of colon (sigmoid colectomy). Sometimes a temporary colostomy is needed; usually reversed later.</p>

<h2>Diverticular bleeding</h2>

<p>Often sudden, painless, and large-volume - bright red or maroon blood. Usually settles on its own but needs hospital assessment. Investigations include colonoscopy, CT angiography, or sometimes intervention by interventional radiology.</p>

<h2>Long-term outlook</h2>

<ul>
<li>Most people with diverticulosis live their whole lives without problems</li>
<li>About 10-25% develop diverticulitis at some point</li>
<li>Of those who have one attack, about 1 in 3 has another</li>
<li>Most second attacks are no worse than the first</li>
<li>Diverticular disease does not increase bowel cancer risk</li>
</ul>
