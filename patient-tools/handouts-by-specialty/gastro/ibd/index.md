Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a group of autoimmune conditions where the immune system attacks the bowel wall. It is not the same as IBS (irritable bowel). The two main forms are Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis. Around 20,000 New Zealanders have IBD - one of the highest rates in the world.&sup1; With modern treatment, most people achieve long periods of remission and live full lives.

<h2>Crohn's disease</h2>

<ul>
<li>Can affect any part of the digestive tract from mouth to anus</li>
<li>Most often affects the end of the small bowel (terminal ileum) and large bowel</li>
<li>Inflammation goes through the full thickness of the bowel wall</li>
<li>Can have skip areas (normal bowel between affected sections)</li>
<li>Complications: strictures, fistulas, abscesses</li>
</ul>

<h2>Ulcerative colitis</h2>

<ul>
<li>Affects only the large bowel (colon and rectum)</li>
<li>Continuous inflammation starting from the rectum, sometimes spreading up</li>
<li>Inflammation only in the inner lining</li>
<li>Complications: severe colitis, toxic megacolon, increased bowel cancer risk after years of disease</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Both conditions:</p>

<ul>
<li>Diarrhoea, sometimes with blood (more common in UC)</li>
<li>Abdominal pain or cramping</li>
<li>Urgency and frequency</li>
<li>Weight loss</li>
<li>Fatigue</li>
<li>Fever during flares</li>
</ul>

<p>Crohn's specifically:</p>

<ul>
<li>Right lower abdominal pain</li>
<li>Mouth ulcers</li>
<li>Anal fissures or fistulas</li>
<li>Failure to thrive in children</li>
</ul>

<p>UC specifically:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bloody diarrhoea is the hallmark</li>
<li>Tenesmus (sense of incomplete emptying with strong urge)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms outside the gut</h2>

<p>Both conditions can affect other systems:</p>

<ul>
<li>Joint pain, especially large joints and lower back</li>
<li>Eye inflammation (uveitis, episcleritis)</li>
<li>Skin rashes (erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum)</li>
<li>Mouth ulcers</li>
<li>Liver inflammation (primary sclerosing cholangitis)</li>
<li>Anaemia, iron deficiency, B12 deficiency</li>
<li>Bone thinning (osteoporosis)</li>
<li>Higher cardiovascular risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<ul>
<li>Blood tests - inflammation markers (CRP), full blood count, iron, B12, folate</li>
<li>Faecal calprotectin - elevated in active inflammation; useful to monitor</li>
<li>Stool tests to exclude infection</li>
<li>Colonoscopy with biopsy - gold standard</li>
<li>Sometimes upper endoscopy and small bowel imaging (MR enterography)</li>
</ul>

<p>Diagnosis often takes months, especially if symptoms are mild.</p>

<h2>Treatment - induction</h2>

<p>For a flare, the aim is to bring inflammation down quickly:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Corticosteroids</strong> (oral prednisone, sometimes IV in hospital, or budesonide for ileal Crohn's) - effective for flares but unsuitable long-term</li>
<li><strong>5-aminosalicylates (5-ASAs)</strong> like mesalazine, sulfasalazine - mainstay for mild-moderate UC</li>
<li><strong>Topical treatments</strong> for proctitis - rectal foam, suppositories, enemas</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment - maintenance</h2>

<p>Long-term to prevent flares:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>5-ASAs</strong> (UC and some Crohn's)</li>
<li><strong>Immunomodulators</strong> - azathioprine, mercaptopurine, methotrexate</li>
<li><strong>Biologics</strong> - injections targeting specific immune signals:
  <ul>
  <li>Anti-TNF: infliximab (Remicade), adalimumab (Humira)</li>
  <li>Anti-integrin: vedolizumab (Entyvio)</li>
  <li>Anti-IL-12/23: ustekinumab (Stelara)</li>
  <li>JAK inhibitors: tofacitinib, upadacitinib (oral)</li>
  </ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>PHARMAC funds many biologics for moderate-severe IBD with specific criteria. Treatment is supervised by gastroenterologists.</p>

<h2>Surgery</h2>

<p><strong>For UC:</strong> total colectomy (removal of the colon) is curative for the bowel disease. About 1 in 5 with UC has surgery. Modern options include ileal pouch-anal anastomosis (J-pouch), preserving continence.</p>

<p><strong>For Crohn's:</strong> surgery is for complications (stricture, fistula, abscess) - it does not cure but resolves specific problems. Many people with Crohn's need surgery at some point.</p>

<h2>Daily living</h2>

<p><strong>Diet:</strong> No single diet works for everyone. Some people identify personal triggers. The Mediterranean diet is associated with better outcomes. During a flare, low-fibre and low-residue diets may help. A specialist dietitian is invaluable.</p>

<p><strong>Smoking:</strong> Strongly worsens Crohn's disease. Quit. Interestingly, smoking is associated with milder UC - but the cardiovascular harms of smoking far outweigh this and quitting is still recommended.</p>

<p><strong>Stress:</strong> Does not cause IBD but can trigger flares. Stress management helps.</p>

<p><strong>Exercise:</strong> Regular moderate exercise is beneficial.</p>

<p><strong>Vaccines:</strong> Stay up to date - especially flu, pneumococcal, COVID, hepatitis B, and Shingrix if on immunosuppressants.</p>

<p><strong>Bone health:</strong> Calcium, vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise; periodic bone density scans.</p>

<p><strong>Mental health:</strong> Anxiety and depression are common in IBD. Tell your team and seek support if struggling.</p>

<h2>Cancer surveillance</h2>

<p>People with longstanding extensive UC or Crohn's colitis (8+ years) have an increased risk of bowel cancer. Surveillance colonoscopy every 1-3 years is recommended.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Most women with IBD have normal pregnancies. Active disease at conception is the biggest risk factor. Plan pregnancy when in remission. Most IBD medications are safe in pregnancy (except methotrexate, which must be stopped). Specialist supervision through pregnancy.</p>

<h2>Children with IBD</h2>

<p>About 1 in 4 IBD diagnoses occur in childhood. Growth, development, and adolescence add complexity. Paediatric IBD teams provide specialist care. School support and family support matter.</p>
