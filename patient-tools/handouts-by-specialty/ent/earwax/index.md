<p>Earwax (cerumen) is a normal protective substance in your ear canal. Sometimes it accumulates and causes blockage, leading to hearing loss and discomfort. Earwax impaction is common and easily treated, but prevention and safe removal are important.</p>

<h2>What earwax impaction feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sudden hearing loss in one or both ears</li>
  <li>Ear fullness or plugged sensation</li>
  <li>Ear discomfort or itch</li>
  <li>Tinnitus (ringing in the ear)</li>
  <li>Ear drainage (if impacted wax is being pushed out)</li>
  <li>Cough (triggered by wax stimulating the vagus nerve)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why impaction happens</h2>

<p>Earwax is produced naturally and normally moves out of the ear canal. Impaction occurs when wax becomes too dry, accumulates faster than it clears, or is pushed deeper into the canal. Risk factors include using cotton buds (which push wax in), hearing aids, earphones, or naturally narrow ear canals. Older adults are at higher risk.</p>

<h2>Removing impacted wax - safe methods</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Do NOT use cotton buds.</strong> Cotton buds push wax deeper into the canal and can damage the eardrum. Avoid them completely.</li>
  <li><strong>Wax softening drops.</strong> Olive oil, almond oil, or commercial cerumenolytic drops softened impacted wax over days, making removal easier. Use twice daily for several days.</li>
  <li><strong>Ear irrigation (syringing).</strong> Your doctor can flush the ear canal with warm water to remove softened wax. This is safe, effective, and quick when done properly. Irrigation works best after wax has been softened with drops for several days.</li>
  <li><strong>Curette removal.</strong> A doctor can use a small curved instrument to gently scoop out wax under direct visualisation. This is safe when done by someone trained.</li>
  <li><strong>Suction.</strong> Some doctors use gentle suction to remove wax. This is safe and effective.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid home water or pressure methods.</strong> Using water pistols, high-pressure devices, or improvised methods risks eardrum damage. Leave removal to professionals.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing impaction</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Never put anything in your ear.</strong> Avoid cotton buds, hairpins, or other objects. The ear canal cleans itself.</li>
  <li><strong>Let wax exit naturally.</strong> The ear canal is self-cleaning. Earwax naturally migrates out.</li>
  <li><strong>If you have hearing aids or earphones, clean them regularly.</strong> Built-up debris on devices can push wax deeper.</li>
  <li><strong>If prone to impaction, use preventive drops monthly.</strong> Regular use of olive oil or cerumenolytic drops prevents buildup if you are susceptible.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage underlying ear conditions.</strong> If you have chronic ear drainage or inflammation, treat it to prevent wax accumulation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you have sudden hearing loss (wax impaction is a common cause and needs ruling out)</li>
  <li>If you have ear fullness not resolving with watchful waiting</li>
  <li>If home wax-softening drops do not help after one week</li>
  <li>If you have ear discomfort or drainage</li>
</ul>

<h2>What NOT to do</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Use cotton buds or any objects to clean your ear canal</li>
  <li>Use water or high-pressure methods to irrigate your own ear</li>
  <li>Try to remove impacted wax yourself with instruments</li>
  <li>Ignore sudden hearing loss hoping it resolves</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I have wax impaction?</li>
  <li>Should I use wax-softening drops first?</li>
  <li>Should you irrigate or remove it?</li>
  <li>What should I do to prevent this happening again?</li>
  <li>Should I avoid cotton buds and hearing aids?</li>
</ul>
