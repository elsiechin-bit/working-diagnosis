<p>Acute otitis media (middle ear infection) is one of the most common infections in young children. Most ear infections are caused by viruses and resolve on their own within a few days. This handout explains what causes ear infections, what helps, and when antibiotics are needed.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Ear pain (often worse at night)</li>
  <li>Tugging or pulling at the ear</li>
  <li>Fever</li>
  <li>Difficulty sleeping</li>
  <li>Temporary hearing loss</li>
  <li>Sometimes fluid or pus draining from the ear if the eardrum ruptures</li>
  <li>In young children, general irritability and poor feeding</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Ear infections occur when fluid builds up in the middle ear behind the eardrum, usually following a cold. The Eustachian tube (which normally drains fluid from the middle ear) becomes swollen or blocked. Most ear infections are caused by viruses and resolve without antibiotics. Some are caused by bacteria and may need treatment.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen (in age-appropriate doses) effectively relieves pain and fever. This is the main treatment for most ear infections.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep the ear dry.</strong> If fluid is draining, dry the ear gently without pushing into the canal.</li>
  <li><strong>Warm compression.</strong> A warm (not hot) flannel held against the ear can help comfort pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Position during sleep.</strong> Elevating the head slightly may help fluid drain and reduce discomfort.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid smoke and allergens.</strong> These can worsen Eustachian tube inflammation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Antibiotics</h2>

<p>Most ear infections are caused by viruses and do not need antibiotics. Antibiotics are considered when infection is likely bacterial (severe pain, high fever, discharge from the ear, signs of serious illness) or if symptoms are not improving after a few days. Your GP will decide if antibiotics are needed based on your child's age, symptoms, and examination findings.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your child has ear pain and fever</li>
  <li>Fluid or pus is draining from the ear</li>
  <li>Pain is severe or your child is very unwell</li>
  <li>Your child is very young (under 6 months) with fever</li>
  <li>Symptoms are not improving after 3 days</li>
  <li>Your child has recurrent ear infections (more than 3 in 6 months)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Recurrent ear infections</h2>

<p>Some children have repeated ear infections. This is common in young children whose Eustachian tubes are still developing. Recurrent infections usually become less frequent as children grow. Your GP can advise on management if your child has recurrent infections.</p>

<h2>Complications</h2>

<p>If the pressure from fluid ruptures the eardrum, you may see fluid or blood draining from the ear. The eardrum usually heals within a few weeks. Persistent hearing loss from fluid in the ear (glue ear) is different from acute infection and is discussed separately.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Does my child need antibiotics?</li>
  <li>What pain relief can I use safely?</li>
  <li>How long will it take to get better?</li>
  <li>When should I bring them back if not improving?</li>
  <li>Can anything prevent ear infections?</li>
</ul>
