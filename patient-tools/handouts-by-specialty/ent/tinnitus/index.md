<p>Tinnitus is the perception of sound (ringing, buzzing, hissing) when no external sound is present. It is very common and usually not serious, but can be distressing and affect quality of life. Understanding what causes it and learning coping strategies helps most people manage tinnitus well.</p>

<h2>What tinnitus is and common causes</h2>

<p>Tinnitus is not a disease but a symptom originating in the ear or brain. Common causes include age-related hearing loss, noise exposure, ear wax impaction, infections, head injury, or certain medicines. Sometimes no obvious cause is found. Most tinnitus is inner ear dysfunction rather than anything dangerous, but checking with your doctor is important to rule out treatable causes like wax impaction or infection.</p>

<h2>What it feels like and how it affects you</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Constant or intermittent ringing, buzzing, hissing, roaring, or whistling sound</li>
  <li>Sound in one ear or both ears</li>
  <li>Sound that fluctuates in loudness</li>
  <li>Sleep disruption - tinnitus often more noticeable at night in quiet environments</li>
  <li>Difficulty concentrating or focusing</li>
  <li>Anxiety or frustration about the sound</li>
  <li>Worsening with stress, fatigue, or stimulants like caffeine</li>
</ul>

<h2>What actually helps - coping and habituation</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Sound therapy and masking.</strong> Background noise helps most people tolerate tinnitus. Try white noise, nature sounds, or quiet music. Many people sleep better with ambient sound. Hearing aids often provide relief even if hearing is near-normal.</li>
  <li><strong>Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT).</strong> These evidence-based therapies help your brain habituate to the sound so it becomes less noticeable. CBT addresses distress and anxiety about tinnitus; TRT combines this with sound therapy.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce avoidance of sound.</strong> Quiet environments can worsen tinnitus perception. Rather than seeking silence, maintain normal activities and background sound.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress and sleep.</strong> Stress and fatigue worsen tinnitus. Relaxation techniques, exercise, good sleep hygiene, and stress management help. Addressing sleep problems is particularly important.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce caffeine and stimulants.</strong> Caffeine, energy drinks, and stimulants can worsen tinnitus in some people. Try reducing intake to see if it helps.</li>
  <li><strong>Address hearing loss if present.</strong> Hearing aids significantly help many people with tinnitus, particularly those with hearing loss alongside tinnitus.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid sudden loud noise.</strong> Protect your ears in noisy environments with earplugs. Noise exposure can worsen tinnitus.</li>
  <li><strong>Join a support group.</strong> Connecting with others who have tinnitus provides practical strategies and reassurance that you are not alone.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Medicines - limited effectiveness but worth discussing</h2>

<p>No medicine consistently cures tinnitus. Some people find relief with certain drugs targeting anxiety or sleep, but evidence is limited. Ginkgo biloba is sometimes tried but evidence is weak. Your doctor may discuss options, but do not expect a cure from medicines. Psychological approaches are more evidence-based.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If tinnitus is new and bothersome</li>
  <li>If tinnitus is unilateral (one ear only) - this needs assessment to rule out rare causes</li>
  <li>If tinnitus is accompanied by hearing loss or dizziness</li>
  <li>If tinnitus suddenly worsens</li>
  <li>If your ear has obvious wax impaction or discharge</li>
  <li>If tinnitus is severely affecting your sleep or quality of life</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>What is causing my tinnitus?</li>
  <li>Do I have hearing loss that needs addressing?</li>
  <li>Should I have a hearing assessment?</li>
  <li>Would hearing aids help?</li>
  <li>Is CBT or TRT available to me?</li>
  <li>Are there medicines worth trying?</li>
  <li>Should I see an ear, nose, and throat (ENT) specialist?</li>
</ul>
