<p>The prostate is a walnut-sized gland that sits under the bladder and wraps around the top of the urethra (the tube that carries urine out of the body). It makes part of the fluid in semen. Most men over 50 will have some change to their prostate at some point, and most of these changes are not cancer. This handout is here to help you think clearly about three separate questions: what your prostate is doing, when symptoms are worth investigating, and whether to have a PSA blood test.</p>

<h2>What the prostate does, and what changes with age</h2>

<p>The prostate gradually enlarges in most men from middle age onward. This benign enlargement (called BPH, or benign prostatic hyperplasia) is the most common reason for prostate-related symptoms in NZ general practice. It is not cancer and does not turn into cancer. Common symptoms include a slower urinary stream, having to go more often (especially at night), urgency, dribbling at the end, or feeling like the bladder has not fully emptied.</p>

<p>Prostatitis (inflammation or infection of the prostate) is a separate condition that can cause pelvic or perineal pain, painful urination, or fever. It is treatable.</p>

<h2>Prostate cancer in New Zealand</h2>

<p>Prostate cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in NZ men. Around 4,000 men are diagnosed each year, and about 700 die from it.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> The lifetime risk is roughly 1 in 8.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> Maori men are diagnosed at lower rates than non-Maori but tend to have worse outcomes once diagnosed, partly because of later presentation and access barriers.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup></p>

<p>The complication is that prostate cancer behaves very differently from man to man. Many prostate cancers grow so slowly that they would never have caused harm in a person's lifetime; some are aggressive and spread quickly. The PSA test cannot reliably tell these apart on its own.</p>

<h2>The PSA test: what it can and cannot tell you</h2>

<p>PSA (prostate-specific antigen) is a protein made by prostate cells. It is measured by a blood test. PSA goes up with prostate cancer, but it also goes up with BPH, prostate infection, recent ejaculation, vigorous cycling, urinary catheterisation, and a digital rectal exam. A raised PSA does not mean cancer; a normal PSA does not rule it out.</p>

<p>New Zealand does not have a national prostate cancer screening programme, because the evidence does not show that screening healthy men with PSA reduces overall mortality enough to outweigh the harms of overdiagnosis and overtreatment.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> Instead, NZ guidance is built around a shared decision: men aged 50 to 70 (or earlier if there is a family history or Maori ethnicity) should be offered an informed conversation about the pros and cons before testing.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup></p>

<h2>What to weigh up</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>What testing can offer.</strong> Earlier detection of some aggressive cancers, while curative treatment is still possible.</li>
  <li><strong>What testing risks.</strong> A raised PSA often leads to further tests (MRI, biopsy) that themselves carry risks. Treatment of slow-growing cancer can cause incontinence, erectile dysfunction, and bowel changes that the cancer itself might never have caused.</li>
  <li><strong>Family and ethnicity.</strong> A first-degree relative with prostate cancer, or being Maori, raises the threshold for a more proactive conversation.</li>
  <li><strong>Your own preference.</strong> Some men strongly prefer to know; others strongly prefer to avoid the cascade of investigations a slightly raised PSA can trigger. Both are reasonable.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor regardless of PSA</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Persistent change in urinary stream, urgency, or frequency that bothers you</li>
  <li>Blood in the urine or semen</li>
  <li>Pain on urinating, fever, or perineal pain</li>
  <li>New bone pain, especially in the back, hips, or pelvis</li>
  <li>Unexplained weight loss or persistent fatigue</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Given my age, ethnicity, and family history, what is my risk?</li>
  <li>If we test and the PSA is raised, what would the next steps actually involve?</li>
  <li>What is the chance a raised PSA leads to treatment I might not have needed?</li>
  <li>Can we agree a plan for repeat testing if we go ahead?</li>
</ul>
