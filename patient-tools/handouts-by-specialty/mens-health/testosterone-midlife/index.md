<p>Testosterone naturally declines with age. The marketing around "low T", testosterone clinics, and at-home gels has run a long way ahead of the evidence. The medical reality is narrower and more useful: a small number of men have genuinely low testosterone with a clear cause, and they benefit from properly supervised replacement. The much larger number of men with fatigue, low libido, weight gain, or mood changes in midlife are usually being affected by something other than testosterone, and replacement is unlikely to help them.</p>

<h2>What normal looks like</h2>

<p>Testosterone is the main male sex hormone, made in the testicles, regulated by the pituitary gland. Levels peak in your twenties, plateau through your thirties, and then drift down by roughly 1 percent a year from around age 30.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> A 60-year-old man typically has a lower testosterone level than a 30-year-old, and that does not mean he has a disease.</p>

<p>The reference range NZ laboratories use for total testosterone in adult men is roughly 8 to 30 nmol/L, though it varies by lab and assay. Levels need to be measured in the morning, ideally between 7am and 11am, and ideally on two separate days, because testosterone fluctuates substantially within a single day and from day to day.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup></p>

<h2>When low testosterone is real</h2>

<p>Clinical hypogonadism is consistently low testosterone with symptoms, in a man with an identifiable cause. Causes include pituitary problems, previous testicular injury or surgery, certain genetic conditions, long-term opioid or steroid use, and chronic systemic illness. The diagnosis requires both:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Clear symptoms (low libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of morning erections, reduced muscle mass, persistent fatigue, low mood)</li>
  <li>Two morning total testosterone levels confirmed below the lab's reference range, with appropriate follow-up tests (LH, FSH, SHBG, prolactin) to identify the cause</li>
</ul>

<p>In NZ, PHARMAC funds testosterone replacement only when these criteria are met and with specialist input.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> Replacement is otherwise unfunded and costs $40 to $80 a month from a pharmacy.</p>

<h2>What "low T" marketing gets wrong</h2>

<p>Symptoms that get attributed to testosterone in midlife - tiredness, weight gain, low mood, reduced libido, lower confidence - overlap heavily with the symptoms of poor sleep, untreated obstructive sleep apnoea, depression, alcohol use, lack of exercise, and the predictable fatigue of running a household with young children or working long hours. These are also vastly more common than actual hypogonadism.</p>

<p>Several large randomised trials have looked at testosterone replacement in men with low-normal levels and non-specific symptoms. The benefits are modest at best - small improvements in sexual function, no meaningful change in mood, vitality, or cognition.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn4">4</a></sup> The benefits are dwarfed by the cost, the inconvenience, and the side effects of long-term replacement in men who do not actually need it.</p>

<h2>Risks of replacement</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Erythrocytosis.</strong> Raised red cell count, which can thicken the blood and increase the risk of clots. Routine monitoring is required.</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular events.</strong> The data is mixed; modern trials suggest replacement is broadly safe in well-selected men with confirmed deficiency, but it is not benign in men whose levels are normal.</li>
  <li><strong>Suppression of natural production and fertility.</strong> Testosterone replacement shuts down the testes' own production. Men hoping to father children should not start replacement without a fertility plan.</li>
  <li><strong>Effects on the prostate.</strong> Replacement does not cause prostate cancer but can accelerate the growth of any pre-existing cancer; baseline PSA is part of a proper assessment.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The proper workup</h2>

<p>If you and your GP decide testosterone is worth checking, the path is:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Two morning fasting samples for total testosterone, on different days</li>
  <li>If consistently low, additional tests: LH, FSH, SHBG, prolactin, ferritin, and HbA1c at minimum</li>
  <li>Consideration of weight, sleep, alcohol, medicines, and depression as alternative explanations</li>
  <li>Specialist (endocrinology or urology) input before starting replacement</li>
</ul>

<p>Direct-to-consumer testosterone clinics that prescribe after a phone consultation and a single home finger-prick test are not following accepted standards in NZ, and the long-term consequences sit with you, not them.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Persistent low libido and erectile difficulties not explained by relationship or medication factors</li>
  <li>Loss of morning erections, with reduced muscle mass and persistent unrefreshing tiredness</li>
  <li>Reduced sexual hair growth, breast tissue development, or testicular shrinkage</li>
  <li>Wanting an honest conversation about whether your symptoms are likely to respond to testosterone</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Are my symptoms more likely to be from low testosterone, sleep, mood, or lifestyle?</li>
  <li>If we test, what level would actually count as low for me?</li>
  <li>What else should we check before starting any treatment?</li>
  <li>If I do start replacement, what monitoring does that commit me to?</li>
</ul>
