<p>Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is the leading cause of irreversible vision loss in New Zealanders over 50.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup> It affects the macula - the small central part of the retina that gives you sharp, detailed vision. It does not affect peripheral vision, which is why people with AMD do not usually go fully blind, but it does affect the things that depend on central vision: reading, driving, recognising faces. The good news is that we now have effective treatment for one of the two main types, and useful steps anyone can take to slow the other.</p>

<h2>The two types</h2>

<p>About 90 percent of AMD is dry (atrophic) AMD. It progresses slowly over years, with the macula gradually thinning and small yellow deposits called drusen appearing under the retina. Vision worsens gradually.</p>

<p>The remaining 10 percent or so is wet (neovascular) AMD. Abnormal blood vessels grow under the macula and leak fluid or blood. Vision can change over weeks or even days. Wet AMD is the form that responds to treatment and the form to recognise quickly.</p>

<h2>What you might notice</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Gradual blurring of central vision; reading getting harder, especially in dim light</li>
  <li>Straight lines (door frames, lampposts) appearing wavy or bent</li>
  <li>A blurred or grey patch in the middle of your vision</li>
  <li>Colours looking less vivid</li>
  <li>Needing more light than you used to for fine tasks</li>
</ul>

<p>Sudden distortion (lines that were straight yesterday and are wavy today) is the symptom that should send you to an optometrist or eye clinic this week, not next month - it is the classic presenting feature of wet AMD, and the earlier wet AMD is treated, the more vision is preserved.</p>

<h2>Self-monitoring with an Amsler grid</h2>

<p>An Amsler grid is a simple chart of squares with a central dot. If you have early AMD, your optometrist will usually give you one and ask you to check each eye separately, once a week, with reading glasses on. Any new wave, blank, or distortion in the grid lines is a reason to be seen.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stop smoking.</strong> Smoking is the single largest modifiable risk factor for AMD - smokers develop AMD earlier and progress faster. Even decades-long smokers benefit from quitting. Free support is available through Quitline (0800 778 778).</li>
  <li><strong>Eat for your eyes.</strong> Diets rich in dark leafy greens, oily fish, and a wide range of coloured fruit and vegetables are associated with slower progression. The Mediterranean dietary pattern has the strongest evidence.</li>
  <li><strong>Protect from UV and blue light.</strong> Wear sunglasses outdoors, especially in NZ summer. UV exposure accumulates over a lifetime.</li>
  <li><strong>AREDS2 vitamin supplements.</strong> A specific combination (vitamins C and E, lutein, zeaxanthin, zinc, copper) reduces the risk of progression from intermediate to advanced AMD by about 25 percent over five years.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup> Useful only at the intermediate stage and not for prevention - your optometrist or ophthalmologist will advise whether AREDS2 is appropriate for you.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage cardiovascular risk factors.</strong> High blood pressure and high cholesterol may contribute. Treating them is good for the eyes as well as the heart.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment for wet AMD</h2>

<p>Wet AMD is treated with anti-VEGF injections into the eye - aflibercept or ranibizumab in NZ.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn3">3</a></sup> These are funded for confirmed wet AMD and are given monthly initially, then less often as the condition stabilises. The injection itself takes seconds and is much more comfortable than it sounds. Most people maintain or improve their vision while on treatment, but treatment is ongoing - stopping usually means relapse.</p>

<h2>When to see your optometrist or doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sudden new distortion of straight lines, especially in one eye</li>
  <li>A new dark or blurred patch in your central vision</li>
  <li>Significant change on your Amsler grid</li>
  <li>You are over 50 and have not had an eye check in the last two years</li>
  <li>You are over 65 and have a family history of AMD</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your eye specialist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I have early, intermediate, or advanced AMD - and which type?</li>
  <li>Should I be taking AREDS2 supplements?</li>
  <li>How often should I have my eyes checked from now on?</li>
  <li>What changes mean I should be seen urgently?</li>
</ul>
