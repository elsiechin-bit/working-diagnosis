<p>Dry eye is one of the most common reasons people come to general practice and to optometrists with an eye complaint. It is rarely dangerous to your sight, but it can be relentlessly uncomfortable and is often dismissed as trivial. The condition has two main mechanisms that often coexist, and matching the treatment to the cause is what turns a frustrating problem into a manageable one. Most people get substantial relief from a stepwise plan that takes a few weeks to work.</p>

<h2>The two main types</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Evaporative dry eye.</strong> The tears evaporate too quickly because the oily layer of the tear film is poor. This is usually caused by meibomian gland dysfunction (MGD) - the tiny oil glands along the lid margins are blocked or producing poor-quality oil. By far the more common type.</li>
  <li><strong>Aqueous-deficient dry eye.</strong> The lacrimal glands do not produce enough watery tear. Less common, but the type to think about in autoimmune conditions like Sjogren's syndrome and rheumatoid arthritis.</li>
</ul>

<p>Many people have a mix of both.</p>

<h2>What you might notice</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Gritty, sandy, or "something in the eye" sensation</li>
  <li>Burning or stinging that comes and goes through the day</li>
  <li>Blurred vision that clears with blinking or a few seconds of staring</li>
  <li>Eyes that paradoxically water - the surface gets so irritated it triggers a reflex tear flood</li>
  <li>Eyes that feel tired by the afternoon, or worse on screens, in air-conditioned offices, or on planes</li>
  <li>Difficulty wearing contact lenses for as long as you used to</li>
  <li>Crusting or sticky eyelids in the morning (suggesting blepharitis as well)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why it happens</h2>

<p>Common contributors include age (especially over 50), being female (hormonal contributions, particularly post-menopause), long hours on screens (we blink less when concentrating), contact lens wear, certain medicines (antihistamines, antidepressants, beta-blockers, diuretics, isotretinoin), and the dry, windy climate that big parts of NZ have for much of the year. Underlying medical conditions worth considering include thyroid eye disease, rheumatoid arthritis, Sjogren's syndrome, and rosacea.</p>

<h2>The stepwise plan</h2>

<p>Most people benefit from working through these in order over a few weeks before deciding what is helping.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Take screen breaks.</strong> The 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes of close work, look at something 20 feet (6 metres) away for 20 seconds. Force yourself to blink fully a few times.</li>
  <li><strong>Warm compresses, daily, for MGD.</strong> A warm flannel or commercial heat mask over closed eyelids for 5 to 10 minutes, followed by gentle massage along the lid margins. This is the single most effective home treatment for the evaporative type, but only if done consistently for at least four to six weeks.</li>
  <li><strong>Lid hygiene.</strong> If your lids are crusted or red along the margin, gentle cleaning with a commercial lid wipe or diluted baby shampoo on a cotton bud, once a day, treats blepharitis that often coexists with MGD.</li>
  <li><strong>Lubricant drops.</strong> Available over the counter in NZ pharmacies. For occasional use, any preserved drop is fine. For more than four times a day, switch to a preservative-free product (single-dose vials or a multidose preservative-free bottle) - long-term use of preserved drops can itself irritate the surface.</li>
  <li><strong>Lubricant gel or ointment overnight.</strong> Useful if your eyes are sticky in the morning or if you sleep with your eyes slightly open.</li>
  <li><strong>Workspace and home setup.</strong> Lower your screen so you look slightly down at it (less eye surface exposed). Use a humidifier if your indoor air is dry. Avoid sitting under a fan or air conditioner that blows directly on your face. Wear wraparound sunglasses outdoors on windy or sunny days.</li>
  <li><strong>Hydrate, and consider omega-3.</strong> Adequate water intake helps mildly. Omega-3 supplements have modest evidence for evaporative dry eye, with effects taking three months to appear.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn1">1</a></sup></li>
</ul>

<h2>When over-the-counter is not enough</h2>

<p>Your GP or optometrist can step things up. Options include prescription drops (ciclosporin emulsion or short courses of topical steroids for inflammation, both unfunded in NZ for dry eye), in-office procedures for MGD (warm device-based therapies, lid expression), punctal plugs to reduce tear drainage, and treatment of underlying conditions like rosacea.<sup class="fnref"><a href="#fn2">2</a></sup></p>

<h2>When to see your doctor or optometrist</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms not improving after six weeks of consistent home measures</li>
  <li>Vision is changing as well as the eye feeling dry</li>
  <li>Pain rather than discomfort, or significant redness in one eye</li>
  <li>Joint pain, dry mouth, or fatigue alongside dry eye - autoimmune conditions worth investigating</li>
  <li>Recent change of medication that lines up with worsening dry eye</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Does this look more like the evaporative or the aqueous type?</li>
  <li>Could any of my current medicines be contributing?</li>
  <li>Should we test for an underlying cause like Sjogren's?</li>
  <li>If lubricants alone are not enough, what is the next step here in NZ?</li>
</ul>
