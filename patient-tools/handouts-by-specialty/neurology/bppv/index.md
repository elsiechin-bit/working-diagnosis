<p>Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) is a common cause of brief, severe dizziness triggered by head position changes. While frightening, BPPV is not serious and responds well to specific head manoeuvres. Most people recover completely with appropriate treatment.</p>

<h2>What happens in BPPV</h2>

<p>BPPV occurs when tiny crystals (calcium carbonate) become dislodged in the inner ear balance system. When you move your head in certain directions (lying down, rolling over, looking up), these crystals move, triggering brief spinning sensation. The spells are short (usually 30 seconds to a few minutes) but intense. BPPV usually follows head injury, but sometimes develops without clear cause, especially in older people.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Sudden, severe spinning sensation triggered by head movement</li>
  <li>Typically brief episodes lasting seconds to minutes, not hours</li>
  <li>Often triggered by rolling over in bed, getting up, tilting head back, or bending forward</li>
  <li>Can be accompanied by nausea or vomiting</li>
  <li>Episodes become less intense with repeated movements (fatigue effect)</li>
  <li>Nausea and balance problems may persist after the spinning stops</li>
  <li>Between episodes you feel fine</li>
</ul>

<h2>Repositioning manoeuvres - these work</h2>

<p>The Epley manoeuvre (or other repositioning manoeuvres) works by moving your head through specific positions to shift the crystals back into their normal place. A physiotherapist or doctor can teach you this manoeuvre. Some people do it themselves at home after instruction. Success rates are high - many people are better after one or two sessions. If done correctly, you may experience brief dizziness during the manoeuvre (this is normal and necessary), then improvement follows.</p>

<h2>What actually helps during BPPV</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Get the right manoeuvre done.</strong> Your doctor will determine which type of BPPV you have (posterior canal is most common) and teach you the appropriate manoeuvre (Epley, Semont, or another).</li>
  <li><strong>Learn the manoeuvre properly.</strong> Incorrect technique reduces effectiveness. Get clear instruction and practice under guidance.</li>
  <li><strong>Do it repeatedly if needed.</strong> If one session does not work completely, repeating the manoeuvre often helps. Most people need 1-2 sessions.</li>
  <li><strong>Do not fear the dizziness during treatment.</strong> Brief dizziness during the manoeuvre means you are treating the problem. It does not mean something is wrong.</li>
  <li><strong>Reassurance and rest.</strong> Between manoeuvres, rest when needed. Avoid the triggering positions temporarily, but do not completely immobilise your head.</li>
  <li><strong>Address nausea.</strong> Anti-nausea medicines (prochlorperazine) help if nausea is severe, but work in parallel with repositioning manoeuvres, not instead of them.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Recovery and preventing recurrence</h2>

<p>Most people recover completely with appropriate manoeuvres. Some get episodes again weeks or months later - if so, the manoeuvre works again. There is no reliable way to prevent recurrence, but some evidence suggests exercises to improve balance and inner ear compensation may help. Once you have had BPPV, ask your doctor for home exercises to do regularly.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you develop sudden positional dizziness</li>
  <li>If spinning episodes are severe or last longer than a few minutes</li>
  <li>If dizziness is accompanied by other neurological symptoms (facial weakness, slurred speech, numbness)</li>
  <li>If dizziness does not improve after repositioning manoeuvres</li>
  <li>If you need teaching the home manoeuvre</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to seek urgent care</h2>

<p>Most positional dizziness is BPPV, but seek urgent care if you have sudden dizziness with facial weakness, slurred speech, severe headache, loss of consciousness, or difficulty walking. These suggest other causes needing urgent assessment.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I have BPPV, or could it be something else?</li>
  <li>Which manoeuvre should I do?</li>
  <li>Should I see a physiotherapist?</li>
  <li>Can I do the manoeuvre at home?</li>
  <li>What positions should I avoid?</li>
  <li>How often should I repeat the manoeuvre?</li>
  <li>Are there exercises to prevent recurrence?</li>
</ul>
