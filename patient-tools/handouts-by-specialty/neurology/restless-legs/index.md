<p>Restless legs syndrome (RLS) is a condition causing an irresistible urge to move your legs, typically in the evening or at night. It is often accompanied by uncomfortable sensations. While not dangerous, RLS can significantly disrupt sleep and quality of life. Many treatments help improve symptoms.</p>

<h2>What it feels like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Strong urge to move legs, especially in the evening and at night</li>
  <li>Uncomfortable sensations described as creeping, crawling, tingling, or aching</li>
  <li>Symptoms improve temporarily with movement - walking, stretching, or rubbing helps</li>
  <li>Symptoms worsen when sitting or lying still</li>
  <li>Sleep disruption - difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep</li>
  <li>Daytime fatigue from poor sleep quality</li>
  <li>Symptoms often worse with stress, caffeine, or alcohol</li>
</ul>

<h2>Primary RLS vs secondary causes</h2>

<p>Primary RLS has no obvious cause and runs in families. Secondary RLS can be caused by iron deficiency, kidney disease, pregnancy, diabetes, or certain medicines. Your doctor will check for iron deficiency and other treatable causes. Identifying and treating secondary causes often improves symptoms significantly.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Check your iron levels.</strong> Low iron (ferritin below 75) worsens RLS. Iron supplementation helps even if your general haemoglobin is normal. Ask your doctor to measure ferritin specifically.</li>
  <li><strong>Avoid triggers.</strong> Reduce or eliminate caffeine and alcohol, especially in the afternoon and evening. These worsen symptoms. Some people are sensitive to other medicines - discuss with your doctor if medicines might be contributing.</li>
  <li><strong>Use non-medicine strategies.</strong> Stretching, massage, heat application, and exercise during the day (but not close to bedtime) help some people. Keeping your legs warm or cool (whichever feels better) provides relief.</li>
  <li><strong>Establish a sleep routine.</strong> Good sleep hygiene helps - cool, dark bedroom, consistent bedtime, avoiding screens before bed. Sleep deprivation worsens RLS symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Move before bed.</strong> A walk or gentle exercise in the afternoon can reduce evening symptoms. However, do not exercise too close to bedtime as it can interfere with sleep.</li>
  <li><strong>Try medicines if non-medicine measures do not help.</strong> Medicines include dopamine agonists (ropinirole, pramipexole), which are first-line for RLS. Alternatively, gabapentin or pregabalin help some people. Low-dose opioids are occasionally used if other medicines do not work. Your doctor will choose based on your symptoms and other health conditions.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage augmentation.</strong> Some people find dopamine agonists gradually become less effective (augmentation). Switching medicines or adjusting doses helps.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Things to avoid</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Caffeine and strong stimulants - especially after mid-afternoon</li>
  <li>Alcohol and sedating antihistamines - these worsen RLS even though they might seem to help sleep</li>
  <li>Sedentary lifestyle - lack of activity worsens symptoms</li>
  <li>Over-the-counter cold and allergy medicines containing antihistamines - these worsen RLS</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you have symptoms suggestive of RLS affecting your sleep</li>
  <li>To check iron levels and ferritin specifically</li>
  <li>To rule out secondary causes (kidney disease, diabetes, medicines)</li>
  <li>To discuss medicines if non-medicine strategies do not help</li>
  <li>If medicines become less effective (augmentation)</li>
  <li>If symptoms worsen or new symptoms develop</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Could I have RLS, and what tests do I need?</li>
  <li>What is my iron level and ferritin?</li>
  <li>Should I try iron supplementation?</li>
  <li>Are any of my current medicines making RLS worse?</li>
  <li>What medicines are available if non-medicine strategies do not help?</li>
  <li>How will we know if the medicine is working?</li>
</ul>
