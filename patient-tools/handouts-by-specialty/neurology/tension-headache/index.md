<p>Tension headaches are the most common type of headache. They cause a dull, pressing sensation around the head or neck, often triggered by stress or muscle tension. While uncomfortable, they are not dangerous and respond well to simple management strategies.</p>

<h2>What they feel like</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Dull, pressing pain (not throbbing)</li>
  <li>Sensation of a tight band around the head</li>
  <li>Pain in the neck, shoulders, or jaw</li>
  <li>Usually affects both sides of the head</li>
  <li>No nausea or vomiting (unlike migraines)</li>
  <li>Not made worse by activity</li>
  <li>Can last from 30 minutes to several hours</li>
  <li>Frequent tension headaches (15 or more per month) constitute chronic tension-type headaches</li>
</ul>

<h2>Why they happen</h2>

<p>Tension headaches are caused by sustained muscle tension, usually in response to stress, poor posture, or anxiety. The muscles of the neck, shoulders, and scalp contract and stay tight, restricting blood flow and causing pain.</p>

<h2>What actually helps</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Pain relief.</strong> Paracetamol or ibuprofen effectively relieves tension headaches when taken early.</li>
  <li><strong>Relax tense muscles.</strong> Heat to the neck or shoulders (heat pad, warm shower) relaxes muscle tension and reduces pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Neck and shoulder stretches.</strong> Gentle stretching of the neck and shoulders helps release tightness. Hold stretches for 30 seconds, several times daily.</li>
  <li><strong>Improve posture.</strong> Poor posture (slouching, hunching over a desk) contributes to tension. Keep your shoulders relaxed and back, look straight ahead rather than down.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress.</strong> Since stress is a primary trigger, stress reduction techniques (deep breathing, meditation, exercise, regular breaks) prevent headaches.</li>
  <li><strong>Regular exercise.</strong> Physical activity reduces muscle tension and stress, preventing headaches.</li>
  <li><strong>Take regular breaks.</strong> If you work at a desk, take breaks every 30 - 60 minutes to stretch and move.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Preventing tension headaches</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Identify your triggers.</strong> Common triggers include stress, poor sleep, skipping meals, and poor posture. Once identified, you can work to avoid or manage them.</li>
  <li><strong>Maintain good sleep.</strong> Consistent, adequate sleep reduces headache frequency.</li>
  <li><strong>Stay hydrated and eat regularly.</strong> Dehydration and hunger trigger tension headaches.</li>
  <li><strong>Manage stress actively.</strong> Regular exercise, relaxation practices, or counselling reduce baseline stress and tension.</li>
  <li><strong>Adjust your workspace.</strong> Ensure your monitor is at eye level, chair supports your back, and you have a supportive desk setup.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When tension headaches become chronic</h2>

<p>If you have tension headaches 15 or more days per month, preventive approaches become important. Chronic tension headaches respond well to antidepressants (amitriptyline) taken at low doses. Regular exercise and stress management are equally important as medication.</p>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Your tension headaches are frequent or getting worse</li>
  <li>The pain pattern has changed</li>
  <li>You are taking pain relief more than 2 - 3 times per week (overuse can worsen headaches)</li>
  <li>You want preventive treatment for chronic tension headaches</li>
  <li>Your headache is accompanied by fever, vision changes, or other symptoms</li>
</ul>

<h2>Headache rebound from overuse of pain relief</h2>

<p>Taking pain relief too frequently (more than 2 - 3 days per week) can paradoxically cause more headaches. If you are using pain relief frequently, talk to your doctor about reducing use gradually and using preventive strategies instead.</p>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Are these definitely tension headaches?</li>
  <li>What are my personal triggers?</li>
  <li>Should I take preventive medicine?</li>
  <li>Is my posture contributing to my headaches?</li>
  <li>What stress management techniques might help?</li>
</ul>
