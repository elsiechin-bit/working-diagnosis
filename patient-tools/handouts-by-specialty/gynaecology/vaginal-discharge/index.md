<p>Vaginal discharge is normal. Almost every person with a vagina has it from puberty to menopause. The amount, colour, and consistency change through the menstrual cycle, with hormonal contraception, in pregnancy, and around menopause. Knowing what is normal for you is the most useful starting point - because it makes changes easier to notice. This handout covers the patterns, the most common causes of changed discharge, and when to see your GP or sexual health clinic.</p>

<h2>Normal discharge</h2>

<p>The vagina cleans itself by producing fluid that carries away dead cells and bacteria. Normal discharge:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Is clear or white</li>
  <li>Has a mild, faintly acidic smell - not offensive</li>
  <li>Varies through the cycle - thinner and stretchy around ovulation (cervical mucus changes), creamier in the second half</li>
  <li>Increases with sexual arousal</li>
  <li>Is more abundant in pregnancy</li>
  <li>Is reduced in lower-oestrogen states - perimenopause, after menopause, on some hormonal contraceptives</li>
  <li>Does not cause itching, burning, or irritation</li>
</ul>

<p>The vagina has its own balanced ecosystem - mostly Lactobacillus species - that keeps the pH acidic (3.8-4.5) and discourages overgrowth of other organisms. Most discharge problems are when this balance is disturbed.</p>

<h2>Things that disturb the balance</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Antibiotics</li>
  <li>Douching, vaginal washes, scented soaps and shower gels around the vulva (these wash out the protective bacteria; don't)</li>
  <li>Hormonal changes - pregnancy, perimenopause, hormonal contraception</li>
  <li>Tight non-breathable underwear, prolonged wet swimwear or activewear</li>
  <li>Diabetes, especially uncontrolled</li>
  <li>Immune suppression</li>
  <li>Sexual activity, particularly with new or multiple partners</li>
</ul>

<h2>The three most common causes of changed discharge</h2>

<h3>Bacterial vaginosis (BV)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>What it is:</strong> Overgrowth of anaerobic bacteria, with a drop in protective Lactobacillus. Not strictly an STI but more common with sexual activity.</li>
  <li><strong>Symptoms:</strong> Thin, grey-white discharge with a fishy smell, often more noticeable after sex. Usually no itching or pain.</li>
  <li><strong>Diagnosis:</strong> Clinical, sometimes with vaginal swab. Can be confirmed in lab if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Treatment:</strong> Metronidazole tablets or topical metronidazole gel. Clindamycin cream is an alternative. NZ-funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Recurrence:</strong> Around 50% within a year. Avoid douching, scented products, intravaginal washes. Some women benefit from longer suppressive regimens.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Vaginal thrush (vulvovaginal candidiasis)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>What it is:</strong> Overgrowth of Candida (yeast) species. Common; not an STI.</li>
  <li><strong>Symptoms:</strong> Thick, white, "cottage cheese" discharge with itching, burning, redness, painful sex. Discharge does not usually smell offensive.</li>
  <li><strong>Diagnosis:</strong> Clinical. Swab if recurrent or atypical.</li>
  <li><strong>Treatment:</strong> Clotrimazole pessary or cream from pharmacy (no prescription), or oral fluconazole 150mg single dose. Both work; choice is preference. Available over the counter at pharmacy. Recurrent thrush (4 or more episodes a year) needs longer treatment courses and investigation for triggers.</li>
  <li><strong>Triggers:</strong> Antibiotics, diabetes, pregnancy, hormonal change, immune suppression. If you keep getting thrush, ask about HbA1c.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Trichomoniasis</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>What it is:</strong> A sexually transmitted infection caused by a parasite. Less common in NZ than BV or thrush but rising.</li>
  <li><strong>Symptoms:</strong> Frothy yellow-green discharge, often with smell, itching, sometimes painful urination. Can be asymptomatic.</li>
  <li><strong>Diagnosis:</strong> Vaginal swab.</li>
  <li><strong>Treatment:</strong> Metronidazole or tinidazole tablets. Partner treatment essential.</li>
</ul>

<h2>STIs that can change discharge</h2>

<h3>Chlamydia and gonorrhoea</h3>
<p>The most common STIs in NZ. Often asymptomatic, especially in women. When discharge is present, it is usually unremarkable in colour but volume increases. Other clues: bleeding between periods or after sex, painful urination, pelvic pain, painful sex.</p>

<p>Untreated chlamydia or gonorrhoea can cause pelvic inflammatory disease, infertility, and chronic pelvic pain. Self-test (vaginal swab or urine) is straightforward through any GP, Family Planning, sexual health clinic, or via mail-order through Just The Facts NZ. Free for under 25s and Maori, Pacific, and rainbow communities at most providers.</p>

<h3>Mycoplasma genitalium</h3>
<p>An STI increasingly recognised. Causes similar symptoms to chlamydia. Tested for in some sexual health clinics. Treatment is more complex due to resistance.</p>

<h3>Genital herpes</h3>
<p>Painful blisters or ulcers on the vulva, often with discharge. Diagnosed by swabbing a fresh blister. Treated with antiviral medicines.</p>

<h2>Other causes of changed discharge</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Foreign body</strong> - retained tampon (often a forgotten one) causes very offensive discharge. The most common reason for "the worst-smelling discharge ever". Examination removes the cause.</li>
  <li><strong>Cervical changes</strong> - cervical ectopy, polyps, infection of the cervix can cause increased discharge or post-coital bleeding.</li>
  <li><strong>Lichen sclerosus or lichen planus</strong> - inflammatory skin conditions affecting the vulva. Cause itching, burning, sometimes a thin discharge. Need diagnosis and treatment by a doctor.</li>
  <li><strong>Vulvovaginal atrophy (genitourinary syndrome of menopause)</strong> - thin discharge, dryness, irritation, painful sex in postmenopausal women. Treated with vaginal oestrogen.</li>
  <li><strong>Allergic reactions</strong> - to soaps, lubricants, detergents, latex condoms. Stop the trigger.</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see a doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Discharge with smell, blood, or unusual colour (yellow, green, grey)</li>
  <li>Discharge with itching, burning, pain, or sores</li>
  <li>Bleeding between periods, after sex, or after menopause</li>
  <li>Pelvic pain or fever</li>
  <li>Painful urination</li>
  <li>Possible exposure to STI - new partner without barrier protection, partner diagnosed with STI</li>
  <li>Recurrent thrush (more than 4 episodes a year)</li>
  <li>Recurrent BV (more than 3 episodes a year)</li>
  <li>Discharge that does not respond to over-the-counter thrush treatment</li>
  <li>Discharge in pregnancy that is heavy, smelly, or accompanied by pain</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps in everyday life</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Don't douche or use vaginal washes.</strong> The vagina cleans itself. Washes disrupt the protective bacterial balance.</li>
  <li>Wash the outside (vulva) with water only, or unscented mild soap if needed.</li>
  <li>Cotton underwear, change daily, change after exercise.</li>
  <li>Avoid prolonged wet swimwear or activewear.</li>
  <li>Don't share towels or wet clothing.</li>
  <li>Wipe front to back after toileting.</li>
  <li>Change tampons or pads every 4-6 hours during your period.</li>
  <li>Use barrier protection (condoms) with new partners until both are STI-tested.</li>
  <li>If you have diabetes, good blood sugar control reduces thrush risk.</li>
</ul>
