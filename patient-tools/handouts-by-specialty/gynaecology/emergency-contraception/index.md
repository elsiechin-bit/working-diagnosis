<p>Emergency contraception is for after sex, not before. It can prevent pregnancy when contraception was not used, the condom broke, the pill was missed, or sex happened without consent. It works best the sooner it is used. There are three options in NZ. None of them cause an abortion - they all work by preventing or delaying ovulation, or preventing implantation, before pregnancy is established.</p>

<h2>The three options</h2>

<h3>Levonorgestrel (Postinor-1, Levonelle)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Form:</strong> Single tablet, one dose</li>
  <li><strong>Window:</strong> Up to 72 hours after sex (effective up to 96 hours but less so)</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> Around 84% if taken within 24 hours, dropping to around 60% at 72 hours[1]</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Delays or prevents ovulation. Does not work if you have already ovulated.</li>
  <li><strong>Where to get it:</strong> Pharmacy without a prescription (over the counter). Free at Family Planning. Often free at participating pharmacies for those eligible.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost:</strong> NZ$15-35 in pharmacies if not eligible for free supply</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> Nausea (uncommon), light bleeding, period may be early or late. If you vomit within 3 hours, take another dose.</li>
  <li><strong>Less effective if:</strong> BMI over 26-30 - consider ulipristal or copper IUD instead</li>
</ul>

<h3>Ulipristal acetate (EllaOne)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Form:</strong> Single tablet, one dose</li>
  <li><strong>Window:</strong> Up to 120 hours (5 days) after sex</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> More effective than levonorgestrel, especially closer to ovulation. Around 85% effective overall, with less drop-off over 5 days.[2]</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Selective progesterone receptor modulator. Delays ovulation even when LH surge has started.</li>
  <li><strong>Where to get it:</strong> Funded in NZ since 2024 (with prescription); available from GP, Family Planning, sexual health clinics. Also over the counter at some pharmacies for around NZ$45-60.</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> Similar to levonorgestrel. Hormonal contraception should be paused for 5 days after taking ulipristal because they can cancel each other out - use condoms in the interim.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Copper IUD (most effective)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Form:</strong> Inserted IUD</li>
  <li><strong>Window:</strong> Up to 5 days after the earliest possible date of ovulation in the cycle - in practice up to 5 days after sex</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> Greater than 99% - the most effective form of emergency contraception by a significant margin[3]</li>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Copper prevents fertilisation and implantation</li>
  <li><strong>Where to get it:</strong> Family Planning, GP with IUD insertion training, some sexual health clinics. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Bonus:</strong> Becomes your ongoing contraception (for up to 5-10 years). Also reversible at any time.</li>
  <li><strong>Side effects:</strong> Insertion can be uncomfortable. Periods may be heavier and crampier.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Which one to use when</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Within 24 hours, BMI under 26:</strong> levonorgestrel works well, is over the counter, cheap and easy</li>
  <li><strong>24-72 hours after sex, or BMI over 26:</strong> ulipristal is more effective</li>
  <li><strong>72-120 hours after sex:</strong> ulipristal or copper IUD</li>
  <li><strong>Up to 5 days, especially if you also want ongoing contraception:</strong> copper IUD - by far the most effective and a complete solution</li>
  <li><strong>Already ovulated this cycle:</strong> oral options will not work; copper IUD is the only effective choice</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do</h2>

<ol>
  <li>The sooner the better. The window is hours, not days, for best effectiveness.</li>
  <li>Pharmacy is fastest for levonorgestrel. Family Planning, GP, or sexual health for ulipristal or IUD.</li>
  <li>Take the tablet immediately when you get home. Do not wait until morning.</li>
  <li>Continue or start your usual contraception. Emergency contraception only works for THIS sex; it does not protect against later acts of sex in the same cycle.</li>
  <li>If your period is more than a week late, take a pregnancy test.</li>
  <li>Consider STI testing 2 weeks after unprotected sex.</li>
</ol>

<h2>Common questions</h2>

<h3>How does it work? Is it the same as an abortion?</h3>
<p>Emergency contraception delays or prevents ovulation, or prevents implantation. It works before pregnancy is established (defined as implantation in the uterine lining). It is not the same as a medical abortion (mifepristone + misoprostol), which is a different process that ends an established pregnancy.</p>

<h3>Will it harm an existing pregnancy?</h3>
<p>If you are already pregnant when you take levonorgestrel or ulipristal, the tablet does not harm the pregnancy. It simply has no effect.</p>

<h3>How often can I use it?</h3>
<p>It is safe to use multiple times if needed, but it is less effective than ongoing contraception and side effects compound. If you are using emergency contraception more than once a year, it is worth talking to a GP or Family Planning about ongoing options.</p>

<h3>Will my period be normal afterwards?</h3>
<p>It might be earlier or later than expected by up to a week. If your period is more than a week late, take a pregnancy test.</p>

<h3>Does anyone else need to know?</h3>
<p>No. Emergency contraception is confidential at any age in NZ. Pharmacists, Family Planning, and GPs cannot tell parents, partners, or anyone else without your permission.</p>
