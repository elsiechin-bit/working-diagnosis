<p>Heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) affects roughly one in four women at some point in their reproductive life. It is the single most common reason for gynaecology referral in NZ. Many women carry on for years thinking their periods are "just heavy" - but heavy bleeding can be effectively treated, often without surgery, and untreated heavy bleeding causes iron deficiency, exhaustion, and disruption to work and social life. This handout walks through what counts as heavy, what causes it, and the options.</p>

<h2>What counts as heavy</h2>

<p>Technically, more than 80ml of blood loss per cycle. Practically, no one measures this. The clinical definition is bleeding that you find heavy and that interferes with your physical, social, emotional, or material quality of life. Some specific signs:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Soaking through a pad or tampon every hour for several hours</li>
  <li>Needing to change protection in the night</li>
  <li>Bleeding through clothes or sheets</li>
  <li>Passing clots larger than a 50-cent coin</li>
  <li>Bleeding lasting more than 7 days</li>
  <li>Symptoms of iron deficiency - tiredness, breathlessness on stairs, pale appearance, dizziness, hair loss</li>
  <li>Avoiding activities, exercise, or wearing certain clothes because of bleeding</li>
</ul>

<p>If any of those apply, it is worth talking to your GP. Many women have lived with these patterns for so long they assume it is normal.</p>

<h2>What causes it</h2>

<h3>No identifiable cause (around 50%)</h3>
<p>The medical term is "heavy menstrual bleeding without underlying pathology". Often hormonal - a small imbalance between oestrogen and progesterone leads to a thicker uterine lining that sheds heavily. This responds well to hormonal treatment.</p>

<h3>Fibroids</h3>
<p>Benign muscle growths in or on the uterus. Common, especially over 35. Cause heavy bleeding, pelvic pressure, sometimes infertility. Confirmed on ultrasound.</p>

<h3>Adenomyosis</h3>
<p>Tissue from the uterine lining growing INTO the muscle wall of the uterus. Causes a tender, enlarged uterus, heavy painful periods, often diagnosed by ultrasound or MRI.</p>

<h3>Polyps</h3>
<p>Small growths from the uterine lining or cervix. Often cause spotting between periods or heavy periods. Visible on ultrasound or hysteroscopy.</p>

<h3>Bleeding disorders</h3>
<p>von Willebrand disease (the most common inherited bleeding disorder) often presents in adolescence with heavy periods. Other clotting problems and platelet disorders too. Worth screening for, especially in younger women.</p>

<h3>Hormonal causes</h3>
<p>Polycystic ovarian syndrome, thyroid dysfunction, perimenopause.</p>

<h3>Endometrial cancer or precancer</h3>
<p>Less common but important to exclude, especially after age 45 or with risk factors (PCOS, obesity, never had children, prolonged unopposed oestrogen, family history).</p>

<h3>IUD (copper) or other contraception</h3>
<p>Copper IUDs commonly cause heavier periods.</p>

<h2>What investigation looks like</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>History</strong> - bleeding pattern, age of onset, family history, medications</li>
  <li><strong>Examination</strong> - speculum, pelvic exam to assess uterus size and tenderness</li>
  <li><strong>Blood tests</strong> - full blood count (anaemia), iron studies, thyroid function, coagulation if suggestive</li>
  <li><strong>Cervical screening</strong> if not up to date</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic ultrasound</strong> - the most useful single test. Transvaginal preferred for detail</li>
  <li><strong>Endometrial biopsy</strong> - usually if over 45, or younger with risk factors for endometrial cancer, or if bleeding pattern is unusual</li>
  <li><strong>Hysteroscopy</strong> - direct view of the inside of the uterus, often combined with biopsy</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment options</h2>

<p>Treatment depends on cause, severity, age, fertility plans, and preference. Many women try several before settling on what suits.</p>

<h3>Tranexamic acid</h3>
<ul>
  <li>An antifibrinolytic - reduces blood loss by around 50%</li>
  <li>Taken only on the heaviest days (usually 2-4 days per cycle)</li>
  <li>Does not affect cycle, contraception, or fertility</li>
  <li>NZ-funded, can be taken alongside other treatments</li>
  <li>Useful first-line, especially if you are trying to conceive or do not want hormones</li>
</ul>

<h3>NSAIDs</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Mefenamic acid, naproxen, ibuprofen</li>
  <li>Reduce blood loss by around 20-30%</li>
  <li>Also help with pain</li>
  <li>Often combined with tranexamic acid</li>
</ul>

<h3>Combined oral contraceptive pill</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Reduces bleeding significantly</li>
  <li>Regularises cycle</li>
  <li>Provides contraception</li>
  <li>Continuous use (skipping the placebo week) gives the best bleeding control</li>
</ul>

<h3>Hormonal IUD (Mirena)</h3>
<p>The most effective non-surgical treatment for heavy bleeding without an underlying structural cause. Reduces bleeding by 70-95%. Around 20% of women have no periods at all by 12 months. Provides contraception. Lasts up to 8 years. Fully funded in NZ.</p>

<p>Mirena is appropriate for most women with heavy bleeding, including those with fibroids (provided the uterine cavity is not significantly distorted) and adenomyosis. For many women it eliminates the need for surgery.</p>

<h3>Progestogen tablets</h3>
<p>Norethisterone or medroxyprogesterone for 21 days each cycle can stop heavy bleeding. Used short-term or for women who cannot use other options.</p>

<h3>GnRH agonists</h3>
<p>Goserelin or leuprorelin - cause temporary medical menopause. Used to shrink fibroids before surgery, or in severe cases where other options have failed. Side effects of menopause - usually short-term use.</p>

<h3>Surgical options</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Hysteroscopic polypectomy or myomectomy</strong> - removal of polyps or small submucosal fibroids via the cervix. Day procedure. Preserves the uterus.</li>
  <li><strong>Endometrial ablation</strong> - destroys the lining of the uterus. Day procedure. Most women have significantly lighter periods or none. Not for women planning pregnancy. Around 10-15% need further intervention within 5 years.</li>
  <li><strong>Myomectomy</strong> - surgical removal of larger fibroids. Preserves the uterus and fertility. Open or laparoscopic.</li>
  <li><strong>Uterine artery embolisation</strong> - radiological procedure that blocks the blood supply to fibroids. Less invasive than surgery. Available in some main centres.</li>
  <li><strong>Hysterectomy</strong> - removal of the uterus. The definitive treatment. Considered when other options have failed, when fibroids are large, or as a planned choice. Recovery 4-6 weeks for laparoscopic, longer for open.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Iron deficiency - do not skip this part</h2>

<p>Heavy bleeding causes iron deficiency in many women, even before anaemia shows on a blood test. Iron deficiency itself causes fatigue, breathlessness, hair loss, restless legs, brain fog, and reduced exercise tolerance - often blamed on "just being busy".</p>

<p>If your ferritin is low (below 30 in many labs, or below the local reference range), iron replacement helps even before anaemia develops. Options:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Oral iron</strong> - ferrous fumarate or ferrous sulphate. Taken every other day is often as effective as daily and better tolerated. Vitamin C with iron improves absorption.</li>
  <li><strong>IV iron</strong> - if oral iron is not tolerated, not effective, or rapid replacement is needed. Available in NZ as Ferinject (ferric carboxymaltose) - one or two infusions usually replenishes stores. Funded in NZ for moderate-severe iron deficiency anaemia or where oral iron has failed.</li>
</ul>
