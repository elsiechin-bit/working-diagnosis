<p>The IUD - intrauterine device - is one of the most effective contraceptives available. It sits inside the uterus, prevents pregnancy for 5 to 10 years depending on type, and is fully reversible. NZ has two main types: copper and hormonal. They work in different ways, suit different people, and have different bleeding patterns. This handout walks through both.</p>

<h2>The two types</h2>

<h3>Copper IUD</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Copper is toxic to sperm. The body's response to a foreign object also creates an environment that prevents fertilisation and implantation. No hormones.</li>
  <li><strong>How long it lasts:</strong> Mostly 5 years (some types up to 10)</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> Greater than 99% (around 1 pregnancy per 1,000 women per year)</li>
  <li><strong>Bleeding pattern:</strong> Periods continue, often heavier and crampier than before, especially in the first 3-6 months</li>
  <li><strong>Best for:</strong> People who want hormone-free contraception, who have tolerable periods and want long-term effectiveness, who want emergency contraception that can become ongoing</li>
  <li><strong>Funded in NZ:</strong> Yes</li>
</ul>

<h3>Hormonal IUD (Mirena, Jaydess)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>How it works:</strong> Releases small amounts of progestogen (levonorgestrel) directly into the uterus. Thickens cervical mucus, thins the uterine lining, sometimes prevents ovulation.</li>
  <li><strong>How long it lasts:</strong> Mirena 5-8 years; Jaydess 3 years</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness:</strong> Greater than 99%</li>
  <li><strong>Bleeding pattern:</strong> Most people bleed irregularly for 3-6 months, then either lighter periods, very light spotting, or no periods at all (around 20% have no periods at 1 year on Mirena)</li>
  <li><strong>Best for:</strong> Heavy or painful periods, people who would prefer lighter or no periods, those for whom hormones are acceptable</li>
  <li><strong>Treats:</strong> Heavy menstrual bleeding, painful periods, endometriosis, can be part of HRT in perimenopause</li>
  <li><strong>Funded in NZ:</strong> Mirena fully funded since 2019; Jaydess funded with Special Authority</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who can have an IUD</h2>

<p>Most people who can get pregnant. The main contraindications are:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Active pelvic infection</li>
  <li>Unexplained vaginal bleeding before investigation</li>
  <li>Known cervical or uterine cancer</li>
  <li>Severe uterine anatomy abnormalities</li>
  <li>Pregnancy</li>
</ul>

<p>You do not have to have had children to have an IUD. The hormonal IUD has a slightly higher first-attempt failure rate of insertion in people who have not had children, but most insertions still succeed.</p>

<h2>Before the appointment</h2>

<ul>
  <li>You can have an IUD inserted at any time in the menstrual cycle, as long as it is reasonable to be sure you are not pregnant</li>
  <li>Many people prefer to time insertion around their period or just after, when the cervix is more open</li>
  <li>Eat a normal meal before - low blood sugar increases the chance of feeling faint</li>
  <li>Take ibuprofen 400-600mg an hour before, unless you cannot take it. Reduces cramping.</li>
  <li>Some people bring a friend; some prefer to come alone. Either is fine.</li>
  <li>STI testing in the previous 6 months for under-25s (mostly chlamydia, sometimes gonorrhoea) is standard practice</li>
</ul>

<h2>The insertion procedure</h2>

<p>Around 5-15 minutes total. Most insertions are done in the GP, Family Planning, or sexual health clinic - no anaesthetic, no operating theatre.</p>

<ol>
  <li>Speculum is inserted to see the cervix, similar to a smear test</li>
  <li>The cervix is cleaned with antiseptic</li>
  <li>The uterus is measured with a thin sound (a flexible measuring stick) - this is when most people feel cramping</li>
  <li>The IUD is inserted through the cervix using a slim applicator. Once in place, the applicator is removed</li>
  <li>The strings (which sit in the upper vagina) are trimmed to length</li>
  <li>Speculum out, you can sit up</li>
</ol>

<p>Pain is variable. Most people describe it as crampy and brief - worse than a smear, better than period pain. A small minority find insertion very painful. Tell your clinician if you do; they can stop, offer pain relief, or arrange insertion under sedation in some cases.</p>

<p>You will usually be advised to rest for 10-15 minutes afterwards because some people feel light-headed or faint.</p>

<h2>The first weeks</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Cramping for 24-48 hours, sometimes longer. Ibuprofen and a heat pack help.</li>
  <li>Some bleeding or spotting for the first few days</li>
  <li>Avoid baths, swimming pools, and sex for 24 hours after insertion to reduce infection risk (your clinician may have specific advice)</li>
  <li>Use backup contraception (condoms or abstain) for 7 days for the hormonal IUD if it was not inserted in the first 7 days of your cycle. The copper IUD is effective immediately.</li>
</ul>

<h2>The first 3-6 months</h2>

<h3>Copper IUD</h3>
<p>Periods may be 30-50% heavier and noticeably crampier than before. Usually settles toward your previous baseline by 3-6 months but stays heavier than pre-IUD for many people.</p>

<h3>Hormonal IUD</h3>
<p>Irregular spotting and bleeding is normal in the first 3-6 months. By 12 months, around 20% of Mirena users have no periods at all and most have very light periods. If unscheduled bleeding persists past 6 months, see your GP.</p>

<h2>Checking the IUD is still in place</h2>

<p>The IUD has two thin nylon strings that sit in the upper vagina. You can check them yourself once a month, ideally just after your period:</p>

<ol>
  <li>Wash your hands</li>
  <li>Crouch or sit on the toilet</li>
  <li>Insert two fingers into the vagina until you feel the cervix (firm, like the tip of your nose)</li>
  <li>The strings should feel like fishing line poking out of a small hole at the centre of the cervix</li>
</ol>

<p>If you cannot feel the strings, do not panic - the IUD is usually still in place. See your GP for a check. Use backup contraception until confirmed.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP urgently</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Severe pelvic pain that is new or worse than your usual</li>
  <li>Fever, chills</li>
  <li>Smelly or unusual discharge</li>
  <li>Pain or bleeding during sex that is new</li>
  <li>You cannot feel the strings AND you have unprotected sex - check pregnancy and IUD position</li>
  <li>You think you might be pregnant - rare with an IUD but possible. Pregnancy with an IUD has a higher chance of being ectopic; needs urgent assessment.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Removal</h2>

<p>Removal is usually a 1-minute procedure. The clinician finds the strings with a speculum and pulls gently. It is much less crampy than insertion. Fertility returns immediately.</p>

<p>You can have a new IUD inserted in the same appointment if you want to switch types or extend.</p>
