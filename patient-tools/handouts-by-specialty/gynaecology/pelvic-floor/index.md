<p>The pelvic floor is the hammock of muscle that runs from your pubic bone at the front to your tailbone at the back. It supports your bladder, uterus (or prostate), and rectum, and controls when you urinate, when you have a bowel motion, and how the abdominal pressure of coughs, sneezes, and lifts is held in. Like any muscle group, it weakens with disuse, pregnancy, surgery, and age - and like any muscle group, it responds well to deliberate exercise. This handout walks through how to find these muscles, exercise them properly, and know if you need professional help.</p>

<h2>Why pelvic floor exercises help</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Stress urinary incontinence</strong> (leaking with cough, sneeze, laugh, exercise) - around 60-70% of women improve significantly with consistent pelvic floor work[1]</li>
  <li><strong>Urge urinary incontinence</strong> (sudden urge with leakage on the way to toilet) - improved by combining pelvic floor work with bladder retraining</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic organ prolapse</strong> - prevents progression of mild prolapse, reduces symptoms, may not reverse anatomical descent</li>
  <li><strong>Pregnancy and postpartum</strong> - reduces incidence and severity of incontinence and prolapse</li>
  <li><strong>After prostate surgery in men</strong> - speeds recovery from urinary leakage</li>
  <li><strong>Sexual function</strong> - both for pleasure and for orgasm strength in some people</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 1: Find the muscles</h2>

<p>Most people get this wrong on the first try. The pelvic floor muscles are NOT your buttocks, abdominals, or thighs. They are the deep, internal muscles you would use to:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Stop the flow of urine mid-stream (try this once or twice to identify - do not do it routinely as a workout, it can cause urinary problems)</li>
  <li>Hold in wind</li>
  <li>"Lift" the muscles around the vagina and anus inward and upward</li>
</ul>

<p>You should feel a gentle drawing-in and lifting of the muscles between the pubic bone and tailbone. Your stomach should not visibly tense, your buttocks should not clench, your thighs should not squeeze. If they do, you are using the wrong muscles.</p>

<p>You can sometimes feel the contraction by:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Inserting a finger into the vagina and contracting - you should feel the muscles tighten around your finger</li>
  <li>Looking in a mirror at the perineum - you should see a gentle "drawing in" rather than a bulging out</li>
  <li>Lying on your back with knees bent - the contraction should feel internal, not external</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 2: The exercise routine</h2>

<p>Two types of contractions are needed - slow holds and fast pulses. Both work different muscle fibre types.</p>

<h3>Slow holds</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Tighten the pelvic floor muscles, lifting them up and in</li>
  <li>Hold for 5 seconds (build up to 10 seconds over weeks)</li>
  <li>Relax fully for 10 seconds - this matters as much as the hold</li>
  <li>Repeat 8-10 times</li>
</ul>

<h3>Fast pulses</h3>
<ul>
  <li>Quick contraction, quick release</li>
  <li>Repeat 10 times</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Do this routine three times a day.</strong> Morning, lunchtime, evening. Or whenever you brush your teeth, queue at the supermarket, or wait for the kettle. Most people do better with several short sessions than one long one.</p>

<h2>Things people get wrong</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Squeezing buttocks, thighs, or stomach.</strong> Place a hand on your belly to check it stays soft.</li>
  <li><strong>Holding your breath.</strong> Breathe normally throughout. The contraction should fit naturally with the exhale.</li>
  <li><strong>Not relaxing fully between contractions.</strong> The release is half the exercise. A pelvic floor that is always tight is a different problem - "overactive pelvic floor" - that can cause its own symptoms.</li>
  <li><strong>Stopping the urine stream as a regular workout.</strong> Useful as a one-off check that you have found the right muscles, but doing it routinely can confuse the bladder reflex.</li>
  <li><strong>Expecting overnight results.</strong> Give it 3 months of consistent practice. Most people see noticeable improvement at 4-6 weeks; significant improvement at 12.</li>
</ul>

<h2>"The Knack" - bracing for cough or sneeze</h2>

<p>One of the most useful applications: deliberately tighten your pelvic floor a fraction of a second BEFORE a cough, sneeze, lift, or laugh. With practice this becomes automatic and significantly reduces stress incontinence in everyday situations.</p>

<h2>Postpartum</h2>

<p>The pelvic floor is stretched and often partially torn during vaginal birth, and weakened by carrying a baby in pregnancy regardless of delivery mode. Restart pelvic floor work as soon as comfortable after birth - usually within a few days. Many maternity providers recommend a session with a pelvic floor physiotherapist at 6-8 weeks postpartum, especially if there were tears or instrumental delivery.</p>

<h2>When pelvic floor exercises are not enough</h2>

<p>If you have done the exercises consistently for 3 months and have not seen improvement, it is time for help. The most common reasons exercises fail:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Wrong muscles being contracted</li>
  <li>Underlying prolapse, anatomical issue, or nerve injury that needs assessment</li>
  <li>Coexisting overactive pelvic floor (paradoxical: the muscles are too tight, not too weak, and need release rather than strengthening)</li>
  <li>Severe stress incontinence that benefits from surgical or pessary intervention</li>
  <li>Mixed or urge incontinence that needs combined approach (medication, bladder retraining, and floor work)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pelvic floor physiotherapy</h2>

<p>A pelvic floor physiotherapist can:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Confirm you are using the right muscles, with biofeedback if needed</li>
  <li>Tailor a programme to your specific pattern (weak, overactive, asymmetric)</li>
  <li>Check for prolapse and refer on if needed</li>
  <li>Provide hands-on techniques for overactive pelvic floor</li>
  <li>Teach electrical stimulation techniques in some cases</li>
  <li>Coordinate with continence advisors, urogynaecologists, and urologists</li>
</ul>

<p>In NZ, pelvic floor physio is mostly private (NZ$120-180 per session) but ACC may fund postnatal or post-surgical referrals; some DHBs provide a limited number of sessions. Many physios accept GP referrals or self-referrals.</p>

<h2>Apps and devices</h2>

<p>Several apps (Squeezy, Tat) provide reminders and timing for pelvic floor sessions. Biofeedback devices that measure contraction strength (Elvie, Pelvic Trainer) can help if you struggle with technique. The exercises themselves are free; the device is an aid, not a requirement.</p>
