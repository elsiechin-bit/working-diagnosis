<p>Perimenopause is the transition into menopause. It usually starts in the early to mid 40s and lasts on average 4-8 years before periods finally stop. Hormones do not decline smoothly during this time - they swing wildly. That swing is what causes most of the symptoms. The good news is that perimenopause is well understood, the symptoms are not "all in your head", and there are now several things that genuinely help.</p>

<h2>What is happening</h2>

<p>The ovaries start running low on eggs. As the supply dwindles, ovulation becomes irregular and the hormone signals between brain and ovaries become erratic. Oestrogen levels can swing high and low within the same week. Cycles become unpredictable. By the time periods stop completely (menopause), oestrogen has settled at a low level - but during perimenopause, the high-low fluctuation is the rule.</p>

<h2>The most common changes</h2>

<h3>Cycle changes</h3>
<p>Periods get shorter, longer, lighter, heavier, more frequent, less frequent, or any combination. Some months you will skip a period, others you will have two close together. This is normal. What is not normal: bleeding so heavy you have to change pad/tampon every hour, bleeding lasting more than 7 days, bleeding between periods (mid-cycle), bleeding after sex, or any bleeding after periods have stopped for 12 months. Those need investigation.</p>

<h3>Hot flushes and night sweats (vasomotor symptoms)</h3>
<p>The most-reported symptom. A sudden wave of heat, often starting in the chest, rising up to the face, sometimes followed by sweating and a chill. Lasts seconds to minutes. Triggered by alcohol, caffeine, spicy food, stress, warm rooms, or nothing at all. Affects roughly 75% of women in perimenopause. Often the most disruptive at night.</p>

<h3>Sleep disruption</h3>
<p>Caused partly by night sweats, partly by hormonal effects on sleep architecture directly. Falling asleep fine, waking at 3am, unable to get back to sleep. Adds to fatigue and mood symptoms.</p>

<h3>Mood and cognition</h3>
<p>Anxiety, low mood, irritability, brain fog, word-finding difficulty. Often the most distressing because it is harder to attribute to hormones. Risk of major depression rises in perimenopause - if it is significant, treat it like depression.</p>

<h3>Physical changes</h3>
<p>Joint aches, muscle stiffness, breast tenderness, weight gain (especially around the middle), hair thinning, vaginal dryness, decreased libido, urinary urgency, recurrent UTIs.</p>

<h3>Heart palpitations and headaches</h3>
<p>Common, often benign, but worth checking out the first time they happen.</p>

<h2>How long does it last</h2>

<p>The average is around 4 years, with a wide range from 1 year to 10+. The typical pattern: cycle changes start first, then 1-2 years later the vasomotor and mood symptoms ramp up, peaking around the time of the final period and gradually settling over the years afterwards. Most women's symptoms are largely settled by 5 years post-menopause.</p>

<h2>What helps</h2>

<h3>Lifestyle</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Cool the room.</strong> Lower bedroom temperature (16-18 degrees), cotton bedding, layered clothing.</li>
  <li><strong>Reduce hot-flush triggers</strong> - alcohol, caffeine, spicy food, smoking. Trial cutting one at a time.</li>
  <li><strong>Regular exercise.</strong> Reduces severity and frequency of vasomotor symptoms; improves sleep, mood, bone density. Aim for resistance training plus aerobic.</li>
  <li><strong>Weight management.</strong> Higher BMI is associated with worse vasomotor symptoms in some studies.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep hygiene.</strong> The separate sleep handout has the detail.</li>
  <li><strong>Strength training.</strong> Especially important: bone loss accelerates around menopause, and resistance exercise is the most effective way to slow it.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Hormone replacement therapy (HRT)</h3>

<p>HRT - now sometimes called Menopausal Hormone Therapy or MHT - is the most effective treatment for moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms and can improve mood, sleep, bone density, and vaginal symptoms.</p>

<p>The risk profile is more favourable than many people remember from older media coverage. The 2002 Women's Health Initiative study used older formulations in older women starting HRT a decade after menopause. Modern guidance, based on more recent evidence:[1]</p>

<ul>
  <li>For most women in their 40s and early 50s starting close to the final period, the benefits of HRT outweigh the risks</li>
  <li>Transdermal oestrogen (patch or gel) does not appear to increase blood clot risk</li>
  <li>Body-identical progesterone (utrogestan) is preferred where possible</li>
  <li>Breast cancer risk increase is small and is concentrated in long-term combined HRT use</li>
  <li>Cardiovascular benefit appears in women starting close to menopause</li>
</ul>

<p>NZ-funded options include estradiol patches and gels, and progesterone (utrogestan, funded since 2024). The Mirena IUD can serve as the progesterone component if you have one in place.</p>

<h3>Non-hormonal medical options</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>SSRIs and SNRIs</strong> (low-dose paroxetine, venlafaxine, escitalopram) - reduce vasomotor symptoms by 50-65%. Useful when HRT is contraindicated or not preferred.</li>
  <li><strong>Gabapentin</strong> - effective for night sweats; sedating, helpful for sleep.</li>
  <li><strong>Clonidine</strong> - older option, less commonly used.</li>
  <li><strong>Fezolinetant</strong> - new non-hormonal class targeting the brain receptor that triggers hot flushes. Approved overseas, not yet funded in NZ.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Vaginal symptoms</h3>

<p>Vaginal dryness, irritation, painful sex, and recurrent UTIs respond well to:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Vaginal moisturisers</strong> (Replens, Hyalofemme) used regularly</li>
  <li><strong>Lubricants</strong> for sex</li>
  <li><strong>Local oestrogen</strong> (Ovestin cream, Vagifem pessaries) - safe, effective, fully funded in NZ. Even women with breast cancer history can usually use these after specialist discussion.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Contraception in perimenopause</h2>

<p>You can still get pregnant in perimenopause, even with irregular periods. Continue contraception until 12 months after your last period if over 50, or 24 months if under 50. The hormonal IUD is a particularly elegant option in perimenopause because it provides contraception, treats heavy bleeding, and can serve as the progesterone component of HRT.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Symptoms are interfering with work, relationships, sleep, or how you feel about yourself</li>
  <li>Heavy bleeding (changing protection more than every 2 hours; clots; bleeding through clothes)</li>
  <li>Bleeding lasting more than 7 days, or between periods, or after sex</li>
  <li>Any bleeding after periods have been absent for 12 months</li>
  <li>Significant low mood, anxiety, or thoughts of self-harm</li>
  <li>To discuss whether HRT is appropriate for you</li>
  <li>Earlier-than-expected onset (under 40) - may be premature ovarian insufficiency, which needs investigation</li>
</ul>
