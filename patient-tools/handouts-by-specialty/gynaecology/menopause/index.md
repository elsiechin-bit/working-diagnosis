<p>Menopause is technically a single day - the day that marks 12 months since your last period. Average age in NZ is 51. After that day, you are postmenopausal. The years either side - perimenopause and the early postmenopausal years - are when symptoms are most prominent. This handout focuses on menopause and the years after, and the treatment options that have changed substantially in the last 5 years.</p>

<h2>What changes at menopause</h2>

<p>Oestrogen settles at a low level. Progesterone is essentially absent (since you are no longer ovulating). The hot-low swings of perimenopause stabilise. Many symptoms reduce gradually, but some persist or appear newly:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats) - persist for an average of 7 years post-menopause; some have them lifelong</li>
  <li>Vaginal dryness, painful sex - tends to worsen with time without treatment</li>
  <li>Recurrent UTIs related to genitourinary syndrome of menopause</li>
  <li>Bone loss - accelerates in the years either side of menopause</li>
  <li>Cardiovascular risk - rises in the years after menopause</li>
  <li>Joint aches, sleep changes, mood, cognitive symptoms - often improve with time but vary</li>
</ul>

<h2>HRT: the modern position</h2>

<p>Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT, also called Menopausal Hormone Therapy or MHT) replaces the hormones the ovaries are no longer making. The understanding of risks and benefits has shifted significantly since the early 2000s.</p>

<h3>The benefits</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Vasomotor symptoms:</strong> the most effective treatment - around 80% reduction in frequency and severity</li>
  <li><strong>Genitourinary symptoms:</strong> systemic or local oestrogen reverses vaginal atrophy, painful sex, recurrent UTIs</li>
  <li><strong>Bone density:</strong> prevents bone loss; reduces fracture risk while taken</li>
  <li><strong>Mood and sleep:</strong> often improve, especially when symptoms are vasomotor-driven</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular:</strong> small reduction in cardiovascular events when started within 10 years of menopause; risk reverses if started later</li>
  <li><strong>Possibly reduces colorectal cancer and type 2 diabetes risk</strong></li>
</ul>

<h3>The risks</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Blood clots:</strong> oral oestrogen increases venous clot risk; transdermal (patch or gel) does not appear to.[1]</li>
  <li><strong>Breast cancer:</strong> small increase in risk with combined HRT (oestrogen + progestogen) used for more than 5-10 years. Oestrogen-only (in women without a uterus) appears not to increase breast cancer risk and may decrease it. The increase is comparable in size to the increased risk from drinking a glass of wine a day or being moderately overweight.</li>
  <li><strong>Stroke:</strong> oral oestrogen carries small increase; transdermal appears not to.</li>
  <li><strong>Endometrial cancer:</strong> only with unopposed oestrogen in someone with a uterus - which is why progestogen is always added in those cases.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The bottom line for most women in their 40s and early 50s</h3>
<p>If you are within 10 years of menopause and under 60, with bothersome symptoms, the benefits of HRT generally outweigh the risks. The exceptions: history of hormone-positive breast cancer, history of blood clot or stroke, severe liver disease, undiagnosed vaginal bleeding, known hormone-sensitive cancers.</p>

<h2>Forms of HRT funded in NZ</h2>

<p>The NZ funded landscape changed in 2024 with the addition of body-identical progesterone (utrogestan).</p>

<h3>Oestrogen</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Estradiol patches</strong> (Estradot) - twice-weekly patch. Transdermal route avoids first-pass liver metabolism, reduces clot risk. NZ funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Estradiol gel</strong> (Sandrena) - daily gel applied to upper arm or thigh. Same advantages as patch. NZ funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Oral estradiol</strong> (Progynova) - daily tablet. Higher clot risk than transdermal. NZ funded.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Progesterone (needed if you have a uterus)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Body-identical progesterone</strong> (Utrogestan) - daily oral capsule, often taken at night (mildly sedating). Funded in NZ since 2024. Closest match to what your body used to produce.</li>
  <li><strong>Mirena IUD</strong> - releases progestogen locally; can serve as the progestogen component of HRT, especially in perimenopause (provides contraception too). Fully funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Synthetic progestogens</strong> (medroxyprogesterone, norethisterone) - older options, still used.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Vaginal oestrogen (for vaginal symptoms only)</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Estriol cream</strong> (Ovestin) - applied vaginally several times a week initially, then 1-2 times a week. NZ funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Estradiol pessary</strong> (Vagifem) - small tablet inserted vaginally. NZ funded.</li>
</ul>

<p>Local vaginal oestrogen has minimal systemic absorption and is generally safe even for women with breast cancer history (after specialist discussion).</p>

<h2>How HRT is started</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Discussion with your GP or a menopause clinic. Cardiovascular risk, breast cancer risk, family history, personal history all factored in.</li>
  <li>Usually start with a moderate-dose patch or gel + utrogestan, or with a Mirena + estradiol</li>
  <li>Settling-in period of 6-12 weeks before assessing whether the dose is right</li>
  <li>Bleeding pattern depends on regimen (sequential = monthly bleed; continuous = no bleed once settled)</li>
  <li>Reviewed annually</li>
</ul>

<h2>Non-hormonal medical options</h2>

<p>For women who cannot or prefer not to take HRT:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>SSRIs/SNRIs</strong> - paroxetine 7.5mg, venlafaxine 37.5-75mg, escitalopram 10-20mg. Reduce vasomotor symptoms by 50-65%. Especially useful with mood symptoms or in breast cancer survivors.</li>
  <li><strong>Gabapentin</strong> - 300-900mg at night. Effective for night sweats and sleep.</li>
  <li><strong>Clonidine</strong> - older, less commonly used.</li>
  <li><strong>Cognitive behavioural therapy</strong> - reduces distress from vasomotor symptoms even when frequency is unchanged. Online programmes (Sleepio for sleep, online CBT for menopause) are accessible options.</li>
  <li><strong>Fezolinetant</strong> - new non-hormonal targeting the NK3 receptor in the brain. Approved overseas, not yet NZ-funded.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term health after menopause</h2>

<p>The shift from premenopausal to postmenopausal physiology raises baseline risks for several conditions. Worth a deliberate conversation with your GP:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Bone health:</strong> DEXA scan if risk factors (early menopause, family history, low BMI, smoking, oral steroids). Adequate calcium and vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise, resistance training. HRT or bone-specific medicines if needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Cardiovascular:</strong> blood pressure, lipids, diabetes screening as part of a 5-yearly NZ cardiovascular risk assessment from age 45.</li>
  <li><strong>Breast cancer screening:</strong> 2-yearly mammograms from age 45 (45-69 funded in NZ; consider continuing 70-74).</li>
  <li><strong>Cervical screening:</strong> until 70 if last screen was negative.</li>
  <li><strong>Colorectal cancer screening:</strong> National Bowel Screening Programme from age 60.</li>
  <li><strong>Vaginal and urinary symptoms:</strong> these tend to worsen over time without treatment. Local oestrogen is the most effective option and safe long-term.</li>
</ul>
