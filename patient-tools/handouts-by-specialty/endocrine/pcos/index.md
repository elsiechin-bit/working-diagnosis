<p>Polycystic ovarian syndrome affects roughly one in ten women of reproductive age, making it one of the most common hormonal conditions in NZ.[1] The name is misleading - "polycystic" suggests cysts on the ovaries, but most women with PCOS have small follicles (immature eggs), not cysts in the way we usually mean. The condition is much more about the hormonal pattern than the ovaries themselves. The good news is that with the right approach, most symptoms are manageable and the long-term health risks can be reduced.</p>

<h2>What PCOS actually is</h2>

<p>PCOS is a hormonal condition with three features. To be diagnosed you need at least two of them:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Irregular or absent periods</strong> - typically fewer than 8 or 9 a year, or cycles longer than 35 days. This reflects irregular or absent ovulation.</li>
  <li><strong>Signs of high androgens</strong> - clinically (acne, excess hair growth in a male pattern, scalp hair thinning) or on a blood test (raised testosterone).</li>
  <li><strong>Polycystic-appearing ovaries on ultrasound</strong> - many small follicles, larger ovaries.</li>
</ul>

<p>Underneath, the central problem is usually insulin resistance - the body's cells respond less well to insulin, so the pancreas makes more of it. High insulin levels then drive the ovaries to make more androgens (male-pattern hormones), and disrupt the signals that trigger ovulation. This is why much of the treatment focuses on insulin resistance, not just on periods.</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Irregular periods, often with long gaps</li>
  <li>Difficulty getting pregnant</li>
  <li>Acne, especially jawline and back</li>
  <li>Excess hair on face, chest, or abdomen (hirsutism)</li>
  <li>Scalp hair thinning, often at the crown</li>
  <li>Weight gain or difficulty losing weight, especially around the middle</li>
  <li>Skin tags, dark velvety patches in skin folds (acanthosis nigricans) - signs of insulin resistance</li>
  <li>Mood changes, sleep problems</li>
  <li>Sometimes no symptoms at all - PCOS is sometimes diagnosed incidentally on ultrasound</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptom severity varies enormously. Some women have all of them prominently; others have only one or two.</p>

<h2>How it is diagnosed</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>History</strong> - menstrual pattern, weight history, fertility plans, family history</li>
  <li><strong>Examination</strong> - blood pressure, weight, signs of androgen excess</li>
  <li><strong>Blood tests</strong> - testosterone, sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG), prolactin, thyroid function, fasting glucose or HbA1c, fasting insulin, lipids. Often LH and FSH (LH:FSH ratio is sometimes elevated but not diagnostic on its own)</li>
  <li><strong>Pelvic ultrasound</strong> - looking for polycystic-appearing ovaries (12 or more small follicles per ovary, or ovarian volume &gt; 10ml)</li>
  <li><strong>Excluding mimics</strong> - thyroid disease, hyperprolactinaemia, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, Cushing's syndrome (rare), androgen-secreting tumour (rare)</li>
</ul>

<p>For adolescents, the diagnostic criteria are stricter because irregular periods and acne are common in normal puberty. Final diagnosis often deferred until 2-3 years after menarche.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>There is no cure but several effective approaches. The right combination depends on which symptoms bother you most and whether you are trying to conceive.</p>

<h3>Lifestyle</h3>
<p>The single most evidence-based intervention. Even modest weight loss (5-10% of body weight) can:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Restore regular ovulation in many women</li>
  <li>Improve insulin sensitivity</li>
  <li>Reduce androgens, leading to better acne and hair</li>
  <li>Reduce long-term diabetes and cardiovascular risk</li>
</ul>

<p>The lifestyle that works combines:</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Resistance and aerobic exercise</strong> - 150 minutes of moderate aerobic plus 2-3 strength sessions per week. The strength training matters - it improves insulin sensitivity independently.</li>
  <li><strong>Lower-glycaemic eating pattern</strong> - protein and fibre at every meal, limited refined carbohydrate, less added sugar. Mediterranean-style eating has the most evidence in PCOS specifically.</li>
  <li><strong>Sleep, stress, and mood</strong> - all influence insulin sensitivity. Worth taking seriously.</li>
</ul>

<p>This is hard. Standard weight-loss advice often fails in PCOS because of the insulin resistance. A registered dietitian with PCOS interest is worth the investment if available.</p>

<h3>Hormonal treatment for irregular periods and skin/hair</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Combined oral contraceptive pill</strong> - regularises bleeding, reduces androgens (and therefore acne and hair). The first-line for most women not currently trying to conceive. NZ-funded options.</li>
  <li><strong>Cyproterone-containing pills</strong> (Diane-35, Estelle) - higher anti-androgen effect, often used for severe acne or hirsutism. Higher clot risk than standard combined pills, so used selectively.</li>
  <li><strong>Hormonal IUD (Mirena)</strong> - controls bleeding, protects the uterine lining (important if you do not have regular periods, see below), but does not address acne or hirsutism. Funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Spironolactone</strong> - anti-androgen for acne and hirsutism. Often combined with the pill. NZ-funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Topical eflornithine</strong> (Vaniqa) - for facial hair, slows regrowth between hair removal. Not funded in NZ.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Insulin sensitisers</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Metformin</strong> - improves insulin sensitivity, can help restore ovulation, modest benefit for weight. Often used in PCOS even without diabetes if there is insulin resistance. NZ-funded.</li>
  <li><strong>Inositol</strong> (myo-inositol, D-chiro-inositol) - over-the-counter supplement, evidence is moderate. Not funded but reasonable to try; usually well tolerated.</li>
  <li><strong>GLP-1 agonists</strong> (semaglutide etc.) - increasingly used off-label in PCOS for weight and metabolic effect. Not currently NZ-funded for PCOS.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Fertility treatment</h3>
<p>If you are trying to conceive:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Lifestyle changes - even modest weight loss can restore ovulation</li>
  <li>Letrozole - first-line for ovulation induction in PCOS, replacing clomiphene</li>
  <li>Metformin - may improve outcomes, often combined with letrozole</li>
  <li>Fertility specialist referral if these do not work, or for IVF</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term health priorities</h2>

<p>PCOS is not "just" a fertility issue. Compared to women without PCOS, women with PCOS have:</p>

<ul>
  <li>Higher risk of type 2 diabetes - around 4-fold</li>
  <li>Higher risk of gestational diabetes</li>
  <li>Increased cardiovascular risk</li>
  <li>Higher rates of anxiety and depression</li>
  <li>Higher rates of obstructive sleep apnoea</li>
  <li>Increased risk of endometrial cancer (because the uterine lining can build up without regular shedding) - this is why women with PCOS who do not have regular periods need either combined pill, hormonal IUD, or scheduled progestogen courses to protect the lining</li>
</ul>

<p>Worth a structured review with your GP including: blood pressure, lipids, fasting glucose or HbA1c, mental health, sleep. Repeat at intervals through life.</p>

<h2>Common myths</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>"PCOS means I can't have children."</strong> Most women with PCOS will eventually conceive, often with simple interventions. Some need fertility help; many do not.</li>
  <li><strong>"PCOS means cysts on my ovaries."</strong> The "cysts" are immature follicles. They are not painful, do not need to be removed, and are not the cause of the syndrome.</li>
  <li><strong>"If I lose weight, PCOS will go away."</strong> Weight loss helps significantly but does not cure. The hormonal pattern often persists.</li>
  <li><strong>"It's all about my periods."</strong> Insulin resistance and metabolic risk are arguably more important long-term.</li>
</ul>
