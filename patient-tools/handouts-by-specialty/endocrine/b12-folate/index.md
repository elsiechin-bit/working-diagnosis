<p>Vitamin B12 and folate are essential nutrients needed for nerve function, red blood cell production, and DNA synthesis. Deficiencies cause anaemia, nerve damage, and cognitive problems. They are easily prevented and treated with dietary changes or supplements.</p>

<h2>What B12 and folate do</h2>

<p>B12 (cobalamin) is needed for nerve function, energy metabolism, and healthy red blood cells. Folate (also called folic acid in supplements) supports DNA synthesis and cell division. Together, they prevent pernicious anaemia and neural tube defects in pregnancy. Both are water-soluble, so your body cannot store excess amounts - you need regular dietary intake or supplementation.</p>

<h2>B12 sources and absorption</h2>

<p>B12 is found naturally only in animal products: meat, fish, poultry, eggs, milk, and dairy products. Plant-based sources are limited unless fortified. B12 requires intrinsic factor (a protein from the stomach) for absorption. People with pernicious anaemia lack intrinsic factor and cannot absorb dietary B12 - they need injections or high-dose supplements.</p>

<h2>Good B12 food sources</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Meat, poultry, and fish (good sources)</li>
  <li>Eggs and dairy products (moderate sources)</li>
  <li>Fortified plant-based milks and breakfast cereals (for vegans)</li>
  <li>Nutritional yeast (fortified varieties)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Who needs B12 supplementation</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Vegans and vegetarians.</strong> Strict plant-based diets lack natural B12. Supplementation or fortified foods are essential.</li>
  <li><strong>Pernicious anaemia.</strong> Intrinsic factor deficiency prevents absorption. B12 injections or very high-dose oral supplements are needed.</li>
  <li><strong>Gastrointestinal conditions.</strong> Coeliac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, or stomach surgery reduce B12 absorption.</li>
  <li><strong>Metformin users.</strong> Long-term metformin slightly reduces B12 absorption - periodic B12 checking is recommended.</li>
  <li><strong>Older adults.</strong> Reduced stomach acid with age makes food B12 harder to absorb. Fortified foods or supplements help.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Folate sources</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Green leafy vegetables:</strong> Spinach, silverbeet, lettuce (high sources)</li>
  <li><strong>Legumes:</strong> Lentils, chickpeas, beans</li>
  <li><strong>Asparagus and broccoli</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Fortified grains and bread</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Avocado, nuts, and seeds</strong></li>
</ul>

<h2>Folate and pregnancy</h2>

<p>Folate prevents neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly). All women of childbearing age should consume adequate folate - ideally 400 micrograms daily from food plus supplementation if planning pregnancy or pregnant. This is particularly important in the first trimester.</p>

<h2>Daily requirements</h2>

<ul>
  <li>B12: Adults need 2.4 micrograms daily (easily met by animal products or fortified foods)</li>
  <li>Folate: Adults need 400 micrograms daily (easily met by vegetables and whole grains)</li>
  <li>Pregnancy: 600 micrograms folate daily, usually via supplementation</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms of deficiency</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>B12 deficiency:</strong> Fatigue, shortness of breath, pale skin, tingling or numbness in hands and feet (nerve damage), memory problems, mood changes, mouth ulcers</li>
  <li><strong>Folate deficiency:</strong> Fatigue, shortness of breath, poor concentration, mood changes, diarrhoea</li>
</ul>

<h2>When to see your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>If you follow a vegan diet or cannot eat animal products</li>
  <li>If you have symptoms of anaemia or nerve problems</li>
  <li>If you are planning pregnancy - to discuss folate supplementation</li>
  <li>If you are pregnant - to ensure adequate folate intake</li>
  <li>If you have gastrointestinal conditions affecting absorption</li>
  <li>If you have been taking metformin long-term - to check B12 levels</li>
</ul>

<h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>

<ul>
  <li>Do I need B12 supplementation (especially if vegan)?</li>
  <li>Should I take folate supplements, especially if planning pregnancy?</li>
  <li>Which supplement should I take?</li>
  <li>How often should my levels be checked?</li>
  <li>If I have pernicious anaemia, do I need injections or oral supplements?</li>
</ul>
