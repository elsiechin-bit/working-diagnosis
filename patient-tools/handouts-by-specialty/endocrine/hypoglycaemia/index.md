Hypoglycaemia, or "hypo", is when blood glucose drops below 4 mmol/L. It mostly affects people on insulin or sulfonylureas (gliclazide, glipizide). Most hypos are mild and easy to fix. Severe hypos are dangerous - knowing how to recognise and treat them is essential for anyone at risk and their family.

<h2>What a hypo feels like</h2>

<p>Early symptoms (the body's warning):</p>

<ul>
<li>Shakiness, trembling</li>
<li>Sweating</li>
<li>Fast heartbeat</li>
<li>Hunger</li>
<li>Anxiety, irritability</li>
<li>Tingling lips</li>
<li>Pale skin</li>
</ul>

<p>Later symptoms (brain not getting enough glucose):</p>

<ul>
<li>Difficulty concentrating</li>
<li>Confusion</li>
<li>Slurred speech</li>
<li>Drowsiness</li>
<li>Blurred vision</li>
<li>Unusual behaviour - sometimes mistaken for drunkenness</li>
</ul>

<p>Severe hypo:</p>

<ul>
<li>Loss of consciousness</li>
<li>Seizure</li>
<li>Cannot self-treat</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common triggers</h2>

<ul>
<li>Missed or late meal</li>
<li>Eating less than usual</li>
<li>More exercise than planned</li>
<li>Too much insulin or sulfonylurea</li>
<li>Alcohol, especially without food</li>
<li>Hot weather (insulin absorbs faster)</li>
<li>Some illnesses (vomiting, diarrhoea)</li>
<li>Some medicines</li>
</ul>

<h2>The 15-15 rule</h2>

<p>For mild to moderate hypo (you can swallow safely):</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Stop what you are doing.</strong> Sit down</li>
<li><strong>Test if you can</strong> - confirms hypo, but if you cannot test, treat anyway</li>
<li><strong>Take 15 g of fast-acting carbohydrate</strong>:
  <ul>
  <li>5-7 jelly beans or glucose tablets</li>
  <li>100 mL (a small glass) of regular soft drink (not diet)</li>
  <li>1 tablespoon of honey or sugar in water</li>
  <li>3-4 teaspoons of jam</li>
  <li>150 mL of fruit juice</li>
  </ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Wait 15 minutes</strong></li>
<li><strong>Test again</strong>. If still under 4 mmol/L, repeat with another 15 g</li>
<li><strong>Once recovered</strong>, eat a longer-acting snack (toast, crackers and cheese, sandwich) - especially if your next meal is more than an hour away or you are on intermediate or long-acting insulin</li>
</ol>

<p>Avoid chocolate or biscuits - the fat slows sugar absorption when you need fast action.</p>

<h2>Severe hypo - what others should do</h2>

<p>If the person is unconscious or fitting:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Do not put anything in their mouth</strong> - choking risk</li>
<li><strong>Call 111</strong></li>
<li><strong>Place in recovery position</strong> (on their side)</li>
<li>If you have <strong>glucagon</strong> (injection or nasal spray), use it - your GP can prescribe this for high-risk patients</li>
<li>Stay with them until help arrives or they recover</li>
</ol>

<p>Once awake, give a sugary drink, then a longer-acting carb.</p>

<h2>Glucagon - the rescue medicine</h2>

<p>Glucagon raises blood sugar by releasing it from the liver. Available as:</p>

<ul>
<li>GlucaGen kit (injection) - prescribed for high-risk patients</li>
<li>Baqsimi (nasal spray) - easier to use, available privately</li>
</ul>

<p>Family or close contacts should know where it is and how to use it. Replace before expiry.</p>

<h2>Hypos at night</h2>

<p>Night-time hypos are scary. Signs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Sweating, restless sleep</li>
<li>Bad dreams</li>
<li>Waking with morning headache</li>
<li>High morning sugar (the body bounces back)</li>
</ul>

<p>If you suspect night hypos, set an alarm to test at 3 am for a few nights, or use a CGM with low alarms. Discuss with your GP - usually treated by reducing evening insulin or eating a small bedtime snack.</p>

<h2>Driving</h2>

<p>The law in NZ requires:</p>

<ul>
<li>Test blood glucose within 2 hours of driving</li>
<li>5 mmol/L or above: safe to drive</li>
<li>4-5 mmol/L: have a snack first</li>
<li>Below 4: do not drive. Treat the hypo and wait at least 45 minutes before driving</li>
</ul>

<p>Carry fast-acting carbs (jelly beans, glucose tablets) in the car at all times.</p>

<p>If you have had two or more severe hypos requiring help, or significant hypo unawareness, NZTA may require a medical clearance.</p>

<h2>Hypo unawareness</h2>

<p>Some people - especially those with long-standing diabetes or frequent hypos - lose the warning signs and only become aware when the brain is affected. This is called hypo unawareness and is dangerous.</p>

<p>Recovery is possible:</p>

<ul>
<li>Avoid all hypos for 2-4 weeks - the warning signs return</li>
<li>Loosen targets slightly</li>
<li>CGM with alarms is hugely helpful</li>
<li>Diabetes specialist input</li>
</ul>

<h2>After a hypo</h2>

<ul>
<li>Eat a longer-acting snack/meal as above</li>
<li>Note what triggered it</li>
<li>Tell your diabetes team if hypos are happening more than once a week, or any severe hypo</li>
<li>Adjust medication or routine to reduce risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Repeated hypos in type 2 diabetes</h2>

<p>If you are on metformin alone and feel "hypo", it is rarely true hypoglycaemia - test to confirm. True hypos in type 2 are usually from insulin or sulfonylureas.</p>

<p>Frequent hypos on a sulfonylurea may indicate the dose is too high or the medicine is not the right fit - discuss switching to a class without hypo risk (DPP-4 inhibitor, SGLT2 inhibitor, GLP-1 agonist) with your GP.</p>
