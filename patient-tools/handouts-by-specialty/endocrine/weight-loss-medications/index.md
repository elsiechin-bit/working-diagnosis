<p>Weight management medications are not a quick fix, and they are not for everyone. But for the right person they can make a meaningful difference, shifting the metabolic dial enough to make sustained lifestyle change more achievable. This handout covers the options currently available in New Zealand and what the evidence actually shows.</p>

<p>No medication works in isolation. Every drug in this class has been trialled alongside dietary and behavioural support, and that is where most of the benefit lives. Stopping medication also typically leads to partial or complete weight regain over time. This is not a personal failure but a physiological reality of how weight regulation works.</p>

<h2>Who might be considered?</h2>

<p>General prescribing criteria are a BMI of 30&#8201;kg/m&#178; or above, or 27&#8201;kg/m&#178; or above with a weight-related health condition such as type 2 diabetes, high blood pressure, obstructive sleep apnoea, or significant joint disease. Your GP will look at the full picture, not just a number.</p>

<h2>Orlistat (Xenical / Alli)</h2>

<p>Blocks the gut enzyme that breaks down dietary fat, so roughly a third of the fat you eat passes through unabsorbed. Does not affect appetite. It is the only Pharmac-funded option for weight management.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Side effects.</strong> Loose oily stools, urgency, and flatulence, directly proportional to how much fat you eat. High-fat meals make these significantly worse. Take a daily multivitamin at a different time to the tablet, as fat-soluble vitamins are less well absorbed.</li>
  <li><strong>Availability.</strong> 120&#8201;mg on prescription (Pharmac-funded with criteria). Also available over the counter at 60&#8201;mg (Alli) with pharmacist involvement, though less effective.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Liraglutide (Saxenda)</h2>

<p>A GLP-1 receptor agonist: it mimics a gut hormone released after eating, slowing gastric emptying and signalling fullness to the brain. Injected daily using a pre-filled pen. The SCALE trial found average weight loss of 8.4% of body weight over 56 weeks, compared with 2.8% on placebo.<sup>1</sup></p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Side effects.</strong> Nausea is very common during the dose escalation phase (typically 5 weeks) and usually settles. Vomiting, constipation, and diarrhoea also occur. Not suitable if you have a personal or family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN2.</li>
  <li><strong>Availability.</strong> Prescription only. Not currently Pharmac-funded for weight management. Must be paid for privately; your GP can discuss costs.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Semaglutide (Ozempic / Wegovy)</h2>

<p>A longer-acting GLP-1 agonist, injected once weekly. At the 2.4&#8201;mg obesity dose (Wegovy), the STEP 1 trial found average weight loss of 14.9% of body weight over 68 weeks. Nearly a third of participants lost more than 20% of their weight. This is the largest effect size of any currently available medication for weight management.<sup>2</sup></p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Side effects.</strong> Similar to liraglutide. Nausea during dose escalation is common but manageable for most people. Same thyroid cancer contraindications apply.</li>
  <li><strong>NZ availability.</strong> Ozempic (lower doses) is Pharmac-funded for type 2 diabetes with specific criteria. Wegovy (the obesity dose) was not funded and had limited availability in New Zealand in early 2025 due to global supply pressures. Your GP will have current information.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Phentermine</h2>

<p>A stimulant that suppresses appetite by increasing noradrenaline in the brain. Produces modest short-term weight loss of around 3&#8211;5&#8201;kg over 12 weeks. Restricted to short-term use as it tends to lose effectiveness as the body adapts.<sup>3</sup></p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Side effects.</strong> Raised heart rate and blood pressure, insomnia, dry mouth, and restlessness. Not suitable for people with cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, or hyperthyroidism.</li>
  <li><strong>Availability.</strong> Prescription only. Schedule 4 controlled substance in NZ. Less commonly used as a first-line option given the short-term restriction and stimulant profile.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<ul>
  <li><strong>It takes time.</strong> GLP-1 agonists require gradual dose escalation over several weeks. Response is assessed at 12&#8211;16 weeks at the full dose. If you have not lost at least 5% of body weight by then, that medication is unlikely to be the right fit.</li>
  <li><strong>Not everyone responds.</strong> A meaningful proportion of people lose little weight on any given medication. If one option is not working, there are others to discuss.</li>
  <li><strong>Weight regain is common after stopping.</strong> Studies following people who stopped semaglutide after 68 weeks found most regained the majority of lost weight within a year.<sup>4</sup> Whether long-term use makes sense is a conversation to have with your GP.</li>
  <li><strong>Cost matters.</strong> Access to the more effective modern medications is largely unfunded in New Zealand. If cost is a barrier, say so explicitly. Your GP can work through what is feasible.</li>
</ul>
