<p class="handout-back"><a href="/patient-tools/handouts-by-specialty/endocrine/hypothyroidism/">&larr; All handouts</a></p>

Hypothyroidism is when the thyroid gland (in the neck) does not produce enough thyroid hormone. It is one of the most common hormone problems, especially in women over 40. Symptoms are often vague and develop slowly. With daily replacement hormone, the vast majority of people feel completely normal.

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<ul>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Weight gain (modest, usually 2-5 kg)</li>
<li>Feeling cold when others are not</li>
<li>Dry skin and brittle hair</li>
<li>Hair thinning, including outer eyebrows</li>
<li>Constipation</li>
<li>Heavy or irregular periods</li>
<li>Slow heart rate</li>
<li>Puffy face, eyes</li>
<li>Hoarse voice</li>
<li>Memory and concentration problems ("brain fog")</li>
<li>Low mood</li>
<li>Muscle aches</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms develop over months. Many people put them down to ageing or stress. Not everyone has all symptoms.</p>

<h2>Common causes</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Hashimoto's thyroiditis</strong> - autoimmune destruction of the thyroid. The most common cause. Often family history</li>
<li><strong>Surgery or radioactive iodine</strong> after treatment of overactive thyroid or thyroid cancer</li>
<li><strong>Some medicines</strong> - amiodarone, lithium, immunotherapy</li>
<li><strong>Iodine deficiency</strong> - rare in NZ since iodised salt and bread fortification</li>
<li><strong>Postpartum thyroiditis</strong> - up to 5% of women after pregnancy; often resolves</li>
<li><strong>Congenital</strong> - newborns are screened</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Two blood tests:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>TSH</strong> (thyroid stimulating hormone) - high in hypothyroidism</li>
<li><strong>Free T4</strong> - low in overt hypothyroidism, normal in subclinical</li>
</ul>

<p>Thyroid antibodies (TPO) often confirm Hashimoto's.</p>

<p>"Subclinical hypothyroidism" means TSH is mildly high but T4 is still in range. It does not always need treatment - the thresholds depend on age, symptoms, and antibodies.</p>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Levothyroxine (Synthroid, Eltroxin) is the standard. It is a synthetic version of the body's natural T4 hormone. See our <a href="/handouts/levothyroxine/">levothyroxine handout</a> for tips.</p>

<p>Starting dose:</p>

<ul>
<li>Most healthy adults: 50-100 mcg daily</li>
<li>Older patients or heart disease: start lower, e.g. 25 mcg, and increase slowly</li>
<li>Pregnancy: dose typically increased</li>
</ul>

<p>TSH is rechecked at 6-8 weeks after starting or changing dose. Adjustments are usually 12.5-25 mcg increments.</p>

<h2>Targets</h2>

<ul>
<li>Most adults: TSH 0.5-4.0</li>
<li>Pregnancy: TSH usually below 2.5 in first trimester, below 3.0 later</li>
<li>Older adults: TSH 1-5 may be appropriate</li>
</ul>

<h2>How long until I feel better?</h2>

<p>Most symptoms improve over 2-3 months as TSH normalises. Some symptoms (hair, skin, memory) can take 6-12 months. If you still feel symptomatic after several months at a normal TSH, ask your GP - some people have other causes contributing (B12 deficiency, depression, sleep apnoea, iron deficiency).</p>

<h2>Lifelong treatment</h2>

<p>Once started, levothyroxine is usually lifelong. The thyroid does not "remember how" to work after years of medication. The exception is some short-term thyroiditis (postpartum, post-viral) which can resolve.</p>

<h2>What does and does not help</h2>

<p>Levothyroxine works for almost everyone. There is no evidence that:</p>

<ul>
<li>Natural desiccated thyroid (Armour, NDT) is better than levothyroxine for most people</li>
<li>Adding T3 helps the majority - the trials are mixed; rarely tried by specialists in selected patients</li>
<li>Iodine supplements help (excess can worsen thyroid disease)</li>
<li>Special diets cure or treat hypothyroidism</li>
<li>Selenium consistently helps - selenium deficiency is rare in NZ</li>
</ul>

<p>Be cautious of "thyroid wellness" supplements - some contain undisclosed thyroid hormone or interact with treatment.</p>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Hypothyroidism in pregnancy raises the risk of miscarriage, prematurity, and developmental issues. Treatment must be optimised. Tell your GP early in pregnancy - dose typically rises 30-50%. Aim for TSH in the lower half of normal.</p>

<h2>Other autoimmune conditions</h2>

<p>If you have Hashimoto's, you have a slightly higher risk of:</p>

<ul>
<li>Coeliac disease</li>
<li>Type 1 diabetes</li>
<li>Vitiligo</li>
<li>Pernicious anaemia (B12 deficiency)</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP may screen for these if symptoms suggest.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP</h2>

<ul>
<li>Annual TSH check once stable</li>
<li>Symptoms of overtreatment (palpitations, tremor, anxiety, weight loss, heat intolerance)</li>
<li>Persistent symptoms despite treatment</li>
<li>Pregnancy planning or pregnancy</li>
<li>New medicines that may interact (some psychiatric medicines, oestrogen)</li>
<li>Significant weight change</li>
</ul>
