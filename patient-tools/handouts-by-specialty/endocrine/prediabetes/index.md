Prediabetes means your blood sugar is higher than normal but not yet at the level of type 2 diabetes. It is a warning sign, not a diagnosis to fear. Most people with prediabetes can prevent or delay diabetes through changes that are small but consistent.

<h2>What the numbers mean</h2>

<p>Diagnosis is based on HbA1c (a blood test reflecting average blood sugar over the past 3 months):</p>

<ul>
<li>Normal: HbA1c below 41 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Prediabetes: HbA1c 41-49 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Diabetes: HbA1c 50 mmol/mol or higher</li>
</ul>

<p>Prediabetes is sometimes also diagnosed by fasting glucose (6.1-6.9 mmol/L) or a glucose tolerance test (2-hour reading 7.8-11.0 mmol/L).</p>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>Without changes, around 1 in 3 people with prediabetes develop type 2 diabetes within 5 years.&sup1; Cardiovascular risk also rises.</p>

<p>The good news: prevention works. The Diabetes Prevention Program showed lifestyle changes reduced progression to diabetes by 58% over 3 years - more than metformin (31%).&sup2;</p>

<h2>Five things that move the dial</h2>

<p><strong>1. Lose 5-10% of body weight</strong> if overweight. The single most powerful change. Even 5 kg can make a difference.</p>

<p><strong>2. Move 150 minutes a week.</strong> Brisk walking counts. Spread across most days. Add 2 sessions of strength work weekly.</p>

<p><strong>3. Eat differently.</strong> Reduce sugary drinks, sweets, white bread, white rice, pastries, takeaways. Increase vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish, lean protein. The Mediterranean and DASH eating patterns both have strong evidence.</p>

<p><strong>4. Sleep 7-8 hours</strong> regularly. Poor sleep raises insulin resistance.</p>

<p><strong>5. Stop smoking</strong> - smokers progress faster.</p>

<h2>Specific food changes that help most</h2>

<ul>
<li>Swap sugary drinks for water, tea, or unsweetened drinks</li>
<li>Cut back on portion of starchy carbs (rice, bread, potato) - aim for 1/4 of plate</li>
<li>Half the plate vegetables</li>
<li>Quarter of the plate protein</li>
<li>Berries and yoghurt instead of biscuits and cake</li>
<li>Choose whole-grain bread over white</li>
<li>Limit alcohol - 10 standard drinks max per week</li>
</ul>

<h2>Should I take metformin?</h2>

<p>Sometimes. Metformin is considered for prediabetes if:</p>

<ul>
<li>HbA1c is at the higher end (45-49 mmol/mol)</li>
<li>BMI over 35</li>
<li>Under age 60</li>
<li>History of gestational diabetes</li>
<li>Lifestyle changes have not worked</li>
</ul>

<p>Discuss with your GP. Metformin is generally well tolerated, helps weight a little, and is funded.</p>

<h2>Other tests your GP may do</h2>

<ul>
<li>Lipids (cholesterol)</li>
<li>Blood pressure</li>
<li>Liver function (fatty liver is common)</li>
<li>Cardiovascular risk calculation (PREDICT)</li>
</ul>

<p>Treating high blood pressure and high cholesterol matters - these often coexist with prediabetes.</p>

<h2>Follow-up</h2>

<p>Repeat HbA1c every 6-12 months to watch the trend. If HbA1c drops back to normal, continue habits and recheck yearly. If it rises into the diabetes range, your GP will discuss treatment.</p>

<h2>Higher-risk groups in NZ</h2>

<p>Maori, Pacific, and South Asian people develop type 2 diabetes at lower BMIs and earlier ages. Annual screening from age 30 (or earlier with risk factors) is recommended in these groups.</p>

<h2>Free programmes in NZ</h2>

<p>Several DHB and PHO programmes offer free diabetes prevention support - dietitian, lifestyle coaching, group programmes. Ask your GP what is available locally. Green Prescription is a free 3-month exercise and lifestyle programme available nationwide.</p>
