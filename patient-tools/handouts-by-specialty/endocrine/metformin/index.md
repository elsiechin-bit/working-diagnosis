Metformin has been used for type 2 diabetes for over 60 years. It is the most prescribed diabetes medicine in the world. It is safe, effective, cheap, and even has a small benefit on weight. The main hurdle is gut side effects, which often settle and can be reduced by simple steps.

<h2>How it works</h2>

<p>Metformin reduces the amount of glucose your liver releases overnight, and helps your body use insulin more efficiently. It does not cause hypos when used alone (unlike insulin or sulfonylureas).</p>

<h2>What to expect</h2>

<ul>
<li>Lowers HbA1c by around 10-15 mmol/mol</li>
<li>Slight weight loss (1-3 kg) for many people</li>
<li>Reduces cardiovascular events independent of glucose effect</li>
<li>May modestly reduce some cancer risks (still being studied)</li>
</ul>

<h2>How to take it</h2>

<ul>
<li>Always take with food - usually breakfast and dinner</li>
<li>Start at 500 mg once daily</li>
<li>Increase weekly: 500 mg twice daily, then 1 g twice daily, up to 1 g three times daily</li>
<li>Modified-release (extended-release) versions are taken once a day with the evening meal</li>
</ul>

<p>Slow titration is the single best way to minimise side effects. There is rarely any reason to push the dose up faster than weekly.</p>

<h2>Side effects - the good news</h2>

<ul>
<li>Most side effects (nausea, loose stools, abdominal cramps, bloating, metallic taste) settle within 2-4 weeks</li>
<li>Around 10% of people cannot tolerate immediate-release metformin even after slow titration</li>
<li>Modified-release metformin is much better tolerated</li>
</ul>

<p>If you have struggled with standard metformin, ask about modified-release.</p>

<h2>Tips to reduce side effects</h2>

<ul>
<li>Always take with food, ideally part-way through the meal</li>
<li>Slow the dose increase</li>
<li>Switch to modified-release (XR/MR)</li>
<li>Try splitting your usual dose into smaller more frequent doses</li>
<li>Many side effects fade after 2-4 weeks</li>
</ul>

<h2>Long-term considerations</h2>

<p><strong>Vitamin B12 deficiency.</strong> Long-term metformin (over 4-5 years) reduces B12 absorption in some people. Your GP will check B12 every 1-2 years, especially if you have neuropathy symptoms or anaemia. B12 deficiency is easily treated with tablets or injections.</p>

<p><strong>Kidney function.</strong> Metformin is cleared by kidneys, so doses are reduced as kidney function falls.</p>

<ul>
<li>eGFR over 60: full dose</li>
<li>eGFR 45-59: usually full dose with monitoring</li>
<li>eGFR 30-44: half-dose maximum (around 1 g daily)</li>
<li>eGFR under 30: stop metformin</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP will check kidney function annually.</p>

<h2>When to stop temporarily</h2>

<p>Stop metformin and contact your GP if you:</p>

<ul>
<li>Have severe vomiting, diarrhoea, or are dehydrated</li>
<li>Are admitted to hospital with serious illness</li>
<li>Are having an iodine contrast scan (CT with dye) - resume 48 hours later</li>
<li>Are having major surgery (your team will guide)</li>
<li>Develop severe kidney impairment</li>
</ul>

<p>Restart once you are well, eating and drinking normally, and your GP says.</p>

<h2>Lactic acidosis - rare but important</h2>

<p>A very rare metformin complication. Symptoms include severe muscle pain, deep rapid breathing, severe abdominal pain, and feeling very unwell. Almost only seen in people with severe kidney impairment or severe illness. Stop metformin and seek emergency care.</p>

<h2>Drug interactions</h2>

<p>Few important interactions, but be cautious with:</p>

<ul>
<li>Contrast dye for CT scans (above)</li>
<li>Heavy alcohol use - increases lactic acidosis risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Metformin is considered safe in pregnancy and is sometimes used in gestational diabetes and PCOS. Your GP or obstetrician will guide.</p>

<h2>Stopping metformin</h2>

<p>Most people stay on metformin long-term. It is rarely stopped because of "side effects" once tolerated. Reasons to stop include severe kidney impairment, severe intolerance, or other compelling reason.</p>

<h2>What metformin will not do</h2>

<ul>
<li>Replace lifestyle changes - diet, exercise, weight matter as much</li>
<li>Cure diabetes - it controls but does not reverse</li>
<li>Cause hypos when used alone (only when combined with insulin or sulfonylureas)</li>
</ul>
