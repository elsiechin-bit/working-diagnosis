Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the immune system destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. Unlike type 2 diabetes, it is not caused by lifestyle, weight, or food. Most people are diagnosed in childhood or young adulthood, but it can occur at any age. Type 1 needs insulin for life. Modern insulin therapy, continuous glucose monitoring, and insulin pumps now allow most people to live full, active lives.

<h2>How it differs from type 2</h2>

<ul>
<li>Caused by autoimmune destruction of beta cells, not lifestyle</li>
<li>Usually presents acutely with weight loss, thirst, polyuria, sometimes diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)</li>
<li>Always needs insulin</li>
<li>Tablets (metformin etc.) are not the main treatment</li>
<li>Glucose can swing more dramatically</li>
</ul>

<h2>Diagnosis clues</h2>

<ul>
<li>Sudden onset over weeks</li>
<li>Significant weight loss</li>
<li>Severe thirst, frequent urination, fatigue</li>
<li>HbA1c usually high (often 70+ mmol/mol)</li>
<li>Glucose at diagnosis often very high (15-30+)</li>
<li>Ketones positive on urine or blood test</li>
<li>Younger age (but adults of any age can develop type 1, sometimes called LADA - latent autoimmune diabetes of adulthood)</li>
<li>Antibodies (GAD, IA-2, ZnT8) positive on blood test</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are unsure whether you have type 1 or type 2, ask your GP about antibody testing.</p>

<h2>The five things to learn early</h2>

<ol>
<li>How to inject insulin (or use a pump)</li>
<li>How to count carbohydrates and match insulin to meals</li>
<li>How to recognise and treat hypos</li>
<li>How to recognise and respond to high glucose and ketones (sick day rules)</li>
<li>How to use a glucose meter or CGM</li>
</ol>

<p>Diabetes education is essential at diagnosis. Most DHBs have a diabetes nurse educator who provides this over several sessions, often with the dietitian and family.</p>

<h2>Insulin types</h2>

<p>Most adults use a basal-bolus regimen:</p>

<p><strong>Basal (long-acting) insulin</strong> - 1-2 injections a day to cover background needs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Glargine (Lantus, Optisulin) - once daily</li>
<li>Detemir (Levemir) - usually twice daily</li>
<li>Glargine U-300 (Toujeo) - once daily, smoother</li>
<li>Degludec (Tresiba) - longest-acting</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Bolus (rapid-acting) insulin</strong> - injected before meals to cover food, plus correction doses:</p>

<ul>
<li>Aspart (NovoRapid)</li>
<li>Lispro (Humalog)</li>
<li>Glulisine (Apidra)</li>
</ul>

<p>Insulin is delivered via pen injector or pump. Pen needles are tiny (4-6 mm) and almost painless when used correctly.</p>

<h2>Insulin pumps and CGMs - funded in NZ</h2>

<p>Both are now PHARMAC-funded for eligible type 1 patients (since 2024).</p>

<ul>
<li>Continuous glucose monitors (Freestyle Libre 2, Dexcom G7) read glucose every minute and alert for highs and lows</li>
<li>Insulin pumps deliver continuous basal plus on-demand bolus insulin</li>
<li>Hybrid closed-loop systems link CGM to pump and partly automate dose adjustment</li>
</ul>

<p>These devices can transform diabetes life. Discuss eligibility with your diabetes team.</p>

<h2>Carb counting</h2>

<p>The main daily skill: count grams of carbohydrate in each meal and inject insulin in proportion. Each person has an "insulin-to-carb ratio" - typically 1 unit of insulin per 8-15 grams of carb. The dietitian and diabetes nurse will guide you.</p>

<p>Apps (Carbs and Cals, MyFitnessPal) help with calorie and carb counting until familiar.</p>

<h2>Sick day rules</h2>

<p>When unwell:</p>

<ul>
<li>Never stop basal insulin - illness raises glucose even when not eating</li>
<li>Test glucose every 2-4 hours</li>
<li>Test ketones if glucose is over 14 or you feel unwell</li>
<li>Drink fluids - sugary if low, sugar-free if high</li>
<li>Take small frequent carbs if not eating - juice, soup, ice blocks</li>
<li>Have a written sick day plan from your diabetes team</li>
</ul>

<p>Call your team or 111 for: vomiting unable to keep fluids down, ketones positive with unwell feeling, breathing fast and deep, drowsiness, persistent very high glucose despite correction.</p>

<h2>Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)</h2>

<p>A serious emergency. Signs:</p>

<ul>
<li>Very high glucose (usually over 14)</li>
<li>Ketones in blood or urine</li>
<li>Nausea, vomiting</li>
<li>Abdominal pain</li>
<li>Deep rapid breathing</li>
<li>Fruity smelling breath</li>
<li>Drowsiness, confusion</li>
</ul>

<p>Call 111. DKA needs hospital treatment with intravenous fluids and insulin.</p>

<h2>Living well with type 1</h2>

<p><strong>Exercise</strong> - planned and unplanned activity affects glucose. Generally needs less basal/bolus and small carb top-ups. Your team will guide.</p>

<p><strong>Alcohol</strong> - reduce evening dose to allow for delayed hypos. Always eat with alcohol.</p>

<p><strong>Driving</strong> - test before driving; over 5 mmol/L safe; carry fast-acting carbs.</p>

<p><strong>Travel</strong> - take more insulin than you need; carry in hand luggage; medical letter for security; account for time zones with long-acting insulin.</p>

<p><strong>Pregnancy</strong> - plan pregnancy in advance; tight pre-pregnancy HbA1c (under 48) is ideal. Specialist team during pregnancy.</p>

<p><strong>Mental health</strong> - "diabetes distress" is real and common. Tell your team if you are burning out. Counselling helps.</p>

<h2>Long-term care</h2>

<p>Annual reviews include:</p>

<ul>
<li>HbA1c every 3 months</li>
<li>Eye photo screening every 1-2 years</li>
<li>Urine ACR and kidney function annually</li>
<li>Foot check annually</li>
<li>Lipids and blood pressure</li>
<li>Coeliac and thyroid antibody screening (more common with type 1)</li>
<li>Diabetes nurse, GP, and dietitian as needed</li>
</ul>

<h2>Family members</h2>

<p>First-degree relatives have a slightly higher risk of type 1 diabetes - roughly 5% lifetime risk for siblings. Know the symptoms (thirst, weight loss, frequent urination) and see a GP early if they appear.</p>
