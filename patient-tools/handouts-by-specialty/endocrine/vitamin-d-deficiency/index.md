Vitamin D is a hormone made in the skin from sunlight, with a small amount from food. It supports calcium absorption, bone health, muscle strength, and the immune system. Many New Zealanders have lower-than-ideal levels in winter, especially in the South Island. Most people do not need testing or supplements - but some specific groups do.

<h2>What vitamin D does</h2>

<ul>
<li>Helps absorb calcium and phosphate for strong bones</li>
<li>Supports muscle function</li>
<li>Plays a role in immune regulation</li>
<li>Severe deficiency causes rickets in children, osteomalacia in adults</li>
</ul>

<h2>Symptoms of deficiency</h2>

<p>Mild to moderate deficiency is usually silent. Severe or prolonged deficiency can cause:</p>

<ul>
<li>Bone pain, especially hips and lower back</li>
<li>Muscle weakness, especially proximal (thighs, shoulders)</li>
<li>Increased fall risk in older adults</li>
<li>Children: rickets - bowed legs, delayed growth, irritability</li>
</ul>

<p>Many vague symptoms (tiredness, low mood, generalised aches) are sometimes blamed on vitamin D but often have other causes. Treating low vitamin D rarely fixes them on its own.</p>

<h2>Who is at higher risk?</h2>

<ul>
<li>Older adults, especially those housebound or in rest homes</li>
<li>People with darker skin (more melanin reduces vitamin D production)</li>
<li>People who fully cover for cultural or religious reasons</li>
<li>Liver or kidney disease</li>
<li>Coeliac disease, Crohn's, after gastric bypass surgery</li>
<li>People taking some medicines (long-term steroids, anticonvulsants)</li>
<li>Exclusively breastfed babies (especially those of darker skin or vitamin D deficient mothers)</li>
<li>South Island residents in winter, especially below Wellington</li>
</ul>

<h2>NZ levels</h2>

<p>Studies show roughly 1 in 20 NZ adults has vitamin D below 25 nmol/L (deficient), and around 1 in 3 have levels below 50 nmol/L (insufficient).&sup1; Levels are higher in summer and lower in winter.</p>

<h2>Testing - who and when</h2>

<p>The Ministry of Health does not recommend routine vitamin D testing. Test is justified for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Older adults with osteoporosis or recurrent falls</li>
<li>Significant unexplained bone pain or muscle weakness</li>
<li>Conditions causing malabsorption</li>
<li>Children with suspected rickets</li>
<li>People with chronic kidney disease</li>
<li>Hyperparathyroidism</li>
</ul>

<p>For most healthy people, supplementation without testing is reasonable if at risk.</p>

<h2>Targets</h2>

<ul>
<li>Below 25 nmol/L: deficient</li>
<li>25-50 nmol/L: insufficient for many</li>
<li>50-75 nmol/L: adequate for most</li>
<li>Above 75 nmol/L: optimal in some contexts</li>
<li>Above 250 nmol/L: too much</li>
</ul>

<h2>Sun exposure - moderate is enough</h2>

<p>In NZ summer (October-March), 6-15 minutes of sun on face, arms, and hands a few times a week is enough for most fair-skinned people. People with darker skin need 3-6 times longer. Avoid sunburn at all costs - skin cancer risk is much greater than vitamin D shortage risk in NZ.</p>

<p>In winter, especially south of Wellington, you cannot make enough vitamin D from sunlight - food and supplements become the source.</p>

<h2>Food sources</h2>

<p>Vitamin D in food is limited:</p>

<ul>
<li>Oily fish (salmon, sardines, tuna, mackerel)</li>
<li>Eggs</li>
<li>Liver</li>
<li>Fortified products (some milks, margarines)</li>
<li>Mushrooms exposed to UV light</li>
</ul>

<p>For most people, food alone cannot fully cover needs in winter.</p>

<h2>Supplements</h2>

<p>Cholecalciferol (D3) is the standard. Available over the counter and on prescription.</p>

<p>Typical doses:</p>

<ul>
<li>Maintenance for at-risk adults: 800-2000 IU daily, or 50,000 IU monthly</li>
<li>Treatment of confirmed deficiency: 50,000 IU weekly for 6-8 weeks, then maintenance</li>
<li>Children: 400-1000 IU daily depending on age and risk</li>
<li>Pregnancy and breastfeeding: 400-1000 IU daily</li>
</ul>

<p>In NZ, the funded preparation is "ColecalciferolGS" 1.25 mg (50,000 IU) capsules - one monthly is a common maintenance regimen.</p>

<p>Excess vitamin D is harmful. Do not exceed 4000 IU/day or 50,000 IU monthly without medical advice.</p>

<h2>Babies</h2>

<p>Routinely recommended for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Exclusively breastfed babies of mothers with darker skin or risk factors</li>
<li>Premature babies</li>
</ul>

<p>Standard advice in NZ: 400 IU daily of vitamin D drops to such babies for the first year. Discuss with your Plunket nurse or GP.</p>

<h2>What vitamin D will not do</h2>

<p>Despite popular claims, robust evidence does not support vitamin D supplementation for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Preventing colds (small modest effect at best)</li>
<li>Treating depression in vitamin D-replete people</li>
<li>Cardiovascular protection in adequate-D people</li>
<li>Cancer prevention (modest, mixed evidence)</li>
</ul>

<p>Vitamin D works to fix vitamin D deficiency. It is not a panacea.</p>
