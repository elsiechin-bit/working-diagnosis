Levothyroxine is one of the most common prescriptions in the world. It works very well when taken correctly. The trick is consistency - the time of day, what you take it with, and the brand all affect how your body absorbs it. Small changes can shift your TSH and how you feel.

<h2>How to take it</h2>

<p>The classical advice is:</p>

<ul>
<li>Once daily on an empty stomach</li>
<li>30-60 minutes before breakfast or any other food/drink (except plain water)</li>
<li>With a glass of water</li>
</ul>

<p>If mornings do not work, an alternative:</p>

<ul>
<li>At bedtime, at least 3-4 hours after eating, with water only</li>
</ul>

<p>Pick whichever fits your routine and stick to it - consistency is more important than the specific time.</p>

<h2>What can interfere with absorption</h2>

<p>Wait at least 4 hours between levothyroxine and:</p>

<ul>
<li>Calcium tablets and calcium-rich foods (milk, cheese, yoghurt) - the biggest interaction</li>
<li>Iron supplements</li>
<li>Magnesium and zinc</li>
<li>Multivitamins</li>
<li>Antacids and reflux medicines (omeprazole, pantoprazole reduce absorption modestly - take levothyroxine first thing)</li>
<li>Coffee and espresso (reduces absorption by up to 30%)</li>
<li>Soy products and very high-fibre meals</li>
<li>Cholestyramine, sucralfate</li>
</ul>

<p>Plain water is fine.</p>

<h2>Brand consistency matters</h2>

<p>Different brands and even different batches of levothyroxine can have slightly different bioavailability. In NZ, Synthroid and Eltroxin are the main brands - both are funded but with different formulations. Try not to switch back and forth without a reason.</p>

<p>If your pharmacy switches the brand on you and you notice symptom changes, mention this to your GP - they can prescribe a specific brand.</p>

<h2>Missed doses</h2>

<ul>
<li>If you miss a dose and remember within a few hours, take it</li>
<li>If you miss a whole day, take the missed dose the next morning along with the regular one (or take it with food and skip - safety is more important than perfection)</li>
<li>Do not double up beyond two days' worth</li>
</ul>

<p>Levothyroxine has a long half-life (about a week), so the body buffers occasional missed doses.</p>

<h2>Side effects</h2>

<p>The right dose feels normal. Symptoms of <strong>too much</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li>Palpitations, fast heart rate</li>
<li>Tremor</li>
<li>Anxiety</li>
<li>Weight loss</li>
<li>Diarrhoea</li>
<li>Feeling hot or sweating</li>
<li>Insomnia</li>
</ul>

<p>Symptoms of <strong>too little</strong>:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tiredness</li>
<li>Cold intolerance</li>
<li>Constipation</li>
<li>Hair thinning</li>
<li>Slow recovery from illness</li>
</ul>

<p>If symptoms suggest dose imbalance, tell your GP - a TSH check usually clarifies.</p>

<h2>Storage</h2>

<ul>
<li>Cool, dry place</li>
<li>Not in the bathroom (humidity affects tablets)</li>
<li>Original packaging</li>
<li>Keep out of children's reach</li>
</ul>

<p>Tablets are tiny but can taste bitter if chewed. Swallow whole if possible.</p>

<h2>What changes the dose you need</h2>

<p>Your dose may need adjustment for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pregnancy - usually increases by 30-50%, sometimes within weeks of conception</li>
<li>Significant weight change (over 10%)</li>
<li>Starting oestrogen (HRT, combined contraceptive) - may need a dose increase</li>
<li>Starting some medicines - phenytoin, carbamazepine, rifampicin (raise the dose); newer cancer drugs</li>
<li>New gut conditions affecting absorption (coeliac, lactose intolerance, gastric surgery)</li>
<li>Aging - dose often decreases over decades</li>
</ul>

<h2>Annual review</h2>

<p>Once stable, an annual TSH check is sufficient for most people. Recheck sooner if symptoms change. Bring your levothyroxine bottle (or a list of dose) to every GP visit.</p>

<h2>Travel</h2>

<ul>
<li>Carry plenty in hand luggage</li>
<li>Time zone changes do not matter much for a few days - just take your usual dose at your usual local time</li>
<li>Carry a medical letter if travelling with prescription medicines</li>
</ul>

<h2>What to do if you cannot take tablets</h2>

<p>If you have nausea, vomiting, or cannot swallow:</p>

<ul>
<li>One missed day is fine</li>
<li>Two or more days - contact your GP</li>
<li>Hospital admissions: tell the team about your levothyroxine - it is sometimes given by other routes</li>
</ul>
