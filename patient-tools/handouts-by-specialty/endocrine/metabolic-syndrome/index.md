Metabolic syndrome is the name for a group of risks that often go together: extra weight around the middle, raised blood pressure, raised blood sugar, and unhelpful cholesterol patterns. On its own no part is dramatic, but together they roughly double cardiovascular risk and increase the chance of type 2 diabetes by about five times.&sup1; The good news: the same lifestyle changes improve all the components at once.

<h2>The five components</h2>

<p>You have metabolic syndrome if you meet 3 or more of these:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Waist circumference</strong> - over 94 cm (men) or 80 cm (women), measured at the level of the navel. Lower thresholds for South Asian, Maori, and Pacific people</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure</strong> - 130/85 or higher, or on treatment</li>
<li><strong>Fasting glucose</strong> - 5.6 mmol/L or higher, or on treatment for diabetes</li>
<li><strong>Triglycerides</strong> - 1.7 mmol/L or higher, or on treatment</li>
<li><strong>HDL cholesterol</strong> - below 1.0 mmol/L (men) or 1.3 (women)</li>
</ol>

<p>Why waist not BMI? Visceral fat (around the organs) is the metabolically active fat that drives the syndrome. A normal-BMI person with a thick waist can still have it.</p>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>Compared with people without metabolic syndrome:</p>

<ul>
<li>Around 2x risk of heart attack and stroke</li>
<li>Around 5x risk of developing type 2 diabetes</li>
<li>Higher risk of fatty liver disease</li>
<li>Higher risk of obstructive sleep apnoea</li>
<li>Higher risk of certain cancers (colon, breast, endometrial)</li>
</ul>

<h2>What drives it</h2>

<ul>
<li>Excess weight, especially central</li>
<li>Insulin resistance - the muscles and liver respond less to insulin, so the pancreas makes more</li>
<li>Genetic predisposition - more common in some families and ethnicities</li>
<li>Sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Diet high in refined carbohydrates and processed foods</li>
<li>Poor sleep, sleep apnoea</li>
<li>Chronic stress</li>
<li>Some medicines (some antipsychotics, beta blockers, steroids)</li>
</ul>

<h2>The five things that help most</h2>

<p><strong>1. Lose 5-10% of body weight</strong> if overweight. The most powerful single change. Even modest loss reverses some components.</p>

<p><strong>2. Move 150 minutes a week.</strong> Aerobic plus 2 sessions of resistance training. Walking, swimming, cycling, gardening all count.</p>

<p><strong>3. Eat differently.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Cut sugary drinks completely</li>
<li>Halve the rice/bread/potato portion</li>
<li>Half the plate vegetables</li>
<li>More fish, legumes, nuts</li>
<li>Olive oil instead of butter</li>
<li>Less processed and takeaway food</li>
</ul>

<p>The Mediterranean and DASH diets have the strongest evidence.</p>

<p><strong>4. Sleep 7-8 hours.</strong> Treat sleep apnoea if you have it.</p>

<p><strong>5. Manage stress.</strong> Chronic stress drives cortisol, which worsens insulin resistance.</p>

<p><strong>Plus: stop smoking, limit alcohol to under 10 standard drinks per week.</strong></p>

<h2>Specific medical treatments</h2>

<p>If risk remains high despite lifestyle:</p>

<ul>
<li>Blood pressure medicines if BP persistently above target</li>
<li>Statin if cardiovascular risk over 5-15% (depending on age and ethnicity)</li>
<li>Metformin if HbA1c high or already prediabetes</li>
<li>SGLT2 inhibitor if diabetes plus heart or kidney concerns</li>
<li>GLP-1 agonist (Ozempic, Trulicity) - effective for both weight and glucose</li>
</ul>

<h2>What gets measured</h2>

<p>Annual review with your GP:</p>

<ul>
<li>Weight and waist</li>
<li>Blood pressure</li>
<li>HbA1c or fasting glucose</li>
<li>Lipids</li>
<li>Liver function (fatty liver is part of the syndrome)</li>
<li>Urine albumin/creatinine ratio</li>
<li>Cardiovascular risk calculation (PREDICT)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Reversibility</h2>

<p>Metabolic syndrome is largely reversible. Studies show that 5-10% weight loss combined with regular activity reverses the syndrome in around half of people.&sup2; The longer you have had it, and the more severe each component, the harder it gets.</p>

<h2>Special groups</h2>

<p><strong>Maori and Pacific peoples</strong> develop the syndrome at lower BMIs and earlier ages. Annual review from age 30 is appropriate.</p>

<p><strong>South Asian peoples</strong> develop the syndrome at lower waist measurements (over 90 cm men, over 80 cm women).</p>

<p><strong>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)</strong> - women with PCOS have a higher rate of metabolic syndrome. Same lifestyle approach helps both.</p>

<h2>Liver and the syndrome</h2>

<p>Metabolic syndrome is closely linked to fatty liver disease (now called MASLD - metabolic-associated steatotic liver disease). About 25% of NZ adults have some degree of fatty liver. See our <a href="/handouts/fatty-liver-masld/">MASLD handout</a>.</p>
