Hepatitis B is a viral infection that affects the liver. About 100,000 New Zealanders are living with chronic hepatitis B - many without knowing.&sup1; Most people with chronic infection feel well for years. The aim of care is to monitor the virus, prevent liver damage, and treat when needed. With modern medicines, life expectancy is normal for most.

<h2>How it spreads</h2>

<ul>
<li>Mother to baby at birth - the most common way globally</li>
<li>Sexual contact</li>
<li>Sharing needles or other drug equipment</li>
<li>Blood-to-blood contact - tattoos, piercings, medical procedures with poorly sterilised equipment</li>
<li>Family contact in countries with high prevalence (sharing toothbrushes, razors)</li>
</ul>

<p>Hepatitis B is not spread through casual contact, food, water, sneezing, hugging, or breastfeeding (when the baby is vaccinated).</p>

<h2>Symptoms</h2>

<p>Acute infection (in adults) can cause:</p>

<ul>
<li>Tiredness, malaise</li>
<li>Loss of appetite</li>
<li>Nausea, vomiting</li>
<li>Pain over the liver area</li>
<li>Yellow eyes and skin (jaundice)</li>
<li>Pale stools, dark urine</li>
<li>Joint aches</li>
</ul>

<p>Many people have no symptoms.</p>

<p>Chronic infection (lasting more than 6 months) usually causes no symptoms for years. Without monitoring and treatment, liver damage develops silently over decades.</p>

<h2>The phases of chronic hepatitis B</h2>

<p>Hepatitis B passes through phases over years. Each is monitored differently:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Immune-tolerant</strong> - high virus, normal liver tests, minimal damage</li>
<li><strong>Immune-active</strong> - high virus, raised liver tests, ongoing damage</li>
<li><strong>Inactive carrier</strong> - low virus, normal tests, stable</li>
<li><strong>Reactivation</strong> - virus comes back active later</li>
</ul>

<p>Treatment decisions depend on which phase you are in.</p>

<h2>Tests</h2>

<p>The hepatitis B blood panel includes:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>HBsAg</strong> - if positive, you have chronic infection</li>
<li><strong>HBeAg and HBeAb</strong> - help identify the phase</li>
<li><strong>HBV DNA</strong> - measures virus level</li>
<li><strong>Liver function (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT)</strong></li>
<li><strong>Liver fibrosis assessment</strong> - FibroScan, FIB-4</li>
<li><strong>Liver ultrasound</strong> and AFP - 6-monthly surveillance for liver cancer in those at risk</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p>Not everyone with chronic hepatitis B needs treatment. Treatment is offered to people in the immune-active phase, those with significant fibrosis or cirrhosis, and certain other groups.</p>

<p>Modern treatments are:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Tenofovir disoproxil (TDF)</strong> or <strong>tenofovir alafenamide (TAF)</strong> - daily tablet, usually lifelong</li>
<li><strong>Entecavir</strong> - similar; daily tablet</li>
<li><strong>Pegylated interferon</strong> - 6-12 months of weekly injections; aims for cure but has more side effects</li>
</ul>

<p>These medicines suppress the virus, reduce inflammation, and substantially lower the risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer. Side effects are usually mild.</p>

<h2>Liver cancer surveillance</h2>

<p>People with hepatitis B have an increased risk of liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma), even without cirrhosis. Surveillance with 6-monthly liver ultrasound and AFP blood test is recommended for:</p>

<ul>
<li>All people with cirrhosis</li>
<li>Asian men over 40</li>
<li>Asian women over 50</li>
<li>African origin over 20</li>
<li>Family history of liver cancer</li>
<li>Those with high virus levels or persistent ALT elevation</li>
</ul>

<p>Free hepatitis B services are available in many NZ regions through the Hepatitis Foundation.</p>

<h2>Vaccination - protection for others</h2>

<p>The hepatitis B vaccine is included in the routine NZ immunisation schedule (given at 6 weeks, 3 months, and 5 months). It is highly effective and provides lifelong protection in most people.</p>

<p>If your family members or sexual partners are not immune, they should be vaccinated.</p>

<p>Babies of mothers with hepatitis B receive an extra injection (immunoglobulin) plus the vaccine within hours of birth. With this, transmission is reduced from up to 90% to under 5%.</p>

<h2>Preventing transmission</h2>

<ul>
<li>Tell sexual partners; ensure they are vaccinated or use condoms</li>
<li>Do not share toothbrushes, razors, or nail clippers</li>
<li>Do not donate blood, organs, or sperm</li>
<li>Cover cuts and clean blood spills with bleach</li>
<li>Tell every healthcare provider</li>
<li>Family members and household contacts should be vaccinated</li>
</ul>

<h2>Day-to-day life</h2>

<ul>
<li>Avoid heavy alcohol - even modest drinking can accelerate liver damage</li>
<li>Maintain healthy weight - fatty liver compounds hepatitis B damage</li>
<li>Stay up to date on flu, pneumococcal, COVID, and hepatitis A vaccines</li>
<li>Be careful with herbal remedies - some are toxic to the liver</li>
<li>Avoid unnecessary medicines, especially paracetamol over 3 g per day</li>
<li>Annual review minimum, more often if active treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pregnancy</h2>

<p>Pregnancy with hepatitis B should be managed by an obstetric team experienced in hepatitis B, with input from a hepatologist:</p>

<ul>
<li>Mother's virus level is checked - high levels may require antiviral treatment in late pregnancy</li>
<li>Baby gets immunoglobulin and vaccine within 12 hours of birth</li>
<li>Baby completes the full schedule plus testing at 9 months to confirm immunity</li>
<li>Breastfeeding is safe once baby is vaccinated</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment is lifelong - usually</h2>

<p>For most people, treatment continues indefinitely. Stopping risks reactivation. Some people achieve "functional cure" (loss of HBsAg) and may stop, but this is uncommon.</p>

<p>Newer treatments aiming for cure are in development.</p>
