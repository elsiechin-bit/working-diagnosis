Antibiotics are among the most important inventions in medicine. They have transformed once-deadly infections into easily treated illnesses. But every antibiotic prescription has costs - side effects, disrupted gut flora, and the rise of resistant bacteria. Your GP saying "no antibiotic this time" is not laziness or rationing. It is good medicine, backed by strong evidence.

<h2>Why most respiratory illnesses do not need antibiotics</h2>

<p>The vast majority of sore throats, coughs, sinus problems, and runny noses are caused by viruses. Antibiotics do nothing to viruses. Specifically:</p>

<ul>
<li>The common cold - viral. No antibiotic helps. Symptoms last 7-10 days</li>
<li>Most sore throats - viral. Even strep throat (which is bacterial) usually settles within a few days without antibiotics in healthy adults, though we treat it in some groups for other reasons</li>
<li>Acute bronchitis - usually viral. The cough can last 2-3 weeks. Antibiotics do not shorten it</li>
<li>Acute sinusitis - 95% are viral. Most settle within 10-14 days</li>
<li>Most ear infections in children over 2 - settle within 3-4 days. We watch and wait for many</li>
<li>Most diarrhoea and gastro - viral or self-limiting bacterial</li>
</ul>

<p>If you receive antibiotics for these and feel better in a few days, it is almost always because the illness was running its course - not because the antibiotics worked.</p>

<h2>The harms of unnecessary antibiotics</h2>

<p><strong>Side effects.</strong> About 1 in 5 people on antibiotics develop diarrhoea, nausea, rash, or thrush. Some people develop allergic reactions, occasionally severe.</p>

<p><strong>Clostridioides difficile colitis.</strong> Antibiotics disrupt the gut, allowing this dangerous bacterium to overgrow. C. difficile causes severe, prolonged diarrhoea and is sometimes life-threatening, especially in older people.</p>

<p><strong>Long-term gut effects.</strong> A single course of antibiotics changes the gut microbiome for months, and may not fully recover. There is emerging evidence linking early antibiotic exposure to increased asthma, allergy, and obesity risk.&sup1;</p>

<p><strong>Resistance.</strong> Bacteria evolve to resist antibiotics. Once-easy infections are getting harder to treat. The WHO calls antibiotic resistance one of the top global health threats. NZ has so far done relatively well, but resistance is rising.</p>

<h2>When antibiotics are clearly needed</h2>

<p>Antibiotics save lives in:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pneumonia (community-acquired)</li>
<li>Pyelonephritis (kidney infection)</li>
<li>Cellulitis (deep skin infection)</li>
<li>Bacterial meningitis</li>
<li>Sepsis</li>
<li>Severe UTIs</li>
<li>Strep throat in some groups (children with rheumatic fever risk; severe symptoms)</li>
<li>Whooping cough (pertussis)</li>
<li>Bacterial pneumonia after flu</li>
<li>Some sexually transmitted infections (chlamydia, gonorrhoea, syphilis)</li>
<li>Specific bites and dirty wounds</li>
</ul>

<p>Your GP is alert to these. They will not withhold antibiotics when they are needed.</p>

<h2>Why "just in case" is not safer</h2>

<p>It is tempting to ask for antibiotics just in case. The reasoning: better safe than sorry. The reality:</p>

<ul>
<li>The risks of antibiotic harm are higher than the risk of an untreated mild viral illness</li>
<li>Treating a viral illness with antibiotics gives no benefit</li>
<li>If you do develop a bacterial complication, your GP can prescribe at that point</li>
<li>"Backup" or "delayed" prescriptions can be a sensible middle ground for some illnesses (e.g. mild sinusitis, mild ear infection) - take them only if you are not improving by a defined day</li>
</ul>

<h2>What helps when antibiotics are not the answer</h2>

<p><strong>Time.</strong> Most viral illnesses peak around day 3-5 and improve over the following week. The cough from bronchitis can persist 2-3 weeks; this is normal.</p>

<p><strong>Symptom relief.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Paracetamol or ibuprofen for fever and pain</li>
<li>Rest and hydration</li>
<li>Saline nasal spray or steam for blocked noses</li>
<li>Honey (over age 1) for cough</li>
<li>Throat lozenges and warm fluids for sore throat</li>
<li>Decongestant nasal sprays - short courses only (under 5 days)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Watching for warning signs.</strong> Your GP will tell you what to look for - new high fever, rapidly worsening symptoms, difficulty breathing, blood in sputum, severe headache, neck stiffness. Come back if these develop.</p>

<h2>What you can do</h2>

<ul>
<li><strong>Trust the conversation.</strong> If your GP discusses antibiotic versus no antibiotic, they have weighed the case. Ask why if you are unsure</li>
<li><strong>Do not pressure.</strong> Studies show GPs prescribe more antibiotics when patients ask, often unnecessarily</li>
<li><strong>Take antibiotics as prescribed</strong> when you are given them - finish the course unless your GP says otherwise. Skipped doses encourage resistance</li>
<li><strong>Do not save leftovers</strong> for next time, or share with family</li>
<li><strong>Do not request antibiotics from overseas pharmacies</strong> or online</li>
<li><strong>Get vaccinated.</strong> Flu and COVID-19 vaccines reduce respiratory illness, and pneumococcal vaccine reduces bacterial pneumonia</li>
<li><strong>Wash your hands</strong> and stay away from others when unwell</li>
</ul>

<h2>Children</h2>

<p>Most childhood illnesses are viral. Antibiotic prescribing for children has fallen substantially in NZ over the past decade, with no rise in serious complications.</p>

<p>Watch and wait is supported for:</p>

<ul>
<li>Most ear infections in children over 2, in good general health</li>
<li>Most sore throats - low rheumatic fever risk children</li>
<li>Most coughs and colds</li>
</ul>

<p>Stricter antibiotic use for children at high risk of rheumatic fever (Maori, Pacific, lower socioeconomic communities) - your GP will follow guidance.</p>

<h2>The bigger picture</h2>

<p>Every antibiotic decision affects you, but also affects everyone. Each unnecessary prescription nudges the world's bacteria towards resistance. Conserving antibiotics now means they will still work when you - or your grandchildren - really need them.</p>
