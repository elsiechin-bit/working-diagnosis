Shingles is a painful, blistering rash caused by the reactivation of the chickenpox virus (varicella zoster), which has been sleeping in nerve roots since you had chickenpox - sometimes decades earlier. About 1 in 3 people will have shingles at some point. Most cases settle within a few weeks, but some people develop long-lasting nerve pain. Early antiviral treatment and the shingles vaccine both help.

<h2>What shingles looks like</h2>

<p>Typical pattern:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Pre-rash phase (1-5 days):</strong> burning, tingling, itching, or shooting pain in a strip of skin on one side of the body. Sometimes flu-like symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Rash phase:</strong> red patches appear in the same strip and develop into clusters of fluid-filled blisters</li>
<li><strong>Crusting phase:</strong> blisters dry out and crust over (5-7 days)</li>
<li><strong>Healing phase:</strong> crusts fall off and skin heals over 2-4 weeks</li>
</ol>

<p>The rash follows a single nerve and is almost always on one side only (not crossing the midline). Common areas: chest, abdomen, back, face, scalp, and around an eye.</p>

<h2>When to see your GP urgently</h2>

<p>See your GP <strong>same-day</strong> if you have:</p>

<ul>
<li>Suspected shingles within the past 72 hours - antivirals work best when started early</li>
<li>Shingles affecting the face, especially around an eye, on the nose, or in the mouth</li>
<li>Shingles in someone immunosuppressed (cancer treatment, HIV, transplant, biologic medicines, high-dose steroids)</li>
<li>Shingles in a pregnant woman or newborn-care contact</li>
<li>Severe pain</li>
<li>Widespread rash beyond a single strip (suggests disseminated zoster - more serious)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Treatment</h2>

<p><strong>Antivirals.</strong> Aciclovir, valaciclovir, or famciclovir for 7 days. Most effective when started within 72 hours of the rash. They reduce the duration of pain and rash, and may reduce the risk of long-term nerve pain.</p>

<p><strong>Pain relief.</strong> Pain can be severe. Options include:</p>

<ul>
<li>Paracetamol regularly</li>
<li>Anti-inflammatories (ibuprofen, diclofenac) if no contraindication</li>
<li>Codeine or stronger opioids short-term for severe pain</li>
<li>Gabapentin, pregabalin, or amitriptyline for nerve pain</li>
<li>Topical capsaicin or lidocaine patches once the rash has crusted</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Skin care.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Keep the rash clean and dry</li>
<li>Loose cotton clothing - avoid friction</li>
<li>Calamine lotion or cool compresses for itch</li>
<li>Cover weeping blisters with a dressing if leaking onto clothes</li>
<li>Do not pick or burst blisters</li>
</ul>

<h2>Is shingles contagious?</h2>

<p>Shingles itself is not transmitted person-to-person, but the fluid in the blisters contains live varicella virus. Direct contact with the blisters can give chickenpox to someone who has never had chickenpox or been vaccinated. Once the blisters have crusted over, you are no longer infectious.</p>

<p>Until then, avoid:</p>

<ul>
<li>Pregnant women who have not had chickenpox</li>
<li>Newborn babies</li>
<li>Anyone immunosuppressed</li>
<li>Sharing towels or close skin contact</li>
</ul>

<h2>Postherpetic neuralgia</h2>

<p>Around 10-20% of people develop pain that persists after the rash has healed - postherpetic neuralgia (PHN). It is more common over age 50 and can last months or years. Treatment options:</p>

<ul>
<li>Gabapentin or pregabalin</li>
<li>Amitriptyline or nortriptyline</li>
<li>Topical capsaicin or lidocaine patches</li>
<li>Specialist pain clinic referral if severe</li>
</ul>

<p>The good news: most PHN does eventually settle, and early antiviral treatment reduces the chance of developing it.</p>

<h2>Shingles in the eye (herpes zoster ophthalmicus)</h2>

<p>Shingles affecting the forehead, eyelid, or tip of nose can damage the eye. Always seek same-day care. Treatment includes oral antivirals plus ophthalmology review. Untreated, it can cause permanent vision damage.</p>

<h2>Vaccination - the best prevention</h2>

<p>The recombinant zoster vaccine, <strong>Shingrix</strong>, is now the recommended shingles vaccine. Two doses, 2-6 months apart. It is over 90% effective at preventing shingles, and the protection lasts at least 10 years.&sup1;</p>

<p>In New Zealand, Shingrix is funded for:</p>

<ul>
<li>People aged 65 (catch-up to age 80)</li>
<li>Severely immunocompromised people aged 18 and over (specific criteria)</li>
</ul>

<p>If you do not qualify for funded vaccination, Shingrix is available privately - cost varies but is around $200-300 per dose. Especially worth considering for people aged 50+ who have had shingles before, or who are at higher risk of complications.</p>

<p>Shingrix can be given alongside flu and COVID vaccines. Common side effects: sore arm, fatigue, mild fever for 1-2 days.</p>

<p>The older live shingles vaccine (Zostavax) is no longer routinely used in NZ since Shingrix became available.</p>

<h2>Other things you can do</h2>

<ul>
<li>Manage stress and sleep - both lower immunity to viruses</li>
<li>Exercise regularly</li>
<li>Eat well</li>
<li>Treat depression and anxiety - chronic stress is associated with reactivation</li>
<li>Stay up to date with chronic disease care - diabetes, immunosuppressive medicines</li>
</ul>
