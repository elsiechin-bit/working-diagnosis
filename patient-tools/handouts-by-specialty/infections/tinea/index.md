Tinea is a group of fungal skin infections caused by dermatophytes - moulds that live on dead skin, hair, and nails. Despite the name "ringworm", no worm is involved. Tinea is common, mildly contagious, and almost always treatable with creams or, for stubborn cases, tablets. Getting the right diagnosis matters: tinea looks similar to eczema and psoriasis, and steroid cream can make it worse.

<h2>Common types</h2>

<p><strong>Tinea corporis (body)</strong> - the classic "ringworm". A round or oval patch with a raised, scaly red edge and clearer skin in the centre. Slowly enlarges over weeks.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea pedis (athlete's foot)</strong> - itchy, scaly skin between the toes (especially the fourth web), or scaling and redness on the soles ("moccasin" pattern), or blisters on the arches.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea cruris (jock itch)</strong> - red, scaly, itchy patches in the groin folds, usually sparing the scrotum. More common in men, often spreads from the feet.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea capitis (scalp)</strong> - patchy hair loss with scaling, sometimes with broken hairs or pus-filled lumps. Mainly in children. Always needs oral treatment.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea unguium / onychomycosis (nails)</strong> - thickened, yellow, crumbly nails. Slow to develop. Toenails more often than fingernails.</p>

<p><strong>Tinea manuum (hand)</strong> - usually one-sided scaling on the palm, with the other hand normal. The "two feet, one hand" pattern is classic.</p>

<h2>Where it comes from</h2>

<ul>
<li>From other people - shared towels, gym mats, communal showers</li>
<li>From animals - cats, dogs, calves (often a faster-spreading inflamed type)</li>
<li>From soil (rare in NZ)</li>
<li>Spread on your own skin - feet to groin, body to scalp</li>
</ul>

<p>Risk factors: warm humid climate, sweating, occlusive shoes, sharing towels, contact sports (rugby, wrestling), public swimming pools and gyms.</p>

<h2>How it is diagnosed</h2>

<p>Often by appearance alone. If unclear, your GP may take a skin scraping for fungal microscopy and culture (results take 2-4 weeks). For the scalp, plucked hairs are sent.</p>

<p>Important: do not apply steroid cream first - it will mask the appearance and make culture less reliable.</p>

<h2>Treatment - skin tinea</h2>

<p>Most body, foot, and groin tinea responds to over-the-counter antifungal creams:</p>

<ul>
<li>Terbinafine 1% cream - apply once or twice daily for 1-2 weeks</li>
<li>Clotrimazole 1% cream - twice daily for 2-4 weeks</li>
<li>Miconazole 2% cream - twice daily for 2-4 weeks</li>
</ul>

<p>Apply to the patch and 2 cm beyond. Continue for at least a week after the rash has cleared. Most cases resolve within 2-4 weeks.</p>

<p>For widespread skin tinea, very inflamed lesions, or treatment failure, oral antifungal tablets may be needed (terbinafine or itraconazole, prescribed by GP, 2-6 weeks).</p>

<h2>Treatment - nail tinea</h2>

<p>Cream is rarely effective for established nail infection. Options:</p>

<ul>
<li>Oral terbinafine 250 mg daily for 6 weeks (fingernails) or 12 weeks (toenails) - cure rate around 70-80%</li>
<li>Itraconazole "pulse" therapy</li>
<li>Topical lacquers (amorolfine, ciclopirox) - lower success rate, useful only for very mild distal infection</li>
<li>Leave it alone - if mild and not bothering you, treatment is optional. Recurrence after treatment is common</li>
</ul>

<p>Before starting tablets, your GP will check liver function and may confirm fungal nail with culture. Tell your GP if you take other medicines - terbinafine and itraconazole have several drug interactions.</p>

<h2>Treatment - scalp tinea</h2>

<p>Always needs oral antifungal (griseofulvin or terbinafine) for 4-8 weeks. Cream alone does not penetrate the hair root. Affected children should not share hats, combs, or pillowcases. School attendance is fine once treatment has started.</p>

<h2>Practical tips</h2>

<ul>
<li>Dry skin thoroughly after washing, especially between toes and in skin folds</li>
<li>Change socks daily, more if feet sweat. Cotton or moisture-wicking socks beat synthetic</li>
<li>Alternate shoes day to day to let them dry out</li>
<li>Antifungal foot powder helps prevent athlete's foot recurrence</li>
<li>Wear flip-flops in communal showers and changing rooms</li>
<li>Hot wash towels (60°C) and bedding during treatment</li>
<li>Treat the feet at the same time as the groin - they often go together</li>
<li>Treat household pets if they have hair loss or scaling - the family vet can confirm</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is not tinea</h2>

<p>Several conditions are mistaken for tinea:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Eczema</strong> - itchy, scaly, but no central clearing or advancing edge</li>
<li><strong>Psoriasis</strong> - thick silvery scale, symmetrical (both elbows, both knees)</li>
<li><strong>Pityriasis rosea</strong> - oval pink patches on the trunk, may begin with a single larger "herald patch"</li>
<li><strong>Granuloma annulare</strong> - smooth ring without scale</li>
<li><strong>Discoid eczema</strong> - round itchy patches, very dry and scaly</li>
</ul>

<p>If your "ringworm" is not improving with antifungal cream after 2-3 weeks, see your GP - the diagnosis may be wrong.</p>
