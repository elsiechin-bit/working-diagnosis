Fever in children

Fever is a protective immune response. Most childhood fevers are caused by harmless viral infections and resolve without treatment.

What is fever and why does it happen? ›

Fever is a core body temperature of 38°C or above. It is a physiological response to infection or inflammation — elevation of the temperature set point via cytokine signalling activates immune cells and is generally protective.

The height of the fever correlates poorly with severity of illness. A child with 39.5°C who is alert, interactive, and drinking is reassuring; a child with 38.5°C who is limp and difficult to rouse is not. Behaviour and responsiveness matter more than the number. Fever from infection does not cause brain damage; the body's thermoregulatory mechanisms prevent temperatures rising to harmful levels under normal circumstances.

Most childhood fevers are caused by self-limiting viral infections (rhinovirus, influenza, rotavirus, hand-foot-mouth). Bacterial infections (strep throat, UTI) are less common. Serious infections (meningitis, sepsis) are uncommon but require urgent exclusion when red-flag features are present.

How to take a temperature ›

Use a digital thermometer. Method choice depends on age:

  • Rectal (under 2 years) — most accurate; ≥38°C is fever
  • Tympanic/ear (over 6 months) — convenient and reasonably accurate; pull the ear back before inserting
  • Axillary (armpit) — least accurate; add 0.5°C for an estimate of core temperature
  • Temporal artery (forehead) — acceptable accuracy if used correctly

Avoid glass thermometers. Do not use rectal measurement in immunocompromised children without medical guidance. A single accurate reading is more useful than multiple inaccurate ones.

Treating fever at home ›

The goal is comfort, not normalising the temperature. Fever itself is not harmful and does not require treatment if the child is otherwise comfortable.

Antipyretics: paracetamol (15 mg/kg every 4–6 hours, maximum 4 doses/24 hours) or ibuprofen (5–10 mg/kg every 6–8 hours, for children over 3 months and over 5 kg, not in dehydrated children). Alternating antipyretics is not recommended by most guidelines due to risk of dosing errors.

Do not use aspirin in children under 16 — risk of Reye syndrome.

Hydration: offer fluids frequently. Fever increases insensible losses. Breastfeeding should continue.

Tepid sponging is not recommended — it causes discomfort without meaningful benefit.

Dress the child comfortably; do not over-bundle or underdress.

Febrile seizures ›

Febrile seizures occur in approximately 2 to 4 percent of children aged 6 months to 5 years during a fever. They are distressing to witness but rarely dangerous and do not cause permanent brain damage. Understanding what to do can help you stay calm and keep your child safe.

What happens: A febrile seizure usually starts suddenly with the child's muscles stiffening or jerking rhythmically. The child may lose consciousness, their eyes may roll back, and they may be unresponsive during the seizure. Most febrile seizures last less than 5 minutes and are generalised (affecting both sides of the body). After the seizure ends, the child usually regains consciousness but may be sleepy or confused for a short time.

What to do: If your child has a seizure, stay calm and follow these steps. Lay them on their side in the recovery position to keep the airway open. Do not put anything in the mouth. Time how long the seizure lasts. Stay with your child and keep them safe from hard objects. If it is the first febrile seizure or if the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes, or if seizures continue in clusters, call 111. Even after a brief seizure that stops, seeking medical evaluation is sensible.

Recovery and reassurance: After the seizure, the child is usually very sleepy and may want to sleep for several hours. This is normal. Once recovered, examination and possibly blood tests will be done to identify the cause of fever. Febrile seizures do not cause epilepsy or permanent brain damage. Less than 1 percent of children who have a febrile seizure go on to develop epilepsy. The risk of another febrile seizure in future episodes of fever is about 30 to 40 percent, but repeat seizures do not worsen outcomes.

Prevention: Aggressive fever treatment does not reliably prevent febrile seizures, so the usual approach is to manage fever for comfort rather than to aggressively lower temperature. If your child has had a febrile seizure, your GP may discuss whether preventive medication is warranted in future febrile illnesses, though this is rarely necessary.

When to seek help by age

Fever alone doesn't always need assessment, but age and symptoms can change that.

Age Seek help if... Go to ED immediately if...
Under 3 months Any fever 38°C or higher. See GP or ED same day. Fever 38°C+ plus looks unwell, poor feeding, or bulging fontanelle
3-6 months Fever 39°C or higher or lasting more than 24 hours Laboured breathing, mottled skin, very difficult to wake
6 months to 5 years Fever lasting more than 5 days or source not identified Stiff neck, aversion to bright light, non-blanching rash, altered consciousness
Any age Deteriorating despite treatment, not drinking, or you are worried Any red flag symptom, or if you feel something is seriously wrong (trust your instincts)

⚠ Go to ED or call 111 immediately if your child has:

  • A non-blanching rash (press a clear glass on it; if spots do not fade, this is serious)
  • A stiff neck or persistent aversion to bright light
  • Difficulty breathing or stridor (noisy breathing)
  • Unusual limpness, floppy posture, or inability to wake
  • Altered consciousness or confusion
  • A seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or repeated seizures
  • Signs of severe dehydration: no wet nappy in 12 hours, sunken eyes, very dry lips
  • You are worried. Trust your parental instinct. If something feels seriously wrong, seek help.

You can also call Healthline on 0800 611 116 anytime (free, 24/7) to speak to a registered nurse about whether your child needs assessment.
