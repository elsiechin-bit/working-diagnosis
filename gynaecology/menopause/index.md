<p>The menopause transition is one of the most significant physiological events in a woman's life - and one of the most consequential consultations in general practice. It affects not just quality of life in the short term but long-term cardiovascular and bone health. Primary care has historically managed menopause conservatively, often under-treating significant symptoms. The risk-benefit evidence for menopausal hormone therapy has been substantially revised since the original Women's Health Initiative publications; most of what clinicians learned to fear in the early 2000s was based on a misapplication of data that do not apply to most women presenting in general practice.</p>

<h2>Perimenopause and diagnosis</h2>

<p>The perimenopause begins with the first menstrual irregularity caused by declining ovarian function and ends 12 months after the final menstrual period (the menopause). This transition typically spans 4-7 years. Vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats) are the cardinal symptoms and occur in up to 80% of women; around 20-25% have symptoms severe enough to significantly impair daily functioning and sleep.</p>

<p><strong>Diagnosis is clinical in women over 45.</strong> FSH and oestradiol levels fluctuate widely in the perimenopause and are unreliable for diagnosis - a single normal FSH does not exclude perimenopause. Do not routinely measure hormone levels in women over 45 with characteristic symptoms. If the diagnosis is uncertain, a therapeutic trial of MHT is more informative than a blood test.</p>

<p>In women under 45 with suspected perimenopause, measure FSH and oestradiol on two occasions 4-6 weeks apart. FSH consistently over 25 IU/L in the absence of an alternative cause is consistent with perimenopause. In women under 40, a consistently elevated FSH is premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) and warrants specialist referral.</p>

<p>Other symptoms of the perimenopause beyond vasomotor: sleep disturbance (often the most functionally disabling symptom); mood changes (irritability, low mood, anxiety - frequently misattributed to primary depression or anxiety disorder); cognitive symptoms ("brain fog", word-finding difficulty); joint and muscle aches; and reduced libido. Not all of these are reliably treated by oestrogen replacement, but symptom burden should be comprehensively assessed rather than focused solely on hot flushes.</p>

<h2>Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT)</h2>

<p>MHT is the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and is the gold standard for management of genitourinary syndrome of menopause. It also reduces fracture risk by maintaining bone density.</p>

<p><strong>The WHI reconsidered:</strong> The Women's Health Initiative (2002) found increased risks of breast cancer, VTE, stroke, and cardiovascular disease with conjugated equine oestrogen plus medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA). Subsequent reanalysis showed: the cardiovascular risk was driven by older women (average age 63, more than 10 years post-menopause); the oestrogen-only arm showed a possible reduction in breast cancer risk; and the absolute risk increases were small even in the original study. For women aged 50-60 or within 10 years of menopause, the benefit-to-risk ratio of MHT is generally favourable for vasomotor symptoms, bone health, and quality of life.[^1]</p>

<p><strong>Route of administration matters:</strong> Transdermal oestrogen (patches, gel, spray) is associated with significantly lower VTE and stroke risk than oral oestrogen - because it bypasses first-pass hepatic metabolism. Transdermal is the preferred route for most women, and is particularly important in women with cardiovascular risk factors, migraine, or prior VTE (where it may be used after careful risk assessment). Oral oestrogen is not absolutely contraindicated but should not be the default for women with additional risks.</p>

<p><strong>Progestogen protection of the endometrium:</strong> Any woman with an intact uterus must have progestogen added to oestrogen therapy to prevent endometrial hyperplasia and carcinoma. Women who have had a hysterectomy can take oestrogen alone. The levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena) provides effective endometrial protection when used with systemic oestrogen - a legitimate and often underused option. Oral or transdermal natural progesterone (Utrogestan) is associated with lower VTE risk and possibly lower breast cancer risk than synthetic progestogens and is the preferred choice when oral progestogen is used.[^2]</p>

<p><strong>Duration of treatment:</strong> There is no arbitrary time limit for MHT. Treatment should continue as long as the woman remains symptomatic and benefits outweigh risks, with annual review. The idea that MHT should be limited to 2-5 years is not supported by current guidelines. Women who stop MHT abruptly often have recurrence of severe symptoms; gradual dose reduction is preferable.</p>

<p><strong>Breast cancer:</strong> The risk remains the most common concern raised by patients. The current estimate from the Million Women Study and subsequent meta-analyses suggests a small increased risk with combined MHT (oestrogen plus progestogen), equivalent to approximately 1 extra case per 1,000 women per year of use. The risk with oestrogen alone is much smaller. These numbers should be contextualised with lifestyle risks: obesity, alcohol consumption, and physical inactivity carry comparable or greater breast cancer risk. Women with a history of breast cancer should discuss MHT with their oncologist.</p>

<h2>Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM)</h2>

<p>GSM encompasses vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, vaginal and urethral atrophy, urinary urgency, frequency, and recurrent UTI resulting from oestrogen deficiency. It affects up to 50% of postmenopausal women and, unlike vasomotor symptoms, does not improve spontaneously over time.</p>

<p>Low-dose topical vaginal oestrogen (estriol cream, vaginal tablet, or vaginal ring) is highly effective for GSM and is generally not systemically absorbed at significant levels. It does not require progestogen add-back in women with a uterus. Critically, it is appropriate for most women including those who decline or cannot take systemic MHT - and in the majority of women with prior breast cancer, in discussion with their oncologist.</p>

<p>Vaginal moisturisers (e.g. Replens) and lubricants are helpful adjuncts but do not address the underlying atrophy. They should be recommended alongside - not instead of - topical oestrogen where the latter is indicated and acceptable.</p>

<p>Recurrent UTI in postmenopausal women should prompt prescription of topical vaginal oestrogen: it significantly reduces the frequency of recurrence by restoring the lactobacillus-dominant vaginal flora.</p>

<h2>Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)</h2>

<p>POI is defined as ovarian failure before age 40, affecting approximately 1% of women. Causes include: idiopathic (most common), autoimmune (associated with Addison's disease, thyroid disease), chromosomal (Turner syndrome mosaicism, Fragile X premutation), and iatrogenic (chemotherapy, pelvic radiotherapy, surgical oophorectomy).</p>

<p>Diagnosis requires: at least two FSH measurements over 25 IU/L, taken at least 4-6 weeks apart, in a woman with oligo- or amenorrhoea under age 40. A single elevated FSH is insufficient. Once confirmed, specialist referral is essential.</p>

<p>Management of POI differs from menopause management: these women have profound oestrogen deficiency for potentially 10-15 years longer than the natural menopause age. They have significantly increased risks of cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, cognitive decline, and overall mortality compared with women reaching menopause at the natural age. MHT should be offered and maintained until at least age 51 (natural menopause age) to mitigate these risks. The COCP is an alternative in women who also want contraception, but does not provide the same bone protection as physiological oestrogen replacement. Fertility is severely impaired but not always zero - spontaneous ovulation can occur; contraception should be discussed.</p>

<h2>Non-hormonal options</h2>

<p>For women who cannot or do not wish to use MHT: SSRIs and SNRIs (paroxetine, escitalopram, venlafaxine) reduce hot flush frequency by 40-65%; they are the most evidence-based non-hormonal option. Gabapentin has modest benefit for vasomotor symptoms. Clonidine has limited effect. Fezolinetant (a neurokinin 3 receptor antagonist) is a newer non-hormonal option with emerging evidence and is not yet available in NZ.</p>

<p>Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) targeting hot flush beliefs and triggers reduces distress and improves functioning without reducing flush frequency; an underused option particularly for women with significant health anxiety around menopause symptoms.</p>

<p>Black cohosh, phytoestrogens (soy, red clover), and other herbal preparations have weak or inconsistent evidence; some interact with medications. They are not recommended in women with hormone-sensitive cancers.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Refer to gynaecology or a specialist menopause clinic for: POI under age 40; surgical menopause (bilateral oophorectomy, particularly in young women); complex breast cancer history requiring specialist MHT guidance; MHT failure with persistent severe symptoms; and women who require urodynamic assessment for mixed genitourinary symptoms.</p>
