<div class="topic-body">

<p>Vaginal discharge is one of the most common presentations in general practice. Most cases have a benign, treatable cause, but a systematic approach matters - dyspareunia, abnormal bleeding, and cervical pathology can all initially present as a complaint of discharge. The key initial distinction is physiological versus pathological, and then among pathological causes, whether the aetiology is lower genital tract (vulvovaginal) or upper genital tract (cervicitis, PID).</p>

<h2>Physiological discharge</h2>

<p>Physiological vaginal discharge is clear to white, non-offensive, and varies in volume and consistency across the menstrual cycle. It increases at ovulation (often clear and stretchy), in pregnancy, and with arousal. There is no itch, burning, odour, or dyspareunia. Reassurance and explanation of the physiological cycle are all that is required. Excessive hygiene practices (internal washing, scented products) disrupt the lactobacillus-dominant vaginal flora and should be discouraged as they increase the risk of BV.</p>

<h2>Bacterial vaginosis (BV)</h2>

<p>BV is the most common cause of abnormal vaginal discharge in women of reproductive age. It is caused by polymicrobial overgrowth (predominantly Gardnerella vaginalis, Prevotella species, Mobiluncus species) replacing the normal lactobacillus-dominant flora. BV is not a sexually transmitted infection but is associated with sexual activity and is more common in women with new or multiple partners, IUD users, and those with intrinsic low lactobacillus colonisation.</p>

<p>Presentation: thin, grey-white discharge with a characteristic fishy odour, often more noticeable after unprotected sex or during menstruation. No significant itch or external vulval inflammation (itch suggests candidiasis). Vaginal pH is elevated (above 4.5). Amsel criteria (clinical diagnosis): three of four - thin homogeneous discharge; pH greater than 4.5; fishy odour on 10% KOH application (whiff test); and clue cells on wet preparation (vaginal epithelial cells studded with bacteria, obscuring cell margins).</p>

<p>Treatment: metronidazole 400 mg twice daily for 7 days, or metronidazole 2 g stat (lower cure rate), or intravaginal metronidazole gel once daily for 5 days, or intravaginal clindamycin cream for 7 nights. Metronidazole 400 mg twice daily for 7 days has the highest cure rates. BV recurs in up to 50% of women within 12 months; maintenance intravaginal metronidazole gel (twice weekly) is used for recurrent BV. BV in pregnancy requires treatment (associated with preterm birth); metronidazole is safe in all trimesters at standard doses.</p>

<p>Partners do not require treatment - treating male partners does not reduce recurrence. Female partners should be assessed.</p>

<h2>Vulvovaginal candidiasis (VVC)</h2>

<p>VVC is caused by Candida species, most commonly Candida albicans (responsible for approximately 90% of cases). It is not a sexually transmitted infection. Risk factors include recent antibiotic use, immunosuppression, diabetes, pregnancy, the COCP, and tight synthetic underwear.</p>

<p>Presentation: vulval and vaginal itch (the dominant symptom), with a thick, curdy, white discharge described as "cottage cheese" in appearance. External vulval soreness, redness, and excoriation. Dyspareunia and dysuria. Vaginal pH is normal (below 4.5) - this distinguishes VVC from BV. Wet preparation: pseudohyphae or spores on KOH preparation confirm the diagnosis, though clinical diagnosis is acceptable in typical presentations.</p>

<p>Treatment: fluconazole 150 mg oral stat (first-line, funded); or topical azole (clotrimazole cream or pessary, available OTC). Both are effective for uncomplicated VVC. Topical azoles are preferred in pregnancy (fluconazole is avoided in the first trimester and at high repeated doses due to teratogenicity concerns, though a single 150 mg dose has limited evidence of harm - check current NZF guidance). For severe or recurrent VVC: fluconazole 150 mg every 72 hours for three doses, then maintenance fluconazole 150 mg weekly for 6 months reduces recurrence significantly.</p>

<p>Recurrent VVC (4 or more symptomatic episodes per year confirmed on culture) warrants investigation for predisposing causes: HbA1c, FBC, HIV test, review of medications. Culture and sensitivity should be taken to exclude non-albicans Candida (C. glabrata, C. krusei) which are resistant to fluconazole and require alternative treatment (boric acid pessaries, nystatin, specialist review).</p>

<h2>Trichomoniasis</h2>

<p>Trichomoniasis is caused by the protozoan Trichomonas vaginalis and is an STI. It is less common in NZ than BV or VVC but should not be overlooked, particularly in symptomatic women with new partners or STI risk factors.</p>

<p>Presentation: frothy, green-yellow, malodorous discharge; vulval itch and soreness; vaginal pH elevated. Cervical petechiae ("strawberry cervix") are a specific finding but present in only a minority. Diagnosis: NAAT on self-collected or clinician-collected vaginal swab is more sensitive than wet preparation microscopy. Many women and most men are asymptomatic.</p>

<p>Treatment: metronidazole 2 g stat or 400 mg twice daily for 7 days (higher cure rate). Both partners require treatment simultaneously. Test of cure is not routinely required but should be arranged if symptoms persist. Trichomoniasis is notifiable in NZ.</p>

<h2>Atrophic vaginitis</h2>

<p>In postmenopausal women, oestrogen deficiency causes vaginal and urethral atrophy - pale, dry, fragile vaginal walls with loss of rugae. Symptoms include discharge (often thin, watery or blood-tinged), dyspareunia, itch, and recurrent UTI. Diagnosis is clinical; pH is elevated. Treatment: topical vaginal oestrogen (see Menopause page). Vaginal moisturisers are adjuncts.</p>

<h2>When to investigate for cervicitis or PID</h2>

<p>Upper genital tract infection (cervicitis, PID) should be considered when discharge is accompanied by: cervical motion tenderness; pelvic pain; intermenstrual or postcoital bleeding; systemic features (fever, malaise); or STI risk factors. Take endocervical or vaginal NAAT for chlamydia and gonorrhoea. Do not attribute all discharge to lower genital tract causes without considering upper tract pathology.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Most vulvovaginal conditions are managed in primary care. Refer to sexual health or gynaecology for: recurrent VVC not responding to standard maintenance therapy; non-albicans candidiasis confirmed on culture; persistent symptoms after adequate treatment; STI with resistant organisms; or symptoms where examination is difficult or uncertain.</p>
