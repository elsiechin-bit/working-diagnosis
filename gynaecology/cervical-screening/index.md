<div class="topic-body">

<p>New Zealand transitioned to primary HPV-based cervical screening in 2023, replacing the cytology-based programme that had been in place since 1990. The new programme offers longer screening intervals (every 5 years rather than 3), broader eligibility, self-testing as an option, and improved detection of significant cervical disease - particularly for Maori and Pacific women, who have historically had lower screening participation and higher rates of cervical cancer.</p>

<h2>Programme overview</h2>

<p>Eligible people are those with a cervix aged 25-69, regardless of sexual history or HPV vaccination status. The programme is free. Screening is offered every 5 years for most people, with shorter intervals at the post-colposcopy monitoring pathway. People who have never been sexually active can be offered screening, though the risk of HPV exposure in this group is very low - shared decision-making is appropriate.</p>

<p>People who have been vaccinated against HPV should still screen - no vaccine provides 100% protection against all high-risk HPV types, and many vaccinated people may have had prior HPV exposure.</p>

<h2>The HPV screening pathway</h2>

<p>A clinician-collected cervical sample or a self-collected vaginal sample is tested for high-risk HPV (hrHPV). Results are interpreted as follows:</p>

<p><strong>HPV not detected:</strong> Return to routine 5-yearly screening. This is the most common result.</p>

<p><strong>HPV 16 or 18 detected:</strong> These two types account for approximately 70% of cervical cancers. Reflex cytology is performed on the same sample. If cytology is any abnormality, refer to colposcopy. If cytology is negative or inadequate, refer to colposcopy within 3 months - the combination of HPV 16/18 and any cytology is high risk regardless of cytology result.</p>

<p><strong>Other high-risk HPV detected (not 16/18):</strong> Reflex cytology is performed. If cytology is any abnormality (including borderline/ASC-US), refer to colposcopy. If cytology is negative, repeat combined test in 12 months; if HPV still detected at 12 months, refer to colposcopy. If negative at 12 months, return to routine 5-yearly screening.</p>

<p><strong>Inadequate sample:</strong> Repeat sample within 3 months.</p>

<h2>Self-testing</h2>

<p>Self-collected vaginal swabs for HPV testing are now a formal part of the NZ cervical screening programme. They are appropriate for people who decline clinician-collected testing due to discomfort, cultural reasons, previous trauma, or access barriers. Self-tests have similar sensitivity to clinician-collected tests for HPV detection (though slightly lower for cytology, which is not the primary test). They can be offered in the clinic, at home, or through community outreach.</p>

<p>This is a significant equity tool - offering self-testing to Maori and Pacific women who have not previously engaged with screening should be a deliberate part of practice. Discussing why screening matters, in a way that is culturally safe and non-judgmental, is as important as the technical aspects of the test.</p>

<h2>HPV vaccination and the programme</h2>

<p>Gardasil 9 (9-valent HPV vaccine) is funded for all people in NZ aged 9-26 under the immunisation schedule. It provides protection against HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, and 58. Catch-up vaccination for people aged 27-45 is available but not funded. The programme is gradually shifting the epidemiology of cervical cancer - surveillance data from New Zealand's vaccinated cohorts shows significant reductions in CIN 2/3, the precursor lesions that drive cervical cancer incidence.</p>

<h2>Colposcopy and treatment</h2>

<p>Colposcopy is a specialist-performed examination of the transformation zone of the cervix under magnification, with targeted biopsies of abnormal areas. In NZ, colposcopy is provided through DHB/hospital gynaecology services. Most referrals are seen within 4-8 weeks; urgent referrals (suspected invasion) should be seen within 2 weeks.</p>

<p>Treatment of CIN 2/3 is typically by large loop excision of the transformation zone (LLETZ). CIN 1 is usually managed conservatively with follow-up. After treatment, enhanced surveillance with combined HPV and cytology testing is recommended at defined intervals before return to routine screening.</p>

<h2>Postcoital and intermenstrual bleeding</h2>

<p>Postcoital bleeding (PCB) always warrants cervical examination, regardless of screening history. A normal HPV screen does not exclude cervical ectropion, cervicitis, or visible cervical lesions. Where visible abnormality is present, refer to colposcopy urgently rather than waiting for screening results.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Urgent colposcopy (within 2 weeks): any cervix with visible lesion suspicious of invasive cancer; persistent unexplained postcoital bleeding with abnormal-appearing cervix. Routine colposcopy: as per HPV pathway above. Referral pathway differs by DHB - check local HealthPathways for current thresholds and wait times.</p>
