Gynaecology occupies a large portion of any GP's clinical week. Menstrual problems, contraception, cervical screening, pelvic pain, and the perimenopause are bread-and-butter consultations - often emotionally significant, frequently undertreated, and disproportionately dismissed. This section maps the common presentations by domain with links to individual condition pages.

---

## Menstrual Health

Menstrual disorders are among the most common reasons women present to general practice. Abnormal bleeding, painful periods, and hormonal disturbance affect quality of life significantly and are often normalised by patients - and sometimes by clinicians - for years before investigation.

### Common presentations

- **Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB)** - any bleeding that is too heavy, too frequent, irregular, intermenstrual, or postcoital. In reproductive-age women, structural causes (fibroids, polyps, adenomyosis) and ovulatory dysfunction account for most cases. Postcoital or postmenopausal bleeding always warrants investigation; cervical and endometrial causes must be excluded.[^1]
- **Dysmenorrhoea** - primary (no structural cause; prostaglandin-mediated) or secondary (endometriosis, adenomyosis, fibroids, PID). Secondary dysmenorrhoea often presents with worsening pain over years or pain outside menstruation; these features should prompt further investigation beyond empirical NSAIDs.
- **Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)** - the most common endocrine disorder of reproductive-age women, affecting approximately 8-13% internationally. Presents with irregular periods, clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. Insulin resistance drives much of the pathophysiology and treatment.
- **Amenorrhoea** - primary (no period by age 15 or within 3 years of thelarche) or secondary (absent periods for 3 months in a woman with previously regular cycles, or 6 months if cycles were irregular). Common causes: pregnancy, hypothalamic (stress, exercise, significant weight loss), thyroid dysfunction, hyperprolactinaemia, premature ovarian insufficiency.

### Red flags to exclude

- Postcoital bleeding at any age - cervical pathology until proven otherwise; examine cervix and arrange colposcopy referral
- Postmenopausal bleeding - endometrial cancer until proven otherwise; transvaginal ultrasound and gynaecology referral
- Heavy bleeding + pallor + haemodynamic instability - acute haemorrhage or severe iron-deficiency anaemia; urgent FBC and iron studies
- Amenorrhoea + vasomotor symptoms + age under 40 - premature ovarian insufficiency; measure FSH, LH, oestradiol

## Contraception & Reproductive Planning

Contraception counselling is one of the most consequential conversations in general practice. Long-acting reversible methods (LARCs) remain underused relative to their efficacy and patient acceptability when counselled well. Starting with the most effective options and working down - rather than reaching for the pill first - changes outcomes.

### Common presentations

- **LARC initiation** - copper IUD, levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena, Jaydess), and subdermal implant (Jadelle) are funded in NZ for eligible patients and are the most effective reversible methods available. Failure rates under 1% per year in typical use; efficacy is not user-dependent.
- **Combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP)** - highly effective with perfect use (failure rate 0.3%) but typical-use failure approaches 9%. WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) contraindications must be screened for at initiation and review.
- **Progestogen-only options** - suitable for women who cannot use oestrogen (breastfeeding, migraine with aura, elevated VTE risk). Desogestrel POP (Cerazette) has a more forgiving 12-hour missed-pill window; DMPA (Depo-Provera) injectable every 12 weeks is particularly useful for women who struggle with daily adherence.
- **Emergency contraception** - copper IUD is the most effective option (failure rate under 0.1% within 5 days of unprotected intercourse); ulipristal acetate (EllaOne, prescription) is superior to levonorgestrel (Postinor, OTC) over the full 120-hour window and in women over 70 kg.

### Points to consider

- COCP + migraine with aura - WHO MEC category 4 (absolute contraindication); switch to progestogen-only method
- COCP + age over 35 + heavy smoking - absolute contraindication
- DMPA in adolescents or those with osteoporosis risk factors - bone density implications with prolonged use; discuss alternatives
- IUD insertion - pregnancy test and STI screen before insertion; consider STI treatment before fitting if risk uncertain

## Cervical Screening & Infection

New Zealand's cervical screening programme moved to HPV-based primary screening in 2023. Understanding the new pathway - including longer intervals, genotyping, and how to discuss results - is essential for primary care. STIs frequently present through general practice and are commonly asymptomatic.

### Common presentations

- **Cervical screening** - primary HPV screening is now offered every 5 years from age 25 to 69. HPV-positive results are reflexed to genotype (HPV 16/18 versus other high-risk types) and cytology, with colposcopy referral criteria based on the combination. Self-testing is available for people who decline clinician-collected testing.
- **Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)** - often polymicrobial (Chlamydia trachomatis, Neisseria gonorrhoeae, anaerobes). Presents with pelvic pain, cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness, and often vaginal discharge or fever. Diagnosis is clinical; treat empirically while awaiting swabs.
- **Chlamydia & gonorrhoea** - the majority of STIs in NZ are asymptomatic. Opportunistic testing is appropriate in sexually active people under 25, those with new or multiple partners, and in pregnancy. NZ has significant gonorrhoea antimicrobial resistance; local susceptibility patterns should guide therapy.

### Red flags to exclude

- PID with peritoneal signs, sepsis, or suspected tubo-ovarian abscess - inpatient admission and IV antibiotics
- Cervical motion tenderness + positive pregnancy test - ectopic pregnancy until excluded; urgent referral
- Persistent unexplained postcoital bleeding + abnormal cervix on examination - urgent gynaecology referral

## Pelvic Conditions

Pelvic pain is among the most complex presentations in GP - chronic, often multifactorial, and regularly under-investigated. Endometriosis in particular has an average diagnostic delay of 6-10 years internationally; primary care has a critical role in shortening it.[^2]

### Common presentations

- **Endometriosis** - retrograde menstruation causing ectopic endometrial implants, most commonly on the ovaries, peritoneum, and pouch of Douglas. Classic triad: dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, and subfertility. Cyclic bowel or bladder symptoms are underrecognised. Definitive diagnosis is surgical but empirical hormonal therapy is appropriate while awaiting specialist referral.
- **Uterine fibroids** - benign smooth muscle tumours (leiomyomata), extremely common (present in up to 70% of women by age 50, symptomatic in 25-50%). Submucosal fibroids cause heavy bleeding; intramural and subserosal fibroids cause bulk symptoms (pressure, urinary frequency, dyspareunia). Most are managed conservatively; intervention is indicated for significant bleeding, bulk symptoms, or subfertility.
- **Pelvic organ prolapse** - descent of the uterus, anterior vaginal wall (cystocele), posterior vaginal wall (rectocele), or vaginal vault after hysterectomy. Common after vaginal delivery and with age; often asymptomatic but may cause vaginal heaviness, incomplete bladder or bowel emptying, or urinary incontinence. Conservative management (pelvic floor physiotherapy, pessaries) is effective first-line treatment.

### Red flags to exclude

- Pelvic mass + rapid growth + postmenopausal - ovarian malignancy until excluded; urgent ultrasound and CA125
- Chronic pelvic pain + change in bowel habit + rectal bleeding - lower GI pathology should be considered alongside gynaecological causes
- Unexplained pelvic pain + positive pregnancy test - ectopic pregnancy; urgent referral

## Menopause & Perimenopause

The perimenopause is one of the most symptomatic and medically significant transitions in women's lives - and one that primary care has historically managed poorly. Vasomotor symptoms, sleep disruption, urogenital atrophy, mood disturbance, and accelerating bone and cardiovascular risk all converge in the years around the final menstrual period.

### Common presentations

- **Perimenopausal symptoms** - irregular periods and vasomotor symptoms (hot flushes, night sweats) typically begin 4-5 years before the final period. FSH and oestradiol are variable and unreliable for diagnosis in the perimenopause; clinical diagnosis is appropriate in women over 45 with characteristic symptoms.
- **Menopausal hormone therapy (MHT)** - the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and urogenital atrophy, and the primary prevention strategy for osteoporosis in women who start it before age 60. Risk-benefit was significantly reanalysed after the Women's Health Initiative data were reassessed; for most women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause, benefits of MHT outweigh risks.[^3]
- **Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM)** - vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, and recurrent UTI from oestrogen deficiency. Local vaginal oestrogen is safe, effective, and chronically underused; it does not carry systemic MHT risks and is appropriate for most women, including those with prior breast cancer (in consultation with their oncologist).
- **Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI)** - spontaneous menopause before age 40, affecting approximately 1% of women. Requires specialist confirmation and active management including systemic MHT until at least the natural menopause age to address cardiovascular and bone health.

### Red flags to exclude

- Postmenopausal bleeding - endometrial cancer until proven otherwise; transvaginal ultrasound and gynaecology referral
- Hot flushes + weight loss + palpitations + diarrhoea - exclude hyperthyroidism and carcinoid syndrome
- Night sweats with significant constitutional symptoms - exclude malignancy and infection before attributing to menopause

## Vulval & Vaginal Conditions

Vulval and vaginal symptoms are frequently embarrassing for patients to raise and historically dismissed in clinical encounters. Chronic vulval pain, inflammatory skin conditions, and recurrent infections cause significant quality-of-life impairment and require careful examination and often diagnostic patience.

### Common presentations

- **Vaginal discharge & vulvovaginitis** - the most common cause is bacterial vaginosis (BV), followed by vulvovaginal candidiasis and trichomoniasis. BV is not an STI but is associated with increased risk of pelvic infection; it has a characteristic fishy odour and clue cells on wet prep. Recurrent vulvovaginal candidiasis (4 or more episodes per year) warrants investigation for predisposing factors: immunosuppression, diabetes, antibiotic overuse.
- **Lichen sclerosus** - chronic inflammatory skin condition predominantly affecting the vulva and perianal region. Presents with white, atrophic, crinkled skin; intractable itch; and soreness. Most common in postmenopausal women but occurs at any age. Untreated, causes labial fusion and clitoral phimosis; carries a 4-5% lifetime risk of vulval squamous cell carcinoma. Potent topical steroids are first-line and highly effective.
- **Vulvodynia** - chronic vulval pain without an identifiable structural or dermatological cause; can be unprovoked (spontaneous) or provoked (most commonly at the vestibule). Management is multidisciplinary: pelvic floor physiotherapy, topical lidocaine, amitriptyline or gabapentin for neuropathic pain, and psychological support.

### Red flags to exclude

- Vulval ulcer + lymphadenopathy - herpes simplex, syphilis, or vulval carcinoma; examine carefully and swab
- Long-standing lichen sclerosus + new nodule or non-healing ulcer - vulval SCC; urgent biopsy
- Persistent vulval symptoms not responding to expected treatment - consider biopsy; inflammatory and neoplastic conditions can mimic each other
