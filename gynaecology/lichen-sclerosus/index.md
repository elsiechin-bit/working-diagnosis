<div class="topic-body">

<p>Lichen sclerosus (LS) is a chronic inflammatory dermatosis predominantly affecting the anogenital region. It is most common in postmenopausal women but occurs across all age groups, including prepubertal girls and men (where it affects the glans penis and foreskin). It is underdiagnosed, frequently dismissed as thrush or generalised itch, and undertreated - with real consequences: untreated LS causes progressive architectural destruction of the vulva and carries a 4-5% lifetime risk of vulval squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). The condition is manageable and the SCC risk is significantly reduced by good adherence to topical steroid treatment.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>The classic presentation is intractable vulval itch, worse at night, and soreness. Sexual activity may be painful or impossible due to labial fusion, clitoral phimosis, or introital narrowing. Defecation may be painful if perianal skin is involved (which it often is - the figure-of-eight or hourglass distribution around the vulva and anus is characteristic).</p>

<p>On examination: white, atrophic, crinkled skin ("cigarette-paper" texture) of the labia minora, clitoral hood, interlabial sulci, perineum, and perianal skin. In established disease: loss of the labia minora (resorption), clitoral phimosis (foreskin of clitoris fusing over the glans), and narrowing of the introitus. Fissures in the posterior fourchette are common and may be misattributed to Candida or trauma. Excoriation marks from scratching are common. Ecchymosis (purpura) in the absence of trauma occurs in LS and should not be confused with sexual assault in children.</p>

<p>In girls, LS presents with itch, pain on defecation, perianal involvement, and the classic distribution. It is sometimes mistakenly attributed to sexual abuse; correct diagnosis requires examination by a clinician experienced in paediatric dermatology or LS. LS in children may improve at puberty but does not always resolve.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>Diagnosis is clinical in typical presentations. Biopsy is indicated when: the diagnosis is uncertain; symptoms do not respond to appropriate treatment; there is a suspicious area (irregular surface, nodule, erosion, or erythema that does not respond to treatment) requiring histological assessment to exclude SCC or VIN (vulval intraepithelial neoplasia). A biopsy is not required before starting treatment in a typical case with classic examination findings.</p>

<p>Histology shows: hyperkeratosis (thickened keratin layer), basal layer vacuolation, dermal hyalinisation (homogenised collagen), and a band-like lymphocytic infiltrate beneath the zone of hyalinisation.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p><strong>Potent topical corticosteroid:</strong> clobetasol propionate 0.05% (Dermovate) ointment is the gold standard. The standard induction regimen: once daily for 4 weeks, then alternate nights for 4 weeks, then twice weekly for 4 weeks, then as needed for long-term maintenance. In practice, many women with ongoing disease require continuous maintenance therapy (once or twice weekly) to control symptoms and prevent progression.</p>

<p>Ointment is preferred over cream - it is less irritating, better absorbed, and avoids the preservatives in cream formulations that can cause contact sensitivity. Aqueous cream, SLS-containing products, and soap should be avoided on the affected area.</p>

<p>Application: a finger-tip unit applied to the affected area. It is important to treat the entire affected distribution, including the perianal skin if involved. Many women under-apply; inadequate treatment is a common reason for treatment failure.</p>

<p>Skin atrophy from potent topical steroids is a concern patients often raise; at the doses used for LS maintenance, the benefit-to-risk ratio strongly favours treatment. LS itself causes far more damage to vulval architecture than appropriately used topical steroids.</p>

<p><strong>Emollients and barrier protection:</strong> bland emollients (soft white paraffin, petroleum jelly, coconut oil) applied after topical steroid provide a protective barrier and reduce itch. Avoid scented products, wipes, soap, bubble bath, and fabric conditioner on the affected area.</p>

<p><strong>Tacrolimus or pimecrolimus:</strong> topical calcineurin inhibitors are second-line options for women who cannot use or do not respond to topical steroids. They are steroid-sparing and useful for long-term maintenance in some cases. They can cause stinging initially.</p>

<p><strong>Symptom control:</strong> antihistamines at night (chlorphenamine) may help break the itch-scratch cycle. Cooling measures (ice packs wrapped in cloth) provide short-term itch relief. Lidocaine 5% ointment provides local anaesthesia for women with severe dyspareunia.</p>

<p><strong>Introital narrowing:</strong> regular use of vaginal dilators with emollient, supervised by a physiotherapist, can improve introital stenosis. Surgical division of posterior fourchet adhesions or labial fusion may be necessary in severe cases; this should be undertaken by a clinician experienced in LS to avoid exacerbating the condition.</p>

<h2>Cancer surveillance and long-term monitoring</h2>

<p>Women with LS have approximately a 4-5% lifetime risk of developing vulval SCC. The risk is higher in women with poorly controlled disease, long diagnostic delay, and VIN. Good adherence to topical steroid treatment reduces, though does not eliminate, this risk.</p>

<p>Annual review is recommended for all women with established LS. At review: assess symptom control (itch, soreness, dyspareunia); inspect the vulva for signs of disease progression, architectural change, and any suspicious areas (thickening, erosion, warty or nodular lesion, areas of non-healing ulceration or leucoplakia not responding to treatment). Any suspicious area should be biopsied promptly.</p>

<p>Educate women to self-examine and report new symptoms or changes between reviews: new lump, persistent non-healing sore, change in the appearance of the affected skin, or worsening symptoms despite adequate treatment.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Refer to dermatology or a specialist vulval clinic for: diagnostic uncertainty; failure to respond to appropriately used potent topical steroids after 3 months; suspected VIN or SCC (urgent, 2-week wait referral); significant introital narrowing requiring surgical assessment; and children with suspected LS (require specialist assessment to exclude abuse and for appropriate management). In NZ, specialist vulval clinics are available in major centres through the public hospital system.</p>
