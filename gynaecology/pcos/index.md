<div class="topic-body">

<p>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is the most common endocrine disorder of reproductive-age women, affecting approximately 8-13% depending on the diagnostic criteria used. It is a syndrome, not a single disease - insulin resistance, hyperandrogenism, and ovulatory dysfunction interact to produce a heterogeneous clinical picture. The majority of women with PCOS are not diagnosed; many who present with irregular periods, acne, or difficulty conceiving have never had the diagnosis considered.</p>

<h2>Diagnosis</h2>

<p>The Rotterdam criteria (2003) require two of three features for diagnosis: oligo- or anovulation (irregular or absent periods, typically fewer than 9 cycles per year); clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism; and polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound (12 or more follicles per ovary measuring 2-9 mm, or ovarian volume over 10 mL). Other causes must be excluded before diagnosis.</p>

<p>Clinical hyperandrogenism includes hirsutism (terminal hair in a male pattern: upper lip, chin, chest, linea alba, inner thighs), acne (particularly adult-onset or persistent), and female-pattern hair loss. Biochemical hyperandrogenism: elevated free androgen index or free testosterone (total testosterone with SHBG). Note that total testosterone is often within normal limits in PCOS - free androgen index is more sensitive.</p>

<p>Polycystic ovarian morphology is a sonographic finding, not a diagnosis; it is present in approximately 20-25% of women with regular cycles and no other features. Ultrasound findings alone are insufficient to diagnose PCOS. In adolescents, ultrasound is of limited diagnostic value as polycystic morphology is common during the years following menarche; diagnosis in adolescents should be based on oligo-anovulation and clinical hyperandrogenism.</p>

<h2>Investigations to exclude other diagnoses</h2>

<p>Before confirming a PCOS diagnosis: TSH (hypothyroidism causes irregular periods and can co-exist); prolactin (hyperprolactinaemia); 17-hydroxyprogesterone (to exclude late-onset congenital adrenal hyperplasia, particularly in women with significant hirsutism or a family history); and in those with clinical features of cortisol excess, a dexamethasone suppression test. These exclusions are important because the treatments differ significantly.</p>

<p>Once PCOS is confirmed, assess metabolic risk: fasting glucose or HbA1c, fasting lipids, BMI and waist circumference, blood pressure. Insulin resistance is present in 50-70% of women with PCOS and is the driver of much of the long-term metabolic risk: type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease are all more common. Women with PCOS have approximately a 4-fold increased risk of developing type 2 diabetes.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>Management is targeted to the presenting concern: menstrual irregularity, hyperandrogenism symptoms, metabolic risk, or subfertility. Addressing the underlying insulin resistance is central to all of these.</p>

<p><strong>Lifestyle:</strong> Weight loss of 5-10% in women with overweight or obesity improves insulin resistance, restores ovulation, reduces androgen levels, and improves metabolic risk profile. This is the most effective intervention across all PCOS domains and should be part of every management discussion - sensitively framed, since PCOS makes weight management physiologically harder, not just a matter of willpower. Exercise has independent benefits on insulin sensitivity regardless of weight change.</p>

<p><strong>Menstrual regulation:</strong> The COCP is first-line for menstrual regulation and hyperandrogenism management. Anti-androgenic progestogens (drospirenone, cyproterone acetate) provide additional benefit for acne and hirsutism. The levonorgestrel IUS is appropriate for women who prefer non-systemic oestrogen use and have predominantly bleeding concerns. In women not wanting contraception, cyclical progestogen (e.g. medroxyprogesterone acetate or norethisterone for 10-14 days every 1-3 months) prevents endometrial hyperplasia from unopposed oestrogen in anovulatory cycles. Irregular periods in PCOS carry a risk of endometrial hyperplasia; this is an underappreciated long-term concern.</p>

<p><strong>Metabolic management:</strong> Metformin improves insulin sensitivity, lowers androgen levels, and reduces the risk of progression to type 2 diabetes. It is not funded in NZ specifically for PCOS (only for diabetes) but is inexpensive and widely used. International guidelines including the 2023 PCOS International Evidence-Based Guideline support metformin use for metabolic and menstrual benefits. PHARMAC funding criteria should be confirmed at time of prescribing. Monitoring: FBC and renal function at baseline and after dose titration.</p>

<p><strong>Hirsutism and acne:</strong> The COCP with anti-androgenic progestogen is first-line. Topical treatments for acne (retinoids, azelaic acid, topical antibiotics) are adjuncts. Eflornithine cream (prescription) slows facial hair growth. Cosmetic hair removal (laser, electrolysis) is effective for established hirsutism but addresses the symptom, not the cause.</p>

<p><strong>Subfertility:</strong> Ovulation induction is the goal for women with PCOS who wish to conceive. Letrozole (aromatase inhibitor) is now preferred over clomiphene citrate as first-line ovulation induction - higher live birth rates and lower multiple pregnancy risk. Metformin as an adjunct to ovulation induction improves outcomes in women with insulin resistance. Ovarian drilling is a laparoscopic option where other measures fail. IVF is reserved for women who do not respond to ovulation induction. Refer to a fertility specialist for ovulation induction.</p>

<h2>Long-term monitoring</h2>

<p>Annual review for women with established PCOS should include: HbA1c or fasting glucose, lipid profile, blood pressure, BMI and waist circumference, and discussion of menstrual cycle regularity (to ensure endometrial protection). Pregnancy planning should be flagged - PCOS is associated with increased risk of gestational diabetes, pre-eclampsia, and preterm birth; early obstetric review is appropriate.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Refer to endocrinology or gynaecology for: diagnostic uncertainty; severe or rapidly progressive hyperandrogenism (consider adrenal or ovarian androgen-secreting tumour); subfertility requiring ovulation induction; and where metabolic complexity warrants specialist input. Adolescents with suspected PCOS benefit from early specialist assessment to avoid normalising irregular cycles during the formative years after menarche.</p>
