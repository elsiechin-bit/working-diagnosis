<div class="topic-body">

<p>Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) is one of the most common presenting complaints in general practice, accounting for a significant proportion of gynaecology referrals. The term covers any deviation from normal menstrual pattern - heavy flow, prolonged bleeding, intermenstrual spotting, postcoital bleeding, or irregular cycles. Postmenopausal bleeding is a separate clinical entity that always requires investigation to exclude endometrial malignancy.</p>

<h2>Classification</h2>

<p>The FIGO PALM-COEIN system classifies AUB by cause: structural causes (Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy/hyperplasia) and non-structural causes (Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, Not yet classified). In primary care, the most commonly encountered causes are ovulatory dysfunction, adenomyosis, fibroids, polyps, and iatrogenic causes (hormonal contraception, anticoagulants, antipsychotics). Malignancy must always be excluded, particularly in women over 45 or with risk factors.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>Heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB) is defined clinically as blood loss that interferes with physical, emotional, social, or material quality of life. The pictorial blood assessment chart (PBAC) can help quantify loss; ferritin and FBC are more useful markers of the impact than subjective reporting alone. Women commonly underreport - or normalise - excessive loss.</p>

<p>Intermenstrual bleeding (IMB) and postcoital bleeding (PCB) raise the probability of cervical pathology (ectopic cervical ectropion, polyps, cervicitis, or carcinoma), endometrial polyps, or submucosal fibroids. IMB in a woman taking the COCP - particularly when variable in timing - often reflects missed pills, gastrointestinal absorption issues, or enzyme-inducing medications.</p>

<p>Irregular cycles suggest ovulatory dysfunction: consider hypothyroidism, hyperprolactinaemia, PCOS, or perimenopause as underlying causes. A thyroid function test and prolactin level are first-line investigations in irregular bleeding before more complex workup.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>

<p>First-line investigations: FBC (anaemia), ferritin (iron stores independent of Hb), thyroid function, prolactin. In women with HMB and no obvious structural cause, von Willebrand disease screen is underperformed - approximately 13% of women with HMB have an underlying coagulation disorder, most commonly vWD.</p>

<p>Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal preferred, transabdominal if declined) is the primary imaging modality. It identifies fibroids, adenomyosis (globular uterus, myometrial cysts, heterogeneous texture), polyps (intrauterine echogenic lesions, better seen with saline infusion sonography), and ovarian pathology. Endometrial thickness measurement is most useful in postmenopausal women (greater than 4 mm warrants further assessment).</p>

<p>Endometrial biopsy (Pipelle) is indicated in: women over 45 with AUB, any woman with postmenopausal bleeding, women with risk factors for endometrial cancer (obesity, diabetes, PCOS, tamoxifen use, unopposed oestrogen), or where ultrasound findings are suspicious. Pipelle has good sensitivity for endometrial cancer (approaching 90%) but lower sensitivity for polyps and focal lesions.</p>

<p>Cervical smear / HPV screening should be current before investigation of PCB. Examination of the cervix is mandatory in any woman presenting with PCB or unexplained IMB.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>Management depends on the underlying cause, severity of symptoms, fertility wishes, and patient preferences. In general:</p>

<p><strong>Hormonal management:</strong> The levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena) is the most effective medical treatment for HMB, reducing blood loss by 70-90% and preferable to oral progestogens for most women. The COCP reduces blood loss by approximately 40-50%. High-dose oral progestogens (norethisterone 5 mg three times daily, days 5-26 of cycle) are a short-term option where IUS is not appropriate. Tranexamic acid (antifibrinolytic) reduces blood loss by approximately 50% and is taken only during menstruation; it can be combined with NSAIDs for dysmenorrhoea. NSAIDs alone reduce blood loss by 25-35% with the benefit of also treating dysmenorrhoea.</p>

<p><strong>Iron replacement:</strong> Iron-deficiency anaemia secondary to HMB requires treatment alongside the underlying cause. Oral ferrous sulfate or ferrous fumarate is first-line; IV iron is appropriate where oral supplementation is not tolerated or ineffective.</p>

<p><strong>Surgical management:</strong> Endometrial ablation is appropriate for women who have completed their family and where medical management has failed or is not tolerated. Hysteroscopy allows direct visualisation and targeted treatment of polyps, submucosal fibroids, and focal lesions. Myomectomy or hysterectomy may be indicated for significant fibroid disease; uterine artery embolisation is an alternative for women who wish to avoid surgery.</p>

<h2>Postmenopausal bleeding</h2>

<p>Any vaginal bleeding 12 or more months after the final menstrual period requires investigation to exclude endometrial carcinoma, regardless of the apparent cause. This is the case even if the woman is taking MHT. Arrange transvaginal ultrasound and refer to gynaecology; do not wait for a normal smear to provide false reassurance. Endometrial cancer is the most common gynaecological malignancy in NZ; early detection substantially improves outcomes.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Refer to gynaecology for: postmenopausal bleeding (urgent); suspected endometrial or cervical malignancy (urgent); HMB not responding to first- and second-line medical therapy; structural cause (fibroid, adenomyosis, polyp) causing significant symptoms or subfertility; endometrial biopsy showing hyperplasia or malignancy; and women who wish to discuss surgical options.</p>
