<div class="topic-body">

<p>Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) is infection of the upper genital tract: the uterus, fallopian tubes, and/or ovaries. It is polymicrobial in most cases - Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae are the most important initiating pathogens, with ascending infection then seeding by vaginal and endogenous flora including Gardnerella vaginalis, Bacteroides species, and other anaerobes. PID is significantly undertreated in primary care, with consequences: chronic pelvic pain, tubal factor subfertility, and ectopic pregnancy are all downstream sequelae of inadequate or delayed treatment.</p>

<h2>Presentation</h2>

<p>PID should be diagnosed and treated empirically when a sexually active woman presents with pelvic or lower abdominal pain and one or more of the following: cervical motion tenderness on bimanual examination; uterine tenderness; adnexal tenderness. This clinical threshold is intentionally low - the diagnostic criteria are imprecise, but the cost of undertreatment (tubal damage) outweighs the cost of overtreatment (antibiotics in a patient who does not have PID).</p>

<p>Additional features that increase probability: vaginal discharge (especially mucopurulent); fever (present in only a minority - absence does not exclude PID); raised WBC, CRP, or ESR; positive chlamydia or gonorrhoea NAAT. Symptoms often begin during or immediately after menstruation. A recent new partner or multiple partners increases pretest probability.</p>

<p>The mildest form of PID may present only with cervical motion tenderness and minimal systemic features - this is the most commonly missed presentation in primary care. Significant systemic illness (high fever, peritonism, inability to tolerate oral antibiotics) and tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA) are indications for hospital admission.</p>

<h2>Investigation</h2>

<p>Investigation should not delay treatment. Investigations are aimed at confirming STI aetiology, excluding alternative diagnoses, and assessing severity.</p>

<p>STI testing: endocervical or self-collected vaginal swab for NAAT (Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae); send culture and sensitivity if gonorrhoea is suspected given NZ resistance patterns. Urine NAAT for chlamydia/gonorrhoea is less sensitive than vaginal swabs for PID assessment.</p>

<p>Bloods: FBC (WBC elevation), CRP (raised in moderate-severe PID), LFT and U&E if admission being considered. Urinalysis and urine culture to exclude urinary tract cause. Urine or serum pregnancy test - essential before treatment, both to exclude ectopic pregnancy and because some antibiotics are contraindicated in pregnancy.</p>

<p>Pelvic ultrasound: useful to detect tubo-ovarian abscess (thick-walled adnexal mass, complex fluid), free fluid, and to assess for other causes of pelvic pain. A normal ultrasound does not exclude PID.</p>

<p>Consider hepatitis B and C, and HIV testing as part of an STI screen.</p>

<h2>Management</h2>

<p>Empirical antibiotic therapy should be started before swab results are available when the clinical diagnosis is made.</p>

<p>NZ treatment guidelines (NZSHS): outpatient regimen for mild to moderate PID (most primary care presentations) typically includes doxycycline plus metronidazole, with or without a single dose of ceftriaxone IM/IV to cover gonorrhoea (especially important given NZ gonorrhoea resistance patterns - oral cefixime is no longer considered adequate for treatment of gonorrhoea in NZ). Check current NZSHS guidelines at time of prescribing as recommendations are updated regularly to reflect local resistance data.</p>

<p>Duration of treatment: typically 14 days for the oral regimen. Ensure analgesia is adequate. Review in 72 hours to confirm clinical improvement; if no improvement, reconsider diagnosis and admit for IV antibiotics and surgical assessment.</p>

<p>Partner notification is mandatory: all sexual partners within the past 6 months (or the most recent partner if longer ago) should be notified, tested, and treated, even if asymptomatic. In NZ, patient-delivered partner therapy (PDPT) is an option for some situations - discuss with the NZ sexual health service. Abstain from sex until both patient and partner(s) have completed treatment.</p>

<p>An IUD does not need to be routinely removed if PID is diagnosed - current evidence supports leaving it in situ unless there is no clinical improvement within 72 hours of appropriate antibiotics. Discuss removal versus retention with the patient.</p>

<h2>Complications and follow-up</h2>

<p>Tubo-ovarian abscess: requires admission, IV antibiotics, and possible surgical drainage. Suspect if systemic features, palpable adnexal mass, or failure to respond to outpatient antibiotics. Even without abscess, failure to improve on outpatient antibiotics within 72 hours warrants reassessment and admission.</p>

<p>Long-term sequelae of PID: chronic pelvic pain (affects approximately 20% of women following one episode); tubal factor subfertility (approximately 10-15% after one episode, rising to 30-40% after three or more episodes); ectopic pregnancy risk is increased 6-10-fold. Counselling women about these risks after treatment is part of good clinical care - and part of the motivation for prompt, complete treatment.</p>

<h2>Referral criteria</h2>

<p>Admit for IV antibiotics and gynaecology review: suspected TOA; systemic illness (fever over 38.5 C, peritonism); failure to improve within 72 hours of outpatient treatment; pregnancy; inability to tolerate oral antibiotics; immunosuppression. Refer to sexual health service for partner notification support, complex STI management, or second and third episodes of PID.</p>
