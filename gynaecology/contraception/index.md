<p>Contraception counselling is one of the most consequential conversations in general practice. The method a woman leaves the consultation with - or doesn't - affects years of her life. Getting it right means moving past the pamphlet and into the nuance: her priorities, her health history, her tolerance for the unexpected. Good counselling starts with the most effective options and works down, not the other way around.</p>

<h2>Long-acting reversible contraception (LARC)</h2>

<p>LARCs - the copper IUD, levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena, Jaydess), and subdermal implant (Jadelle in NZ) - are the most effective reversible methods available, with failure rates under 1% per year in typical use. They are funded in New Zealand for eligible patients and should be the starting point of any contraception conversation, not an afterthought.</p>

<p>The <strong>copper IUD</strong> is the only highly effective non-hormonal LARC. It is also the most effective emergency contraception available, with a failure rate under 0.1% when inserted within five days of unprotected intercourse - far outperforming oral options. Fertility returns immediately on removal. It can worsen dysmenorrhoea and heavy periods; not ideal for those who already have heavy or painful periods.</p>

<p><strong>Jadelle</strong> (two-rod levonorgestrel implant) is funded in NZ and lasts up to five years. It is the most effective contraceptive method available, with a failure rate of approximately 0.05% per year. Common counselling points: irregular bleeding is expected and usually improves over time; it does not affect future fertility; it cannot be felt during intercourse. Does not protect against STIs.</p>

<p>The <strong>levonorgestrel IUS</strong> (Mirena 52 mg, licensed 8 years; Jaydess 13.5 mg, licensed 3 years) reduces menstrual blood loss significantly and is funded in NZ as a contraceptive and for heavy menstrual bleeding. The Mirena is particularly useful in women who want contraception and management of HMB simultaneously.</p>

<h2>Combined oral contraceptive pill (COCP)</h2>

<p>With perfect use, the COCP has a failure rate of 0.3%. With typical use - missed pills, gastrointestinal illness, enzyme-inducing medications - it rises to around 9%. That gap is the central clinical reality of pill counselling. The pill works if taken reliably; for many women, it isn't.</p>

<p>WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) category 4 contraindications (do not use) include: migraine with aura, personal history of VTE or stroke, current breast cancer, age over 35 with 15 or more cigarettes per day, and poorly controlled hypertension. Category 3 conditions (risks generally outweigh benefits) warrant shared decision-making and documentation. Always check current MEC tables - the full list is longer than commonly memorised.</p>

<p>The NZF lists several funded COCP options. Levonorgestrel-containing pills (e.g. Levlen ED) carry a lower VTE risk than desogestrel- or drospirenone-containing pills and should be the default first prescription unless there is a specific indication otherwise (e.g. acne, where anti-androgenic progestogens such as drospirenone may be preferred).</p>

<h2>Progestogen-only options</h2>

<p>The <strong>progestogen-only pill (POP, or "mini-pill")</strong> is often underused. It is suitable for women who cannot use oestrogen - including those who are breastfeeding, have migraine with aura, or are at elevated VTE risk. The desogestrel-containing POP (Cerazette) inhibits ovulation in over 97% of cycles and has a more forgiving missed-pill window (12 hours, versus 3 hours for older levonorgestrel POPs).</p>

<p><strong>Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate</strong> (DMPA, Depo-Provera) is a funded injectable given every 12 weeks. It is highly effective and particularly useful for patients who struggle with daily adherence. Clinicians should counsel that return to fertility may be delayed by 6-12 months after stopping, and that bone density decreases with long-term use - generally reversible on cessation, but relevant in adolescents and those with osteoporosis risk factors.</p>

<h2>Emergency contraception</h2>

<p>Two oral options are available in NZ: levonorgestrel 1.5 mg (Postinor, pharmacy OTC) and ulipristal acetate 30 mg (EllaOne, prescription). Ulipristal is more effective across the full 120-hour window and maintains efficacy in women over 70 kg - levonorgestrel's effectiveness decreases with increasing body weight. For any patient where efficacy matters most, copper IUD insertion remains the gold standard.</p>

<p>Emergency contraception prevents, rather than terminates, pregnancy. This distinction is worth making explicitly in the consultation - it is a common source of patient hesitation.</p>

<h2>The consultation</h2>

<p>Good contraception counselling is not about recommending the most effective method - it is about helping the patient identify what fits her life. A woman who understands her options, has her concerns addressed without dismissal, and leaves with a plan she chose is more likely to use contraception effectively than one who leaves with a pamphlet and a prescription she wasn't sure about.</p>

<p>Ask about her priorities. Ask what she has tried before and what she thought of it. Ask what her partner thinks, if relevant and safe to ask. Ask about her plans for having children. These questions take two minutes and make a material difference to the outcome.</p>
