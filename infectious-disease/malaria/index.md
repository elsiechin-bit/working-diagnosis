<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Drug choice follows regional resistance patterns. Sub-Saharan Africa and PNG: atovaquone-proguanil or doxycycline first-line. SE Asia mainland (Thailand-Cambodia-Myanmar border): atovaquone-proguanil or doxycycline only - do not use mefloquine here due to documented P. falciparum resistance.</li>
    <li>Always check the NZF or CDC country tables at time of consultation. Resistance patterns shift; a summary more than 12 months old may be unreliable.</li>
    <li>G6PD testing is mandatory before prescribing primaquine for radical cure of P. vivax or P. ovale. Quantitative enzyme activity level preferred over qualitative testing.</li>
    <li>Standby emergency treatment (SBET) is appropriate for genuinely remote travellers unable to access medical care within 24 hours of fever onset. Preferred agent: artemether-lumefantrine (Riamet). Document carefully.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
  The <a href="/infectious-disease/">Travel overview</a> covers the four main prophylactic agents and their key contraindications. This page covers regional risk stratification, paediatric dosing, clinically significant drug interactions, and the two scenarios that require extra thought: the remote traveller who needs standby emergency treatment, and the returned traveller with <em>Plasmodium vivax</em> or <em>P. ovale</em> who needs radical cure.
</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Regional risk stratification</p>
  <p>Not all malaria risk is equal. Species present, transmission intensity, and local resistance patterns vary by region, and drug choice follows directly from that assessment.</p>

  <p class="tier-label">❍ Paediatric dosing</p>
  <p>Children develop more severe malaria than adults, particularly under 5 years of age. Prophylaxis is appropriate for children travelling to endemic regions.</p>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Radical cure for P. vivax and P. ovale</p>
  <p>
    <em>P. vivax</em> and <em>P. ovale</em> form dormant liver stages (hypnozoites) that are not eliminated by standard prophylactic agents or by artemisinin-based combination therapies used to treat falciparum malaria. These hypnozoites can reactivate months to years after travel, causing relapsing malaria long after the patient has returned to New Zealand.
  </p>
  <p>
    Radical cure with primaquine eliminates the hypnozoite reservoir. It is considered for travellers who have had significant exposure to vivax or ovale endemic areas (particularly Southeast Asia, South Asia, Papua New Guinea, and the Pacific) and who wish to reduce the risk of future relapse.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>G6PD screening is mandatory before prescribing primaquine.</strong> Primaquine causes acute haemolysis in patients with G6PD deficiency, which is common in populations from malaria-endemic regions. Quantitative G6PD testing (enzyme activity level) is preferred over qualitative testing as it detects heterozygous females who may have intermediate activity and remain at risk. Standard dosing: primaquine 30 mg base daily for 14 days with food. In G6PD-deficient patients, refer to infectious diseases or a travel medicine clinic for specialist management.
  </p>
  <p>
    Primaquine is contraindicated in pregnancy. This is a specialist discussion for most NZ GPs; ID or travel medicine clinic input is appropriate.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Standby emergency treatment (SBET)</strong> is appropriate for travellers who are genuinely remote and may be unable to access medical care for 24 hours or more after developing fever. The traveller carries a full treatment course of an appropriate antimalarial, with written instructions to self-treat if they develop fever and cannot access a medical facility within 24 hours. Preferred SBET regimen: artemether-lumefantrine (Riamet): 4 tablets at 0, 8, 24, 36, 48, and 60 hours, taken with food. SBET is not a replacement for seeking medical care - the patient should still reach a facility for blood film confirmation and to ensure treatment has worked. Document the prescribing and counsel carefully.
  </p>
