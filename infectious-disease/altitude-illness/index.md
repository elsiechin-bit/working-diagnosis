<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>AMS is diagnosed when headache at altitude is present plus total Lake Louise Score of 3 or above. If ataxia or altered consciousness is present - stop using the score. That is HACE, which is a clinical emergency. Test for ataxia with tandem gait (10 heel-to-toe steps) on any symptomatic trekker.</li>
    <li>Descent is always the definitive treatment for HACE and HAPE. Emergency medications (dexamethasone for HACE, nifedipine for HAPE) buy time - they do not replace descent.</li>
    <li>For SE Asia, Peru, or Kilimanjaro itineraries: acetazolamide prophylaxis is appropriate for compressed ascent profiles or prior AMS history. Ibuprofen 600 mg TDS is a reasonable alternative for patients who are sulfonamide-allergic or cannot tolerate acetazolamide's side effects.</li>
    <li>Standard Everest Base Camp trek itineraries (12-14 days from Lukla with rest days at Namche and Dingboche) are generally adequate. Kilimanjaro 5-day routes are too fast for the altitude - 7-day routes have significantly better summit success and lower AMS rates.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>The <a href="/travel/">Travel overview</a> covers the three altitude illness syndromes (AMS, HACE, HAPE) and the basics of acetazolamide prophylaxis and emergency treatment. This page provides the diagnostic criteria in usable form, practical acclimatisation schedules for the treks NZ patients commonly attempt, the evidence for ibuprofen as an alternative prophylactic agent, and field management protocols for HACE and HAPE when descent is delayed.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Lake Louise Score and AMS diagnosis</p>
  <p>Acute mountain sickness (AMS) is diagnosed when headache is present AND total Lake Louise Score is 3 or above, having arrived at altitude in the preceding 6-12 hours. A score of 3-5 indicates mild AMS; 6 or above indicates severe AMS. Do not use the score if ataxia or altered consciousness is present - those features indicate HACE regardless of score.</p>

  <p><strong>Testing for ataxia:</strong> tandem gait test - ask the person to walk heel-to-toe in a straight line for 10 steps. Inability to maintain the line without stumbling, or loss of balance while stationary with feet together and eyes closed (Romberg test), indicates ataxia in the altitude context. These tests take 30 seconds and should be done routinely on any symptomatic trekker at altitude.</p>
  <p>The general acclimatisation rule above 3,000 m: do not increase sleeping altitude by more than 300-500 m per night, and take a rest day for every 1,000 m gained. Ibuprofen 600 mg TDS (starting 6 hours before ascent) has RCT evidence for AMS prevention and is a reasonable alternative to acetazolamide in patients who are sulfonamide-allergic or intolerant of its side effects (paraesthesia, polyuria, altered taste of carbonated drinks).<sup>1,2</sup> Ibuprofen is less appropriate in older patients, those with renal impairment, or those who will be exercising vigorously at altitude.</p>

        <td style="padding: 10px 0; color: var(--ink-soft);">Tadalafil 10 mg BD or sildenafil 50 mg TDS. Phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors reduce pulmonary vascular resistance. Used as alternative or adjunct when nifedipine is unavailable or not tolerated.</td>
      </tr>

  <p>Absolute contraindications to high-altitude travel above 3,500 m are uncommon but include prior HACE or HAPE (high recurrence risk), sickle cell disease, severe pulmonary hypertension, and severe uncontrolled heart failure. Most patients with well-controlled cardiac or respiratory disease can ascend with appropriate precautions and specialist clearance. A previous episode of AMS does not preclude future altitude travel - it indicates that a slower ascent profile and/or acetazolamide is particularly warranted.</p>
