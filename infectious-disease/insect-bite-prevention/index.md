Counsel pregnant travellers and those planning pregnancy specifically about destinations with active Zika transmission, and advise appropriate insect bite precautions during the day as well as at night.</li>
  </ul>

<p>Chemoprophylaxis and vaccines address specific pathogens, but insect bite prevention is the upstream intervention that reduces exposure to multiple vector-borne diseases simultaneously. It is often covered briefly at the end of the pre-travel consultation and then forgotten. This page provides the detail needed to give specific, actionable advice: which repellents work, at what concentrations, for how long, and how to layer them with other physical measures.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Vector timing and repellent selection</p>
  <p><strong>Why vector timing matters:</strong> the two clinically most important mosquito genera for NZ travellers bite at different times. Anopheles mosquitoes (malaria vectors) primarily bite from dusk to dawn, with peak activity in the two hours after sunset and before sunrise. Outdoor exposure in the evening and sleeping without protection are the highest-risk periods. Aedes mosquitoes (dengue, Zika, chikungunya, yellow fever vectors) primarily bite during the day, particularly around dawn and dusk. Aedes aegypti, the principal dengue vector, is a peri-urban species that breeds in standing water around human habitation - flower vases, tyres, containers. Air-conditioned urban hotel rooms provide little protection against it because the mosquito enters during daylight. A traveller using only a bed net and evening DEET for Bangkok or Bali has good malaria protection but minimal dengue protection.</p>
  <p><strong>DEET (N,N-diethyl-meta-toluamide):</strong> the most extensively studied and widely available insect repellent, effective against mosquitoes, ticks, sand flies, and other biting arthropods. Higher concentrations provide longer duration of protection, not greater intensity. A 10% formulation protects for approximately 2 hours; 30% for approximately 6 hours; 50% for up to 12 hours. For most SE Asia or Pacific destinations, 30-40% provides practical all-day coverage with a single application; 50% DEET is appropriate for very high-risk areas (rural sub-Saharan Africa, rainforest travel in endemic regions). Safe for children aged 2 months and older - use 10-30% concentrations under 12 years, avoid hands, eyes, and irritated skin. Safe in pregnancy: the risk of vector-borne diseases outweighs any theoretical risk from the repellent itself, and WHO and CDC both recommend DEET use in pregnancy for travellers to endemic areas.<sup>1,2</sup> Application: apply sunscreen first, then DEET on top; do not apply under clothing; wash hands after application; do not spray directly onto the face.</p>
  <p><strong>Picaridin (icaridin):</strong> comparable efficacy to DEET at equivalent concentrations with a better cosmetic profile - no plastic damage, no greasy texture. Standard NZ and Australian formulations are 9.5-20%. A 20% picaridin formulation provides approximately 8-12 hours of mosquito protection. Safe from 2 months of age. A practical alternative for patients who find DEET's texture objectionable or who need to apply repellent to synthetic fabrics (DEET dissolves some plastics and synthetic textiles).</p>
  <p><strong>Permethrin for clothing and bed nets:</strong> a synthetic pyrethroid insecticide used on clothing, bed nets, and gear - not on skin. It is a contact insecticide that kills or repels insects landing on treated fabric. Permethrin-treated clothing provides approximately 30-70% additional bite reduction beyond repellent on skin alone.<sup>3</sup> In high-risk settings, combining permethrin-treated clothing with DEET on exposed skin is substantially more protective than either alone. Permethrin spray can be applied at home before departure; treatment lasts approximately 6 washes or 6 weeks of field use. Pre-treated clothing (ExOfficio Bugs Away, Craghoppers NosiLife) is also available from outdoor retailers.</p>
  <p>Insecticidal bed nets (long-lasting insecticidal nets, LLIN) use permethrin or other pyrethroids embedded in net fibres, remaining effective for 3-5 years without re-treatment. For rural sub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea, or other high-transmission areas, advise patients to use a net over any bed or sleeping mat, tuck it under the mattress all around (Anopheles can find a 1 cm gap), apply DEET before getting under the net, and check for holes before use.</p>
  <p><strong>Other physical measures:</strong> long-sleeved shirts and long trousers in the evening for malaria risk, and during the day for dengue risk; light-coloured clothing; socks and closed shoes when walking on grass or soil in tick-endemic areas (Europe, North America, Central Asia); air-conditioned accommodation with closed windows in high-transmission zones; electric mosquito-repellent vaporisers (pyrethroid plug-ins) for indoor use at night in rooms without screens.</p>

  <p>For tick-borne diseases, advise travellers to check for ticks after any outdoor activity in endemic areas - particularly the scalp, behind the ears, groin, and axillae. Remove attached ticks by grasping as close to the skin surface as possible with fine-pointed tweezers and pulling steadily outward; do not twist. Crushing, burning, or applying petroleum jelly increases disease transmission risk by inducing regurgitation.</p>
