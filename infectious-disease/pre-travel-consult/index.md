<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Ideal lead time is 6-8 weeks for multi-dose vaccine courses and a mefloquine trial. Last-minute presentations are still worthwhile - atovaquone-proguanil starts 1-2 days before entry into an endemic area, and single-dose vaccines (hepatitis A, typhoid Vi, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis dose one) can all be given at any point. Document timing constraints clearly in the notes.</li>
    <li>Yellow fever: a single dose confers lifelong immunity in immunocompetent adults; the ICVP is valid for life (WHO 2016 update). Yellow fever vaccination must be administered by a registered Yellow Fever Vaccination Centre - not all general practices are registered. Check the IMAC website for the current NZ list.</li>
    <li>All travel-specific vaccines (hepatitis A and B, typhoid, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, rabies pre-exposure, meningococcal) are unfunded in NZ. Frame cost expectations before the consultation - combined hepatitis A and B courses and multi-dose rabies pre-exposure series are expensive.</li>
    <li>Medical evacuation from rural SE Asia or the Pacific can cost NZ$50,000-100,000 or more. Ask all travellers whether their travel insurance covers medical evacuation; credit card travel insurance frequently excludes pre-existing conditions or has low emergency medical limits.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>The pre-travel consultation is one of the few occasions in general practice where a single appointment can meaningfully prevent serious illness. Done well, it covers risk assessment, vaccination, chemoprophylaxis, and safety counselling in a structured way. Done poorly, it misses the patient who returns with typhoid because nobody asked whether they were visiting family in a village rather than staying in a hotel.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Timing, triage, and yellow fever certificates</p>
  <p><strong>Timing:</strong> the ideal lead time is 6-8 weeks before departure, allowing completion of multi-dose vaccine courses (hepatitis B requires three doses over two months; rabies pre-exposure requires three doses over 28 days), a mefloquine trial before departure (mefloquine should be started three weeks before travel to detect neuropsychiatric intolerance while still in New Zealand), and seroconversion after live vaccines. Many patients present far later - a patient walking in the week before departure is not an opportunity to refuse the consultation but to maximise what can still be done. Atovaquone-proguanil chemoprophylaxis starts 1-2 days before entry into an endemic area and requires no trial period. Document the timing constraint in the notes.</p>
  <p><strong>Complexity triage - who needs specialist input:</strong> consider referring to a travel medicine clinic for immunocompromised travellers (solid organ transplant, haematopoietic stem cell transplant, active malignancy, biologic immunosuppression, HIV with CD4 below 200); pregnant travellers considering malaria-endemic destinations, especially with chloroquine-resistant areas; travellers with complex multi-country itineraries including both yellow fever-endemic and non-endemic countries (certificate timing and validity requirements are intricate); travellers to areas with active outbreaks of viral haemorrhagic fever or unusual endemic disease; children under 2 years travelling to high-risk regions; and travellers with a prior severe reaction to a travel vaccine or antimalarial. For uncomplicated itineraries, general practice is the right setting. Travel medicine clinics in NZ's main centres often attached to ID departments or specialist travel health services welcome telephone advice for GPs with borderline cases.</p>
  <p><strong>Yellow fever certificates:</strong> yellow fever vaccination must be administered by a registered Yellow Fever Vaccination Centre; the International Certificate of Vaccination or Prophylaxis (ICVP - the "yellow card") must be issued by an authorised centre. Not all general practices are registered. Check the IMAC website for the current NZ list of authorised centres. A single dose confers lifelong immunity in immunocompetent adults and the certificate is now valid for life (WHO update, 2016).<sup>1</sup> For immunocompromised travellers, a booster may be recommended - specialist input required. The vaccine is contraindicated under 9 months of age and in immunocompromised patients; a medical exemption letter may be issued by an authorised centre where the vaccine is genuinely contraindicated, but this does not protect the traveller biologically in endemic areas.</p>
</div>

  <p>Travel-specific vaccines (hepatitis A, hepatitis B, typhoid, yellow fever, Japanese encephalitis, rabies pre-exposure, meningococcal) are all unfunded in New Zealand. Patients pay privately for both the vaccine and administration. For itineraries where budget is a genuine constraint, prioritise vaccines with the highest risk-benefit ratio for that specific destination and travel style.</p>
  <p><strong>Travel health kit</strong> for high-risk regions: oral rehydration sachets (Gastrolyte or equivalent) - minimum 6; standby antibiotic (azithromycin or ciprofloxacin as appropriate to region - see <a href="/infectious-disease/travellers-diarrhoea/">Traveller's diarrhoea</a>); loperamide for adults; insect repellent with DEET (30-50% for high-risk areas) or picaridin (see <a href="/infectious-disease/insect-bite-prevention/">Insect bite prevention</a>); permethrin spray for treating clothing and bed nets; broad-spectrum sunscreen; wound care basics (antiseptic, adhesive dressings, blister treatment); thermometer; sufficient supply of all regular prescription medications in original labelled containers with a GP letter; copy of vaccination certificate and key medical documents. For trekkers to altitude: acetazolamide if prescribed, and a written altitude illness action plan (see <a href="/infectious-disease/altitude-illness/">Altitude illness</a>).</p>
</div>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Documentation and insurance</p>
  <p>Record the consultation clearly: destination, dates of travel, vaccines given with lot numbers, chemoprophylaxis prescribed, and any specific counselling provided (safe food and water, insect precautions, sun protection, sexual health, road safety). If a patient declines a recommended vaccine or prophylaxis, document this with the reason. For medically complex patients, a travel-specific clinical summary is useful if the patient needs medical attention abroad.</p>
  <p>Travel insurance is not medical advice, but worth raising. Patients underestimate evacuation costs from remote destinations: medical evacuation from rural SE Asia or the Pacific can cost NZ$50,000-100,000 or more. Existing credit card travel insurance frequently excludes pre-existing conditions, has low emergency medical limits, or has been quietly amended in recent product changes - encourage patients to read the policy, not just the marketing summary.</p>
</div>
