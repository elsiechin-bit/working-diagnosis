<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Traveller's diarrhoea affects 20-60% of travellers to high-risk regions within the first two weeks of travel. Most episodes are mild, bacterial, and self-limiting within 3-5 days without antibiotics.</li>
    <li>Antibiotic choice depends on destination. For SE Asia (Bali, Thailand, Cambodia): azithromycin, not ciprofloxacin - fluoroquinolone resistance in Campylobacter exceeds 90% in some regions. For other destinations, ciprofloxacin or azithromycin both appropriate.</li>
    <li>Loperamide is contraindicated under 2 years and not recommended under 12. In adults with dysentery (bloody diarrhoea) or high fever, loperamide is also contraindicated - motility inhibition worsens invasive infection.</li>
    <li>Persistent diarrhoea more than 2 weeks after return: investigate for Giardia, Cryptosporidium, and Entamoeba histolytica. Fever in a returned traveller from a malaria-endemic region requires malaria exclusion first.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>Traveller's diarrhoea (TD) is the most common travel-related illness, affecting 20-60% of travellers to high-risk regions within the first two weeks of travel.<sup>1</sup> The <a href="/infectious-disease/">Travel overview</a> covers food and water precautions, standby antibiotic selection by region, and the use of loperamide. This page goes deeper on the organisms responsible (which shapes what to test for on return), a severity-based management framework for counselling patients before they leave, and the approach to paediatric travellers.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Causative organisms by region</p>
  <p>Most TD in the first week of travel is bacterial. Enterotoxigenic <em>Escherichia coli</em> (ETEC) accounts for 40-75% of cases globally and is the primary target of the Dukoral oral vaccine.<sup>2</sup> The causative organism varies by region, which has direct implications for standby antibiotic choice.</p>

  <p>Indications to seek GP review on return: ongoing diarrhoea more than 2 weeks after return (think Giardia, Cryptosporidium, <em>E. histolytica</em>); any bloody diarrhoea; fever in a returned traveller from a malaria-endemic region (exclude malaria before attributing to gastrointestinal illness); significant weight loss; or signs of dehydration.</p>
  <p>Stool microscopy for ova and cysts (three specimens on separate days), stool culture, and Giardia antigen are the initial investigations for returned travellers with persistent diarrhoea. For febrile returned travellers, the workup is broader - see <a href="/travel/returning-traveller/">Fever in the returned traveller</a>.</p>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Paediatric considerations and post-infectious sequelae</p>
  <p>The principles are the same as in adults but the risk is higher: infants and young children dehydrate faster and deteriorate more quickly. Several important differences from adult management apply:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Loperamide is not recommended under 12 years of age</strong> and is contraindicated under 2 years - it can cause serious CNS adverse effects in young children.</li>
    <li>ORS (Gastrolyte, Pedialyte) is the cornerstone of paediatric management. Parental counselling on ORS preparation and signs of dehydration (sunken fontanelle, dry mucous membranes, decreased urine output, lethargy) is essential.</li>
    <li>For children with moderate to severe TD requiring antibiotic treatment, azithromycin is preferred: 10 mg/kg/day for 3 days (maximum 500 mg/day). Ciprofloxacin is less preferred in children due to theoretical concerns about cartilage toxicity, though used when azithromycin is unavailable.</li>
    <li>Breastfed infants should continue breastfeeding during diarrhoeal illness.</li>
    <li>Any infant under 6 months with significant diarrhoea while travelling should be assessed by a medical professional rather than managed with standby treatment alone.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome (PI-IBS) affects approximately 10-15% of patients who had an acute infectious gastroenteritis, with higher rates after severe bacterial TD.<sup>4</sup> PI-IBS typically presents as altered bowel habit, bloating, and abdominal pain beginning within weeks of the acute illness and persisting for months. The mechanism involves gut microbiome disruption, low-grade mucosal inflammation, and altered gut motility. Management follows standard IBS principles: dietary modification (low-FODMAP, fibre adjustment), antispasmodics as needed, and reassurance regarding the benign course. Resolution occurs in most patients over 6-12 months. Persistent severe symptoms, weight loss, or alarm features warrant investigation to exclude inflammatory bowel disease, which can be triggered by infectious gastroenteritis in predisposed individuals.<sup>5</sup></p>
