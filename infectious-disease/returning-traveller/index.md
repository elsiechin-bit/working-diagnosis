<div class="tier-1">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ At a glance</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Any febrile returned traveller from a malaria-endemic area has malaria until proven otherwise - regardless of whether they took prophylaxis, and regardless of the fever pattern. Order a thick and thin blood film and malaria RDT simultaneously. A single film has only 75-90% sensitivity; repeat at 12-24 hour intervals up to three films before excluding malaria if clinical suspicion is high.</li>
    <li>Thrombocytopenia plus leukopenia in a returned traveller with fever = dengue or malaria until proven otherwise. Order dengue NS1 antigen (most sensitive in days 1-5) plus IgM/IgG serology (positive from day 5-6).</li>
    <li>Several travel-acquired infections are notifiable in NZ - including malaria, typhoid, hepatitis A, dengue, leptospirosis, and any suspected viral haemorrhagic fever. Notification is via EpiSurv; do not wait for laboratory confirmation for suspected VHF.</li>
    <li>All confirmed or strongly suspected falciparum malaria requires hospital admission for IV treatment in NZ. Call ID on-call before referring if uncertain.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>The returned traveller with fever is not a rare presentation in New Zealand general practice - it is a regular one, particularly after summer holidays and around periods of high outbound travel to Southeast Asia, India, and the Pacific. The <a href="/infectious-disease/">Travel overview</a> covers incubation periods and the major diagnoses to consider. This page provides the operational detail: what to order, how to interpret it, what cannot wait, and when to admit.</p>

<div class="tier-2">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Malaria workup and the tropical screen</p>
  <p>Any febrile returned traveller from a malaria-endemic area has malaria until proven otherwise. Malaria-endemic areas relevant to NZ travellers include sub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, Vanuatu, South and Southeast Asia, and parts of Central and South America. Fiji, Samoa, Tonga, the Cook Islands, Bali's main tourist areas, and Thailand's cities are not endemic. Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, and Vanuatu are.</p>
  <p>Order simultaneously: thick and thin blood film (thick film for sensitivity, thin film for species identification) and malaria rapid diagnostic test (RDT; detects HRP2 for P. falciparum, pLDH or aldolase for non-falciparum species). Use together - each catches different cases. Do not delay antimalarial treatment if the patient is critically unwell with suspected falciparum malaria; call ID on-call and initiate empirical IV artesunate while results are pending.</p>
  <p>The tropical screen below guides investigation of a febrile returned traveller who is not critically unwell. Not all investigations are required for every patient - let the clinical picture and travel history guide selection.</p>

<div class="tier-3">
  <p class="tier-label">❍ Notifiable diseases, admission criteria, and asymptomatic screening</p>
  <p>Several travel-acquired infections are notifiable to the Medical Officer of Health - the obligation falls on the attending clinician, not the laboratory. Key notifiable travel-related diseases include malaria, typhoid and paratyphoid fever, hepatitis A, leptospirosis, dengue fever, chikungunya, Zika virus infection, and any suspected viral haemorrhagic fever. Notification is via the online EpiSurv system or by phone to the local Public Health Unit. Do not wait for laboratory confirmation for diseases where clinical suspicion alone triggers notification (e.g. suspected VHF - contact ID immediately and implement infection control before further assessment).</p>
  <p>Criteria for urgent hospital admission: confirmed or strongly suspected falciparum malaria (all cases require admission for IV treatment in NZ); any altered consciousness or neurological signs; haemodynamic instability; severe thrombocytopenia (platelets below 50 x 10<sup>9</sup>/L) or active bleeding; significant acute renal impairment; jaundice with liver failure features; respiratory compromise; inability to tolerate oral fluids or medications; suspicion of typhoid with systemic toxicity; or any suspicion of viral haemorrhagic fever.</p>
  <p>Post-travel screening for asymptomatic infection is warranted for specific exposures, not for all returned travellers. Freshwater exposure in sub-Saharan Africa: schistosomiasis serology at 6-8 weeks after last exposure (serology is negative in the first 6 weeks). Extended travel in Southeast Asia or tropical regions: Strongyloides serology (stool microscopy misses Strongyloides in the majority of cases - serology is required; Strongyloides can cause life-threatening hyperinfection syndrome if the patient ever receives corticosteroids or immunosuppression). HIV and STI screening after potential exposure; hepatitis B and C serology if there was significant exposure risk from medical procedures, tattoos, or sexual contact in high-prevalence areas.</p>
