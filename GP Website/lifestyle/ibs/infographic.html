Irritable Bowel
Syndrome

One of the most common conditions a GP sees. Not dangerous, not imaginary, and very manageable with the right approach.

What is IBS?

A functional gut disorder: the bowel looks completely normal on any scan or scope, but does not work the way it should.

Orange: large intestine
main site of IBS activity

  • Looks normal Scans and scopes show nothing wrong. IBS is a problem of function, not structure.
  • Works differently Muscle contractions are poorly regulated, and the gut is more sensitive to pain than normal. The orange dots on the diagram show common areas of heightened sensitivity.
  • Not dangerous IBS does not cause bowel cancer, does not lead to inflammatory bowel disease, and does not shorten life expectancy.
  • Rome IV criteria Recurrent belly pain, at least one day per week, linked to a change in bowel frequency or stool form. That is what defines IBS clinically.

How common

~11%

of people worldwide, making IBS one of the most common conditions seen in general practice.1

Typical onset

20s–40s

Most people develop symptoms as younger adults, though IBS can appear at any age.

How it is diagnosed

Clinical

No blood test or scope diagnoses IBS. It is made on history and examination, after ruling out other conditions.2

Post-infectious IBS

~10%

of people who have gastroenteritis go on to develop IBS in the months that follow.3

IBS is classified into subtypes based on your predominant bowel pattern. Knowing your subtype matters, because it shapes which treatments are most likely to help. Use the tab to explore yours.

Which type do you have?

IBS is classified by your predominant bowel pattern. Most people recognise themselves in one of these, though patterns can shift over time.

IBS with constipation (IBS-C)

What it feels like

Hard or lumpy stools more than a quarter of the time. Bloating and a feeling of incomplete emptying are common. Pain tends to ease after a bowel movement. Urgency is less typical than in IBS-D.

What tends to help most

Soluble fibre (oats, linseeds, psyllium), adequate hydration, regular activity. Osmotic laxatives like macrogol can be useful. The low FODMAP diet helps identify food triggers. Linaclotide has specific evidence for IBS-C.4

Worth knowing

Wheat bran is commonly thought to help with constipation, but it can worsen bloating and pain in IBS. Soluble fibre is generally better tolerated. If you have tried increasing fibre and felt worse, this is likely why.

IBS with diarrhoea (IBS-D)

What it feels like

Loose or watery stools more than a quarter of the time. Urgency is common and can be distressing. Pain often precedes a bowel movement and eases afterwards. Morning urgency after meals is a frequent pattern.

What tends to help most

The low FODMAP diet has the strongest evidence for reducing frequency. Loperamide reduces urgency. Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (like amitriptyline) work well by slowing transit and reducing gut sensitivity, not by treating mood.5

Worth knowing

Post-infectious IBS (following gastroenteritis) is most commonly IBS-D. If your symptoms started after a gut infection, you are in good company. This subtype often improves more over time than others.

IBS with mixed bowel habits (IBS-M)

What it feels like

Both hard/lumpy and loose/watery stools, each making up more than a quarter of bowel movements. The bowel cannot settle into a predictable pattern, and symptoms can swing between constipation and diarrhoea within days.

What tends to help most

The low FODMAP diet is still the best starting point. A symptom and food diary is particularly useful for identifying patterns. Psychological approaches (gut-directed hypnotherapy, CBT) work across all subtypes and suit an unpredictable pattern well.6

Worth knowing

IBS subtype is not fixed. Many people shift between subtypes over months or years. If your pattern changes substantially, it is worth mentioning to your GP, particularly if any new symptoms appear alongside the change.

Why does IBS happen?

There is no single cause. IBS is the end result of several overlapping problems. Select any tile to read more.

🧠
The Gut-Brain Axis

Your gut and brain are in constant two-way conversation. In IBS, that conversation is disrupted.

📡
Visceral Hypersensitivity

The gut becomes more sensitive than usual, so normal sensations are felt as pain.

🌊
Altered Gut Motility

The muscles that move food through the gut work too fast, too slow, or inconsistently.

🦠
Microbiome and Infection

Past gut infections and changes in gut bacteria can trigger or sustain IBS.

What helps?

IBS is manageable for most people, but usually requires a combination of approaches. Think of these as layers, not alternatives. Select each to expand.

🥦
Start here
Diet
›

The low FODMAP diet has the strongest evidence base of any dietary intervention for IBS.7 FODMAP stands for fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides and polyols: carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small bowel and fermented by bacteria in the large bowel, producing gas and drawing in water.

The diet involves a strict restriction phase (usually 4 to 6 weeks), followed by careful reintroduction to identify your personal triggers. It works best with guidance from a dietitian, as it is easy to do incorrectly and can be unnecessarily restrictive if you skip the reintroduction phase.

Soluble fibre (oats, linseeds, psyllium husk) tends to help across subtypes. Wheat bran, by contrast, worsens symptoms for some people despite being commonly recommended.

Often underrated
Lifestyle
›

Regular physical activity has good evidence for improving IBS symptoms, via both gut motility and nervous system effects.8 A brisk 30-minute walk most days is enough to make a meaningful difference. You do not need to run marathons.

Sleep quality and stress management are not optional extras. They are core to managing a condition driven partly by the gut-brain axis. Poor sleep reliably worsens IBS; improving it reliably helps. This deserves more attention than it usually gets.

Meal timing also matters. Large, rich, or fatty meals trigger stronger gut contractions. Regular, moderate-sized meals tend to produce fewer symptoms than erratic eating patterns.

💬
Strong evidence, under-used
Psychological Therapies
›

Gut-directed hypnotherapy and cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) have some of the strongest evidence of any IBS treatment, and are considered first-line options in NICE guidelines.6 This is not about being told symptoms are imaginary.

These therapies retrain the gut-brain communication pathways driving the condition. Gut-directed hypnotherapy specifically targets gut function, not general relaxation, and the evidence for it is robust. CBT addresses thought patterns that amplify symptom perception and avoidance behaviours that reduce quality of life.

Access in New Zealand is variable. Some primary care practitioners and psychologists with a gastrointestinal interest offer these approaches. Your GP can advise on what is available locally.

💊
Symptom relief
Medications
›

Medications treat symptoms rather than the underlying condition, and they work best as part of a broader plan rather than as a standalone fix.

Antispasmodics (mebeverine, hyoscine) reduce cramping. Loperamide reduces urgency and diarrhoea frequency in IBS-D. Osmotic laxatives (macrogol) help in IBS-C. Linaclotide has better trial evidence for IBS-C than standard laxatives.

Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline) are used at doses well below those needed for depression. They work in IBS by reducing visceral hypersensitivity and slowing gut transit, making them particularly useful in IBS-D. SSRIs have weaker evidence but may help where anxiety is prominent.5

Your GP will tailor medications to your subtype and symptom pattern. If something has not worked after a proper trial, it is worth revisiting rather than continuing indefinitely.

Antispasmodics Loperamide (IBS-D) Macrogol (IBS-C) Low-dose tricyclics Linaclotide (IBS-C)

IBS is chronic for most people, but its course is variable. Many people have long stretches with minimal symptoms, punctuated by flares often triggered by stress, illness, or diet. It does not progress to cancer or inflammatory bowel disease.
