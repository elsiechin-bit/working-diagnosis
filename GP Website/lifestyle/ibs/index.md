# Irritable Bowel Syndrome

❍ *Your gut, explained*

Irritable bowel syndrome, or IBS, is one of the most common conditions a GP sees — and also one of the most misunderstood. It is not "all in your head," it is not dangerous, and it does not lead to bowel cancer. But it can be genuinely disruptive to daily life, and it deserves a proper explanation rather than a shrug and a pamphlet.

---

## What is IBS?

IBS is a *functional gut disorder*. That means the bowel looks completely normal on any scan or scope, but it does not work the way it should. The muscle contractions that move food through your gut are poorly regulated — sometimes too fast, sometimes too slow, sometimes both at different times. The gut also becomes more sensitive than usual, so sensations that most people would not notice (like gas or a slight stretch in the bowel wall) are perceived as pain or urgency.

It is defined using the Rome IV criteria: recurrent abdominal pain, on average at least one day per week over the past three months, associated with a change in bowel frequency, a change in stool form, or both — and with the pain linked to defaecation.¹ In plain terms: if your belly regularly hurts and your bowels are unpredictable, IBS is a diagnosis worth considering.

IBS is split into subtypes based on what your stools are mostly like: IBS with predominant constipation (IBS-C), IBS with predominant diarrhoea (IBS-D), and mixed IBS (IBS-M). Knowing your subtype matters because it shapes which treatments are likely to help.

---

## How common is it?

Very. Worldwide prevalence sits around 11%, and New Zealand figures are consistent with that.² It is more common in women, and tends to present in younger adults, though it can appear at any age. It accounts for a substantial proportion of all GP consultations and is one of the most common reasons for gastroenterology referral — though most people are managed well in primary care.

---

## Why does it happen?

Researchers have not landed on a single cause, and that is because there probably is not one. IBS is best thought of as the end result of several overlapping problems.

The *gut-brain axis* is central. Your gut has its own extensive nervous system (sometimes called the enteric nervous system) that is in constant two-way communication with your brain. In IBS, this signalling is disrupted. Stress, anxiety, and mood do not cause IBS in the way that a germ causes an infection — but they do influence gut function, and they can amplify symptoms considerably. This is why treating IBS as purely a bowel problem, while ignoring the nervous system, usually does not work well.

*Visceral hypersensitivity* is the technical term for the heightened pain sensitivity described above. It is a real, measurable phenomenon — people with IBS have lower pain thresholds in their gut compared with people without it, even though the gut tissue itself is healthy.³

*Altered gut motility* accounts for the bowel habit changes. In IBS-D, transit is faster than normal; in IBS-C, it is slower. In mixed IBS, the bowel seems unable to make up its mind.

*Post-infectious IBS* is a well-recognised subtype. Roughly 10% of people who have a bout of gastroenteritis go on to develop IBS in the months that follow, suggesting that inflammation and changes to the gut microbiome can trigger the condition.⁴

*The microbiome* — the community of bacteria living in your gut — is altered in many people with IBS, though exactly what this means causally is still being worked out.

## How is it diagnosed?

IBS is a clinical diagnosis — it is made based on your history and examination, not on a test. There is no blood test or scope that diagnoses IBS. What tests *can* do is rule out other conditions that look similar: coeliac disease (a blood test for tissue transglutaminase antibodies is a reasonable screen), inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's or colitis), and thyroid disorders, among others.

Your GP will want to know about so-called "red flags" — symptoms that suggest something other than IBS needs to be investigated. These include rectal bleeding, unexplained weight loss, symptoms that consistently wake you from sleep, a family history of bowel cancer or inflammatory bowel disease, and new symptoms starting after age 50. If any of these are present, further investigation is appropriate before settling on an IBS diagnosis.

In the absence of red flags, in someone with a classic symptom pattern, extensive testing is usually unnecessary and can be counterproductive — normal results are reassuring, but the process of testing can sometimes reinforce anxiety about the gut rather than reduce it.

## What helps?

IBS is manageable for most people, though it often requires a combination of approaches rather than a single fix.

**Diet** is usually the first place to start. A low FODMAP diet — which temporarily restricts fermentable carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small bowel and fermented by bacteria in the large bowel — has the strongest evidence base of any dietary intervention for IBS.⁵ It involves a strict restriction phase followed by careful reintroduction to identify your personal triggers. It works best with guidance from a dietitian, as it is easy to do incorrectly and unnecessarily restrictive long-term. Common culprits include wheat, onion, garlic, lactose, and some fruits. Soluble fibre (found in oats, linseeds, and psyllium) tends to help across subtypes; insoluble fibre (wheat bran) can worsen symptoms in some people.

**Lifestyle** matters more than it tends to get credit for. Regular physical activity has good evidence for improving IBS symptoms, probably through both gut motility and psychological mechanisms.⁶ Sleep quality and stress management are not optional extras — they are core to managing a condition driven partly by the gut-brain axis.

**Psychological therapies**, particularly gut-directed hypnotherapy and cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), have strong evidence for IBS and are considered first-line options in NICE guidelines.⁷ These are not about being told your symptoms are imaginary — they work by retraining the gut-brain communication pathways that are causing the trouble. Access in New Zealand is variable, but some primary care practitioners and psychologists with a gastrointestinal interest can provide these.

**Medications** have a role, though they treat symptoms rather than the underlying condition. Antispasmodics (such as mebeverine or hyoscine) can help with cramping. Low-dose antidepressants — particularly tricyclics like amitriptyline — are used not primarily for mood but because they dampen visceral hypersensitivity and slow gut transit, which is helpful in IBS-D.⁸ Loperamide can reduce urgency and diarrhoea. For IBS-C, osmotic laxatives (like macrogol/polyethylene glycol) can help, though the evidence for newer agents like linaclotide is stronger. Your GP will tailor medications to your subtype and symptom pattern.

**Probiotics** are popular and the evidence is mixed — some formulations show modest benefit, most do not show meaningful benefit, and the field is complicated by the sheer variety of products. They are unlikely to cause harm, but are best considered an optional extra rather than a cornerstone of treatment.

## What should I expect long-term?

IBS is a chronic condition for most people, but its course is variable. Symptoms wax and wane — many people have long stretches with minimal symptoms, punctuated by flare-ups often triggered by stress, dietary indiscretion, illness, or no identifiable reason at all. A meaningful proportion of people see significant improvement over years, particularly those who engage with both dietary and psychological approaches.

It does not progress to inflammatory bowel disease or bowel cancer. It does not shorten life expectancy. Understanding this — genuinely internalising it rather than just being told it — tends to reduce the anxiety around symptoms, which in turn can reduce the symptoms themselves.

If you have had IBS for years and find it is interfering substantially with your life, it is worth revisiting with your GP. Treatments have evolved, and what was available a decade ago is not the complete picture now.

---

## A note on the gut-brain connection

It is worth dwelling on this for a moment, because it is where IBS often gets mishandled. When a GP mentions that stress plays a role, some people hear: "you are making it up" or "it is anxiety." That is not what is being said.

The gut-brain connection is anatomical and physiological. Your vagus nerve runs from your brainstem to your gut and carries far more information upward than downward. Your gut produces roughly 95% of the body's serotonin. Psychological states have measurable effects on gut motility, secretion, and pain sensitivity in *everyone* — in IBS, those effects are amplified. Treating the nervous system is treating the gut. The two are not separate.

The gut health team at your local DHB/Te Whatu Ora may have a dietitian with FODMAP expertise.
