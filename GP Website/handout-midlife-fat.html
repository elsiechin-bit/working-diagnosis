Metabolic health

Weight Changes
in Middle Age

Why it happens, where it goes, and what you can actually do about it.

Why does fat increase in middle age?

Most people notice their weight creeping up from their late thirties or forties, even when their eating habits have not changed. This is not simply a matter of willpower. Several biological shifts converge during midlife.

Muscle loss

From around age 30, muscle mass declines at roughly 3 to 8% per decade2 and the rate accelerates after 60. Because muscle burns more energy at rest than fat does, losing it slows your metabolism. The result: the same diet that maintained your weight at 30 can lead to gradual fat gain at 45.

Hormonal changes in women

The perimenopause and menopause (typically ages 45 to 55) bring a sharp decline in oestrogen. This shift redirects fat from the hips and thighs to the abdomen.3,4 Visceral fat (stored around the internal organs) increases even in women whose total weight barely changes.

Hormonal changes in men

Testosterone declines gradually from around age 30 to 40. Lower testosterone is associated with reduced muscle mass and increased central fat deposition. This process is more gradual than female menopause, but the metabolic effect is real.

Lifestyle shifts

Midlife often brings less incidental physical activity, disrupted sleep, and higher sustained stress.1 Each of these independently promotes fat storage, even before diet changes.

Where does the fat go, and why does it matter?

Not all fat is equal. There are two main types:

  • Subcutaneous fat sits just under the skin (around hips, thighs, upper arms). It is largely inert from a metabolic standpoint.
  • Visceral fat wraps around the liver, pancreas, and other abdominal organs. It is metabolically active, releasing inflammatory signals and fatty acids directly into the portal circulation.5

Higher visceral fat is independently linked to:

  • Type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance5
  • Cardiovascular disease — raised triglycerides, lower HDL cholesterol, raised blood pressure5
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease
  • Certain cancers (bowel, breast, endometrial)

Measuring your risk: waist circumference

Waist circumference is a practical proxy for visceral fat. Measure at the midpoint between your lowest rib and the top of your hip bone, after a gentle exhale. WHO thresholds6 are shown below. For people of Asian, South Asian, or Polynesian descent, lower thresholds may apply — ask your GP.

Group Increased risk High risk
Women > 80 cm (31.5 in) > 88 cm (34.6 in)
Men > 94 cm (37 in) > 102 cm (40.2 in)

What actually helps?

There is no single fix, but the evidence points clearly toward a set of habits that work together.

  1. Resistance (strength) training Building and preserving muscle mass is the most effective way to counteract the metabolic slowdown of ageing.7 Aim for two or more sessions per week targeting all major muscle groups. You do not need a gym: bodyweight exercises (squats, lunges, push-ups, rows) work well.
  2. Aerobic exercise Brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or dancing for 150 to 300 minutes per week at moderate intensity helps reduce visceral fat directly, independent of weight loss on the scales.
  3. Adequate protein Higher protein intake supports muscle repair and promotes satiety. Aim for protein at every meal: eggs, legumes, fish, chicken, dairy, or tofu. Spreading intake across meals is more effective than loading it all at dinner.
  4. Diet quality over calorie counting Reducing ultra-processed foods (biscuits, chips, fast food, sweetened drinks) has a disproportionate effect on visceral fat compared with simply cutting total calories. Prioritise vegetables, whole grains, legumes, fruit, nuts, and minimally processed proteins.
  5. Sleep Short sleep duration raises ghrelin (hunger hormone) and lowers leptin (fullness hormone), making overeating much more likely.8 Seven to nine hours per night is the target for most adults. If you sleep poorly and cannot resolve it, discuss this with your GP.
  6. Stress management Chronic stress drives elevated cortisol, which specifically promotes abdominal fat deposition.9 Regular exercise helps, as does time in nature, adequate social connection, and specific therapies for anxiety or burnout if needed.

When to talk to your GP

Most middle-aged weight change is gradual and does not require urgent attention. Come and see us if:

  • You have gained more than 5 kg over 6 to 12 months without a clear reason
  • Your waist circumference is in the high-risk zone above
  • You have a personal or family history of type 2 diabetes or cardiovascular disease
  • You notice symptoms that might suggest thyroid disease: fatigue, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, or hair thinning
  • You have tried lifestyle changes consistently for 3 to 6 months without effect
  • You are interested in discussing medications or structured programmes for weight management

Key takeaways

  • Middle-age fat gain is driven by biology, not just behaviour. Be kind to yourself.
  • Visceral (belly) fat is more harmful than fat elsewhere. Waist circumference matters more than weight alone.
  • Resistance training is the most underused tool for metabolic health in midlife.
  • Sleep, stress, protein, and diet quality all have measurable effects.
  • Small, consistent changes outperform short-term diets every time.
