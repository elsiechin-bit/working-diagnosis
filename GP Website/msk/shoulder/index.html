Shoulder Pain

Most shoulder pain in adults over 50 comes from one of four conditions. Each has a different natural history and responds to different treatment. Getting the diagnosis right matters.

What is causing my shoulder pain? ›

In adults over 50, shoulder pain usually falls into one of four categories. Each behaves differently, so identifying which one you have changes how it should be managed.

Rotator Cuff Disease
The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles and their tendons that surround the shoulder joint and keep the upper arm bone stable. With age and use, these tendons can become inflamed (tendinopathy) or develop tears, ranging from small partial tears to complete full-thickness ruptures. Rotator cuff disease is the most common cause of shoulder pain in adults over 50. Pain is typically felt at the outside of the upper arm, worsens with overhead activity, and may disturb sleep when lying on the affected side. A painful arc of movement between roughly 60 and 120 degrees of elevation is a classic sign.

Frozen Shoulder
Also called adhesive capsulitis, frozen shoulder involves inflammation and scarring of the capsule surrounding the joint. The result is progressive loss of movement in all directions, especially rotation outward. It typically passes through three phases: a painful "freezing" phase lasting months, a "frozen" phase where pain eases but stiffness is severe, and a "thawing" phase where movement gradually returns. People with diabetes are significantly more likely to develop frozen shoulder and often have a more prolonged course. Total duration without treatment is typically one to three years, though some people take longer.

Shoulder Arthritis
Osteoarthritis of the main shoulder joint (the glenohumeral joint) is less common than arthritis of the hip or knee, but becomes more prevalent after age 60. Deep, aching pain, a grating sensation, and loss of movement in multiple directions are typical. It can develop on its own or as a late consequence of old rotator cuff tears (rotator cuff arthropathy). A separate form of arthritis commonly affects the smaller acromioclavicular (AC) joint at the top of the shoulder, causing pain that is well-localised to the very top and worsens with reaching across the body.

AC Joint Disease
The acromioclavicular joint connects the collarbone to the bony point at the top of the shoulder. It is a common site of both acute injury and age-related arthritis. AC joint pain sits squarely at the top of the shoulder, often with a distinct tender spot, and is reproduced by reaching across the chest or lifting across the midline. It commonly coexists with rotator cuff problems and can be difficult to separate on examination alone.

Your GP will use your history, examination findings, and sometimes imaging to work out which condition (or combination) is causing your symptoms.

Rotator cuff: tendons and tears ›

The rotator cuff consists of four muscles and their tendons: supraspinatus (the most commonly affected), infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis. Together, they stabilise the ball of the shoulder joint within the socket and control rotation. The supraspinatus tendon runs through a narrow space under the bony arch of the acromion, which makes it particularly vulnerable to wear and compression.

Tendinopathy refers to a degenerative process within the tendon itself, where the normal tendon structure is disrupted by repetitive loading or ageing. This causes pain and reduced function but does not necessarily lead to a tear. Tendinopathy responds well to a structured exercise programme that gradually loads the tendon.

Tears range from partial-thickness (part of the tendon is damaged) to full-thickness (a complete hole in the tendon). Many full-thickness tears are surprisingly well tolerated. Research consistently shows that roughly one in three people over the age of 70 have a full-thickness rotator cuff tear visible on imaging, yet many of them have no shoulder pain at all.1 This means that finding a tear on a scan does not automatically explain someone's pain, and conversely, severe pain can occur with only minor tendon changes.

What helps: A supervised physiotherapy programme focused on strengthening the rotator cuff and scapular stabilisers is the mainstay of treatment and is effective for most people with tendinopathy and even many with partial or full-thickness tears. A corticosteroid injection into the subacromial space can reduce pain and help people engage with physiotherapy, though the benefit diminishes after a few months and repeated injections may harm tendon integrity. Surgery (usually arthroscopic repair) is considered for people with significant tears who have not improved after a full course of physiotherapy, particularly those who are younger and more physically active.

Frozen shoulder: what to expect ›

Frozen shoulder is one of the more frustrating conditions in musculoskeletal medicine, largely because it takes so long, the pain can be severe in the early phase, and there is no treatment that reliably shortens the overall course dramatically. Understanding the natural history helps set realistic expectations.

Phase 1: Freezing (roughly 2 to 9 months). The shoulder becomes increasingly painful, often with severe night pain and pain at rest. Movement begins to be lost. This is the most distressing phase.

Phase 2: Frozen (roughly 4 to 12 months). Pain begins to ease but stiffness reaches its worst. Simple tasks like reaching behind your back, fastening a bra, or combing hair become difficult. People are often surprised to find daily activities more restricted once the acute pain settles.

Phase 3: Thawing (roughly 5 to 24 months). Movement gradually returns. Most people achieve a functionally good result, though some retain mild residual stiffness.

Diabetes and thyroid disease are the strongest risk factors for frozen shoulder. People with diabetes are two to four times more likely to develop it and often have a longer, more difficult course.5 Good glycaemic control may help but does not prevent the condition entirely.

What helps: In the freezing phase, pain management (analgesics, NSAIDs, and particularly a corticosteroid injection) can provide meaningful relief and allow sleep and physiotherapy. Evidence supports corticosteroid injection for short-term pain relief in the freezing and early frozen phases, with the benefit typically lasting 6 to 12 weeks.4 Physiotherapy helps maintain movement and guides recovery, though aggressive stretching during the freezing phase can worsen pain. Hydrodilatation (distension arthrography), in which the shoulder capsule is stretched by injecting fluid under X-ray guidance, is a recognised procedure available in New Zealand and may accelerate recovery in the frozen phase. For people who do not progress with conservative treatment, manipulation under anaesthetic or arthroscopic capsular release can be effective, though most people would improve with time regardless.

Shoulder arthritis: glenohumeral and AC joint ›

Glenohumeral osteoarthritis affects the main ball-and-socket joint of the shoulder. It is significantly less common than hip or knee arthritis but shares the same underlying process: gradual loss of cartilage, bone remodelling, and inflammation. Unlike the hip and knee, the shoulder bears no body weight, which may partly explain why it develops arthritis less often. However, its large range of motion makes cartilage loss symptomatic early. Pain is typically deep and aching, felt in the front and side of the shoulder, and worsens with activity and end-of-range movement. A grating or clicking sensation is common. Stiffness is present but tends to be less global than in frozen shoulder.

Management follows the same principles as arthritis elsewhere: analgesia, physiotherapy to maintain strength and movement, activity modification, and corticosteroid injection for flares. Total shoulder replacement (arthroplasty) gives good long-term results for those with severe, function-limiting disease that has not responded to conservative management. It is a major surgical undertaking and is considered carefully alongside other health factors, particularly in older patients.

Acromioclavicular joint arthritis is very common and often found incidentally on imaging in people over 50. Many people have radiological AC joint OA without any symptoms. When it is symptomatic, the pain is well localised to the very top of the shoulder. A useful test is the cross-body adduction test, where bringing the arm across the chest loads the AC joint and reproduces the pain. Treatment includes activity modification, analgesics, physiotherapy, and a targeted AC joint injection, which can be very effective. Surgical resection of the outer end of the clavicle (Mumford procedure) is occasionally needed for severe or persistent cases but is uncommon.

It is worth noting that rotator cuff disease and AC joint arthritis often coexist, as both relate to the same anatomical region and similar ageing processes. Examination and imaging help identify which is contributing most to symptoms.

Self-management: what helps ›

Regardless of the underlying diagnosis, several general measures can help manage shoulder pain day to day while you work through a more specific treatment plan.

Modify activity, but don't stop moving. Avoiding the specific activities that provoke severe pain (especially heavy overhead work) makes sense in the short term. However, complete rest weakens the muscles around the joint and slows recovery. Most people benefit from keeping the shoulder gently active within a tolerable range while reducing aggravating loads.

Heat and cold. Heat from a warm pack or shower can ease muscular aching and stiffness, particularly useful in the morning. Cold applied to the shoulder may reduce acute inflammation and numb pain after activity. Try both and use whichever helps you most.

Sleep position. Shoulder pain is notorious for disturbing sleep, particularly when lying on the affected side. Sleeping on your back or unaffected side, with the affected arm supported on a pillow in a comfortable position, can help. A body pillow to prevent rolling onto the sore shoulder is a practical and inexpensive intervention.

Pain relief. Paracetamol provides background pain relief and is safe for most people taken regularly at prescribed doses. NSAIDs (such as ibuprofen or naproxen) can be more effective for inflammatory pain and are appropriate for short courses if you have no contraindications. Avoid NSAIDs if you have kidney disease, peptic ulcer disease, or are taking blood thinners, and use with caution in older adults. Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel) applied directly to the shoulder may provide local benefit with fewer systemic side effects and are a reasonable option for older patients.

Posture and ergonomics. Rounded shoulders and a forward head posture increase load on the rotator cuff tendons and AC joint. Being aware of your posture during desk work, particularly keeping the shoulder blades gently set back, can reduce symptom provocation. This is not a cure, but it reduces unnecessary mechanical load through the day.

Avoid prolonged immobility. For frozen shoulder in particular, gentle movement through the day prevents the capsule from tightening further. A few minutes of gentle pendulum exercises (letting the arm hang and making small circles) several times a day can help maintain range during the frozen phase without provoking excessive pain.

Physiotherapy and exercises ›

Physiotherapy is the single most important non-surgical treatment for almost all shoulder conditions. The evidence for supervised exercise, particularly for rotator cuff tendinopathy and tears, is strong and comparable to surgical outcomes in many studies.2

What a physiotherapist does. A physiotherapist will assess the shoulder to confirm the working diagnosis, identify any contributing factors (such as scapular dyskinesis, posture, or strength imbalances), and design a progressive exercise programme. They may also use hands-on techniques such as joint mobilisation, massage, or taping to reduce pain and improve movement as an adjunct to exercise. Importantly, they teach you the exercises in a way that is specific to your shoulder and progresses appropriately over time.

Rotator cuff exercise. For tendinopathy and partial tears, a structured programme typically begins with low-load isometric exercises and progresses to isotonic strengthening using resistance bands or weights, targeting the specific rotator cuff muscles and scapular stabilisers. The programme usually runs for 8 to 12 weeks and requires consistency to be effective. A significant proportion of people avoid surgery with a properly completed physiotherapy course.2

Frozen shoulder exercise. The approach changes depending on the phase. During the painful freezing phase, the priority is maintaining range of movement without provoking severe pain, using gentle pendulum exercises and range-of-motion work within a comfortable zone. Aggressive stretching during this phase may worsen pain without benefit. As the shoulder moves into the thawing phase, more active stretching and strengthening become appropriate.

Shoulder arthritis exercise. For glenohumeral OA, physiotherapy focuses on maintaining strength around the joint, preserving range of motion, and optimising movement patterns. Strengthening exercises reduce joint loading and improve function. Low-impact activities such as swimming and hydrotherapy are particularly well tolerated.

How to access physiotherapy in NZ. You can self-refer to a physiotherapist or ask your GP for a referral. ACC covers physiotherapy for shoulder injuries caused by an accident. For non-injury conditions, physiotherapy is available through private practice (out-of-pocket or with health insurance) or community allied health services if publicly funded referral is available in your region.

Investigations: do I need a scan? ›

Imaging is not always necessary and can sometimes be misleading. However, for shoulder conditions, it plays a more useful role than in conditions like non-specific back pain, because identifying the specific diagnosis (especially the nature and size of a rotator cuff tear) genuinely influences management.

X-ray is typically the first imaging investigation. It shows bone, not tendons or soft tissue, so it will not show a rotator cuff tear directly. However, it is useful for excluding fracture, identifying glenohumeral or AC joint OA (joint space narrowing, osteophytes), and looking for calcific deposits in the tendons (calcific tendinopathy). The position of the humeral head relative to the acromion on X-ray can also indirectly suggest a large cuff tear.

Ultrasound is an excellent and widely available tool for imaging the rotator cuff tendons. In experienced hands, it has good accuracy for identifying tendinopathy, partial tears, and full-thickness tears. It is also real-time, relatively inexpensive, and can guide injections. Many radiology practices in New Zealand offer diagnostic shoulder ultrasound. It is the preferred first-line imaging for suspected rotator cuff disease.

MRI provides the most detailed picture of all shoulder structures, including tendons, cartilage, labrum, and bone. It is the best modality for surgical planning and for complex cases where the diagnosis is uncertain. However, it is more expensive, less available, and takes longer than ultrasound. It is typically requested when surgery is being considered or when the clinical picture is unusual.

The imaging paradox. A common and important limitation of shoulder imaging is that it reveals incidental findings in many people without pain. Roughly one in three people over 70 have a rotator cuff tear visible on imaging but no shoulder symptoms.1 AC joint degeneration is near-universal over the age of 60. Glenohumeral OA may be present on X-ray in a person whose shoulder pain actually comes from their rotator cuff. Your GP and physiotherapist interpret imaging in the context of your clinical presentation, rather than treating the scan in isolation.

Injections, procedures, and surgery ›

Corticosteroid injection. An injection of corticosteroid (a powerful anti-inflammatory) into or around the shoulder joint is a commonly used and generally effective short-term intervention. For rotator cuff tendinopathy, a subacromial injection can reduce pain and facilitate physiotherapy, though the benefit typically lasts 6 to 12 weeks and does not address the underlying tendon problem. For frozen shoulder, injection in the freezing phase provides the most reliable pain relief and is strongly supported by evidence.4 For AC joint pain, a targeted AC joint injection is often very effective. Risks are low but include temporary pain flare, skin thinning, and with repeated injections, possible tendon weakening. Most guidelines suggest limiting to two or three injections in any given area.

Hydrodilatation (distension arthrography). This procedure involves injecting a combination of local anaesthetic, corticosteroid, and saline into the shoulder capsule under X-ray guidance, stretching the contracted capsule. It is used specifically for frozen shoulder and is available in New Zealand through radiology departments. Evidence suggests it provides benefit over injection alone for frozen shoulder, though the quality of evidence is moderate.6 It is generally well tolerated and can accelerate the thawing phase.

Manipulation under anaesthetic (MUA) and arthroscopic release. For frozen shoulder that has not resolved after 12 to 18 months of conservative management, surgical options are considered. MUA involves stretching the shoulder under general anaesthetic to break adhesions. Arthroscopic capsular release involves a keyhole procedure to cut the contracted capsule directly. Both can be effective and are available through orthopaedic services in New Zealand, though waiting times for elective surgery are significant. A decision to pursue surgery needs to weigh the expected natural history (most frozen shoulders do eventually resolve) against the impact on quality of life and function.

Rotator cuff repair. Arthroscopic repair of torn rotator cuff tendons is appropriate for selected patients, particularly younger, active individuals with significant full-thickness tears who have not improved with physiotherapy. Evidence does not support routine surgery for all cuff tears, especially in older patients, where the outcomes of physiotherapy are often equivalent to surgery at two-year follow-up. Surgery involves a keyhole repair of the tendon and requires a prolonged rehabilitation period of 3 to 6 months. Discussion with an orthopaedic surgeon helps clarify whether repair is likely to improve your function and quality of life beyond what conservative treatment can achieve.

Shoulder replacement (arthroplasty). For severe glenohumeral OA causing significant pain and functional loss unresponsive to conservative measures, total or partial shoulder replacement can provide excellent pain relief and improved movement. Long-term outcomes are good, particularly for anatomic total shoulder replacement in the context of an intact rotator cuff. People with rotator cuff arthropathy (severe OA secondary to cuff tear) may be offered a reverse total shoulder replacement, which has a different mechanical design suited to this situation. Shoulder arthroplasty is a major procedure and is considered carefully, particularly in older patients with other health conditions.

Red flags: urgent review

The following features are uncommon but require prompt assessment by your GP.

Symptom Possible concern Action
Fever, hot and swollen joint, severe pain at rest Septic arthritis (joint infection) Go to Emergency Department immediately
Shoulder pain after significant trauma with deformity or inability to move arm Fracture or dislocation Go to Emergency Department
Unintentional weight loss, night pain unresponsive to all analgesia, history of cancer Malignancy (primary or secondary) See GP urgently
Shoulder pain with drooping eyelid, small pupil, or arm weakness and numbness Pancoast tumour (lung apex) See GP urgently
Rapid progression of weakness without significant pain (especially in an older person) Massive cuff tear or neurological cause See GP for early assessment
Widespread joint pain, rash, fatigue, or morning stiffness affecting multiple joints Inflammatory arthritis (e.g. rheumatoid) See GP for assessment and blood tests

⚠ Go to ED or call 111 if you have:

  • A hot, red, severely swollen shoulder with fever, especially if you cannot move it at all
  • A fall or injury with obvious deformity of the shoulder or upper arm
  • Complete sudden inability to lift the arm after a pop or snapping sensation following injury

A hot, swollen joint with fever may be septic arthritis, which can permanently damage the joint within hours. This is a medical emergency.
