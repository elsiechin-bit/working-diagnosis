What is bedwetting and why does it happen? ›

Nocturnal enuresis is involuntary urination during sleep in a child aged 5 or older. It is common, not caused by behavioural problems or laziness, and is not the child's fault.

Three biological factors converge: excess nocturnal urine production (delayed or blunted ADH rise), reduced functional bladder capacity, and impaired arousal from sleep in response to bladder signals. When all three coincide, the bladder empties before waking occurs.

There is a strong genetic component: one affected parent confers ~44% risk; two affected parents ~77%. Most children achieve dryness naturally — approximately 15% per year without treatment. By age 10, prevalence is around 5%; by adolescence, under 2%.

Primary versus secondary bedwetting ›

Primary nocturnal enuresis — never reliably dry at night for more than 6 consecutive months. The most common form; reflects the developmental factors above. Rarely attributable to medical or psychological causes.

Secondary nocturnal enuresis — recurrence after at least 6 months of dryness. Warrants GP assessment to identify triggers: urinary tract infection, new-onset diabetes mellitus or insipidus, constipation, obstructive sleep apnoea, or significant psychosocial stressors. Secondary enuresis is less common but has a higher rate of identifiable causes.

What families can do at home ›

Before turning to specialist treatments, there are several practical steps that form the foundation of bedwetting management. These are appropriate for all children and should be maintained alongside any other treatment used.

Fluid intake during the day: encourage your child to drink 6 to 8 drinks (mostly water) spread through the day. Children who drink too little can develop a bladder that is not used to holding a normal volume, which worsens bedwetting. Do reduce fluids in the last 1 to 2 hours before bed, but do not restrict fluids overall during the day.

Before-bed routine: encourage your child to use the toilet twice before sleep, about 30 minutes apart, to fully empty the bladder. Avoid caffeinated drinks (fizzy drinks, energy drinks) in the afternoon and evening as these increase urine production.

Bowel health: constipation significantly worsens bedwetting and must be addressed first. Aim for soft, regular stools. Increase fruit, vegetables, and water. If constipation is present, treating it is the most important first step and often alone leads to improvement in bedwetting.

Reward charts: for children aged 5 to about 8, a star chart for agreed targets (such as using the toilet before bed, or drinking well during the day) can be motivating. Focus the chart on things the child can control, not wet versus dry nights. Praise any effort and progress. Do not punish wet nights or express disappointment. Bedwetting causes enough distress already and needs warmth and reassurance from parents.

Practical management: a waterproof mattress protector keeps the mattress dry. Having a spare set of bedding and pyjamas ready reduces the disruption of a wet night. Involve older children appropriately in changing sheets as a matter-of-fact routine, not as punishment. Pull-up pants at night are reasonable to reduce distress and washing, though they do not treat bedwetting. Some families phase them out once other treatment is underway.

Lifting: waking your child to use the toilet before you go to bed is commonly done but does not treat the underlying problem and has not been shown to improve long-term outcomes. It can also disrupt the child's sleep cycle. It is a reasonable short-term coping strategy but is not a treatment.

Bedwetting alarms - the most effective long-term treatment ›

The bedwetting alarm (enuresis alarm) is the most effective long-term treatment for nocturnal enuresis and is the first-line recommendation in international guidelines. When used consistently, about 65 to 70 percent of children achieve dryness. It also has the lowest relapse rate of any treatment.

How it works: a moisture sensor is worn in the underwear or placed on a bedside mat. When urine begins to flow, a loud alarm sounds or vibrates. Over weeks and months, the brain gradually learns to respond to the sensation of a full bladder and either wakes the child before voiding or suppresses the urge during sleep. It is a process of conditioning rather than a quick fix.

What makes it succeed: the child should be at least 7 years old and motivated to become dry. Younger children often do not have the arousal awareness to benefit fully. The child (not just the parents) should want to try the alarm. A parent will usually need to help wake the child in the early weeks, especially if the child sleeps deeply. The alarm needs to be used every single night. Progress should be tracked.

What to expect: initial weeks often bring little change. Many children sleep through the alarm at first. Gradually, the child begins to wake at the alarm, then before it, and eventually wakes spontaneously or stays dry. Full dryness is usually achieved after 8 to 16 weeks. If there is no progress at all after 4 weeks of consistent use, a GP review is worthwhile.

Relapse: about 1 in 6 children who become dry with the alarm relapse within 6 months. If this occurs, the alarm can be restarted successfully and relapse rates on the second course are lower.

Types: wearable alarms (body-worn, clip to underwear) and bedside mat alarms are both available. Wearable alarms tend to be more effective because the signal is closer to the child. Both types are available in NZ through pharmacies and medical suppliers such as Continence NZ. Some DHBs and community health services have loan alarm programmes.

When medications are used ›

Medications are not a cure for bedwetting but can be useful in specific situations. They are generally considered after behavioural and alarm approaches, or alongside them.

Desmopressin (DDAVP, Minirin): desmopressin is a synthetic version of vasopressin, the hormone that normally reduces urine production overnight. It is very effective at reducing or stopping bedwetting on the nights it is taken. It works from the first dose and is particularly useful for short-term situations such as sleepovers, school camps, or travel where managing bedwetting at home is not practical. Desmopressin is available as a tablet taken before bedtime. It is funded in NZ on prescription.

The most important safety instruction with desmopressin is fluid restriction: your child must not drink more than a small glass of water in the 8 hours after taking the tablet. Desmopressin reduces urine output; drinking large amounts of fluid while on the medication can cause the body to retain water and lower blood sodium (hyponatraemia), which in rare cases causes headache, confusion, or seizures. This is easily prevented by following the fluid restriction.

Desmopressin does not produce a lasting cure. When the medication is stopped, bedwetting typically returns. For this reason, it is primarily used situationally or as a bridge while awaiting the alarm to take effect. Some guidelines recommend combining desmopressin with the alarm for faster results in children with high urine output at night.

Other medications: oxybutynin (an anticholinergic) is occasionally used when there is a clear overactive bladder component alongside bedwetting, such as daytime urgency, frequency, or a very small functional bladder capacity. Imipramine (a tricyclic antidepressant) was previously used for bedwetting but is no longer recommended for routine use because of relapse rates and potential cardiac toxicity in overdose. These medications are only prescribed by a GP after assessment and are not first-line options.

⚠ See your GP if your child has any of the following

  • Bedwetting that started again after 6 or more months of being reliably dry at night (secondary enuresis)
  • Daytime wetting, urgency, or urinating very frequently
  • Pain, burning, or stinging when passing urine
  • Noticeably increased thirst alongside large volumes of pale urine (rule out diabetes)
  • Constipation, soiling, or infrequent bowel movements
  • Loud snoring or pauses in breathing during sleep
  • Significant emotional distress, school refusal, or behavioural change related to bedwetting
  • No improvement after 4 weeks of consistent bedwetting alarm use
  • A child who is 10 or older and has not responded to home measures
