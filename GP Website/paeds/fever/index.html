Fever in children

Fever is a protective immune response. Most childhood fevers are caused by harmless viral infections and resolve without treatment.

What is fever and why does it happen? ›

Fever is a core body temperature of 38°C or higher, measured rectally or with an ear or forehead thermometer. Fever is not an illness itself but rather a sign that your child's immune system is responding to an infection or, rarely, to another condition. When the body detects a threat like bacteria or viruses, it raises its temperature set point. This higher temperature helps activate immune cells and makes the environment less hospitable for many pathogens. Fever is one of the body's most effective defence mechanisms.

The key thing to understand is that fever itself is rarely dangerous. A temperature of 40°C or even 41°C will not cause brain damage or harm your child's organs. The body tightly controls temperature, and fevers from infection rarely exceed 40 to 41°C unless your child has heatstroke (which is rare and different from fever). Your child's appearance, behaviour, and responsiveness matter far more than the thermometer reading. A child with a temperature of 39.5°C who is alert, playing, and drinking is doing much better than a child with 38.5°C who is limp and difficult to rouse.

Most childhood fevers are caused by self-limiting viral infections like colds, flu, rotavirus, or hand-foot-and-mouth disease. These viruses run their course in a few days and get better on their own. Bacterial infections like strep throat or urinary tract infections can also cause fever but are less common. Serious infections like meningitis or sepsis are uncommon but require urgent assessment, which is why your GP or Healthline assessment is important if your child has warning signs.

How to take a temperature ›

Getting an accurate temperature reading is important for deciding whether your child has a fever and how to manage it. Different thermometers measure temperature from different sites, and the readings can vary.

Rectal thermometer (under 2 years): The most accurate method for babies and toddlers under 2 years old is a digital thermometer placed gently in the rectum. Use a fresh digital thermometer and apply a small amount of lubricant or petroleum jelly. Insert it only about 1 to 2 centimetres and wait for the beep. This is the gold standard because it measures core body temperature directly. A rectal temperature of 38°C or higher is fever.

Ear (tympanic) thermometer (over 6 months): Ear thermometers measure infrared radiation from the eardrum and are quick and fairly accurate. They work best in children over 6 months. Pull the ear back gently to straighten the ear canal, insert the probe tip gently, and press the button. A reading of 38°C or higher is fever. These can be less accurate if wax blocks the canal or the probe is not inserted correctly.

Forehead (temporal) thermometer (over 3 months): These non-contact infrared thermometers are convenient and non-invasive. Gently place the thermometer flat against the forehead and move it slowly toward the ear. They are reasonably accurate but can be affected by sweating or environmental temperature. Generally, a forehead reading of 38°C or higher suggests fever, though some sources recommend adding 0.5°C for comparison with rectal readings.

Axillary (underarm) thermometer: Axillary readings are the least accurate and are not recommended for fever assessment. A child's thermometer placed under the arm for 3 to 5 minutes can underestimate the true temperature by 0.5 to 1°C, so axillary readings are unreliable for decision-making.

Avoid mercury thermometers: Old mercury thermometers are inaccurate and dangerous if broken. Use digital thermometers instead. Strip thermometers (plastic strips placed on the forehead) are not reliable and should not be used.

Treating fever at home ›

The goal of treating fever is not to bring the temperature down to a specific number but rather to help your child feel more comfortable and to ensure they stay hydrated and eating. A comfortable fever response is part of fighting infection.

Keep your child cool: Dress your child lightly. Avoid excessive bundling or blankets even though they feel cold. Light clothing and a light sheet allow heat to escape. Keep the room at a comfortable temperature, not too hot.

Offer fluids frequently: This is one of the most important things you can do. Fever increases fluid losses through sweating and respiration. Offer water, breastmilk (for infants), water-based drinks, or dilute juice frequently in small amounts. Do not force drinking, but offer regularly throughout the day. Avoid excessive sugar. Watch for signs of dehydration: fewer wet nappies or no tears when crying.

Paracetamol (Calpol, Panadol): Paracetamol reduces fever and helps relieve discomfort. Dose by weight, not age, according to the package. For infants under 3 months, do not give paracetamol without GP advice. For older children, a typical dose is 15 milligrams per kilogram every 4 to 6 hours, not exceeding 5 doses in 24 hours. Do not exceed the maximum daily dose for your child's weight.

Ibuprofen (Nurofen): Ibuprofen is an alternative pain and fever reliever suitable for children over 3 months and weighing at least 5 kilogrammes. Dose is typically 10 milligrams per kilogramme every 6 to 8 hours. Do not use ibuprofen if your child is dehydrated, vomiting, or has a history of asthma, stomach problems, or kidney disease. Do not alternate paracetamol and ibuprofen routinely unless one alone is not adequately controlling discomfort.

Never give aspirin: Do not give aspirin to children under 16 years because of the risk of Reye syndrome, a rare but serious condition. Stick to paracetamol or ibuprofen.

Rest and support: Let your child rest as much as they want. Most children with fever feel tired and benefit from a quiet environment. Keep them home from school or childcare until fever has gone for at least 24 hours and they feel well enough to participate normally.

Feeding: Offer small, frequent meals and favourite foods. Do not force eating, but encourage small sips of broth, yoghurt, or soft food when appetite returns. Offer foods at a cool temperature.

Febrile seizures ›

Febrile seizures occur in approximately 2 to 4 percent of children aged 6 months to 5 years during a fever. They are distressing to witness but rarely dangerous and do not cause permanent brain damage. Understanding what to do can help you stay calm and keep your child safe.

What happens: A febrile seizure usually starts suddenly with the child's muscles stiffening or jerking rhythmically. The child may lose consciousness, their eyes may roll back, and they may be unresponsive during the seizure. Most febrile seizures last less than 5 minutes and are generalised (affecting both sides of the body). After the seizure ends, the child usually regains consciousness but may be sleepy or confused for a short time.

What to do: If your child has a seizure, stay calm and follow these steps. Lay them on their side in the recovery position to keep the airway open. Do not put anything in the mouth. Time how long the seizure lasts. Stay with your child and keep them safe from hard objects. If it is the first febrile seizure or if the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes, or if seizures continue in clusters, call 111. Even after a brief seizure that stops, seeking medical evaluation is sensible.

Recovery and reassurance: After the seizure, the child is usually very sleepy and may want to sleep for several hours. This is normal. Once recovered, examination and possibly blood tests will be done to identify the cause of fever. Febrile seizures do not cause epilepsy or permanent brain damage. Less than 1 percent of children who have a febrile seizure go on to develop epilepsy. The risk of another febrile seizure in future episodes of fever is about 30 to 40 percent, but repeat seizures do not worsen outcomes.

Prevention: Aggressive fever treatment does not reliably prevent febrile seizures, so the usual approach is to manage fever for comfort rather than to aggressively lower temperature. If your child has had a febrile seizure, your GP may discuss whether preventive medication is warranted in future febrile illnesses, though this is rarely necessary.

When to seek help by age

Fever alone doesn't always need assessment, but age and symptoms can change that.

Age Seek help if... Go to ED immediately if...
Under 3 months Any fever 38°C or higher. See GP or ED same day. Fever 38°C+ plus looks unwell, poor feeding, or bulging fontanelle
3-6 months Fever 39°C or higher or lasting more than 24 hours Laboured breathing, mottled skin, very difficult to wake
6 months to 5 years Fever lasting more than 5 days or source not identified Stiff neck, aversion to bright light, non-blanching rash, altered consciousness
Any age Deteriorating despite treatment, not drinking, or you are worried Any red flag symptom, or if you feel something is seriously wrong (trust your instincts)

⚠ Go to ED or call 111 immediately if your child has:

  • A non-blanching rash (press a clear glass on it; if spots do not fade, this is serious)
  • A stiff neck or persistent aversion to bright light
  • Difficulty breathing or stridor (noisy breathing)
  • Unusual limpness, floppy posture, or inability to wake
  • Altered consciousness or confusion
  • A seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or repeated seizures
  • Signs of severe dehydration: no wet nappy in 12 hours, sunken eyes, very dry lips
  • You are worried. Trust your parental instinct. If something feels seriously wrong, seek help.

You can also call Healthline on 0800 611 116 anytime (free, 24/7) to speak to a registered nurse about whether your child needs assessment.
