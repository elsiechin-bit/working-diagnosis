# Shingles — What You Need to Know

❍ *Active illness, pain, and prevention*

Shingles is something a lot of people have heard of but few expect to get themselves. It is caused by the varicella-zoster virus — the same virus that causes chickenpox. After a chickenpox infection, the virus goes dormant in nerve cells near the spinal cord and brain. Decades later, often prompted by a dip in immune function, it can reactivate as shingles.

About one in three people will develop shingles at some point in their lifetime.¹ Most cases are unpleasant but manageable. Some cause lasting nerve pain that takes months or years to settle. A small number affect the eye or ear and require urgent attention. The good news is that there is now an effective vaccine.

---

## What does shingles feel like?

Shingles usually begins with a few days of pain, burning, or sensitivity in a specific area of skin, often before any rash appears. That pre-rash phase is easy to mistake for a muscle strain, a dental problem, or something else entirely.

Then the rash arrives. It appears as a band or stripe on one side of the body — typically the chest, abdomen, or lower back — following the path of a single nerve (called a *dermatome*). Occasionally it affects the face or scalp. The rash starts as red patches, then blisters, then crusts over about seven to ten days. The blisters are contagious.

The pain can be surprisingly intense. Many people describe it as burning, stabbing, or electric. In some cases it is the most painful thing they have experienced.

---

## Do I need to see a GP quickly?

**Yes, and sooner is better.** Antiviral treatment is most effective when started within 72 hours of the rash appearing. If you suspect shingles, aim to be seen within that window. Do not wait to see whether it settles on its own.

There are situations that need urgent attention — same day or emergency:

**Shingles near the eye.** If the rash involves the tip of the nose, the forehead, or the area around the eye, the eye itself may be at risk. This is called ophthalmic shingles and requires urgent review, usually with an ophthalmologist. Vision can be permanently affected if it is not treated promptly.²

**Shingles affecting the ear.** A rash around the ear, particularly combined with facial weakness on the same side or a change in hearing, may indicate Ramsay Hunt syndrome, which involves the facial nerve. This also requires urgent assessment.²

**If you are immunocompromised.** People on chemotherapy, long-term steroids, immunosuppressants, or living with HIV need prompt assessment, as shingles can be more widespread and severe.

**If the rash crosses the midline.** Shingles that spreads to both sides of the body is uncommon and suggests more significant immune suppression — seek same-day review.

For typical shingles in an otherwise healthy adult, a same-day or next-day GP appointment is appropriate.

---

## Treatment

**Antiviral medication**

The main treatment is an antiviral — either valaciclovir (1000 mg three times daily for seven days) or aciclovir (800 mg five times daily for seven days).³ Valaciclovir is generally preferred because it is easier to take and achieves better drug levels in the body.

Antivirals are most effective when started within 72 hours of rash onset. Starting after 72 hours is still worthwhile in some situations — particularly if new blisters are still forming, if you are older, or if the rash affects the face — so it is worth being seen even if that window has passed.

**Pain management**

Pain in the acute phase can be significant, and treating it well matters. Options include paracetamol, ibuprofen (if appropriate for you), and in some cases short-term opioids or a nerve-calming medication such as gabapentin or amitriptyline. Your GP will tailor this to what is safe and appropriate for you.

**Keeping the rash clean and comfortable**

Loose, non-scratchy clothing and a cool damp cloth can help. Keep the rash area clean. Calamine lotion is sometimes soothing. Avoid bursting blisters.

---

## Can I pass it on?

You cannot give someone shingles directly. But a person who has not had chickenpox, or who has never been vaccinated against chickenpox, can catch chickenpox from contact with your shingles blisters. Chickenpox can be serious in pregnancy or in people with a weak immune system.

While your blisters are active and weeping, avoid close contact with:
- Pregnant people who have not had chickenpox or been vaccinated
- Newborns
- People with a weakened immune system (for example, people on chemotherapy or immunosuppressants)

Once the blisters have fully crusted over, you are no longer contagious. You can usually return to normal activities at that point.

## Post-herpetic neuralgia — when the pain lingers

The most common complication of shingles is *post-herpetic neuralgia* (PHN) — pain that persists in the area of the rash after the skin has healed. It occurs because the virus has damaged the nerve fibres, and those fibres take time (sometimes a long time) to recover, if they do at all.

PHN affects roughly 10 to 15 percent of people who get shingles, but the risk rises significantly with age.¹ In people aged 70 and over, the figure may be closer to one in three. Treatment is possible — medications such as gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline, duloxetine, and topical lidocaine or capsaicin patches can all reduce PHN pain — but it can be difficult to treat and may persist for months or years.

This is one of the most compelling reasons to get vaccinated before shingles ever strikes, and to treat it promptly if it does.

## The shingles vaccine (Shingrix)

### What is it?

Shingrix is a recombinant (non-live) shingles vaccine. It works by priming the immune system to respond strongly if the dormant varicella-zoster virus tries to reactivate. It is given as two injections, spaced two to six months apart, into the upper arm.

Unlike the older live vaccine (Zostavax), Shingrix does not contain weakened live virus — which means it is suitable for people with a weakened immune system as well as healthy adults.

### How effective is it?

Very. In adults aged 50 to 69, Shingrix reduces the risk of developing shingles by around 97 percent.⁴ In adults aged 70 and over, efficacy is approximately 91 percent.⁴ Protection against post-herpetic neuralgia is similarly high — around 89 to 91 percent.⁴

Importantly, protection appears to be durable. Studies following participants out to ten years have shown that efficacy remains high with no significant waning over that period, though long-term data is still accumulating.⁵

### Who should get it?

**Healthy adults aged 50 and over** are the primary target group. The risk of shingles and its complications increases with age, and Shingrix provides strong protection well into older age.

**Adults aged 18 and over who are immunocompromised** are recommended to receive Shingrix regardless of age, as they face a higher risk of developing shingles and of it being severe. This includes people on immunosuppressants for autoimmune conditions, people on chemotherapy or post-transplant medications, and people living with HIV.

**People who have had shingles before** can still benefit from Shingrix, as natural infection does not provide the same reliable protection the vaccine does against future episodes.

**People who have previously received Zostavax** (the older live vaccine) can and should receive Shingrix — it provides substantially better protection.

### Funded status in New Zealand

As of 2026, Shingrix is funded in New Zealand for people aged 65 and over, and for immunocompromised adults aged 18 and over.⁶ For adults aged 50 to 64 who are not immunocompromised, the vaccine is available but not currently funded, meaning it involves an out-of-pocket cost. Funding schedules are reviewed periodically — your GP or pharmacist can give you the current picture.

If you are in a funded group, the vaccine is available through your GP or a Shingrix-stocking pharmacy at no charge.

### Side effects

Side effects from Shingrix are common and are generally a sign that the immune system is responding. Most people experience some degree of soreness, redness, or swelling at the injection site. Systemic effects such as fatigue, muscle aches, headache, shivering, or fever affect roughly half of people after each dose and typically settle within two to three days.⁴

These reactions can be more noticeable than those from many other adult vaccines, and it is worth knowing this in advance so they are not alarming. Serious allergic reactions are rare.

### Can I have it alongside other vaccines?

Yes. Shingrix can be given at the same visit as other vaccines such as influenza or the COVID-19 booster. If you are coming in for your annual flu jab, it is worth asking whether Shingrix is due at the same time.

---

## A note on previous chickenpox

You may be unsure whether you have had chickenpox. Most New Zealanders aged over 40 are likely to have had it at some point, even if they do not remember. A blood test can confirm immunity if there is genuine doubt, though for practical purposes most adults in this age group are assumed to have had prior exposure and are therefore eligible for the shingles vaccine.
