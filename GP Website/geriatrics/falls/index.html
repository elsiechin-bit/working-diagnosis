Falls Prevention

Falls are not inevitable. Most stem from modifiable risk factors. Targeted exercise, home changes, and medication review can significantly reduce your risk.

Why falls happen as we age ›

Falls result from the interaction of age-related physical changes and environmental hazards.

Intrinsic factors include muscle weakness (sarcopenia), impaired vestibular balance, slower reflexes, orthostatic hypotension, reduced visual acuity, painful or deformed feet, cognitive impairment, and polypharmacy.

Extrinsic factors include loose rugs, poor lighting, slippery floors, absent grab rails, clutter, unsecured cords, and ill-fitting footwear. Most falls in older adults occur at home in familiar environments — familiarity encourages inattention.

Medicines and falls ›

Medications are among the most modifiable fall risk factors. High-risk classes include:

  • Sedatives and hypnotics (benzodiazepines, zopiclone) — impair balance, coordination, and reaction time
  • Antihypertensives — can cause orthostatic hypotension, especially in combination or at high doses
  • Antidepressants (TCAs, SSRIs) — TCAs have anticholinergic effects; SSRIs can cause hyponatraemia
  • Antipsychotics — impair balance, increase orthostatic hypotension
  • Opioids — sedation and confusion
  • Diuretics — volume depletion and electrolyte disturbance

A medication review by your GP is one of the most high-yield interventions available. Reducing or substituting high-risk medicines is often possible.

Strength, balance and exercise ›

Exercise is the single most effective intervention for falls prevention. Walking alone is insufficient — what works is targeted balance training and progressive resistance exercise.

The evidence is robust: structured programmes reduce falls significantly, including in frail older adults. Exercise should be progressive, ideally supervised initially, and tailored to baseline fitness. Fear of exercise causing injury is largely unfounded; inactivity worsens the muscle and balance deficits that cause falls.

Ask your GP about an appropriate programme. Physiotherapy referral or a community falls prevention class is often the best starting point.

Making your home safer ›

Most falls in older adults occur at home. Simple modifications significantly reduce hazard.

Key changes: remove or secure loose rugs; improve lighting in hallways, stairs, and bathrooms; install grab rails at the toilet, shower, and bath; use a non-slip bath mat; ensure stairs have secure handrails on both sides; tape down or remove extension cords.

Footwear matters: wear supportive, well-fitting shoes with non-slip soles indoors. Loose slippers without grip are a common fall cause, including during night-time trips to the toilet.

If you live alone, consider a personal alarm or medical alert device. A home assessment by an occupational therapist can identify hazards you may have stopped noticing.

After a fall ›

If you fall, prioritise safety over speed. If uninjured and able, roll to your side, push up to hands and knees, move to a sturdy chair, and rise slowly. Rest for a few minutes before standing fully to allow blood pressure to stabilise.

If you cannot get up, keep warm, call for help, and move as much as possible to reduce the risk of pressure injury. This is why a personal alarm is valuable if you live alone.

After any fall, see your GP. Even if no injury has occurred, a fall is a signal to review medications, blood pressure, vision, footwear, and home safety. An unwitnessed fall with any loss of consciousness warrants same-day assessment to exclude cardiac or neurological causes.

When to seek medical attention

Contact your GP or go to hospital after any fall, or immediately if you have:

  • Any head injury, or if you take blood thinners, even without obvious injury
  • Hip pain, groin pain, or inability to weight-bear on one leg (possible fracture)
  • Severe pain anywhere
  • New onset of falls in someone who has not previously fallen (warrants assessment for underlying illness)
  • Inability to get up from the floor
