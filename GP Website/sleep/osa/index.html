What is obstructive sleep apnoea? ›

In OSA, the pharyngeal muscles relax excessively during sleep, causing the airway to narrow or close. Breathing pauses for at least 10 seconds; the brain detects falling oxygen and briefly arouses the sleeper to restore airway tone — often with a gasp — before sleep resumes. This cycle can repeat dozens or hundreds of times per night without recall.

Severity is classified by the Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI): mild 5–14/hr, moderate 15–29/hr, severe ≥30/hr. OSA is distinct from simple snoring; it is the repeated oxygen desaturation and sleep fragmentation that drive the associated consequences — hypertension, atrial fibrillation, metabolic syndrome, and elevated cardiovascular risk.

Symptoms and risk factors ›

Characteristic symptoms:

  • Loud snoring, often irregular and punctuated by silence
  • Witnessed apnoeas — bed partner observes breathing stop, sometimes followed by gasping
  • Unrefreshed sleep despite adequate duration
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness — falling asleep in passive situations, during meetings, or while driving
  • Morning headaches (from hypercapnia during apnoeas)
  • Cognitive impairment — poor concentration, memory difficulties, mental fog
  • Nocturia and mood disturbance

Some people with significant OSA report reasonable sleep — typically those with longstanding disease who have lost the reference point of restorative sleep.

Risk factors: male sex, obesity (especially central), increasing age, large neck circumference (>40 cm in women, >43 cm in men), retrognathia, nasal obstruction, alcohol use, and sedative medications. OSA is also more prevalent in hypothyroidism and acromegaly.

How is OSA diagnosed? ›

Diagnosis of OSA is based on a combination of clinical assessment and a sleep study. If your GP suspects OSA based on your symptoms and examination, they will usually arrange or refer for sleep testing.

Clinical assessment: your GP will ask about your sleep symptoms, daytime sleepiness (sometimes using a standardised questionnaire called the Epworth Sleepiness Scale), and review risk factors including weight, neck circumference, and alcohol use. They will examine your throat, noting tonsil size and airway width. Screening questions about witnessed apnoeas are important - asking a bed partner to comment on your breathing during sleep is often more informative than anything you can report yourself.

Home sleep testing (Level 3 or Level 4 study): for most adults with typical symptoms and no major comorbidities, a home-based sleep test is appropriate and is the most common first-line investigation in New Zealand. You wear a small device overnight that records breathing effort, airflow, oxygen levels, and heart rate. The results are interpreted by a sleep physician or respiratory specialist. This type of test is convenient and usually well-tolerated.

In-laboratory polysomnography (Level 1 study): a full in-laboratory sleep study records additional parameters including brainwave activity (EEG), limb movements, and muscle activity. It is recommended when home testing is inconclusive, when another sleep disorder (such as restless legs syndrome, narcolepsy, or parasomnias) is also suspected, or when OSA is being assessed in the context of significant heart or lung disease.

What happens after the sleep study: results are usually reported back to your GP within a few weeks. If OSA is confirmed and meets criteria for treatment, you will typically be referred to a respiratory physician or sleep service for discussion of treatment options. In New Zealand, CPAP machines are subsidised through ACC for some patients or available through District Health Board sleep services depending on severity and eligibility criteria.

It is worth noting that pulse oximetry alone (wearing a fingertip oxygen monitor overnight) is not sufficient to diagnose OSA, as it does not measure breathing effort or airflow. A dedicated sleep study is necessary for accurate diagnosis.

Lifestyle changes that help ›

Lifestyle changes alone are unlikely to fully resolve moderate or severe OSA, but they can meaningfully reduce the number and severity of events and improve the effectiveness of other treatments. For people with mild OSA or who are borderline for treatment, lifestyle measures can sometimes be sufficient.

Weight loss: the most impactful lifestyle change for most people with OSA. A 10% reduction in body weight has been associated with an approximately 26% decrease in AHI in people who are overweight or obese. Weight loss can be achieved through dietary changes, increased physical activity, or - increasingly - through GLP-1 receptor agonist medications (such as semaglutide) where appropriate. Even modest weight loss improves OSA severity and daytime symptoms.

Avoid alcohol and sedatives, especially in the evening: alcohol relaxes airway muscles and worsens OSA significantly. Avoiding alcohol for at least three hours before sleep reduces its impact on airway tone. Benzodiazepines and sedating antihistamines have a similar effect and should be avoided where possible. If you are taking a prescribed sedative for another reason, discuss this with your GP.

Positional therapy: OSA is often worse when sleeping on your back (supine position) because gravity causes the tongue and soft tissues to fall backwards into the airway. Sleeping on your side reduces this effect. About 50 to 60 percent of people with OSA have positional OSA, where the AHI is at least twice as high in the supine position as in lateral (side) positions. Simple strategies include sewing a tennis ball into the back of a sleep shirt, or using a positional pillow or device to encourage side sleeping.

Treat nasal obstruction: if chronic nasal congestion is contributing to mouth breathing and worsening OSA, treating the underlying cause (allergic rhinitis with nasal corticosteroid sprays, or referral for structural issues such as a deviated septum) can reduce OSA severity and also improve CPAP comfort and compliance if CPAP is required.

Sleep hygiene: irregular sleep schedules and sleep deprivation can worsen OSA severity. Maintaining a consistent sleep and wake time, and aiming for adequate sleep duration (seven to nine hours for adults), supports better sleep quality overall. Avoid caffeine after midday if sleep onset is a problem.

Treatments for OSA ›

Several effective treatments exist for OSA. The choice depends on OSA severity, anatomy, personal preference, and tolerance. For moderate to severe OSA, treatment is important not just for symptoms but to reduce long-term cardiovascular risk.

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP): CPAP is the most effective treatment for OSA and the first-line recommendation for moderate to severe disease. A machine delivers a continuous stream of pressurised air through a mask worn over the nose (or nose and mouth) during sleep. The air pressure acts as a pneumatic splint, holding the airway open and preventing collapse. Most people notice a dramatic improvement in energy and daytime alertness within the first few nights of effective CPAP use. The main challenge is adjustment to wearing a mask - this typically improves over two to four weeks with support from a CPAP provider. Modern CPAP machines are quiet and compact. In New Zealand, CPAP is available through DHB respiratory services; some patients may qualify for subsidised equipment depending on severity and eligibility.

Mandibular Advancement Device (MAD): a MAD is a custom-fitted oral appliance, made by a dentist with expertise in sleep dentistry, that holds the jaw slightly forward during sleep. This widens the airway at the level of the tongue and soft palate. MADs are less effective than CPAP for severe OSA but are better tolerated by some people. They are a reasonable first-line option for mild to moderate OSA and for people who cannot tolerate CPAP. Side effects can include jaw soreness, tooth tenderness, and changes to bite alignment - these are usually manageable and reversible.

Positional therapy devices: for people with clearly positional OSA (events predominantly in the supine position), a wearable positional device that vibrates gently when the wearer rolls onto their back can be an effective and simple intervention. Evidence supports its use in positional OSA, though it is not suitable for severe non-positional disease.

Surgery: surgical options are generally reserved for people who have failed or cannot tolerate CPAP and MAD, or who have a specific correctable anatomical problem. Options include tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy (most effective when large tonsils are the primary obstruction), uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP - surgical trimming of soft palate tissue), maxillomandibular advancement (jaw surgery - most effective for OSA overall but a significant operation), and hypoglossal nerve stimulation (an implanted device that stimulates the tongue nerve to maintain muscle tone during sleep - effective but not widely available in New Zealand). Surgery is usually managed through specialist ENT or maxillofacial services after discussion of realistic outcomes.

Treating contributing conditions: where OSA is associated with hypothyroidism, nasal polyps, or enlarged tonsils, treating the underlying condition should be the first step and may resolve or substantially improve OSA without further intervention.

⚠ See your GP urgently or seek assessment promptly if:

  • A bed partner has witnessed you stopping breathing during sleep - this is the single most important symptom of OSA
  • You have fallen asleep while driving, or feel you are a risk behind the wheel due to sleepiness - in New Zealand you have a legal obligation to inform the NZTA if a medical condition (including untreated severe OSA) affects your driving fitness
  • You wake repeatedly gasping or choking during the night
  • You have uncontrolled high blood pressure, particularly if it is resistant to medication - OSA is a common reversible cause
  • You have known heart failure, atrial fibrillation, or have had a stroke - OSA worsens all of these conditions and treatment should be prioritised
  • You work in a safety-critical occupation (aviation, commercial driving, heavy machinery) - fitness to work requirements may apply and your GP can advise
  • You have severe daytime sleepiness significantly affecting work, relationships, or daily function
  • Symptoms appear suddenly or rapidly worsen - consider thyroid disease, weight gain, or a medication change as a contributing cause
