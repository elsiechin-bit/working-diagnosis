What is tinnitus? ›

Tinnitus is the perception of a sound that has no external source. People describe it as ringing, buzzing, hissing, roaring, whooshing, clicking, or a high-pitched tone. It can be constant or intermittent, affect one ear or both, and vary in loudness from barely noticeable to persistently intrusive. The sound exists only in your auditory system, not in the environment around you.

Tinnitus itself is a symptom, not a disease. Almost everyone has experienced brief tinnitus - the ringing that follows exposure to loud music or a sudden noise, for example. This transient form is harmless and fades within minutes. Persistent tinnitus, lasting more than a few months, is the form that leads people to seek advice from a GP.

About 15 percent of adults experience tinnitus to some degree, making it one of the most common complaints seen in general practice and audiology. Of those, roughly 1 in 5 find it moderately or severely distressing at some point, particularly in the early months after it first appears. However, the majority of people with tinnitus reach a point of habituation - the brain learns to filter the sound, much as it filters out background noise, and it becomes less prominent in conscious awareness. Understanding this is one of the most helpful things you can know about tinnitus.

Tinnitus is almost never a sign of anything life-threatening. However, certain patterns (particularly tinnitus that pulses with the heartbeat, or tinnitus in only one ear) do need to be assessed by a GP to rule out specific underlying causes.

What causes tinnitus? ›

Tinnitus arises from changes in how the auditory system processes sound signals. When the inner ear or the auditory nerve is damaged or altered, the brain can respond by generating its own signals to "fill the gap". This is the most common explanation for why tinnitus accompanies hearing loss. In most cases, no single identifiable cause is found - it is the result of accumulated changes in the hearing system over time.

The most common associations include:

  • Noise-induced hearing loss: prolonged or repeated exposure to loud noise (occupational noise, loud music, machinery) is the leading cause of hearing damage and tinnitus. The damage is usually permanent but tinnitus often stabilises over time.
  • Age-related hearing loss (presbycusis): gradual decline in hearing with age is normal. Tinnitus frequently accompanies this process and is very common in people over 60.
  • Earwax buildup: a blocked ear canal can cause temporary tinnitus. Clearing the wax often resolves it. This is one of the most easily treatable causes.
  • Middle ear conditions: fluid behind the eardrum (glue ear), otitis media, or eustachian tube dysfunction can all produce tinnitus, often alongside a sensation of fullness or muffled hearing.
  • Medications: a number of medicines can cause or worsen tinnitus as a side effect. These include high-dose aspirin and NSAIDs, loop diuretics (furosemide), aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin), quinine, and certain chemotherapy agents. If tinnitus started soon after a new medicine, tell your GP.
  • Head and neck injury: trauma to the head, neck, or jaw can trigger or worsen tinnitus by affecting nerves or blood vessels near the ear.
  • Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction: jaw joint problems are a recognised cause of tinnitus, often associated with clicking sounds or jaw pain.
  • Meniere's disease: episodes of vertigo, tinnitus (usually low-pitched or roaring), fullness in the ear, and fluctuating hearing loss occurring together suggest Meniere's disease, which needs specialist assessment.
  • Vascular causes (pulsatile tinnitus): when tinnitus has a rhythmic quality that matches the heartbeat, it suggests blood flow is being heard near the ear. This requires investigation to exclude high blood pressure, anaemia, abnormal blood vessels, or (rarely) a tumour near the ear.

In many people, especially those with long-standing tinnitus related to hearing loss, no treatable cause is found. This does not mean nothing can be done. Most management is focused on reducing the impact of tinnitus on daily life rather than eliminating the sound itself.

What does the type of sound suggest? ›

The character of tinnitus - its pitch, rhythm, and whether it is in one or both ears - can point towards different causes and helps your GP decide what assessment is needed. Most tinnitus is harmless, but some patterns deserve prompt investigation.

High-pitched ringing or hissing in both ears is the most common pattern and almost always reflects noise-induced or age-related hearing loss. It is rarely caused by anything serious.

Low-pitched roaring or rushing in one ear, particularly when it comes in episodes alongside vertigo and a feeling of fullness, raises the possibility of Meniere's disease. This warrants GP assessment and usually audiology referral.

Pulsatile tinnitus (a rhythmic sound that beats with your heartbeat, like a whooshing or thumping) is different from most tinnitus and always warrants GP assessment. It can reflect high blood pressure, increased blood flow (as in anaemia or pregnancy), or - rarely - a vascular abnormality or glomus tumour near the ear.

Tinnitus in one ear only (unilateral tinnitus), particularly if it has been present for some months or is associated with any hearing change, facial numbness, or balance problems, should be assessed by a GP. Acoustic neuroma (a benign tumour of the hearing nerve) is uncommon but presents this way. It is usually slow-growing and manageable if detected, but prompt assessment is sensible.

Clicking tinnitus is less common and often originates from the middle ear (muscle spasms around the eustachian tube) or the temporomandibular joint. It is rarely serious but benefits from GP review if persistent.

Managing tinnitus at home ›

There is no cure for most tinnitus, but there is strong evidence that how you respond to and think about tinnitus significantly affects how intrusive it becomes. The brain has a remarkable ability to reclassify background sounds as unimportant and stop attending to them - this process is called habituation. The strategies below support that process.

Avoid silence: tinnitus is loudest when there is no background sound, because the contrast between the tinnitus and silence is greatest. Using background sound during the day and at night reduces this contrast and helps the brain filter the tinnitus. Gentle music, a fan, rainfall sounds, or a white noise machine can all work well. The background sound should be at a low level - audible but not distracting.

Protect your hearing from further damage: further loud noise exposure worsens hearing loss and tinnitus. Use hearing protection (earplugs or earmuffs) in noisy environments - concerts, power tools, lawnmowing, shooting. Avoid listening through headphones at high volumes. This is one of the most important things you can do to prevent worsening.

Manage stress and sleep: tinnitus is almost always perceived as louder and more distressing when you are tired, stressed, or anxious. Good sleep hygiene matters: a consistent bedtime routine, a cool and quiet room, and a sound machine to prevent the silence that makes tinnitus more prominent. Stress management - whether through exercise, relaxation techniques, or mindfulness - can make a substantial difference to how tinnitus affects daily life.

Reduce caffeine and alcohol (if they are triggers): for some people, caffeine and alcohol worsen tinnitus. This varies individually. If you notice your tinnitus is louder after coffee or alcohol, reducing intake is worth trying.

Avoid focusing on the tinnitus: monitoring the tinnitus closely, measuring it throughout the day, and repeatedly testing whether it has changed all have the opposite effect to habituation. They keep the auditory cortex focused on the sound rather than de-prioritising it. This is difficult to do, but understanding the mechanism is genuinely helpful. Think of tinnitus like the hum of a refrigerator - it is always there, but you stop noticing it when your attention is elsewhere.

Stay active and engaged: keeping busy with work, hobbies, exercise, and social activities helps occupy attention away from tinnitus. Withdrawal and avoidance tend to make tinnitus more central to daily experience.

Treatments that can help ›

While there is currently no medication that reliably eliminates tinnitus, several evidence-based approaches can significantly reduce its impact.

Hearing aids: if tinnitus is accompanied by hearing loss (which it is in the great majority of cases), hearing aids are often the single most helpful intervention. By amplifying environmental sound, they enrich the acoustic environment, reduce the contrast with tinnitus, and restore auditory input that the brain is trying to compensate for. Many people find that wearing hearing aids makes tinnitus substantially less noticeable. A referral to an audiologist through your GP can arrange hearing assessment and fitting if appropriate.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT): CBT has the strongest evidence base for reducing the distress and impact of tinnitus, even though it does not reduce the perceived loudness of the sound itself. Tinnitus-focused CBT helps identify and change unhelpful thought patterns about tinnitus (catastrophising, excessive monitoring, fear of it worsening) and develops strategies for living well alongside it. It is typically delivered over 6 to 12 sessions. A GP referral to a psychologist or CBT therapist with experience in chronic health conditions is the usual pathway.

Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT): TRT combines structured counselling about the mechanisms of tinnitus with long-term sound enrichment, with the goal of accelerating habituation. It is delivered by trained audiologists and typically takes 12 to 24 months of follow-up. Evidence for TRT is positive but less robust than for CBT. It is available through some audiology services in NZ.

Sound therapy and sound maskers: wearing a device that generates a low-level broadband sound in the ear reduces the contrast with tinnitus and helps with habituation. This can be a standalone sound generator or built into a hearing aid. It is not the same as masking (trying to drown out the tinnitus with loud sound, which does not produce lasting benefit).

Treating underlying causes: if a specific cause is identified - earwax, ear infection, medication side effect, blood pressure - addressing that cause can reduce or resolve tinnitus. This is why a GP assessment to identify treatable contributing factors is a worthwhile first step.

Medications: there is no medication approved specifically for tinnitus. Some medications used to treat anxiety and depression (such as antidepressants) can help if significant psychological distress accompanies tinnitus, by improving mood and sleep rather than by directly affecting the sound. Your GP can discuss this if distress is a significant part of your experience.

Tinnitus types

Different tinnitus patterns suggest different causes and levels of urgency.

Type Description Common associations What to do
High-pitched ringing, both ears Constant or intermittent tone or hiss in both ears Noise-induced or age-related hearing loss GP or audiologist referral; not urgent unless sudden onset
Low-pitched roaring, one ear Fullness, fluctuating hearing, and episodes of vertigo alongside tinnitus Meniere's disease; middle ear fluid See GP - audiology referral usually needed
Pulsatile (beating with pulse) Whooshing or thumping that synchronises with heartbeat High BP, anaemia, vascular anomaly, glomus tumour See GP promptly - investigation needed
One-sided only (unilateral) Persistent ringing or tone in one ear only Acoustic neuroma, sudden sensorineural hearing loss, wax See GP - rule out acoustic neuroma if prolonged
Clicking Rhythmic or sporadic clicking sound TMJ dysfunction, middle ear muscle spasm GP review if persistent; TMJ or dental assessment
New tinnitus with sudden hearing loss Hearing loss and tinnitus that began over hours to days Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) Urgent GP same day - time-sensitive treatment available

⚠ See your GP promptly if you have any of the following

  • Tinnitus that pulses or beats in time with your heartbeat
  • Tinnitus in one ear only, especially if it has been present for more than a few weeks
  • Tinnitus that began alongside a sudden drop in hearing in one or both ears - this requires urgent same-day GP assessment as steroid treatment within days may help
  • Tinnitus with dizziness, vertigo, or loss of balance
  • Tinnitus following a head or neck injury
  • Facial weakness, numbness, or changes in vision alongside tinnitus
  • Tinnitus you believe may be caused by a new medication
  • Significant distress, anxiety, or depression related to tinnitus - effective support is available and you do not need to manage this alone

You can also call Healthline on 0800 611 116 any time (free, 24/7) to talk through whether your symptoms need assessment.
