## Dyspepsia

Dyspepsia (indigestion) is one of the most common presentations in primary care. The key is distinguishing functional dyspepsia (no structural pathology despite investigation) from organic dyspepsia (peptic ulcer, GORD, gastric cancer) and managing appropriately based on red flag features.

## Definition and epidemiology

Dyspepsia is postprandial fullness, early satiation, epigastric pain, or epigastric burning. It affects 20–40% of the adult population; most have functional dyspepsia (no organic pathology on investigation). Organic dyspepsia accounts for 10–30% and includes peptic ulcer disease, GORD, malignancy, and inflammatory conditions.

## Pathophysiology

Dyspepsia results from impaired gastric accommodation, reduced antral contractions, accelerated gastric emptying (paradoxically in some), or increased visceral sensitivity. Functional dyspepsia has no identified structural, chemical, or infectious basis. Organic dyspepsia arises from mucosal inflammation, ulceration, malignancy, or severe motility disturbance.

H. pylori infection increases dyspepsia prevalence and is causally linked to peptic ulcer and gastric cancer in endemic regions. Psychosocial factors (stress, anxiety, depression) amplify symptoms.

## Clinical presentation

Patients describe heartburn, burning, bloating, fullness after small meals, nausea, or vague upper abdominal discomfort. Onset is often postprandial (within 30 minutes of eating). Some experience nocturnal symptoms disrupting sleep. Duration ranges from weeks to years; intermittent or constant patterns vary.

Functional dyspepsia presents with postprandial distress (fullness, satiation) and/or epigastric pain/burning without alarm features. Symptoms fluctuate with stress, diet, and menstrual cycle in women.

## Alarm symptoms (red flags)

Age >60 (gastric cancer risk increases sharply), anaemia, weight loss >3 kg, persistent vomiting, dysphagia, haematemesis, melaena, family history of gastric cancer, or palpable mass warrant urgent OGD. These features indicate likely organic pathology and require exclusion of malignancy.

## Initial assessment

Take a focused history: symptom character, onset, duration, relationship to meals, alleviating/exacerbating factors, impact on quality of life. Ask about alarm symptoms, NSAID use (peptic ulcer risk), H. pylori risk factors (crowded housing, poor sanitation, endemic regions), smoking, and psychological stressors.

Examine for anaemia (pallor), weight loss, epigastric tenderness, hepatomegaly, or masses. Check vital signs for signs of acute pathology.

## Investigations

In younger patients (<60 years) without alarm features, clinical diagnosis suffices and investigation is not recommended routinely. If alarm symptoms present or age >60, refer for endoscopy to exclude malignancy and peptic ulcer.

H. pylori testing is indicated if dyspepsia is new or if considering long-term PPI therapy (to avoid unnecessary PPI use in uninfected individuals). Testing methods include breath test (non-invasive, post-proton pump inhibitor washout 2 weeks), serology (detects exposure but not active infection reliably), stool antigen (acceptable sensitivity), or biopsy (during endoscopy if performed).

Full blood count detects anaemia suggesting mucosal blood loss or systemic disease. Urea and electrolytes check renal function before PPI if indicated. LFTs are normal in uncomplicated dyspepsia but abnormality suggests hepatic or biliary pathology.

## Diagnosis

Functional dyspepsia is diagnosed by Rome IV criteria: postprandial distress (fullness, early satiation) and/or epigastric pain/burning for ≥3 months with symptom onset ≥6 months before diagnosis, in the absence of structural disease on investigation (normal endoscopy if performed) and without evidence of IBS.

Organic dyspepsia requires endoscopic, laboratory, or imaging evidence of disease (peptic ulcer, erosive oesophagitis, gastric cancer, etc.).

## Management algorithm

**Step 1: Lifestyle and dietary modification**
Advise smaller, more frequent meals; reduce fat, spicy foods, caffeine, alcohol, and fizzy drinks; avoid eating within 2–3 hours of bedtime. Encourage stress reduction, regular exercise, and weight loss if overweight. Address smoking and excessive NSAID use.

**Step 2: Antacids and alginates**
Calcium carbonate (500–1500 mg), magnesium carbonate, or aluminium hydroxide offer rapid relief. Alginates (sodium alginate 500 mg) form a raft on the gastric surface, protecting mucosa and reducing reflux. Use as-needed for symptom relief.

**Step 3: H₂ receptor antagonist or proton pump inhibitor**
If symptoms persist after lifestyle modification and antacids/alginates, offer a trial of ranitidine 150 mg BD or famotidine 20 mg BD for 2 weeks. Alternatively, start PPI (omeprazole 20 mg daily, lansoprazole 30 mg daily) for 4 weeks. Assess response; if inadequate, switch to the alternative agent or increase PPI dose.

**H. pylori testing and treatment**
If H. pylori is detected, eradication is indicated. First-line in New Zealand: omeprazole 20 mg BD + amoxycillin 1 g BD + clarithromycin 500 mg BD for 7 days (or lansoprazole equivalent). Confirm eradication with breath test or stool antigen ≥4 weeks after therapy completion.

**Long-term management**
Once dyspepsia responds to treatment, attempt step-down by reducing PPI dose or frequency, or switching to as-needed antacids. Avoid prolonged PPI use in non-ulcer dyspepsia without documented benefit. Reassess at 4 weeks; if symptoms recur at lower doses, resume previous effective dose.

## Referral and endoscopy

Refer for OGD if alarm symptoms present, age >60 with new-onset dyspepsia, symptoms persist despite adequate trial of PPI (4 weeks) and lifestyle modification, or recurrent symptoms requiring frequent medication. Endoscopy excludes peptic ulcer, malignancy, and severe inflammatory disease. If endoscopy is normal, diagnosis of functional dyspepsia is confirmed.

## Special populations

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
Dyspepsia is common; however, alarm symptoms are more likely to indicate serious pathology. Lower threshold for endoscopy. Monitor for PPI side effects (hypomagnesaemia, fracture risk, B12 deficiency, C. difficile).

**Pregnant women**
Dyspepsia is very common due to progesterone-induced gastric relaxation. First-line is lifestyle modification and antacids (calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide safe). H2 antagonists (ranitidine, famotidine) are safe; PPIs generally safe but reserve for severe symptoms.

**NSAID use**
Dyspepsia in NSAID users suggests peptic ulcer risk. Discontinue if possible; if continued, offer PPI co-prescription (omeprazole 20 mg daily) for gastroprotection.

## Complications of untreated dyspepsia

Chronic dyspepsia rarely causes structural complications in functional cases. Organic dyspepsia may progress: peptic ulcer can perforate (acute abdomen) or bleed (anaemia, haematemesis). Gastric cancer in endemic regions carries poor prognosis if diagnosed late; early endoscopy in alarm cases improves outcomes.

## Monitoring and follow-up

At 4 weeks, review symptom response to initial management. If improved, continue current therapy and consider step-down at 8 weeks. If unresponsive, switch to alternative PPI or increase dose, confirm H. pylori status, and consider endoscopy if not performed and alarm features present.

For H. pylori-treated patients, confirm eradication at ≥4 weeks post-treatment using breath test or stool antigen. Retest if symptoms persist after confirmed eradication.

## Field notes

Functional dyspepsia is a diagnosis of exclusion. Resist pressure to prescribe long-term PPI without documentation of benefit or alarm features. Most patients improve with lifestyle modification. H. pylori eradication in positive patients often resolves dyspepsia permanently. When in doubt about alarm features, endoscope—false reassurance delays cancer diagnosis. Psychosocial factors matter; cognitive behavioural therapy and stress management improve outcomes in refractory functional dyspepsia.
