## Constipation

Constipation is one of the most common primary care complaints, affecting 10–20% of the general population. The key is identifying alarm features warranting investigation versus functional constipation responding to simple interventions.

## Definition and epidemiology

Constipation is defined as <3 bowel movements per week, or straining, hard stools, sensation of incomplete evacuation, or manual manoeuvres required for defecation, persisting for ≥3 months. Prevalence is 10–20% in developed countries, increasing with age and female sex. Most cases are functional (no structural pathology).

## Classification and pathophysiology

Functional constipation is subclassified by Rome IV criteria into three main groups:

**Normal-transit constipation**
Stool transit time is normal; patients perceive themselves as constipated despite normal defecation frequency. Often reflects psychological factors, dietary habits, or symptom amplification.

**Slow-transit constipation**
Reduced colonic motility delays stool movement. Associated with reduced colonic peristalsis and abdominal distension. More common in women and older adults.

**Outlet obstruction (defecatory dysfunction)**
Pelvic floor dysfunction, straining without effective propulsion, or anatomical obstruction (stricture, mass). Anismus (paradoxical contraction of external anal sphincter during defecation) is a common mechanism in young women.

Secondary causes (medications, endocrine disorders, neurological disease) account for 5–15% of cases. Opioids, anticholinergics, calcium channel blockers, iron supplements, and diuretics frequently cause constipation. Hypothyroidism, hypercalcaemia, and Parkinson's disease contribute.

## Clinical presentation

Patients report infrequent stools, straining, hard or lumpy stools, sensation of incomplete evacuation, or need for manual disimpaction. Associated abdominal bloating, pain, or discomfort is common. Symptom duration varies from weeks to years; intermittent or continuous patterns differ.

Alarm symptoms include age >50 at symptom onset, unintentional weight loss, anaemia, blood in stool (visible or occult), family history of colorectal cancer, or change in bowel habit (previously regular, now constipated). These demand investigation.

## Assessment

Focused history: stool frequency, consistency, straining, incomplete evacuation, manual manoeuvres, abdominal pain, bloating, weight loss. Ask about medications (opioids, anticholinergics, iron, calcium, diuretics). Review dietary fibre and fluid intake, physical activity, and bowel habit changes.

Examine for abdominal distension, palpable loops, tenderness, or masses. Digital rectal examination assesses anal tone, faecal impaction, masses, or structural abnormalities. Assess for pelvic floor dysfunction (assess perineal descent, voluntary anal sphincter contraction).

## Investigations

In younger patients (<50 years) without alarm features, clinical diagnosis suffices. No routine investigation is needed.

In age >50 or with alarm symptoms (weight loss, anaemia, blood per rectum, family history of colorectal cancer, change in bowel habit), refer for colonoscopy to exclude malignancy, polyps, diverticular disease, or inflammatory bowel disease.

Full blood count detects anaemia (suggesting bleeding or systemic disease). Urea and electrolytes assess renal function and hypercalcaemia (secondary cause). Thyroid function (TSH) screens for hypothyroidism.

Rome IV diagnostic criteria: ≥2 of (1) straining, (2) lumpy/hard stools, (3) sensation of incomplete evacuation, (4) sensation of anorectal obstruction, (5) manual manoeuvres, or (6) <3 bowel movements/week for ≥3 months, with symptom onset ≥6 months prior.

Colonic transit study (radiopaque marker study) is reserved for slow-transit constipation suspected after initial management trial. Anorectal manometry and defecography assess pelvic floor dysfunction in refractory cases.

## Management algorithm

**Step 1: Lifestyle and dietary modification**
Increase dietary fibre to 25–35 g/day (soluble fibre: oats, beans; insoluble: whole grains, vegetables, fruits). Increase fluid intake to ≥2 L/day. Encourage regular physical activity (30 minutes, ≥5 days/week). Establish a regular toilet routine, allowing adequate time without rushing. Cease or reduce opioid analgesics if possible.

Review medications; cease iron supplements, calcium, diuretics, or anticholinergics if medically appropriate. For opioid-induced constipation, consider alternative analgesia or add laxative prophylaxis from initiation.

**Step 2: Osmotic laxatives**
If lifestyle modification is insufficient after 1–2 weeks, trial osmotic laxative. Polyethylene glycol (macrogol, e.g. GoLYTELY) 17 g daily in water is first-line (excellent safety profile, non-absorbed). Lactulose 15–30 mL daily is an alternative (causes bloating in some). Magnesium hydroxide 400–800 mg daily is acceptable. Trial duration is 2–4 weeks; assess response.

**Step 3: Stimulant laxatives**
If osmotic laxative is insufficient, add stimulant laxative (senna 15 mg at night, bisacodyl 5–10 mg at night). Use as-needed or regular dosing depending on response. Avoid long-term dependence; titrate down once motility improves.

**Step 4: Advanced therapies**
For slow-transit constipation refractory to Step 1–3, consider linaclotide (290 μg daily) or lubiprostone (24 μg BD); these enhance gut motility. Cost and availability limit use in primary care; specialist input is often required.

For outlet obstruction and pelvic floor dysfunction, refer for pelvic floor physiotherapy. Biofeedback training improves defecatory coordination. Botulinum toxin injection into the anal sphincter is reserved for severe anismus.

**Faecal impaction management**
Manual disimpaction under analgesia or sedation is required if impaction does not respond to aggressive laxative therapy. Enemas (sodium phosphate, mineral oil) soften and mobilise impacted stool. Continue high-dose osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol 2–4 L daily for 3–5 days post-disimpaction).

## Complications of constipation

**Faecal impaction**
Hard stool accumulates in the rectum and colon, causing pain, overflow diarrhoea, and faecal incontinence. Risk increases in older adults, sedentary individuals, and opioid users. Manual disimpaction is required if laxatives fail.

**Anal fissure and haemorrhoids**
Straining and hard stools cause anal mucosal tears (fissures) or haemorrhoidal swelling. Present with pain on defecation and visible blood per rectum. Most resolve with stool softening and topical treatments; surgical intervention is rarely needed.

**Megacolon**
Severe, chronic constipation causes gross colonic dilatation and atony. Risk is elevated in chronic laxative abuse and conditions impairing motility (Hirschsprung disease, Chagas disease in endemic regions). Presents with abdominal distension and pain; plain abdominal X-ray shows dilated colon. Management is intensive: cease stimulant laxatives, use osmotic agents, and consider anorectal manometry and specialist assessment.

## Monitoring and follow-up

At 2–4 weeks, assess response to initial therapy. If improved, continue current regimen and gradually reduce laxatives as motility improves. If unresponsive, check compliance and review for secondary causes (medications, hypothyroidism, hypercalcaemia). Consider specialist referral for further investigation (transit study, anorectal manometry) if constipation is severe or refractory.

For older adults and opioid users, anticipate constipation and prescribe laxatives prophylactically. Regular follow-up ensures optimal management and prevents impaction.

## Special populations

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
Constipation is common due to reduced motility, medications, and reduced mobility. Polypharmacy frequently includes constipating agents. Assess medication burden and deprescribe if possible. High risk of impaction; maintain fluid and fibre intake, and prescribe osmotic laxatives early if needed.

**Pregnancy**
Progesterone-induced reduced motility combines with iron supplements to cause constipation in up to 40% of pregnant women. First-line is dietary fibre (fruit, vegetables, whole grains) and hydration. Osmotic laxatives (lactulose, macrogol) are safe. Stimulant laxatives are generally safe but reserve for unresponsive cases.

**Opioid-induced constipation**
Nearly universal in chronic opioid users. Prophylactic osmotic laxative (macrogol 17 g daily) commences with opioid initiation. Stimulant laxatives may be needed. Naloxone-oxycodone combinations reduce opioid-induced constipation but cost limits use.

## Field notes

Functional constipation is a diagnosis of exclusion; ensure alarm features are absent before reassuring. Fibre supplementation alone often fails in severe constipation—osmotic laxatives are essential. Avoid long-term stimulant laxative use, which damages colonic motility; use only as adjunct to osmotic therapy. Pelvic floor dysfunction is under-recognised in young women with straining—consider physiotherapy referral early. Opioid-induced constipation is predictable; prophylaxis saves patients from impaction and hospital admission. Elderly and immobile patients are at high risk for serious complications; proactive laxative use is warranted.
