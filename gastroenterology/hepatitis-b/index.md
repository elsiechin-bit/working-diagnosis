## Hepatitis B

Hepatitis B is a major infectious cause of chronic liver disease worldwide, affecting 250 million people globally and 5% of the NZ population. Diagnosis requires serological testing; many cases are asymptomatic but infectious. Primary care has a crucial role in screening, vaccination, and identifying patients at risk of progression to cirrhosis.

## Definition and epidemiology

Hepatitis B is a DNA virus transmitted via blood, sexual contact, or vertical transmission (perinatal). HBsAg (surface antigen) indicates current infection (acute or chronic). Chronic hepatitis B is defined as HBsAg persistence >6 months. Prevalence is high in migrants from sub-Saharan Africa, Asia-Pacific, and Eastern Europe (5–15%), healthcare workers (0.5%), and IV drug users (5–10%). In NZ, Māori and Pacific Islander populations have higher seroprevalence. Transmission to non-immune individuals approaches 100% if HBeAg+ and 30% if HBeAg−.

## Pathophysiology

HBV replicates in hepatocytes and is released into blood as virions containing HBsAg. Host immune response drives hepatic inflammation. In acute hepatitis B, resolution occurs in >95% of immunocompetent adults; progression to chronicity is rare. In infants and young children, progression to chronic infection approaches 90%. In chronic infection, viral replication (HBV DNA >10^5 IU/mL) drives ongoing hepatic inflammation, fibrosis, and eventual cirrhosis in 20–30% over 10–20 years. Hepatocellular carcinoma develops in cirrhotic livers at 5% annually.

## Clinical presentation

**Acute hepatitis B**
Fever, malaise, myalgia, jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, abdominal discomfort. Most recover fully; fulminant hepatitis (ALT >3000 IU/L, coagulopathy, encephalopathy) occurs in <1% but is life-threatening.

**Chronic hepatitis B**
Often asymptomatic until cirrhosis develops. Some report fatigue, malaise, or right upper quadrant discomfort. Asymptomatic carriers may have incidental HBsAg+ result on routine screening.

**Cirrhosis**
Ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome, hepatocellular carcinoma. Signs include spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, splenomegaly.

## Diagnosis

**Serology**
HBsAg (surface antigen) indicates current infection (acute or chronic). Anti-HBc (core antibody) confirms exposure; present in acute, chronic, and resolved infection. HBeAg (e antigen) indicates active viral replication. Anti-HBe (e antibody) indicates lower viral load. Anti-HBs (surface antibody) indicates immunity (vaccination or recovery).

Interpretation:
- HBsAg+ with anti-HBc+ and HBeAg+ = acute or chronic hepatitis B with high viral load
- HBsAg+ with anti-HBc+ and HBeAg− = chronic hepatitis B with lower viral load
- HBsAg− with anti-HBc+ and anti-HBs+ = resolved infection (immune)
- HBsAg− with anti-HBs+ only = vaccination-induced immunity (no prior exposure)

**Viral load**
HBV DNA quantification by PCR quantifies viral replication. Threshold >10^5 IU/mL indicates high replication and antiviral therapy indication.

**Hepatitis D co-infection**
Anti-HDV testing if HBsAg+. Hepatitis D (delta) only occurs with HBV; HDV RNA indicates active co-infection with accelerated progression to cirrhosis.

## Assessment of disease severity

**Synthetic function**
INR, albumin, and platelet count assess hepatic synthetic function and portal hypertension. Elevated INR or low albumin suggests cirrhosis.

**Fibrosis staging**
FibroScan (transient elastography) measures liver stiffness and predicts fibrosis stage non-invasively. <5.8 kPa suggests F0-F1; 5.8–9.2 kPa F2; >9.2 kPa F3-F4. FIB-4 index aids non-invasive assessment: (Age × AST) / (Platelet count × √ALT). Score <1.3 (age ≤65) suggests low fibrosis risk.

**Hepatocellular carcinoma risk**
APRI score: (AST / ULN) / Platelet count × 100. Score >2 suggests advanced fibrosis.

## Management

**Vaccination of contacts**
All non-immune household and sexual contacts require HBV vaccination (0, 1, 6 months schedule). Check anti-HBs at 1 month post-vaccination; non-responders may need higher-dose vaccine.

**Antiviral therapy**
Indicated for HBsAg+ patients with: HBV DNA >10^5 IU/mL and HBeAg+, or HBV DNA 10^4–10^5 IU/mL with ALT >2× ULN, or evidence of cirrhosis (any HBV DNA level). First-line antivirals: tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 300 mg daily or entecavir (ETV) 0.5 mg daily. Both achieve HBV DNA undetectability in >95% and prevent cirrhosis progression. Specialist initiation is standard.

**Monitoring without antiviral therapy**
HBsAg+ with low viral load (HBV DNA <10^4 IU/mL) and normal ALT: 6-monthly LFTs, HBV DNA, and 12-monthly FibroScan. If ALT rises above ULN or FibroScan increases, refer for antiviral assessment.

**Lifestyle advice**
Avoid alcohol (accelerates fibrosis). Safe sex; contact tracing. Pregnant women: check HBV DNA; if high, antiviral therapy reduces perinatal transmission.

## Monitoring

For HBsAg+ on antiviral therapy: 3-monthly HBV DNA and LFTs initially, then 6-monthly when stable. Continue indefinitely; therapy is long-term. HBe seroconversion (HBeAg− to anti-HBe+) indicates response but virological response (HBV DNA undetectable) is the goal.

For HBsAg+ not on therapy: 6-monthly HBV DNA, ALT, and LFTs. Annual FibroScan or AST/platelet assessment. Escalate to antiviral therapy if disease activity increases.

For patients with cirrhosis: specialist surveillance for hepatocellular carcinoma (AFP and ultrasound ± CT 6-monthly). Endoscopy screening for varices if evidence of portal hypertension.

## Complications

**Cirrhosis**
Decompensation with ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, or hepatorenal syndrome. Requires specialist management.

**Hepatocellular carcinoma**
Develops in 5% of cirrhotic patients annually. Screening with AFP and imaging is standard once cirrhosis diagnosed.

**Acute liver failure**
Fulminant hepatitis with coagulopathy and encephalopathy. Rare but life-threatening; requires urgent admission and transplant assessment.

## Special populations

**Healthcare workers**
All HBV-non-immune healthcare workers require vaccination. Post-vaccination anti-HBs ≥10 mIU/mL confirms protective immunity. Annual exposure risk assessment; if exposure occurs, immediate post-exposure prophylaxis (HBV immunoglobulin + vaccine booster).

**Pregnancy**
HBsAg+ pregnant women: check HBV DNA. If >10^6 IU/mL, antiviral therapy (tenofovir or lamivudine) reduces perinatal transmission. Infant receives HBV vaccine and immunoglobulin within 12 hours of birth.

**Migrants from endemic regions**
Screen on arrival with HBsAg and anti-HBc. Vaccinate non-immune individuals. Counsel on transmission routes and contact tracing.

**HIV co-infection**
HBV/HIV co-infected patients require tenofovir-based antiretroviral therapy (dual therapy for both HBV and HIV). Liaise with HIV specialist.

## Field notes

Screen opportunistically for HBsAg in migrants, healthcare workers, and high-risk groups; seroprevalence is substantially higher than in NZ-born populations. Many HBsAg+ individuals are asymptomatic but infectious; mandatory contact tracing and vaccination of partners and household members is essential. HBV DNA quantification and HBeAg status determine treatment urgency; specialist assessment is standard for HBsAg+ patients. Antiviral therapy is highly effective; most patients achieve viral suppression and prevent cirrhosis progression on tenofovir or entecavir. Monitor for hepatocellular carcinoma in cirrhotic patients. Acute hepatitis B is rare in vaccinated populations; fulminant hepatitis requires immediate admission and transplant assessment.
