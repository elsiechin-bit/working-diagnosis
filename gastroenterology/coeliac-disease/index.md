## Coeliac Disease

Coeliac disease is an autoimmune disorder triggered by dietary gluten in genetically susceptible individuals. Despite affecting ~1% of developed populations, most cases remain undiagnosed. Primary care plays a crucial role in early recognition and appropriate investigation.

## Definition and epidemiology

Coeliac disease is a permanent autoimmune enteropathy triggered by dietary gluten in individuals carrying HLA-DQ2 (95%) or HLA-DQ8 (5%) alleles. Prevalence is ~1% in developed countries; however, 75% of cases remain undiagnosed. Secondary prevalence is high in first-degree relatives (10–15%), patients with type 1 diabetes (3–5%), and those with autoimmune thyroiditis (2–5%).

## Pathophysiology

Gluten (wheat, barley, rye) contains immunogenic peptides that are deamidated by tissue transglutaminase (tTG), an enzyme abundant in intestinal epithelium. Deamidated peptides bind HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 molecules on antigen-presenting cells, activating CD4+ T cells. This drives a chronic adaptive immune response with villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes.

Resulting small intestinal mucosal damage impairs nutrient absorption, causing malabsorption and nutritional deficiency. Disease is triggered only in genetically predisposed individuals; HLA-DQ2/DQ8 is necessary but insufficient (present in 30–40% of population but only 1% develop coeliac disease).

## Clinical presentation

Presentation varies from classic to atypical to asymptomatic forms.

**Classic presentation**
Diarrhoea, steatorrhoea, abdominal bloating, pain, nausea, weight loss, and failure to thrive (in children) appear months after gluten introduction in infancy or early childhood. More common in younger children.

**Atypical presentation**
Iron deficiency anaemia, fatigue, osteoporosis, neurological symptoms (peripheral neuropathy, ataxia), or dermatological manifestations (dermatitis herpetiformis, alopecia areata) may be the sole presentation. Anaemia is the most frequent non-GI presenting symptom in older patients.

**Asymptomatic presentation**
Serological screening in at-risk groups (first-degree relatives, type 1 diabetes, autoimmune thyroiditis) identifies individuals with positive serology but no symptoms; biopsy confirms mucosal damage despite lack of GI symptoms.

**Dermatitis herpetiformis**
Blistering, intensely pruritic rash on elbows, knees, buttocks, scalp, and extensor surfaces characterises dermatitis herpetiformis (DH), the cutaneous form of coeliac disease. IgA deposition at the dermal-epidermal junction on immunofluorescence is diagnostic; intestinal villous atrophy is present in >95% despite variable GI symptoms.

## Diagnosis

**Serology**
Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) is first-line screening; sensitivity and specificity both >95%. Endomysial IgA (EMA) and deamidated gliadin peptide IgA (DGP-IgA) are alternatives with similar accuracy. Testing must occur while patient is consuming gluten (4–6 weeks minimum); gluten avoidance for ≥2 weeks reduces test sensitivity and may yield false negatives.

Check total serum IgA; if <0.7 g/L (IgA deficiency, 2–3% of population), tTG-IgA and EMA may be falsely negative. Use tTG-IgG or deamidated gliadin peptide IgG in IgA-deficient patients.

Positive serology warrants referral for endoscopic duodenal biopsy to confirm diagnosis.

**Endoscopy and histology**
Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy with ≥4 duodenal biopsies (second and third portions) demonstrates villous atrophy (Marsh classification I–IV). Histology shows flattened or absent villi, crypt hyperplasia (crypt-to-villous ratio >1:1), and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (≥20 per 100 epithelial cells). This confirms coeliac disease in seropositive individuals.

**HLA testing**
HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8 testing is reserved for patients with negative serology but high clinical suspicion (e.g., dermatitis herpetiformis with negative serology). Negative HLA testing essentially excludes coeliac disease (>99% sensitive).

## Severity and classification

Coeliac disease severity is classified histologically:

- **Marsh 0:** normal mucosa
- **Marsh I:** increased intraepithelial lymphocytes only
- **Marsh II:** crypt hyperplasia only
- **Marsh III:** villous atrophy (IIIa partial, IIIb subtotal, IIIc total)
- **Marsh IV:** hypoplastic atrophy (rare, severe disease)

Most diagnosed patients have Marsh III (villous atrophy).

## Investigations for complications and nutritional status

At diagnosis, assess for malabsorption-related deficiencies:

Full blood count detects iron deficiency anaemia (most common; 10–50% of patients) and macrocytic anaemia (B12 or folate deficiency). Urea and electrolytes assess renal function and identify hypoalbuminaemia (malnutrition marker). Liver function tests assess hepatic involvement (elevated transaminases in 15–30% due to associated autoimmune hepatitis). Bone profile and 25-OH vitamin D level assess osteomalacia risk; DEXA scanning detects osteoporosis (present in 20–30% at diagnosis). Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) is repeated at 12 months to assess dietary adherence (negative serology indicates good compliance; persistent positive suggests ongoing gluten exposure).

## Management

**Dietary management**
Strict gluten-free diet is the only treatment. Gluten is present in wheat, barley, rye, and cross-contaminated oats. Referral to a specialist dietitian ensures comprehensive dietary education, meal planning, and monitoring of nutritional recovery. Most patients require 3–6 months to achieve full mucosal healing; however, symptoms often improve within 2–4 weeks.

**Nutritional supplementation**
Iron supplementation (ferrous sulphate 325 mg daily or equivalent) corrects iron deficiency; reassess Hb at 3 months. Vitamin B12 supplementation (parenteral if intrinsic factor antibodies present, indicating pernicious anaemia; oral if deficiency alone) addresses B12 deficiency. Calcium and vitamin D supplementation (calcium carbonate 1000 mg + vitamin D3 400–800 IU daily) supports bone health, especially if osteoporosis present. Folate supplementation is given if folate deficiency is documented.

**Follow-up and monitoring**
At 6 weeks post-diagnosis, review symptom resolution, dietary compliance, and nutritional tolerability. At 3 months, reassess bloods (FBC, urea and electrolytes, LFTs) to monitor nutritional recovery. At 12 months, repeat tTG-IgA to assess dietary adherence; persistent elevation suggests ongoing gluten contamination. Repeat endoscopy at 1–2 years confirms mucosal healing (optional if symptomatically well and serology normalised).

Annual review includes assessment of symptom stability, dietary compliance, nutritional status (annual FBC, Hb, iron studies, B12, folate, calcium, vitamin D), and screening for complications. DEXA scanning at diagnosis and repeated every 3–5 years assesses bone density and osteoporosis risk.

## Complications

**Secondary lactose intolerance**
Villous atrophy reduces lactase expression; 20–30% of newly diagnosed patients are transiently lactose-intolerant. Usually resolves after mucosal healing (2–6 months); trial lactose-free dairy initially, then reintroduce.

**Osteoporosis**
Present at diagnosis in 20–30% due to malabsorption of calcium and vitamin D. Emphasise calcium and vitamin D supplementation and physical activity. Bone density typically improves on gluten-free diet.

**Dermatitis herpetiformis**
DH requires gluten-free diet; additionally, dapsone (50–100 mg daily) provides rapid symptom relief (within days) but does not heal intestinal mucosa. Dapsone is withdrawn once mucosal healing achieved on gluten-free diet (usually 6–12 months).

**Refractory coeliac disease**
1–3% of coeliac patients develop refractory disease: persistent villous atrophy despite strict gluten-free diet for ≥12 months. Type I (preserved intraepithelial lymphocytes) responds to corticosteroids; Type II (aberrant intraepithelial lymphocytes) has poorer prognosis and increased risk of enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma. Requires specialist gastroenterology input.

**Increased malignancy risk**
Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL) is rare but increases with undiagnosed/non-adherent coeliac disease. Early diagnosis and strict adherence reduce risk substantially. Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma and small intestinal adenocarcinoma also carry marginally elevated risk.

## Screening and case-finding

Screen first-degree relatives of coeliac patients with serology ± HLA testing. Screen patients with type 1 diabetes, autoimmune thyroiditis, and selected other autoimmune conditions. Consider screening in patients with unexplained anaemia, osteoporosis, or dermatitis herpetiformis.

## Special populations

**Children**
Classic presentation is more common in childhood. Introduce gluten-containing foods at 4–6 months of age (delayed introduction does not prevent coeliac disease). At diagnosis, many children have short stature and delayed puberty; expect catch-up growth within 1–2 years on gluten-free diet.

**Pregnancy**
Untreated coeliac disease is associated with adverse outcomes (miscarriage, fetal growth restriction, anaemia). Screen in preconception counselling and manage with strict gluten-free diet and nutritional supplementation.

## Field notes

Coeliac disease is vastly underdiagnosed; maintain high suspicion in anaemia without cause, chronic diarrhoea, or autoimmune disease. Serological testing must occur on gluten-containing diet; a negative test in a gluten-free patient is unreliable. Biopsy confirmation is essential even with positive serology—diagnose based on histology, not serology alone. Dermatitis herpetiformis is pathognomonic; all patients require intestinal assessment. Dietary adherence is challenging; strong dietitian support is crucial. Monitor bone health and nutritional status; initial supplementation is often required. Refractory coeliac disease is rare but serious; consider in patients with persistent symptoms despite apparent compliance.
