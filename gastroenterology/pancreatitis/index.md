## Acute Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis is inflammation of the pancreas triggered by gallstones, alcohol, medications, or hypertriglyceridemia. Incidence is 5–10 per 100,000 per year in developed countries. Most cases (70–80%) are mild and self-limited; 20–30% progress to severe disease with organ failure and high mortality. Early recognition and aggressive fluid resuscitation are essential.


## Definition and epidemiology

Acute pancreatitis is acute inflammation of the pancreas with elevated serum amylase or lipase (>3× upper limit normal) and characteristic abdominal pain. Diagnosis requires 2 of 3 criteria: (1) characteristic abdominal pain (epigastric, radiating to back), (2) elevated amylase/lipase >3×ULN, (3) characteristic imaging findings (CT showing pancreatic inflammation or oedema). Leading causes are gallstones (40–50%) and alcohol (25–35%); others include hypertriglyceridemia, post-ERCP, medications (azathioprine, sulfonamides, diuretics, L-asparaginase), and trauma. In 10–15%, no cause is identified (idiopathic).

## Pathophysiology

Triggers cause premature trypsinogen activation within the pancreas, leading to autodigestion of pancreatic tissue, acinar cell necrosis, and release of pancreatic enzymes into circulation. Inflammatory cascade activates complement, cytokines, and coagulation cascades, driving systemic inflammation. Most cases resolve with enzymatic action limited to pancreatic tissue; severe cases progress to pancreatic necrosis, peripancreatic fluid collections, and multi-organ failure.

## Clinical presentation

**Mild-moderate pancreatitis**
Sudden epigastric or left-sided pain radiating to the back (often dramatic onset). Associated nausea and vomiting. Pain lasts hours to days. Examination reveals epigastric tenderness; peritonism is absent. Vital signs are usually normal.

**Severe pancreatitis**
Severe pain, persistent vomiting, tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnoea suggesting shock or organ failure. Signs of systemic inflammatory response: fever, altered mental status. Cullen's sign (periumbilical bruising) or Grey Turner's sign (flank bruising) indicate severe necrotising pancreatitis (rare, indicates poor prognosis).

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Onset and character of pain (epigastric, radiating to back). Associated symptoms (nausea, vomiting). Prior episodes of pancreatitis. Risk factors: gallstones, alcohol use (amount, duration), recent ERCP, medications, hypertriglyceridemia, family history of pancreatitis.

**Examination**
Vital signs assess haemodynamic stability. Epigastric or left upper quadrant tenderness is typical; peritonism suggests severe disease or complication (perforation). Cullen's or Grey Turner's signs indicate severe necrotising disease.

**Investigations**
Serum amylase or lipase (elevated >3×ULN confirms diagnosis). Lipase is more specific than amylase. Amylase peaks at 24–48 hours and declines rapidly; lipase is elevated longer, aiding late diagnosis.

Full blood count: elevated WBC suggests inflammation and predicts worse prognosis. Urea and electrolytes assess renal function (acute kidney injury is a marker of severity). Liver function tests: elevated transaminases may suggest biliary obstruction (gallstone cause). Glucose: elevated glucose predicts worse prognosis. LDH and albumin: used in severity scoring. Calcium: hypocalcaemia indicates severe necrosis (deposits in fat from fatty acid soaps).

Coagulation (INR, PT, APTT) if severe disease.

Triglycerides: if elevated (>10 mmol/L), hypertriglyceridemia is likely cause.

Abdominal CT with contrast: deferred until after initial resuscitation (typically 48–72 hours after onset). Shows pancreatic oedema, necrosis, peripancreatic fluid, and complications (infected necrosis, abscess). Reserved for severe disease, diagnostic uncertainty, or complication assessment; not needed for mild disease.

Abdominal ultrasound: can assess for gallstones (potential cause) but is limited in pancreatic imaging (obscured by bowel gas).

## Severity assessment

**APACHE II score**
Multifactorial severity score incorporating age, physiological parameters, and laboratory values. APACHE >6 predicts severe pancreatitis with ~15–20% mortality; APACHE >8 predicts 30–50% mortality. Simple bedside assessment: age >55, WBC >15K, glucose >200, LDH >1.5×ULN, albumin <3.5 are associated with worse outcomes.

**Modified Marshall score**
Assesses organ system dysfunction (renal, cardiovascular, pulmonary). Used to track recovery in severe disease.

**Ranson criteria**
Alternative scoring system (>3 criteria on admission or 24 hours predicts severe disease with high mortality).

## Management

**Admission**
All acute pancreatitis requires hospital admission for IV fluids, monitoring, and supportive care.

**Fluid resuscitation**
Aggressive IV rehydration is cornerstone of management. Fluid deficit is substantial (may be 2–3 L or more). Initial resuscitation: 250–500 mL/hour of balanced crystalloid (normal saline or Hartmann's solution) depending on age, renal function, and severity. Target: urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hour; heart rate <120 bpm. Most mild-moderate cases resolve with fluids alone.

**Supportive care**
Analgesia: opioids (morphine 10 mg IV 4-hourly) for pain control. Fasting: nothing by mouth initially; diet advanced gradually as pain resolves. Antiemetics: metoclopramide 10 mg TDS for nausea. Proton pump inhibitor if stress ulcer risk high.

**Monitoring**
Vital signs, urine output hourly initially. Daily serum amylase/lipase and renal function. APACHE or Modified Marshall score daily in severe disease.

**Specific interventions**
Antibiotics: reserved for severe pancreatitis (APACHE >6) or evidence of infection (fever, imaging showing necrosis). Prophylactic antibiotics (fluoroquinolone or cephalosporin) may reduce infected necrosis rates in severe disease.

ERCP: indicated if evidence of bile duct obstruction (jaundice, dilated bile duct on imaging) suggesting retained stone. Timing: within 24–48 hours. Not indicated for uncomplicated gallstone pancreatitis without obstruction.

Nutritional support: enteral feeds via nasogastric tube are preferred over total parenteral nutrition if prolonged NPO status expected. Early feeding reduces complications.

**Specific cause management**
Gallstone pancreatitis: after acute phase resolves, elective cholecystectomy is performed to prevent recurrence.

Alcohol-related: counselling on abstinence; referral to addiction services if needed.

Hypertriglyceridemia: fibrate therapy (fenofibrate 200 mg daily) and dietary fat restriction.

Medication-induced: identify and discontinue offending agent.

## Complications

**Local complications**
Peripancreatic fluid collection: fluid around pancreas without walling off. Usually reabsorbs; no specific treatment needed.

Pancreatic pseudocyst: walled-off fluid collection, develops over weeks. May be asymptomatic or cause pain/obstruction. Can be observed if asymptomatic; intervention (percutaneous drainage or endoscopic therapy) if symptomatic.

Pancreatic necrosis: non-viable pancreatic parenchyma; seen on contrast CT. Infected necrosis develops in 10–20% of necrotising pancreatitis; presents as persistent fever, elevated CRP, and leukocytosis after initial improvement. Requires intervention (percutaneous drainage ± debridement or endoscopic therapy).

**Systemic complications**
Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): occurs in severe pancreatitis; managed with lung-protective ventilation.

Acute kidney injury: develops in 10–30% of severe pancreatitis; often prerenal from hypovolaemia (reversed with aggressive fluid resuscitation).

Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC): rare but severe; platelet count drops, PT prolonged.

Hypocalcaemia: severe pancreatitis causes calcium deposition in necrotic tissue. Symptomatic hypocalcaemia requires IV calcium; asymptomatic mild hypocalcaemia is corrected gradually.

## Monitoring and follow-up

For mild-moderate pancreatitis: discharge once oral diet tolerated, pain controlled, and amylase/lipase declining. Follow-up at 1–2 weeks: assess recovery, identify cause, and arrange definitive treatment (e.g., cholecystectomy if gallstone cause).

For severe pancreatitis: ICU/high-dependency care monitoring. Daily severity assessment. Repeat imaging (CT) at 48–72 hours to assess necrosis extent. Intervention (drainage, debridement) if evidence of infected necrosis.

## Prevention of recurrence

Gallstone pancreatitis: elective cholecystectomy after acute phase resolves (typically 4–6 weeks). Prevents >90% recurrence.

Alcohol-related: alcohol cessation reduces recurrence substantially.

Hypertriglyceridemia pancreatitis: fibrate therapy and dietary fat restriction reduce recurrence.

Idiopathic or other causes: management depends on identified etiology; prophylactic measures vary.

## Field notes

Pancreatitis is a spectrum from mild (uncomplicated, self-limited) to severe (necrotising, multi-organ failure). Early recognition and aggressive fluid resuscitation are life-saving; do not delay referral. APACHE >6 or organ failure indicates severe disease requiring ICU admission. Identify cause: gallstones are most common (urgent cholecystectomy after recovery), alcohol second (counselling on abstinence). CT is deferred until 48–72 hours; early CT rarely changes management. Infected necrosis develops in late phase; high suspicion (persistent fever, rising CRP after initial improvement) warrants imaging and intervention. Mild pancreatitis may be managed with supportive care alone; most resolve within days. Pseudocyst and chronic pancreatitis are complications of recurrent disease; prevention (e.g., cholecystectomy) is crucial.
