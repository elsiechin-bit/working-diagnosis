## Metabolic-Associated Fatty Liver Disease

Metabolic-associated fatty liver disease (MASLD, formerly non-alcoholic fatty liver disease or NAFLD) is the leading cause of liver disease in developed countries. Most cases are benign, but a significant minority progress to cirrhosis or hepatocellular carcinoma. Primary care recognition and risk stratification are crucial.

## Definition and epidemiology

Metabolic-associated fatty liver disease (MASLD) is hepatic steatosis (accumulation of triglycerides comprising ≥5% of liver weight) in patients with metabolic risk factors (obesity, type 2 diabetes, dyslipidaemia, hypertension) without significant alcohol consumption (<30 g/day in men, <20 g/day in women). MASLD affects 25–35% of developed populations. Prevalence increases with obesity: 50–75% in obese individuals, up to 90% in those with morbid obesity and type 2 diabetes.

## Pathophysiology

Insulin resistance is central. Impaired hepatic insulin signalling increases hepatic de novo lipogenesis (conversion of carbohydrate to fat) and reduces hepatic fatty acid oxidation. Increased free fatty acid flux from adipose tissue exacerbates hepatic lipid accumulation. Mitochondrial oxidative stress, endoplasmic reticulum stress, and gut dysbiosis drive hepatic inflammation and fibrosis progression in a subset of patients.

Most patients (70%) have simple steatosis (lipid-only without inflammation); 20–30% develop hepatic steatohepatitis (NASH)—inflammation and hepatocyte necrosis alongside steatosis. Of those with NASH, 10–15% develop advanced fibrosis and cirrhosis over 10 years; simple steatosis is generally non-progressive.

## Clinical presentation

MASLD is usually asymptomatic and discovered incidentally on imaging done for other reasons or when LFTs are checked. Some patients report mild fatigue or right upper quadrant discomfort, though this is non-specific. Hepatomegaly may be palpable.

By the time patients present with symptoms of cirrhosis (ascites, jaundice, bleeding, encephalopathy), advanced disease is established.

## Assessment

**History and examination**
Assess for metabolic risk factors: obesity (BMI ≥30), type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, dyslipidaemia, hypertension. Ask about alcohol consumption (clarify actual amounts; MASLD excludes significant alcohol use). Examine for obesity, hepatomegaly, signs of cirrhosis (ascites, spider naevi, palmar erythema).

**Investigations**
Abdominal ultrasound is first-line: demonstrates hepatic steatosis (echogenic liver parenchyma, loss of definition of hepatic vessels). Ultrasound can assess for features of cirrhosis (surface irregularity, ascites, splenomegaly) but has limited sensitivity for fibrosis stage.

LFTs: ALT elevation is mild (usually 1–3 times upper limit normal); AST often <ALT (ALT/AST ratio <1 suggests NAFLD; ratio >2 suggests alcoholic liver disease). Bilirubin, ALP, and synthetic function are usually normal unless cirrhosis present.

Hepatitis serology (A, B, C) excludes viral hepatitis. Autoimmune markers (ANA, anti-smooth muscle, anti-LKM) exclude autoimmune hepatitis.

**Non-invasive fibrosis assessment**
FibroScan (transient elastography) measures liver stiffness and predicts fibrosis stage non-invasively. Results are expressed in kilopascals (kPa): normal <5.8 kPa suggests F0-F1; 5.8–9.2 kPa suggests F2; >9.2 kPa suggests F3-F4. FibroScan has limitations in obese patients (BMI >40) and is unreliable in acute hepatitis or cholestasis.

FIB-4 index (fibrosis-4) uses age, AST, and platelet count: FIB-4 = (Age × AST) / (Platelet count × √ALT). Score <1.3 in age ≤65 or <2.67 in age >65 indicates low risk of advanced fibrosis. APRI (AST to platelet ratio index) and other scores aid risk stratification.

Liver biopsy is gold standard but rarely needed; reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or assessment of NASH grade when treatment decisions hinge on fibrosis stage.

## Staging

**Simple steatosis (NAFL)**
Steatosis without inflammation on biopsy; generally non-progressive. Ultrasound shows steatosis; LFTs are normal or mildly abnormal.

**NASH (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis)**
Steatosis with inflammation and hepatocyte necrosis on biopsy. LFTs often more elevated (ALT >2× ULN). 20–30% of MASLD patients; higher risk of fibrosis progression.

**Fibrosis stages (METAVIR)**
F0: no fibrosis; F1: portal fibrosis; F2: portal fibrosis with few septa; F3: numerous septa without cirrhosis; F4: cirrhosis. FibroScan and serum biomarkers aid non-invasive staging.

## Management

**Weight loss**
10% body weight reduction improves hepatic steatosis and inflammation; 15% weight loss may reverse NASH. Combined dietary and exercise interventions are most effective. Referral to dietitian and exercise specialist aids adherence.

**Metabolic risk factor management**
Glycaemic control in diabetes (target HbA1c <7%, or individualized target). Blood pressure control (target <130/80 mmHg). Statin therapy for dyslipidaemia (reduces cardiovascular risk and has modest anti-inflammatory hepatic effects).

**Alcohol cessation**
Strict limitation or avoidance. Even mild alcohol intake may accelerate fibrosis in NASH.

**Avoid hepatotoxic drugs**
Acetaminophen (paracetamol), NSAIDs, and other hepatotoxins should be used judiciously or avoided. Herbal supplements (especially those with hepatotoxic potential) should be reviewed.

**Pharmacological therapy**
Pioglitazone and vitamin E have been studied but show modest benefit and are not standard first-line therapy. GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) are emerging as beneficial for weight loss and metabolic health; considered for NASH with fibrosis. Specialist input is recommended before initiation.

## Monitoring

For patients with simple steatosis and no fibrosis markers (FIB-4 <1.3, FibroScan <5.8 kPa):
Annual LFTs, metabolic monitoring. Repeat FibroScan every 2–3 years or if risk factors worsen. Reassure that progression is unlikely.

For patients with NASH or evidence of fibrosis (FIB-4 >1.3, FibroScan 5.8–9.2 kPa):
6–12 monthly LFTs, metabolic monitoring. FibroScan annually. Consider specialist referral for assessment of treatment options (pioglitazone, vitamin E, GLP-1 agonists).

For patients with advanced fibrosis (FIB-4 >2.67, FibroScan >9.2 kPa, or clinical cirrhosis):
Specialist management. Surveillance for hepatocellular carcinoma (AFP, imaging annually if cirrhosis). Screening for varices (endoscopy) if portal hypertension features present.

## Complications

**Fibrosis and cirrhosis**
10–15% of NASH patients develop cirrhosis over 10 years. Cirrhosis presents with decompensation (ascites, bleeding, encephalopathy) and hepatocellular carcinoma risk.

**Hepatocellular carcinoma**
Risk is elevated in cirrhotic livers; annual screening with AFP and imaging is recommended.

**Cardiovascular disease**
MASLD patients have increased cardiovascular mortality from underlying metabolic syndrome; aggressive cardiovascular risk factor management is essential.

## Special populations

**Type 2 diabetes**
MASLD occurs in >50% of diabetic patients. Screen with ultrasound and FibroScan at diagnosis. Intensive metabolic management reduces progression.

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
Age is a risk factor for advanced fibrosis. FIB-4 score >2.67 mandates specialist assessment. Screen for cirrhosis features (ascites, INR, platelets).

**Obesity and metabolic syndrome**
Weight loss is cornerstone of management. Bariatric surgery improves MASLD histology in some; consider in morbid obesity if weight loss fails.

**Pregnancy**
MASLD is not a contraindication to pregnancy; however, monitor LFTs during pregnancy (weight-gain-induced LFT changes can occur). Discuss weight management postpartum.

## Field notes

MASLD is increasingly common; screen opportunistically in obese, diabetic, or metabolically at-risk patients. Most cases (70%) are simple steatosis with low progression risk; reassurance and lifestyle support suffice. FibroScan is non-invasive and cost-effective for fibrosis stratification; use liberally in newly diagnosed MASLD to avoid missing advanced disease. Weight loss is the most effective intervention; refer to dietitian for structured programs. GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide) show promise for NASH but are not yet standard first-line; specialist consultation is prudent. Cardiovascular mortality exceeds hepatic mortality in MASLD; aggressive management of diabetes, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia is vital. Do not assume normal LFTs exclude advanced fibrosis; FibroScan may reveal cirrhosis with normal LFTs in a minority.
