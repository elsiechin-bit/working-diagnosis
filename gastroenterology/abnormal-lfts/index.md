## Abnormal Liver Function Tests

Abnormal liver function tests (LFTs) are common in primary care, with most being transient or mild. However, systematic assessment distinguishes benign from serious hepatic disease and directs appropriate referral.

## Definition and epidemiology

Abnormal liver function tests refer to elevation of one or more of: alanine transaminase (ALT), aspartate transaminase (AST), alkaline phosphatase (ALP), gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT), or bilirubin above normal laboratory range. Prevalence of abnormal LFTs on population screening is 1–3%, but many are transient or benign. Significant liver disease (cirrhosis, hepatitis) affects 1–2% of developed populations; many are undiagnosed.

## Pathophysiology and classification

**Hepatocellular pattern**
Elevation of ALT and/or AST (often >ALP) indicates hepatocyte injury. Causes include viral hepatitis, alcoholic liver disease, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), autoimmune hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, and cirrhosis. ALT is more specific for liver injury; AST is less specific (also elevated in muscle injury, haemolysis).

**Cholestatic pattern**
Elevation of ALP and/or GGT (often >ALT/AST) indicates cholestasis or bile duct obstruction. Causes include biliary obstruction (gallstones, stricture, malignancy), primary biliary cholangitis (PBC), primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC), and drug-induced cholestasis.

**Mixed pattern**
Elevation of both hepatocellular and cholestatic enzymes suggests cirrhosis, severe hepatitis, or some drug reactions.

**Unconjugated hyperbilirubinaemia**
Elevated unconjugated bilirubin with normal ALT/AST suggests haemolysis or Gilbert's syndrome (benign, unconjugated bilirubin <85 μmol/L, exacerbated by stress/illness).

**Conjugated hyperbilirubinaemia**
Elevated conjugated (direct) bilirubin with elevated ALP indicates cholestasis or hepatocellular disease with impaired excretion.

## Aetiologies

**Viral hepatitis**
Hepatitis A (acute, self-limited; ALT often >1000 IU/L), hepatitis B (acute or chronic), hepatitis C (often asymptomatic; chronic in 80%).

**Alcoholic liver disease**
ALT <400 IU/L (often <300); AST often exceeds ALT (AST/ALT ratio >2 suggests alcoholic disease). Elevated GGT and MCV are supportive.

**Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)**
ALT mildly elevated (1–3 times upper limit normal); AST often lower than ALT. Associated with obesity, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome. Ultrasound shows hepatic steatosis.

**Autoimmune hepatitis**
ALT elevated; autoantibodies present (ANA, anti-smooth muscle, anti-LKM). More common in women; may present acutely or as chronic disease.

**Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)**
Cholestatic pattern (ALP, GGT elevated); anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA) positive in 95%. Chronic progressive disease; predominantly affects middle-aged women.

**Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC)**
Cholestatic pattern; associated with inflammatory bowel disease (70%); male predominance; associated with cirrhosis risk and cholangiocarcinoma.

**Drug-induced liver injury**
Hepatocellular pattern (acetaminophen, statins, NSAIDs) or cholestatic pattern (anabolic steroids, oral contraceptives, azathioprine). Onset usually within 3 months of drug initiation.

**Cirrhosis**
Mixed pattern; may have mild enzyme elevation. INR, albumin, bilirubin worsen with progression. Ultrasound shows features of portal hypertension.

**Hepatocellular carcinoma**
May have minimal enzyme elevation; alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) often elevated; imaging shows lesion.

## Clinical assessment

**History**
Onset of abnormal tests (acute vs chronic discovery). Associated symptoms: jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, abdominal pain, pruritus, fatigue, weight loss. Alcohol consumption (amount, duration, pattern). Drug history (acetaminophen, statins, NSAIDs, herbal supplements). Travel history (endemic hepatitis regions). Transfusion or IV drug use history. Family history of liver disease or autoimmune disease. Sexual history or shared household contacts (hepatitis B, C).

**Examination**
Jaundice, spider naevi, palmar erythema, gynaecomastia (cirrhosis signs). Abdominal examination: hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, ascites, or bruits (portal hypertension). Signs of hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, asterixis), bleeding (bruising, petechiae).

## Investigations

**Serological testing**
Hepatitis A serology (HAV IgM for acute; HAV total antibody for past exposure or immunity). Hepatitis B: HBsAg (surface antigen; indicates current infection), anti-HBc (core antibody; past/current infection), anti-HBs (surface antibody; immunity after vaccination or recovery). Hepatitis C: anti-HCV antibody (indicates past or current infection); if positive, HCV RNA confirms active infection.

**Autoimmune markers**
Antinuclear antibody (ANA), anti-smooth muscle antibody (ASMA), anti-liver-kidney microsome antibody (anti-LKM), anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA), anti-centromere antibody (ACA). Elevated immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgM, IgA) support autoimmune hepatitis.

**Coagulation and synthetic function**
Prothrombin time (PT) / International Normalised Ratio (INR) assesses synthetic function; elevation suggests advanced liver disease. Albumin: low levels (<30 g/L) suggest cirrhosis or severe liver disease. Platelets: low counts (<100K) suggest portal hypertension/splenomegaly.

**Imaging**
Abdominal ultrasound is first-line: assesses liver echotexture (steatosis, cirrhosis), bile duct dilatation (cholestasis), gallstones, ascites, and splenomegaly. MRI/CT may be needed for characterisation of lesions or cirrhosis features.

## Management

**Mild transient elevation without symptoms**
Repeat LFTs in 2–4 weeks; many resolve spontaneously. If normal repeat, reassure. If persistent, investigate further.

**Hepatocellular pattern**
Hepatitis serology, autoimmune markers, ultrasound. Alcohol history. Assess for metabolic risk factors (obesity, diabetes). If viral or autoimmune hepatitis suspected, refer to gastroenterology for liver biopsy assessment and treatment.

**Cholestatic pattern**
Hepatitis serology, autoimmune markers (AMA, ANA), ultrasound to assess for obstruction and cirrhosis. Gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) elevation confirms hepatic source (ALP elevation alone may be bone-derived). Direct (conjugated) bilirubin >50% of total bilirubin confirms cholestasis.

**Markedly elevated transaminases (ALT >1000 IU/L)**
Suggests acute hepatitis (viral, autoimmune, drug-induced, ischaemic). Urgent hepatitis serology, autoimmune markers, INR, albumin. If INR prolonged (>1.5) or jaundice present, urgent specialist assessment and consider admission.

**Jaundice with cholestasis**
Ultrasound urgently to assess for biliary obstruction (stones, stricture, malignancy). If dilated bile ducts, ERCP may be indicated. Specialist referral.

**Suspected cirrhosis**
Clinical and biochemical evidence (ascites, spider naevi, thrombocytopenia, elevated INR, low albumin) warrant urgent specialist assessment. Transient elastography (FibroScan) or liver biopsy confirms cirrhosis. Screen for varices (endoscopy) and hepatocellular carcinoma (AFP, imaging).

**NAFLD**
Weight loss, physical activity, manage metabolic risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia). Most NAFLD is non-progressive; advanced fibrosis requires specialist assessment (FibroScan).

## Monitoring and follow-up

Mild elevation without features of liver disease: repeat LFTs at 2–4 weeks. If persistent after 2–3 months, investigate further (serology, imaging, autoimmune markers).

Viral hepatitis or autoimmune disease: specialist management, monitoring for cirrhosis progression, and treatment initiation as indicated.

Cirrhosis: regular monitoring for decompensation (ascites, varices, encephalopathy, hepatocellular carcinoma). Surveillance endoscopy for varices if portal hypertension present. Annual AFP and imaging screening for hepatocellular carcinoma.

## Complications of liver disease

**Cirrhosis and decompensation**
Ascites, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, hepatic encephalopathy, variceal bleeding, renal failure (hepatorenal syndrome), and hepatocellular carcinoma are life-threatening complications. Management requires specialist input and often hospital admission.

**Hepatic encephalopathy**
Altered mental status, asterixis, and confusion from elevated ammonia and neurotoxins. Managed with lactulose, dietary protein restriction, and treatment of precipitants (infection, bleeding, medication).

**Hepatocellular carcinoma**
Develops in cirrhotic livers; AFP may be elevated. Diagnosis by imaging; treatment options include surgery, transplantation, or palliative care depending on stage and liver function.

## Special populations

**Older adults**
May have mild chronic enzyme elevation from fatty liver or medication side effects. Investigate persistent abnormalities to exclude serious pathology. Monitor drug interactions in polypharmacy.

**Pregnancy**
Mild ALP elevation is normal (placental source). Acute hepatitis or jaundice in pregnancy requires urgent investigation. Avoid hepatotoxic drugs.

## Field notes

Transient mild enzyme elevation is common and often resolves; repeat testing before extensive investigation. Viral hepatitis serology is crucial; don't miss hepatitis B or C, which are treatable if identified. Autoimmune hepatitis is frequently missed; maintain suspicion in elevated LFTs without obvious cause. Cirrhosis detection is critical—ensure ultrasound and synthetic function assessment in persistent abnormalities. NAFLD is increasingly common; address metabolic factors (weight, diabetes, dyslipidaemia). Alcohol is a leading cause; direct, non-judgmental questioning about consumption is essential.
