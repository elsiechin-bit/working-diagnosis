## NZ Bowel Screening Programme

The National Bowel Screening Programme (NBSP) is New Zealand's population-based colorectal cancer screening initiative, offering free Faecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) to all citizens aged 50–74 years. Primary care plays a crucial role in promoting uptake, interpreting results, and ensuring timely colonoscopy referral for positive cases.


## Definition and programme overview

The National Bowel Screening Programme (NBSP) invites all NZ citizens aged 50–74 years to complete a Faecal Immunochemical Test (FIT) every 2 years. FIT detects microscopic blood in stool with high sensitivity and specificity for advanced colorectal neoplasia (cancer and high-grade dysplasia polyps). The programme aims to detect colorectal cancer at an early, more treatable stage and to remove precancerous polyps. Target participation is 60%+; current NZ uptake is ~45%, with lower uptake in Māori and Pacific Islander populations and lower socioeconomic groups.

## Epidemiology and rationale

Colorectal cancer is the second leading cause of cancer death in NZ. Incidence is 40–50 per 100,000 per year. Early detection reduces mortality by 15–20%. Age 50 is chosen as screening start because incidence increases substantially beyond this age. Age 74 is the upper limit due to diminishing cost-effectiveness and competing mortality risks in older ages. Screening interval of 2 years balances cancer detection (most interval cancers are FIT-negative) with unnecessary endoscopy burden.

## Screening pathway

**Invitation and kit distribution**
Eligible patients (50–74 years) are invited by letter with FIT kit. Alternatively, kits are available from general practice, pharmacies, and online. Patient completes test at home (sampling takes ~1 minute), seals sample in container, and returns via post (prepaid envelope provided). Processing occurs at central laboratory.

**FIT technology**
FIT detects human globin in stool (plant and animal globin do not interfere). Globin is present in blood from any site in the GI tract (oesophageal varices, gastric ulcer, colonic bleeding). Sensitivity for colorectal cancer is >90%; sensitivity for advanced adenomas (≥10 mm) is 70–80%. Specificity is ~95%.

**Result interpretation**
Negative FIT (<10 µg/g): No or trace blood detected. Low risk of advanced neoplasia; routine rescreening in 2 years.

Positive FIT (≥10 µg/g): Blood detected. Indicates need for colonoscopy to investigate source.

Borderline/indeterminate FIT (5–10 µg/g in some assays): Some laboratories use this category; interpretation varies but typically prompts repeat FIT in 6–12 months or colonoscopy depending on laboratory guidance.

## Management of FIT-positive results

**Colonoscopy referral**
All FIT-positive patients require colonoscopy referral via standard DHB pathways. Referral urgency depends on FIT result level and clinical context.

Standard urgency: FIT moderately positive (10–25 µg/g), no alarm symptoms. Colonoscopy within 6–8 weeks.

Urgent referral: FIT strongly positive (>25 µg/g), anaemia (Hb <130 g/L men, <120 g/L women), or alarm symptoms (weight loss, change in bowel habit, persistent bleeding, abdominal pain). Colonoscopy within 2–4 weeks.

**Interim management**
Patient counselling: explain that FIT-positive does not mean cancer; many benign causes of bleeding exist (haemorrhoids, fissure, polyps, diverticulosis). Reassure that colonoscopy is safe and diagnostic.

Avoid reassuring based on assumed benign cause (e.g., "this is just haemorrhoids") without investigation; colonoscopy is essential to confirm.

## FIT-negative pathway

**Negative FIT management**
Reassure patient; risk of advanced neoplasia is low. Routine rescreening recommended in 2 years. No further investigation needed unless alarm symptoms present (change in bowel habit, weight loss, persistent bleeding despite negative FIT); these warrant clinical assessment and possible colonoscopy regardless of FIT status.

**Interval cancers**
Cancers that develop between screening tests (FIT-negative but cancer present within 2 years) account for 5–15% of colorectal cancers. Most are not detected by FIT (FIT-negative interval cancers); others are missed by colonoscopy. Reassure that screening is not perfect but substantially reduces risk.

## Special populations and considerations

**Never screened**
Patients aged 50–74 who have never had FIT or colonoscopy are eligible. Encourage completion of first FIT.

**Prior colonoscopy**
If colonoscopy within last 10 years was normal and complete, FIT screening may not be necessary; individual risk assessment guides decisions. Abnormal prior findings may require more frequent surveillance.

**Symptoms or alarm features**
Do not delay diagnostic colonoscopy for symptomatic patients awaiting NBSP invitation or FIT result. Patients with alarm symptoms (blood per rectum, change in bowel habit, weight loss, anaemia) require urgent investigation regardless of NBSP status.

**High-risk individuals**
Those with personal history of colorectal cancer or advanced adenoma, family history of colorectal cancer or hereditary syndromes (Lynch syndrome, familial adenomatous polyposis), or inflammatory bowel disease require individualised surveillance strategies (colonoscopy every 3–5 years or more frequently) and may not be suited to FIT-based screening alone.

**Māori and Pacific Islander populations**
Higher incidence and earlier age of presentation; lower uptake of NBSP. Targeted engagement and culturally appropriate materials improve participation.

**Socioeconomic disparities**
Lower-income groups have lower NBSP uptake. Flexible distribution (community clinics, pharmacies, libraries) and dedicated health worker support improve access.

## Primary care role

**Promotion and uptake**
Use preventive health visits, annual health checks, or consultations for other conditions to promote NBSP participation. Distribute FIT kits; normalise screening as routine preventive care. Address barriers: language, health literacy, cultural concerns, fear of colonoscopy.

**Result communication**
Communicate FIT results clearly and in writing. For negative results, reassure and reinforce rescreening in 2 years. For positive results, explain need for colonoscopy, provide referral pathway, and offer support. Ensure patient understanding before referral.

**Colonoscopy referral**
Timely referral via DHB endoscopy pathways. Document urgency and clinical context. Follow up post-colonoscopy to ensure completion and to document findings (adenomas found, polyps removed, recommendations for surveillance).

**Repeat screening**
Actively re-invite eligible patients at 2-year rescreening interval; send reminder letters or texts. Track uptake and participation rates.

## Contraindications to screening

**Age <50 or >74 without special indication**
Screening outside recommended age range is not part of NBSP; individualised assessment required.

**Short life expectancy** (<5–10 years)
Screening unlikely to benefit; individualised decision-making required.

**Prior total colectomy**
No need for colorectal screening.

**Inability to tolerate colonoscopy**
NBSP relies on FIT for screening; if positive, colonoscopy still required. Alternative approaches (CT colonography) may be considered but are outside NBSP scope.

## Field notes

NBSP is highly cost-effective; promote uptake relentlessly in eligible populations. FIT is simple and non-invasive; barriers are often knowledge or engagement rather than clinical. Never assume FIT-positive bleeding is benign; colonoscopy is mandatory. Most FIT-positive patients have benign findings (haemorrhoids, polyps) but ~3–5% have cancer; investigation is essential. Track rescreening intervals to ensure patients return at 2 years; automated recall systems improve adherence. Disparities in uptake persist; cultural competency and targeted outreach improve participation in high-risk populations.
