## Topic body

### Definition and pathophysiology

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is chronic reflux of gastric contents into the oesophagus causing symptoms and/or tissue damage. Pathophysiology involves:

- **Lower oesophageal sphincter (LES) incompetence**: reduced tone (normal LES pressure 15–30 mmHg; GORD typically <10 mmHg), increased transient relaxations
- **Impaired oesophageal clearance**: reduced acid clearance due to decreased saliva, impaired peristalsis, or supine position
- **Gastric factors**: increased intragastric pressure, delayed gastric emptying, increased stomach acid production
- **Acid vs bile reflux**: acid reflux most common (~70%); bile reflux (alkaline) in post-surgical patients or severe GORD

### Epidemiology

- **Prevalence**: 20–40% of population experience reflux symptoms monthly; 10–20% weekly
- **Risk factors**: obesity (BMI >30), pregnancy, smoking, alcohol, large meals, recumbent position
- **Temporal**: symptoms worse after meals, at night, when lying flat
- **No sex difference** in prevalence, though pregnancy increases risk

### Clinical presentation

**Typical symptoms**
- **Heartburn (pyrosis)**: burning sensation behind sternum or epigastrium, radiating to neck/throat
- **Regurgitation**: acid/food refluxing into mouth, especially when lying flat or bending forward
- **Waterbrash**: sudden excess saliva (mouth fills with acidic liquid)
- **Dysphagia**: difficulty swallowing (suggests oesophageal narrowing or Barrett's; needs investigation)

**Atypical/extra-oesophageal symptoms**
- **Cough**: often dry, worse at night or when lying flat
- **Hoarseness**: laryngeal irritation from acid reflux
- **Asthma-like symptoms**: wheeze, chest tightness, dyspnoea (aspiration of gastric contents)
- **Throat clearing**: chronic irritation
- **Chest pain**: can mimic angina; typically pleuritic, reproducible, relieved by antacids

**Nocturnal GORD**
- Worse when lying flat (loss of gravity assistance to oesophageal clearance)
- Disrupts sleep, reduces sleep quality
- Associated with increased risk of respiratory complications (aspiration)

### Diagnosis

**Clinical diagnosis**
- Typical heartburn + regurgitation + symptom relief with acid suppression = GORD diagnosis
- No investigations required for straightforward presentation

**When to investigate**
- **Alarm symptoms present**: dysphagia, weight loss, persistent vomiting, gastrointestinal bleeding, anaemia (Hb <110 g/L)
- **Failed PPI trial**: inadequate symptom relief after 8 weeks on standard PPI dose
- **Chronic GORD** (>10 years): screen for Barrett's oesophagus
- **Atypical presentation**: diagnostic uncertainty, extraoesophageal symptoms only
- **Young age** (<45 years) **with alarm symptoms**: lower threshold for endoscopy

**Upper endoscopy (OGD)**
- Indications: alarm symptoms, failed PPI, diagnostic uncertainty
- Findings in GORD:
  - **Erosive oesophagitis**: mucosal breaks, erythema, ulceration (grades A–D severity)
  - **Barrett's oesophagus**: pink/salmon coloured mucosa replacing squamous epithelium (intestinal metaplasia on biopsy)
  - **Hiatus hernia**: proximal stomach herniates through diaphragm (not diagnostic of GORD but associated)
  - **Normal**: 50–60% of GORD patients have endoscopically normal oesophagus (non-erosive GORD)
- Biopsy indications: Barrett's diagnosis (confirm intestinal metaplasia), surveillance if Barrett's known, atypical appearance

**Ambulatory pH monitoring/impedance monitoring**
- Indications: diagnostic uncertainty, failed PPI therapy, suspected refractory GORD
- Measures percentage of time pH <4, number of reflux episodes, symptom-reflux correlation
- Gold standard for GORD diagnosis; not routinely needed if clinical diagnosis clear

**Other investigations** (rarely needed)
- **Barium swallow**: poor sensitivity for reflux; may show hiatus hernia but not diagnostic of GORD
- **Oesophageal manometry**: assessment of LES pressure and peristalsis; reserved for dysphagia or pre-surgical evaluation
- **Gastric emptying study**: if gastroparesis suspected as contributing factor

### Severity classification

**Non-erosive GORD (NERD)**
- Symptoms present but normal endoscopy
- Accounts for 60–70% of GORD cases
- Symptom severity not correlated with acid exposure (some with high acid exposure asymptomatic, some with low exposure very symptomatic)

**Erosive GORD**
- Mucosal breaks on endoscopy (grades A–D)
- Grade A–B: <50% oesophageal circumference involved
- Grade C–D: more extensive; associated with stricture/Barrett's risk

**Barrett's oesophagus**
- Intestinal metaplasia (columnar epithelium with goblet cells) replacing squamous epithelium
- Increased adenocarcinoma risk (annual incidence 0.1–0.3%)
- Surveillance protocol: repeat endoscopy annually or 2–3 yearly depending on dysplasia grade

### Management

**Lifestyle modification** (all patients)
- **Weight loss**: each 5 kg reduces symptom severity; aim BMI 20–25 kg/m²
- **Dietary changes**:
  - Avoid large meals (small, frequent meals preferred)
  - Avoid fatty, spicy foods, chocolate, citrus, tomatoes, caffeine, alcohol
  - No food 2–3 hours before bed
- **Positional**: elevate head of bed 15–30 cm (blocks or wedge pillow, not just extra pillows)
- **Sleep position**: left lateral preferred (lower LES pressure on right side)
- **Smoking cessation**: reduces LES tone
- **Avoid triggers**: NSAIDs (switch to paracetamol), beta-blockers (if alternatives available), anticholinergics, calcium channel blockers (if alternative available)

**Pharmacological management**

**Step 1: Antacids/alginates**
- **Antacids** (calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide): rapid relief; dose PRN
- **Alginates** (sodium alginate): form raft on stomach surface, mechanically block reflux
- Used for occasional symptoms or adjunct to PPI
- Limited evidence; symptom duration short-lived (30 min–2 hours)

**Step 2: H2-receptor antagonists**
- **Ranitidine 150 mg BD or 300 mg nocte** (though ranitidine availability variable in NZ; check formulary)
- **Famotidine 20 mg BD**
- Moderate acid suppression; less effective than PPI
- May be used for mild symptoms or as adjunct to PPI for nocturnal GORD
- Slower onset (30–60 min) vs immediate relief with antacids

**Step 3: Proton-pump inhibitor (PPI)** — first-line for moderate-severe GORD
- **Standard dose**:
  - Omeprazole 20 mg daily or BD (most common; least expensive)
  - Lansoprazole 30 mg daily or BD
  - Pantoprazole 40 mg daily or BD
  - Esomeprazole 20 mg daily or BD
- **Efficacy**: 80–90% symptom relief with PPI; superior to H2 antagonists
- **Onset**: 3–7 days for symptom improvement; full effect at 4 weeks
- **Duration**: maintain 4–8 weeks, then assess need for continuation
- **Long-term safety concerns**:
  - **Hypomagnesaemia** (PPI-induced, rare but can cause arrhythmias): check magnesium if on PPI >1 year
  - **Vitamin B12 deficiency**: achlorhydria impairs B12 absorption; rare but monitor if on PPI >1 year
  - **Calcium absorption**: increased fracture risk with long-term PPI (mechanism unclear)
  - **Clostridium difficile**: increased risk with PPI use; use lowest effective dose
  - **Chronic kidney disease**: possible association (evidence mixed)
  - **Pneumonia**: small increased risk (not absolute contraindication)

**Dose adjustment**
- If standard dose inadequate after 4 weeks: increase to BD dosing (e.g. omeprazole 20 mg BD)
- If BD dose fails: switch PPI type (esomeprazole, lansoprazole) or refer for investigation
- Review need for PPI every 6–12 months; step down to lowest effective dose

**Step down/stopping**
- Once symptoms controlled (4–8 weeks): attempt step down to lower dose or less frequent dosing
- Some patients require long-term low-dose PPI; others can stop after period of control
- Rebound hyperacidity may occur; advise patient of possible temporary worsening if stopping

**Refractory GORD** (inadequate symptom relief despite PPI or high-dose therapy)
- **Causes**:
  - Poor adherence (assess with pharmacy refill data)
  - Inadequate PPI dose (step up to BD dosing first)
  - Wrong diagnosis (consider IBS, functional dyspepsia, achalasia)
  - Bile reflux (PPI doesn't suppress bile; may need cholestyramine or ursodeoxycholic acid, specialist input)
  - H. pylori infection (rare but check if not recently tested)
- **Investigations**:
  - Repeat endoscopy if alarm symptoms or prolonged course
  - pH monitoring to confirm reflux correlating with symptoms
- **Specialist referral** for diagnostic clarity and management options

**Surgical option: Laparoscopic fundoplication**
- **Indication**: refractory GORD with documented pathological reflux, patient preference for surgery, young age (to avoid lifelong PPI)
- **Procedure**: wraps fundus around LES to increase pressure; 90% effective initially but ~30% symptom recurrence by 5 years
- **Complications**: postoperative dysphagia, gas bloating, inability to vomit/belch
- **Not standard first-line** (medical therapy preferred initially)

### Special populations

**Pregnancy**
- GORD affects 30–50% of pregnant women (hormonal effects, mechanical pressure)
- **Safe treatments**: antacids, alginates, H2 antagonists (ranitidine)
- **PPI safety**: generally considered safe (omeprazole, lansoprazole most data); risk-benefit favours treatment if needed
- Avoid: NSAIDs (teratogenic, especially third trimester), metoclopramide (limited safety data)

**Elderly patients**
- More severe GORD, higher complication risk (peptic ulcer, stricture, Barrett's)
- Lower threshold for endoscopy and PPI therapy
- Monitor for drug interactions (PPIs inhibit CYP2C19, affecting warfarin, clopidogrel, others)
- PPI-associated fracture risk higher; ensure calcium/vitamin D sufficiency

**Post-bariatric surgery**
- Increased reflux risk post-bypass surgery (altered anatomy, reduced LES function)
- PPI therapy often needed long-term
- Surgical revision may be necessary if severe refractory reflux

### Complications of GORD

**Oesophagitis and mucosal damage**
- Erosions progress to ulceration if untreated
- Risk of scarring and stricture formation (dysphagia develops)
- Bleeding risk (usually minor; massive haemorrhage rare)

**Barrett's oesophagus**
- Develops in 10–15% with chronic GORD
- Increased adenocarcinoma risk: 0.1–0.3% annually
- **Screening**: endoscopy with biopsies if GORD >10 years, age >45, male, smoking history
- **Surveillance protocol if Barrett's confirmed**:
  - **Non-dysplastic**: repeat OGD in 1 year, then 2–3 yearly if persistent
  - **Low-grade dysplasia (LGD)**: repeat OGD at 6 weeks to confirm (dysplasia can be focal), then 6–12 monthly
  - **High-grade dysplasia (HGD)**: urgent referral for endoscopic therapy (radiofrequency ablation, endoscopic mucosal resection) ± surveillance endoscopy

**Peptic ulcer disease**
- GORD predisposes to ulcer formation (acid exposure); H. pylori and NSAIDs major cofactors

**Stricture formation**
- Chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis and narrowing
- Dysphagia develops (solids > liquids)
- Requires endoscopic dilatation; recurrence common without maintenance PPI

**Adenocarcinoma**
- Risk increased 2–5 fold with GORD; absolute risk still low (~1/1000 per year)
- Prevention: PPI reduces adenocarcinoma risk; eradication of H. pylori reduces gastric cancer risk
- Early detection: prompt investigation of alarm symptoms

### Red flags and urgent referral

- **Dysphagia**: difficulty swallowing solids; suggests stricture or malignancy → endoscopy
- **Weight loss**: unintentional, progressive → endoscopy and investigation
- **Persistent vomiting**: suggests gastric outlet obstruction or peptic ulcer → endoscopy
- **Gastrointestinal bleeding**: haematemesis or melaena → urgent endoscopy
- **Anaemia**: Hb <110 g/L; iron deficiency → colonoscopy to exclude lower GI source, endoscopy for upper
- **Severe reflux despite maximum therapy**: refractory GORD → pH monitoring, specialist assessment
- **Acute severe chest pain**: mimic of angina; EKG and cardiac assessment needed to exclude ACS before attributing to GORD

### Monitoring and follow-up

**After PPI initiation**
- Review at 4 weeks: assess symptom control, medication adherence
- If adequate relief: continue PPI; plan reassessment in 2–3 months
- If inadequate relief: increase to BD dosing or switch PPI type; refer if still uncontrolled

**Long-term management**
- Annual review of need for PPI (attempt step down or discontinuation)
- Screen for B12 and magnesium deficiency if on PPI >1 year
- Monitor bone health; ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D
- Remind of red flags and need to return if alarm symptoms develop

**Chronic GORD surveillance** (if GORD >10 years)
- Endoscopy with biopsy to screen for Barrett's oesophagus
- If Barrett's confirmed: follow surveillance protocol based on dysplasia grade
