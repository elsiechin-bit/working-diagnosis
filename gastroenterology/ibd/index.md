## Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)—comprising Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis—is a chronic relapsing-remitting disorder characterised by immune-mediated intestinal inflammation. Primary care plays a crucial role in early recognition, initial investigation, and ongoing monitoring of these complex conditions.

## Definition and epidemiology

IBD comprises two main conditions:

**Ulcerative colitis (UC)**
Continuous mucosal inflammation limited to the colon and rectum. Affects 100–200 per 100,000 in developed countries.

**Crohn's disease (CD)**
Transmural inflammation affecting any part of the GI tract (mouth to anus), characteristically with skip lesions (patchy involvement). Affects 50–100 per 100,000.

Peak incidence is 15–35 years, with secondary peak 60–70 years. More common in developed nations and Nordic/Western European ancestry. Genetic susceptibility (NOD2, IL23R mutations) combined with environmental triggers (smoking, diet, dysbiosis, infections) underpins pathogenesis.

## Pathophysiology

Loss of intestinal barrier function, dysbiosis (altered bacterial flora), and aberrant mucosal immune response (excessive Th17 and Th1 cell activation) drive chronic inflammation. In UC, inflammation is limited to mucosa and submucosa. In CD, transmural inflammation causes fibrosis, strictures, fistulas, and abscesses.

Smoking worsens CD but paradoxically improves UC. NSAIDs may exacerbate disease. Stress and infections trigger flares.

## Clinical presentation

**Ulcerative colitis**
Diarrhoea (often bloody), abdominal cramps, urgency, and tenesmus are cardinal. Disease is continuous from rectum proximally. Mild disease: <4 stools/day, no systemic symptoms. Moderate: 4–6 stools/day, some systemic symptoms (malaise, fever). Severe: >6 stools/day, fever, tachycardia, anaemia, weight loss, and signs of toxicity.

**Crohn's disease**
Diarrhoea (usually non-bloody unless terminal ileum involved), abdominal pain (often RLQ if ileitis), weight loss, and systemic symptoms (fever, malaise). Non-obstructive abdominal pain may suggest strictures; abscess formation presents with localised tenderness and fever. Fistulas (perianal, enterocutaneous) are characteristic.

**Extraintestinal manifestations**
Joint involvement (peripheral arthritis in 20%, axial spondylitis in 5–10%), skin lesions (erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum), ocular disease (uveitis, episcleritis), hepatobiliary disease (PSC in 5% of UC), and aphthous ulcers are common. Some manifest before bowel symptoms.

## Diagnosis

**Clinical presentation**
Bloody diarrhoea with cramping pain, urgency, and systemic symptoms in a young patient raises suspicion for IBD.

**Colonoscopy and histology**
Gold standard. Colonoscopy shows continuous mucosal inflammation in UC (erythema, granularity, ulceration, loss of vascular pattern) versus patchy involvement with skip lesions in CD. Biopsy demonstrates characteristic histology: chronic inflammation (lymphocytes, plasma cells), crypt distortion, and crypt abscesses. In CD, non-caseating granulomas are present in 30–50% of cases.

**Laboratory investigations**
Full blood count detects anaemia (iron deficiency from blood loss or inflammation-induced anaemia). White cell count and platelets are elevated in active disease. Urea and electrolytes assess dehydration and renal function. LFTs assess hepatic involvement and nutritional status (albumin). CRP and ESR are elevated in active disease but are non-specific. Faecal calprotectin (>50 μg/g suggests inflammation) is useful for screening and monitoring disease activity; elevated in IBD but also in other intestinal inflammatory conditions (infection, polyps).

Serological markers (ANCA in UC, anti-CBP in CD) are supportive but not diagnostic.

## Severity classification

**Mild-moderate UC/CD**
<4 stools/day, no fever, normal CRP, Hb >100 g/L, albumin >30 g/L, no weight loss.

**Moderate-severe UC/CD**
4–6 stools/day, low-grade fever, CRP 20–50 mg/L, anaemia, or mild weight loss.

**Severe UC/CD**
>6 stools/day, fever, CRP >50 mg/L, severe anaemia (Hb <80 g/L), weight loss >10%, or signs of systemic toxicity. Severe UC with toxic features (fever, tachycardia, abdominal distension, >6 stools/day) risks toxic megacolon—admit urgently.

## Management

**Mild-moderate ulcerative colitis**
5-ASA (mesalamine/sulfasalazine): mesalamine 800 mg three times daily (orally for left-sided, enema/suppository for proctitis) induces remission in 50–60% of mild-moderate cases. Allow 4 weeks for response assessment. Maintenance therapy continues to prevent relapse.

**Moderate-severe disease or inadequate response to 5-ASA**
Corticosteroids: prednisolone 40 mg daily (tapered over 8–12 weeks) induces remission in 70–80%. For severe colitis, admit for IV hydrocortisone 100 mg four times daily. Once remission achieved (usually 2–4 weeks), taper and transition to maintenance therapy.

**Maintenance therapy and biologic escalation**
Once remission achieved, maintenance therapy prevents relapse. 5-ASA (mesalamine 800 mg three times daily) or immunosuppressants (azathioprine 1.5–2.5 mg/kg daily, 6-mercaptopurine 1–1.5 mg/kg daily) are standard. Modern practice increasingly favours early biologic therapy for moderate-severe disease or frequent relapsers. TNF inhibitors (infliximab, adalimumab, golimumab) induce and maintain remission in 50–60% of patients. Vedolizumab (anti-integrin) or ustekinumab (anti-IL-12/IL-23) are alternatives for TNF-refractory disease. All biologics require TB screening, vaccination, and monitoring.

**Crohn's disease management**
Similar escalation: 5-ASA for mild disease (less effective than in UC), corticosteroids for moderate-severe, and immunosuppressants or biologics for maintenance. Biologic therapy is more frequently needed in CD; TNF inhibitors are first-line, followed by vedolizumab or ustekinumab. Perianal disease (fistulas, abscesses) requires antibiotics (metronidazole, ciprofloxacin), drainage if needed, and biologics for durable closure.

**Specialist care**
All newly diagnosed IBD requires gastroenterology referral for biologic therapy optimization, surveillance protocols, and monitoring. Ongoing monitoring includes annual assessment for complications, malignancy screening (colorectal cancer surveillance colonoscopy every 1–2 years after 8–10 years disease duration, earlier if extensive disease or primary sclerosing cholangitis).

## Complications

**Acute severe colitis and toxic megacolon**
Fever, abdominal pain, distension, tachycardia, and >6 stools/day suggest severe colitis. Toxic megacolon (colonic dilatation >6 cm on plain X-ray) is a medical emergency with risk of perforation. Manage with IV fluids, high-dose IV corticosteroids, cessation of antimotility agents, and intensive monitoring. Surgery (colectomy) may be required if unresponsive.

**Perforation**
Free perforation presents as acute abdomen and peritonitis; requires emergency surgery.

**Severe bleeding**
Blood loss from mucosal inflammation is usually modest. Massive bleeding is rare but requires transfusion and colonoscopic or angiographic intervention.

**Strictures and obstruction (CD)**
Chronic inflammation and fibrosis cause stricturing. Presents with abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Management is medical (biologic therapy to reduce inflammation) or surgical (strictureplasty or resection) if refractory.

**Fistulas and abscesses (CD)**
Perianal fistulas are common in CD. Complex fistulas are difficult to manage; antibiotics, biologics, and/or surgical drainage are required.

**Colorectal cancer**
Increased risk after 8–10 years disease duration, especially with extensive colitis or primary sclerosing cholangitis. Surveillance colonoscopy every 1–2 years is recommended; manage dysplasia if found.

**Extraintestinal complications**
Arthritis, uveitis, and primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) require specialist management. PSC increases cholangiocarcinoma risk and may progress to liver failure.

## Monitoring and follow-up

At diagnosis, establish baseline disease severity and location via colonoscopy. Assess for complications and extraintestinal manifestations. Arrange gastroenterology referral for initiation of appropriate therapy, including biologic therapy if indicated.

At 4–8 weeks post-treatment initiation, assess symptom response, CRP/calprotectin, and FBC. Adjust therapy if inadequate response.

Annual review includes clinical assessment, endoscopic surveillance for UC (if indicated), monitoring for complications, medication adherence, and side effects. Screen for osteoporosis (DEXA if prolonged corticosteroid use), B12 deficiency (if terminal ileum involved in CD), and depression (increased risk in IBD).

## Special populations

**Pregnancy**
Many IBD drugs are safe in pregnancy (5-ASA, corticosteroids, azathioprine, most biologics). Disease activity rather than treatment poses the greater risk to fetal outcomes. Preconception counselling and specialist input are recommended. Avoid certain antibiotics (metronidazole if possible, tetracyclines).

**Older adults**
IBD is increasing in older patients; diagnosis may be delayed. Manage as per younger patients, but monitor for drug interactions and comorbidities.

## Field notes

IBD is a spectrum disease; diagnosis requires histology from colonoscopy. Early recognition and referral to specialist care improves outcomes—do not delay. Biologic therapy has transformed IBD management; modern practice favours early use in moderate-severe disease. Extraintestinal manifestations often precede bowel symptoms; maintain high suspicion. Patients are at risk for depression and anxiety; screen regularly and support mental health. Smoking cessation in CD is crucial; support cessation efforts strongly.
