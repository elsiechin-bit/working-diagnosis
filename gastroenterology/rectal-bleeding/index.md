## Rectal Bleeding

Rectal bleeding is one of the most common presentations in primary care, with >80% of cases due to benign anorectal causes. However, systematic assessment is essential to identify the minority with colorectal malignancy or other serious pathology.


## Definition and epidemiology

Rectal bleeding is visible blood per rectum (bright red on toilet paper or in stool) or occult blood detected on faecal occult blood testing. Prevalence is high; up to 20% of adults report rectal bleeding at some point. Most (80–90%) is due to benign anorectal causes (haemorrhoids, anal fissure). Colorectal cancer accounts for 5–10% of cases presenting with bleeding, with higher rates in those >50 years or with alarm features.

## Aetiology

**Benign causes (90% of bleeding)**
Haemorrhoids (internal or external, prolapsing), anal fissure (often post-defecation pain and spotting), diverticular disease, angiodysplasia (small vascular lesions, particularly in elderly), polyps (benign or premalignant), and traumatic injury (from instrumentation or sexual activity).

**Malignant causes (5–10%)**
Colorectal cancer, with higher prevalence in age >50, family history of colorectal cancer, or alarm symptoms.

**Inflammatory causes**
Inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis or Crohn's disease), infectious colitis (bacterial, viral, parasitic), or diverticulitis.

## Clinical assessment

**History**
Onset and duration of bleeding (acute vs chronic, persistent vs intermittent). Character (bright red on tissue vs mixed in stool vs dark blood). Associated symptoms (pain at defecation suggests fissure; pain-free bleeding suggests haemorrhoids or higher lesion; diarrhoea/mucus/urgency suggest IBD; constitutional symptoms suggest malignancy).

Ask about alarm symptoms: age, weight loss, change in bowel habit (constipation to diarrhoea or vice versa), family history of colorectal cancer, anaemic symptoms (tiredness, dyspnoea), or prolonged bleeding (>2–4 weeks).

Medications: NSAIDs increase bleeding risk (especially if underlying pathology); anticoagulants or antiplatelets may amplify bleeding.

**Examination**
Inspect perianal skin for haemorrhoids, fissures, skin tags, or lesions. Digital rectal examination assesses anal tone, detects masses, and may trigger pain (fissure) or discomfort (haemorrhoids). Note blood on examining finger, stool consistency, and any masses.

**Investigations**
Full blood count detects anaemia from chronic blood loss. Mean corpuscular volume (MCV) <80 suggests iron deficiency; serum iron, ferritin, and TIBC confirm iron deficiency anaemia. Urea and electrolytes assess renal function.

Flexible sigmoidoscopy visualises the rectum and lower 60 cm of colon, allowing direct identification of haemorrhoids, fissures, polyps, masses, or colitis. Sigmoidoscopy is first-line in primary care if available and is acceptable if no alarm features and age <50.

Colonoscopy visualises the entire colon and allows biopsy or polypectomy. Indicated if alarm features present, age >50, or when flexible sigmoidoscopy suggests proximal pathology (e.g., polyp or mass) or is inconclusive.

Faecal occult blood test (FOBT) is positive in ~50% of colorectal cancer cases and has limited sensitivity; not a substitute for direct visualisation.

## Management by cause

**Haemorrhoids (internal)**
Bright red bleeding on toilet paper or splashing in pan, pain-free or minimal discomfort. Internal haemorrhoids are above the dentate line (not visible externally unless prolapsing). Conservative management: increase fibre (25–35 g/day), hydration, stool softeners (macrogol), and topical preparations (hydrocortisone 1% cream for inflammation/itching). Ligation (rubber band) or sclerotherapy performed by specialist reduces bleeding if conservative measures fail. Surgery (haemorrhoidectomy) is reserved for severe or refractory cases.

**External haemorrhoids**
Visible, palpable lumps at anal margin; often painful if thrombosed. Conservative management as above. Thrombosed external haemorrhoid causing severe pain may require evacuation of clot under local anaesthetic for relief.

**Anal fissure**
Acute severe pain with defecation, often with visible blood spotting. Small tear in anal epithelium. Conservative: increase fibre, hydration, stool softeners. Topical anaesthetic (lignocaine 5%) and corticosteroid cream (hydrocortisone 1%) aid healing. Calcium channel blocker (diltiazem 2% cream) or nitrate cream (glyceryl trinitrate 0.2% cream) increases blood flow and promotes healing; apply twice daily. Most heal within 4–6 weeks. Chronic fissure unresponsive to topical therapy may require referral for botulinum toxin injection or lateral internal sphincterotomy.

**Diverticular disease**
Usually painless bleeding, may be brisk. Managed per diverticular disease topic. Colonoscopy excludes other sources.

**Angiodysplasia (vascular malformations)**
Small red lesions in right colon; more common in elderly or patients with chronic kidney disease. Usually painless bleeding. May be multiple. Treated with endoscopic thermal coagulation or argon plasma coagulation.

**Polyps**
Bleeding is often occult (detected on FOBT) or minor spotting. Colonoscopy with biopsy and polypectomy is curative. Surveillance colonoscopy intervals depend on polyp pathology and number.

**Colorectal cancer**
Presents with bleeding (often painless), change in bowel habit, weight loss, or anaemia. Colonoscopy with biopsy confirms diagnosis. Urgent oncology referral for staging and treatment.

**Inflammatory bowel disease**
Bloody diarrhoea with cramping pain suggests IBD. Colonoscopy with biopsy confirms diagnosis. Management per IBD topic.

## Decision-making: when to investigate further

**Age <50 without alarm features, painless bright red bleeding, normal DRE**
Likely haemorrhoids. Flexible sigmoidoscopy in primary care confirms if available. Conservative management. Refer if symptoms persist >4 weeks or worsen.

**Age <50 with alarm features (weight loss, anaemia, family history, change in bowel habit, prolonged bleeding)**
Colonoscopy referral.

**Age >50 with rectal bleeding**
Colonoscopy is indicated (colorectal cancer risk is 5–10% even with apparently benign bleeding).

**Any age with alarm features**
Urgent colonoscopy.

## Monitoring and follow-up

For benign causes treated conservatively, reassess at 4 weeks. If bleeding persists, consider further investigation if not already done. Document that alarm features are absent and colonoscopy can be deferred if young and reassured.

For patients with alarm features or findings of polyps, masses, or colitis, refer for appropriate specialist management and surveillance.

For iron deficiency anaemia, recheck Hb at 3 months after source identified and treatment initiated. Aim for Hb >120 g/L in women, >130 g/L in men.

## Special populations

**Older adults (≥65 years)**
Malignancy risk is higher; colonoscopy is indicated for any new-onset bleeding. Angiodysplasia and diverticular disease are more common. Assess comorbidities before colonoscopy; may need anaesthetic assessment if frail.

**Anticoagulated or antiplatelet therapy**
Patients on warfarin, DOACs, aspirin, or clopidogrel have increased bleeding risk. Assess for sinister causes (do not assume bleeding is medication-related). Continue anticoagulation/antiplatelet therapy during investigation unless explicitly stopped by cardiologist/stroke specialist.

**Pregnancy**
Rectal bleeding is common due to haemorrhoids and constipation from progesterone. Reassure; conservative management (fibre, hydration, stool softeners) is safe. Avoid invasive investigation unless alarm features; defer colonoscopy unless essential.

## Field notes

Never assume rectal bleeding is from haemorrhoids, particularly in age >50 or with alarm features. Colonoscopy is the key investigation; ensure access is timely for those with warning signs. Painful bleeding post-defecation is typically fissure; painless bright red bleeding on tissue is usually haemorrhoids, but colonoscopy remains indicated if age >50. Many GPs miss colorectal cancer because they reassure patients prematurely; maintain high suspicion and refer liberally. Iron deficiency anaemia from bleeding demands investigation to identify and treat source; do not treat anaemia alone without identifying cause.
