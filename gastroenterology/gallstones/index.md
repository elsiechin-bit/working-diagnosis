## Gallstones and Biliary Colic

Gallstones affect 10–15% of the developed population, increasing with age, female sex, and obesity. Most are asymptomatic and require no intervention. Symptomatic biliary colic—acute right upper quadrant pain triggered by stone obstruction of the cystic duct—requires management. Complications (acute cholecystitis, cholangitis, pancreatitis) are relatively uncommon but serious.

## Definition and epidemiology

Gallstones are cholesterol or pigment concretions within the gallbladder. Asymptomatic gallstones (cholelithiasis) are detected incidentally on imaging done for other reasons; prevalence is 10–15% population-wide, rising to 30% in those >70 years. Female sex, obesity, rapid weight loss, and age increase risk (risk factors remembered as "4 Fs": female, fat, forty, fertile). Symptomatic biliary colic occurs in 1–3% of those with asymptomatic gallstones annually. Complications (cholecystitis, cholangitis, pancreatitis) occur in <1% annually but are serious.

## Pathophysiology

Cholesterol supersaturation in bile precipitates cholesterol crystals, which aggregate to form gallstones. Gallbladder dysmotility contributes. Stones are clinically silent until they obstruct the cystic duct, triggering pain (biliary colic). Persistent obstruction causes gallbladder inflammation (acute cholecystitis). Migration into the common bile duct causes cholangitis (if infected) or jaundice. Migration into the pancreatic duct triggers pancreatitis.

## Clinical presentation

**Asymptomatic gallstones**
No symptoms; discovered incidentally on imaging. Most remain asymptomatic indefinitely; no treatment is needed.

**Biliary colic**
Sudden severe right upper quadrant or epigastric pain, often nocturnal (2–4 hours after eating, especially fatty meal). Duration 30 minutes to several hours; typically resolves spontaneously. Pain may radiate to back or right shoulder. Associated nausea is common; vomiting is less common. Examination may reveal right upper quadrant tenderness but no peritonism; vital signs are normal.

**Acute cholecystitis**
Prolonged RUQ pain (>6 hours), persistent fever (>38°C), right upper quadrant tenderness with guarding or rebound suggesting peritoneal irritation. Tachycardia and tachypnoea may be present. History may include recent biliary colic. Complications include perforation (risk increases after 72 hours of obstruction) or gangrene.

**Cholangitis**
Fever + jaundice + RUQ pain (Charcot's triad); may progress to altered mental status and hypotension (sepsis). Suggests bile duct obstruction with infection. Medical emergency.

**Acute pancreatitis**
Severe epigastric pain radiating to back, persistent vomiting, elevated amylase/lipase. Associated with gallstone migration into pancreatic duct.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Onset and location of pain. Temporal pattern (acute vs chronic, time of day). Radiation. Associated symptoms (nausea, vomiting, fever). Meals preceding pain (especially fatty). Prior episodes. Risk factors (female, obesity, rapid weight loss, age >40).

**Examination**
Vital signs assess hemodynamic stability. Jaundice (suggests obstruction or advanced disease). Abdominal examination: right upper quadrant tenderness (Murphy's sign—inspiratory arrest on palpation of RUQ during inspiration suggests cholecystitis). Peritonism (suggests peritoneal involvement—cholecystitis, perforation).

**Investigations**
Abdominal ultrasound is first-line: demonstrates gallstones (echogenic foci with acoustic shadow), gallbladder wall thickening or pericholecystic fluid (cholecystitis), and common bile duct dilatation (obstruction). Ultrasound is >95% sensitive for stones.

LFTs assess for obstruction or hepatic involvement. Elevated GGT and ALP suggest obstruction; elevated bilirubin confirms cholestasis. Mildly elevated transaminases are common. Markedly elevated amylase or lipase suggests pancreatitis.

WBC count assesses for infection. Elevated WBC suggests cholecystitis or cholangitis.

ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography) is reserved for confirmed or strongly suspected common bile duct stones with obstruction (jaundice, dilated bile duct on imaging). MRCP (magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography) is non-invasive alternative for suspected duct obstruction.

## Management

**Asymptomatic gallstones**
No treatment needed. Routine surveillance is not recommended; patients should be counseled to report symptoms if they develop. Cholecystectomy is generally not recommended unless specific high-risk situations exist (porcelain gallbladder, gallstones >3 cm, solitary stone in sickle cell patients due to increased hemolysis).

**Biliary colic**
Analgesia: paracetamol 1 g four times daily or ibuprofen 400 mg three times daily (if no contraindication). Avoid NSAIDs if pancreatitis suspected. Hydration and rest. Pain typically resolves spontaneously within hours.

Counsel patient on triggers (fatty foods) to avoid future episodes.

**Recurrent biliary colic**
Offered elective cholecystectomy (open or laparoscopic) if symptoms recur frequently and significantly impact quality of life. Laparoscopic approach is standard; conversion to open surgery is needed in ~5% due to adhesions or anatomy.

Preoperative assessment includes imaging to confirm anatomy and exclude common bile duct stones.

**Acute cholecystitis**
Admit for IV hydration, fasting, and IV antibiotics (cephalosporin such as cefotaxime 2 g TDS + metronidazole 500 mg TDS, or piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g TDS). Imaging (ultrasound or CT) confirms diagnosis. Most cases are managed conservatively; acute cholecystectomy is performed if patient deteriorates or peritonitis develops. Delayed cholecystectomy (after inflammation resolves, typically 6–12 weeks) is increasingly performed for selected uncomplicated cases.

**Cholangitis**
Urgent admission. IV antibiotics, IV hydration, and ERCP with sphincterotomy and stone extraction are standard. Antibiotics: same as cholecystitis. ERCP achieves ductal drainage and reduces sepsis. High mortality if untreated; prompt recognition and referral are essential.

**Pancreatitis**
Admit for IV hydration, fasting, analgesia, and monitoring. Measure amylase and lipase. Imaging assesses pancreatic inflammation. Admit to high dependency care if severe (APACHE >6, organ failure). ERCP is reserved for patients with evidence of persisting biliary obstruction (jaundice, dilated duct) or cholangitis.

## Monitoring and follow-up

For acute biliary colic resolved with conservative management: reassure patient. Advise on symptom triggers. Consider referral for cholecystectomy if recurrent pain.

For patients undergoing cholecystectomy: routine preoperative assessment. Post-operative follow-up at 2 weeks; most resume normal activity within 4 weeks (laparoscopic) or 6–8 weeks (open).

## Complications

**Acute cholecystitis**
Inflamed, thickened gallbladder wall, often with pericholecystic fluid. Risk of perforation increases after 72 hours; can lead to bile peritonitis and sepsis.

**Cholangitis**
Bile duct obstruction with infection. Presents as fever, jaundice, RUQ pain. Medical emergency; untreated mortality approaches 50%.

**Acute pancreatitis**
Gallstone migrating into pancreatic duct triggers inflammation. Severity ranges from mild (amylase mildly elevated) to severe (organ failure, necrosis). See pancreatitis topic for management.

**Gallstone ileus**
Rare (1–4% of bowel obstruction): stone erosion into duodenum creates fistula; stone lodges in distal ileum causing obstruction. Presents as small bowel obstruction. Diagnosis by imaging (pneumobilia and stone visible). Requires surgery.

**Mirizzi syndrome**
Stone in cystic duct compresses common bile duct, causing jaundice without duct stone. Diagnosed on imaging; may require surgery or ERCP.

## Special populations

**Pregnancy**
Gallstones are more common in pregnancy due to increased cholesterol saturation and gallbladder stasis. Biliary colic in pregnancy is managed conservatively; surgery is performed only for acute cholecystitis or uncontrolled symptoms (risk of preterm labour). Timing of surgery is ideally second trimester if needed.

**Rapid weight loss**
Post-bariatric surgery or very low-calorie diet increases gallstone formation. Ursodeoxycholic acid (300 mg TDS) reduces stone formation in high-risk patients; evidence is modest.

**Cirrhosis**
Gallstones are more common due to altered bile composition. Generally asymptomatic; cholecystectomy is avoided due to surgical risk in cirrhotic patients.

## Field notes

Most gallstones are incidental and asymptomatic; do not screen for or treat them. Biliary colic is self-limited; analgesia and reassurance suffice. Recurrent episodes warrant consideration of elective cholecystectomy. Acute cholecystitis is a surgical emergency; high fever, peritonism, and prolonged obstruction increase risk of perforation. Cholangitis is more dangerous; fever + jaundice + RUQ pain = urgent ERCP. Pancreatitis triggered by gallstones requires hospital admission and monitoring. Post-cholecystectomy diarrhoea occurs in 10–15%; usually self-limited. Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is safe and standard; open surgery is rarely needed.
