Travel Medicine

Pre-travel consults, prophylaxis, vaccines, and returning traveller assessment for New Zealand general practice.

Travel Medicine

Going somewhere?

A working reference for pre-travel consults and returning traveller assessment — built for the NZ general practice consultation, not the airport clinic.

New Zealanders travel frequently and widely — to the Pacific, Southeast Asia, South Asia, South America, sub-Saharan Africa, and beyond. Travel medicine is one of the few areas in general practice where a consultation that happens weeks before any illness can prevent serious harm. The pre-travel consult also tends to be the consultation where a patient is healthy, motivated, and actually listening.

This section covers the frameworks and drug choices you'll reach for most. For destination-specific vaccine requirements and the full NZ-funded travel vaccine table, see the Vaccines pillar. For country-specific malaria risk maps and prophylaxis tables, the NZF and CDC Travelers' Health are the authoritative real-time references — check them at the time of consultation, not from memory.

The pre-travel consultation

Ideally, patients present at least 4–6 weeks before departure — enough time to start a multi-dose vaccine course and allow seroconversion. For complex itineraries (multi-country, remote, immunocompromised traveller), 3 months is better. In practice, many patients present the week before they leave. Work with what you have: prioritise single-dose vaccines and chemoprophylaxis, and document the timing constraint clearly.

The consultation has two parts that run in parallel: risk assessment and risk reduction. The risk assessment shapes everything else — a two-week beach resort in Bali and a six-week overland trip through rural India require completely different responses, even though both might be described by the patient as “SE Asia.”

✦ Risk assessment — things to establish

Domain What to ask
Itinerary Exact countries and regions visited, not just the country name. Duration. Urban vs rural. Whether visiting friends and relatives (VFR travellers have higher TD, typhoid, and malaria risk than tourists).
Activities Trekking (altitude?), fresh-water exposure (schistosomiasis, leptospirosis), animal contact (rabies), cycling or motorbike (injury risk), remote areas (access to healthcare?), sexual health context.
Accommodation Air-conditioned hotel vs local guesthouses vs camping significantly changes insect vector exposure and food safety.
Traveller health Immunosuppression, pregnancy, chronic disease (cardiac, respiratory, renal), current medications (interactions with prophylaxis agents), prior vaccination history, allergies (sulfonamide — relevant to atovaquone-proguanil).
Purpose Tourist, VFR, missionary, aid/volunteer work, business. Purpose shapes exposure pattern substantially.
Practicalities Travel insurance (medical evacuation? pre-existing conditions?), healthcare access at destination, what medications to carry in case of illness.

Beyond the clinical checklist, the pre-travel consultation is an opportunity to check that routine vaccines are up to date — particularly MMR (a common gap in NZ adults born between the mid-1960s and early 1990s), and tetanus/diphtheria/pertussis. With measles circulating globally at elevated levels, this check is worth making explicit even when the patient presents solely for a malaria prescription.

All travel vaccines in NZ are unfunded — patients pay privately for vaccine and administration, unless the vaccine is on the funded schedule for another indication (e.g. influenza for those aged 65 and over). Frame this expectation early; some patients book the consult assuming the state covers travel vaccines the same way it covers their childhood schedule.

Malaria prophylaxis

Malaria remains a genuine life-threatening risk for NZ travellers to sub-Saharan Africa, Papua New Guinea, and parts of South and Southeast Asia. The key principle is simple: a febrile returned traveller from a malaria-endemic area has malaria until proven otherwise. Everything else in this section exists to make that scenario less likely.

Prophylaxis decisions are driven by the destination’s Plasmodium species profile and local drug resistance patterns. Always verify current recommendations against the NZF or CDC country tables at the time of consultation — resistance patterns shift, and relying on memory or a table more than a year old is genuinely risky.

✦ Chemoprophylaxis options — comparison

Drug Dosing Suitable for Key cautions Notes
Atovaquone-proguanil
(Malarone®)		 Daily. Start 1–2 days before entering endemic area; continue 7 days after leaving. All regions including chloroquine-resistant and mefloquine-resistant areas (SE Asia borders). Short trips (low total cost). Avoid in pregnancy (limited data) and if eGFR <30. Not for children <5 kg. Most expensive option. Good GI tolerability if taken with food. Preferred for short trips. Paediatric formulation available.
Doxycycline Daily. Start 1–2 days before; continue 4 weeks after leaving. All resistant regions. Cheapest option. Good for longer trips. Contraindicated in pregnancy and children under 8 years. Photosensitivity. Oesophageal ulceration if taken without water/lying down. Vaginal thrush. Take in the evening with a full glass of water, remain upright 30 min. Cheap and well-studied. Bonus: some activity against leptospirosis and rickettsial disease.
Mefloquine
(Lariam®)		 Weekly. Start 3 weeks before departure; continue 4 weeks after. Sub-Saharan Africa, parts of South Asia. Not recommended for SE Asia (high P. falciparum resistance in Thailand/Cambodia/Myanmar border regions). Contraindicated in seizure disorders, history of psychiatric illness, cardiac conduction abnormalities. Screen for neuropsychiatric history before prescribing. Neuropsychiatric side effects in 5–30%: vivid dreams, anxiety, depression, dizziness. Rarely, psychosis or seizures. Start 3 weeks before to detect neuropsychiatric intolerance before departure.
Chloroquine Weekly. Start 1–2 weeks before; continue 4 weeks after. Only for areas with documented chloroquine-sensitive malaria: Central America (north of the Panama Canal), parts of the Caribbean, some Middle East. Avoid in psoriasis (may exacerbate). Retinal toxicity at high cumulative doses (not relevant for short-term prophylaxis). Largely superseded for most NZ travellers due to widespread resistance. Verify NZF country table before recommending.

Counselling points regardless of agent: Chemoprophylaxis reduces risk but is not 100% protective. Bite prevention remains essential and complementary — DEET-containing repellent, permethrin-treated clothing, bed nets in endemic rural areas. Patients should seek urgent medical review and mention their travel history if they develop fever within 3 months of return.

P. vivax and P. ovale dormant liver stages are not covered by the agents above. Terminal prophylaxis with primaquine is occasionally indicated for travellers with significant vivax-endemic exposure who want to eliminate the hypnozoite reservoir. Check G6PD status before prescribing — primaquine causes haemolysis in G6PD deficiency. This is a specialist discussion for most NZ GPs; consider ID or travel medicine clinic input for at-risk patients.1

Traveller’s diarrhoea

Traveller’s diarrhoea (TD) affects 20–60% of travellers to high-risk regions within the first two weeks of travel, making it the most common travel-related illness.2 Defined as three or more loose stools per 24 hours with at least one accompanying symptom (nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, fever, or blood in stool), it is self-limiting in most cases but can disrupt trips substantially. Severe cases — particularly those with bloody diarrhoea or high fever — require more active management.

High-risk destinations: South Asia (India, Pakistan, Nepal, Bangladesh — highest risk), Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, the Middle East. Low-risk: Western Europe, North America, Australia, Japan. The Pacific island nations are generally intermediate-risk. Visiting friends and relatives in high-risk areas carries higher risk than tourist travel to the same regions, as food is more likely to be home-prepared without tourist-grade food safety practices.

Food and water safety: “Boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it.” Specifically: avoid tap water, ice in drinks, raw salads washed in tap water, unpeeled fruit, shellfish, and street food that isn’t freshly prepared and hot. Sealed bottled water and hot cooked food prepared to order are generally safe. This guidance is somewhat aspirational for VFR travellers eating at relatives’ homes.

Management: Oral rehydration is the cornerstone — commercial sachets (Gastrolyte, Hydralyte) or homemade ORS (1 litre clean water, 6 teaspoons sugar, half teaspoon salt). Loperamide is useful for symptomatic control in adults with watery, non-bloody diarrhoea without high fever. Do not use loperamide if there is bloody diarrhoea or fever above 38.5°C — these suggest invasive infection where motility inhibition is harmful.

Standby antibiotic prescribing is appropriate for travellers going to high-risk areas, particularly those for whom illness would be especially disruptive (remote trekking, expedition travel) or dangerous (immunocompromised patients).

✦ Standby antibiotics by region

Region Preferred agent Rationale
SE Asia (Thailand, Bali, Vietnam, Cambodia) Azithromycin 1 g stat, or 500 mg daily × 3 days High prevalence of fluoroquinolone-resistant Campylobacter in SE Asia. Azithromycin is preferred over ciprofloxacin for this region.
South Asia (India, Nepal, Pakistan) Azithromycin 500 mg daily × 3 days Fluoroquinolone resistance widespread. Azithromycin or rifaximin where available. Ciprofloxacin increasingly unreliable.
Africa, Latin America, Middle East Azithromycin 500 mg daily × 3 days, or ciprofloxacin 500 mg BD × 3 days Fluoroquinolone resistance lower than Asia. Either agent reasonable. Azithromycin preferred if mixed itinerary.
Severe / bloody / febrile Seek local medical care; do not treat with standby alone Dysentery (Shigella, Salmonella typhi) and amoebic colitis require assessment and culture before treatment where possible.

Dukoral (oral cholera vaccine, inactivated) provides approximately 60% short-term protection against ETEC — the most common cause of TD — in addition to cholera. Reasonable for travellers to very high-risk areas (relief workers, long-stay travellers to sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia). Two doses 1–6 weeks apart, completed at least one week before travel. ETEC protection wanes after approximately 3 months.3

Altitude sickness

Altitude illness encompasses three syndromes of increasing severity: acute mountain sickness (AMS), high altitude cerebral oedema (HACE), and high altitude pulmonary oedema (HAPE). All are caused by inadequate acclimatisation to the reduced partial pressure of oxygen at altitude, but HAPE and HACE are life-threatening emergencies requiring immediate descent, not watchful waiting.

NZ patients heading to altitude include trekkers in Nepal (Everest base camp trek reaches 5,364 m; Annapurna circuit peaks at ~5,416 m), visitors to Cusco (3,400 m) or La Paz (3,640 m), Kilimanjaro trekkers (5,895 m), and those travelling through Ladakh or the Tibetan plateau. Machu Picchu itself (2,430 m) rarely causes serious AMS, but the overland route through Cusco often does.

Recognising the syndromes

AMS presents as headache at altitude (typically above 2,500 m) plus at least one of: fatigue, dizziness, nausea/vomiting, or difficulty sleeping. The Lake Louise Score formalises this; a score of 3 or above with headache in the appropriate context confirms AMS. Symptoms typically emerge 6–12 hours after reaching altitude and resolve with acclimatisation over 12–48 hours if ascent stops.

HACE is AMS plus ataxia (test with tandem gait) or altered consciousness. This is a medical emergency. Do not attribute ataxia to tiredness or intoxication in a high-altitude context.

HAPE is the most lethal of the three syndromes and can occur without preceding AMS. Early features: decreased exercise tolerance and dry cough. Late features: dyspnoea at rest, pink frothy sputum, cyanosis, crackles. HAPE can evolve rapidly over hours. Any combination of dyspnoea at rest and cough at altitude should prompt immediate descent.

Prevention

Graded ascent is the cornerstone. Above 3,000 m: don’t increase sleeping altitude by more than 300–500 m per night, and take a rest day every 3 days. “Climb high, sleep low” reflects the fact that daytime excursions to higher altitude followed by return to a lower sleeping altitude are well-tolerated. Adequate hydration, avoiding alcohol in the first 24–48 hours at altitude, and avoiding sedatives (which blunt the hypoxic ventilatory response) all help.

Acetazolamide prophylaxis is appropriate when graded ascent is not possible (e.g. flying directly to Cusco or Lhasa) or when a patient has had significant AMS previously.

✦ Acetazolamide (Diamox) prescribing guide
Dose (prophylaxis) 125–250 mg twice daily. Start 24–48 hours before ascent; continue for 2 days after reaching maximum altitude.
Mechanism Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor → bicarbonate diuresis → mild metabolic acidosis → increased respiratory drive → improved oxygenation. Pharmacologically mimics acclimatisation.
Side effects Paraesthesia of fingers, toes, and lips is universal and doesn’t indicate toxicity. Polyuria (counsel on hydration). Altered taste of carbonated drinks (worth warning about). Rarely transient myopia.
Contraindications Sulfonamide allergy (exercise caution; cross-reactivity debated). Severe renal impairment. Use with caution with metformin (both affect renal tubular secretion).
Dose (treatment of AMS) 250 mg twice daily, plus stopping ascent and resting at current altitude. If symptoms worsen or HACE/HAPE features develop, immediate descent is required regardless of medication.

Emergency treatment for HACE and HAPE

For both HACE and HAPE, descent is the definitive treatment and must not be delayed for medication. Even 300–1,000 m of descent can be life-saving. For HACE where immediate descent is impossible: dexamethasone 8 mg IM/IV stat then 4 mg every 6 hours; portable hyperbaric chamber (Gamow bag) if available. For HAPE where descent is impossible: nifedipine 30 mg modified-release (then 30 mg 12-hourly), supplemental oxygen; sildenafil or tadalafil as second-line. These drugs buy time for descent — they do not substitute for it.4

Fit to fly

GPs are increasingly asked to certify fitness to fly — either proactively (a patient asks before a planned trip) or reactively (a patient recently hospitalised wants to know when they can travel). The relevant physiology: cabin pressure in commercial aircraft is maintained at an equivalent altitude of 1,800–2,400 m, meaning ambient oxygen partial pressure is approximately 75% of sea level. Healthy passengers tolerate this easily; certain conditions do not.

✦ Minimum interval before flying

Condition / event Minimum interval (general guidance)
Uncomplicated MI 3 days (short-haul, low-risk, symptom-free); 10 days (long-haul). Cardiology input for high-risk cases.
CABG 10–14 days minimum; cardiology sign-off required.
Stroke / TIA 3 days after TIA; 10 days after minor stroke. Secondary prevention must be optimised.
Pneumothorax Fully resolved on CXR. Minimum 7 days after radiological resolution. Some guidance states 6 weeks for spontaneous pneumothorax (recurrence risk).
Major abdominal surgery 10 days (intestinal gas expands at cabin altitude). Uncomplicated laparoscopic procedures: 24–48 hours.
Colonoscopy 24 hours (insufflated gas absorbs quickly).
Active pneumonia Not fit to fly until clinically resolved and afebrile. Consider in-flight O₂ needs for several weeks after recovery in significant parenchymal disease.
Severe COPD / chronic hypoxia Consider hypoxia inhalation test (HIT) if resting SpO₂ ≤95% or FEV₁ <50% predicted. Predicted in-flight PaO₂ <6.6 kPa requires supplemental oxygen — arrange with airline in advance.
Pregnancy Uncomplicated single pregnancy generally safe until 36 weeks. Most airlines request a letter from 28–32 weeks; some restrict travel after 35–36 weeks (international). Contraindicated with placenta praevia, severe pre-eclampsia, or active labour.

VTE risk and prevention in-flight

Long-haul flights (above 4 hours) approximately double the risk of VTE, with risk increasing per 2-hour increment above this threshold — the absolute risk in healthy low-risk travellers remains small (approximately 1 in 4,600 flights), but rises substantially with additional risk factors.5

For all long-haul travellers: encourage aisle seating and regular ambulation, maintain hydration, and avoid alcohol and sedatives. Below-knee graduated compression stockings (18–22 mmHg) reduce in-flight oedema and are reasonable for any long-haul traveller; evidence supports their use in reducing asymptomatic DVT.

For higher-risk passengers (prior DVT/PE, known thrombophilia, active malignancy, recent surgery, pregnancy, OCP/HRT plus additional risk factors): discuss LMWH prophylaxis — a single dose of enoxaparin (Clexane) 40 mg SC within 2 hours before a long-haul flight is the standard approach. Aspirin is not recommended as VTE prophylaxis for air travel based on current evidence. Stockings and ambulation apply regardless.

The returning traveller

The returned traveller with illness is a different consultation to the pre-travel one. The key discipline is resisting the temptation to anchor on exotic diagnoses — gastroenteritis is still usually gastroenteritis; respiratory tract infections are still usually viral. However, a subset of presentations require systematic thinking about what the destination makes possible, because missing malaria, typhoid, or viral haemorrhagic fever has serious consequences.

The first question in any febrile returned traveller from a malaria-endemic region is always: could this be malaria? Malaria can present with any fever pattern. The classical periodicity is often absent in early or partially treated disease. Order a blood film (thin and thick) plus a malaria rapid antigen test in parallel — sensitivity of a single film is approximately 75–90% for falciparum; if clinical suspicion remains and the first film is negative, repeat at 12–24 hour intervals, up to three times, before excluding.6

Incubation periods as a diagnostic tool

Knowing the time between return and symptom onset narrows the differential substantially.

✦ Incubation periods in returned travellers

Condition Incubation Key features / notes
Malaria (P. falciparum) 7–14 days (up to 3 months) Fever, rigors, headache, myalgia. Any severity from mild illness to cerebral malaria. Most symptomatic within 4 weeks of return. Sub-Saharan Africa is the dominant source region.
Malaria (P. vivax / P. ovale) 2 weeks to months; up to 1–2 years Dormant liver hypnozoites can reactivate months to years after travel. A returned traveller from SE Asia or the Pacific presenting with fever a year later can still have malaria.
Dengue fever 3–14 days High fever, severe headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia/arthralgia (“breakbone fever”), maculopapular rash. Thrombocytopenia and leukopenia on FBC. SE Asia and Latin America. Dengue NS1 antigen + serology.
Typhoid fever 6–30 days Stepwise rising fever, relative bradycardia, rose spots (rare), diarrhoea or constipation, splenomegaly. South Asia highest risk. Blood cultures ×3 before antibiotics; Widal test is insufficiently specific.
Rickettsial disease 1–14 days African tick bite fever (R. africae) is common in safari travellers. Fever, headache, myalgia, eschar at bite site, regional lymphadenopathy. Doxycycline is curative — don’t wait for serology to treat.
Leptospirosis 2–30 days (typically 5–14) Freshwater/soil exposure (white-water rafting, rice paddies). Biphasic: flu-like illness then (10%) Weil’s disease — jaundice, renal failure, haemorrhage. Doxycycline or penicillin.
Hepatitis A 15–50 days Jaundice, dark urine, raised transaminases, anorexia. Self-limiting in most. HAV IgM confirms acute infection. Notifiable in NZ.
Schistosomiasis Katayama fever: 4–8 weeks post-exposure Freshwater exposure in sub-Saharan Africa (including popular safari destinations). Cercarial dermatitis immediately post-exposure; Katayama fever: fever, urticaria, eosinophilia. Serology negative in first 6–8 weeks. Treat with praziquantel; refer ID.
Viral haemorrhagic fever 2–21 days (Ebola/Marburg) Very rare. If febrile traveller returning from an active outbreak region — consult ID immediately before proceeding. Standard infection control while assessing; don’t wait for results.
Giardia / chronic diarrhoea 7–21 days Persistent, offensive loose stools; bloating; no fever. Stool microscopy + ova/cysts ×3 (sensitivity improves with repeat). Metronidazole 400 mg TDS ×5 days empirically if high suspicion while results pending.

Skin presentations and eosinophilia

Cutaneous larva migrans (CLM) is common in NZ travellers returning from SE Asia and the Caribbean — the serpiginous, intensely pruritic track caused by dog or cat hookworm larvae migrating through skin (typically from beach sand contamination of feet or buttocks). Ivermectin 200 mcg/kg single dose is first-line; albendazole 400 mg daily for 3 days is an alternative.

Eosinophilia in a returned traveller warrants systematic evaluation. The differential includes Strongyloides (stool microscopy often negative — use serology), Schistosoma (serology at 6–8 weeks post-exposure), hookworm, Toxocara, and tropical pulmonary eosinophilia (filarial). Persistently elevated eosinophilia without a clear diagnosis warrants ID referral.

When to refer to infectious diseases: confirmed or suspected malaria; typhoid; schistosomiasis; any suspected VHF; unexplained persistent fever or eosinophilia; any unwell child returned from the tropics. ID input is almost always available by phone for urgent cases — don’t delay treatment waiting for an outpatient appointment when the clinical picture is serious.

Special populations

Immunocompromised travellers

The key constraint is live vaccine contraindications. Yellow fever (live attenuated), oral typhoid (Vivotif — live), MMR, varicella, and BCG are all contraindicated in patients on immunosuppression, with solid organ or haematopoietic stem cell transplants, with active malignancy, or with advanced HIV (CD4 below 200 cells/μL). For HIV with CD4 200–499, yellow fever is a risk-benefit decision that warrants ID input. Inactivated vaccines (Hep A, Hep B, injectable typhoid Typhim Vi, killed polio, meningococcal) are safe but may produce a blunted serological response.

Where a live vaccine is contraindicated for a patient considering travel to a yellow fever endemic area, a medical exemption letter may be issued — but this doesn’t change the biological risk. The patient remains genuinely at risk in endemic areas and should understand this. Complex immunocompromised travellers benefit from a pre-travel consultation involving their specialist and a travel medicine clinic.

Pregnancy

Travel in the second trimester is generally safest for low-risk destinations. The main concerns: live vaccine contraindications (same list as above); malaria risk (P. falciparum in pregnancy carries substantially increased risk of severe disease, miscarriage, preterm labour, low birthweight, and maternal death — advise avoiding endemic areas if possible); and Zika virus (vertical transmission with risk of congenital Zika syndrome — check current outbreak advisories before counselling).

For malaria prophylaxis in pregnancy: chloroquine is safe and has been used for decades, but limited by widespread resistance. Mefloquine can be used after the first trimester where risk-benefit supports it. Doxycycline is contraindicated (risk to fetal bone and teeth). Atovaquone-proguanil has limited safety data and is generally avoided. The practical implication: a pregnant woman travelling to a high chloroquine-resistance area (sub-Saharan Africa, much of SE Asia) has no clearly safe and fully effective prophylaxis option — the best answer may be to defer the trip.

Infants and young children

Children develop more severe malaria than adults — particularly under 5 years. Atovaquone-proguanil is licensed from 5 kg bodyweight; mefloquine can be used in children over 5 kg; doxycycline is contraindicated under 8 years. Yellow fever vaccine is contraindicated under 9 months. Dehydration in traveller’s diarrhoea is a greater risk in infants — carry ORS sachets and know the signs of dehydration. Loperamide is not recommended in children under 12.

Older travellers and polypharmacy

Several interactions are clinically significant in older patients on multiple medications. Mefloquine with beta-blockers or digoxin increases the risk of bradycardia and conduction abnormalities — a real concern in cardiac patients. Doxycycline absorption is reduced by antacids, calcium supplements, and iron — separate by at least 2 hours. Atovaquone-proguanil is generally the safest option in complex polypharmacy and should be avoided if eGFR is below 30. Altitude poses additional cardiac risk in older patients with ischaemic heart disease or heart failure.
