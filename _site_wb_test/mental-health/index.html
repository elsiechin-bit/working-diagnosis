Mental Health

A working GP's guide to recognising and managing common mental health conditions in primary care — depression, anxiety, psychosis, addiction, perinatal, and youth mental health.

Mental Health

Mind, mood,
and meaning.

Mental health is the leading cause of disease burden in Aotearoa. General practice is where most of it lands — and where most of it can be genuinely helped.

One in five New Zealanders will experience a mental health problem in any given year.¹ The majority will first present — or never present at all — to a GP. This page is a working reference for those consultations: what to look for, what the evidence actually supports, and what to do next in a fifteen-minute slot.

A note on framing: diagnosis names are shorthand, not verdicts. They guide treatment decisions; they shouldn't foreclose curiosity about why this person, at this time, is struggling.

---

Depression & low mood

Major depressive disorder affects around 6% of New Zealanders at any one time.² The PHQ-9 remains the most validated brief screening tool in primary care, with a score ≥10 having sensitivity of 88% and specificity of 85% for major depression.³ But the score is a prompt, not a diagnosis — always ask about duration, functional impact, and suicidality directly.

What the evidence supports: For mild-to-moderate depression, structured exercise, behavioural activation, and brief psychological therapies (particularly CBT) have effect sizes comparable to antidepressants with fewer adverse effects.⁴ For moderate-to-severe depression, SSRIs remain first-line pharmacotherapy. Sertraline has the best evidence base for combined efficacy and tolerability across meta-analyses.⁵ Advise patients that onset of effect takes 2–4 weeks and that discontinuing abruptly after several months carries a real discontinuation syndrome risk.

In NZ primary care: BPAC's depression management pathway and HealthPathways Canterbury are the most pragmatic local references. Access and Choice funding (2022 onwards) has expanded funded brief-therapy access substantially — check your local Primary Mental Health and Addictions (PMHA) service for current referral criteria.

---

Anxiety disorders

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health conditions globally and in NZ.¹ Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), panic disorder, social anxiety disorder, and health anxiety each present differently — and conflating them leads to under-treatment. The GAD-7 (threshold ≥10) performs well for GAD and moderate-to-severe anxiety across disorders.⁶

What the evidence supports: CBT has the strongest evidence base across anxiety disorders, with effects that outlast those of pharmacotherapy at follow-up.⁷ SSRIs and SNRIs are effective adjuncts or alternatives where therapy is unavailable or declined. Benzodiazepines should not be first-line: they provide short-term symptom relief but worsen long-term outcomes, drive tolerance, and carry significant dependence risk — particularly in older adults and those with concurrent substance use.⁸

Panic disorder responds particularly well to psychoeducation alone in mild cases. Explaining the physiology of the panic cycle — hyperventilation → hypocapnia → peripheral vasoconstriction → paraesthesia → more fear — demystifies the experience and interrupts catastrophic interpretation. This takes ten minutes and is underused.

Psychosis & bipolar disorder

Psychosis and bipolar spectrum disorders are less common but carry disproportionate morbidity. Early psychosis — the period surrounding a first episode — is a critical window: duration of untreated psychosis (DUP) predicts long-term outcome, and DUP under 3 months is associated with significantly better functional recovery.⁹

Red flags for early psychosis in general practice: social withdrawal over weeks to months, perplexity or odd reasoning, decline in work or study function, new-onset sleep reversal, or a family member reporting the person "isn't themselves." These warrant urgent mental health team referral, not watchful waiting.

Bipolar I and II are frequently misdiagnosed as unipolar depression — partly because patients present depressed more often than manic, and partly because hypomania is often experienced as productive and not volunteered. Always screen for past hypomanic or manic episodes before starting an antidepressant in a patient with recurrent depression. Antidepressant monotherapy in unrecognised bipolar disorder can precipitate mixed states or rapid cycling.¹⁰

In NZ: secondary mental health services (DHB/Te Whatu Ora locality teams) hold ongoing care for most patients with psychosis and bipolar I. The GP role is monitoring metabolic effects of antipsychotics (weight, glucose, lipids, prolactin), medication adherence, and physical health — a genuinely important function that is often under-resourced.

Addiction & substance use

Substance use disorders are common, under-screened, and underestimated in clinical complexity. Alcohol alone contributes to around 800 deaths and 18,000 hospitalisations annually in NZ.¹¹ Cannabis use disorder affects an estimated 9% of those who ever use, rising to 17% in daily users.¹²

Brief intervention works: For hazardous alcohol use (AUDIT-C ≥5, or AUDIT ≥8), a structured 5–15 minute brief motivational intervention reduces consumption at 12 months with a number needed to treat of around 8.¹³ This is one of the better NNTs in primary care and is deliverable within a standard consultation.

For opioid use disorder, buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone) is funded in NZ and is the evidence-based standard of care. Methadone remains available via specialist programmes. GPs with appropriate training can prescribe buprenorphine — the NZAM and BPAC provide local guidance. Withholding or delaying opioid agonist therapy substantially increases overdose mortality risk.¹⁴

For alcohol use disorder with physical dependence, ambulatory detoxification with a diazepam reducing regimen (CIWA-Ar guided) is appropriate for low-to-moderate severity withdrawal. High severity — prior seizures, high AUDIT scores, significant comorbidity — warrants inpatient assessment.

Perinatal mental health

Perinatal mental health problems — spanning pregnancy through the first postnatal year — affect up to 20% of women and around 10% of partners.¹⁵ They are the leading cause of maternal mortality in high-income countries when indirect causes are included.¹⁶ They are also the most commonly missed.

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) is the validated screening tool for the perinatal period; a score ≥13 warrants clinical assessment. The EPDS is recommended at the booking visit and 6-week postnatal check in NZ, though implementation is inconsistent.¹⁷

What the evidence supports: Psychological therapies are first-line for mild-to-moderate perinatal depression and anxiety. For moderate-to-severe illness, or where the woman has a pre-existing psychiatric condition, pharmacotherapy is often appropriate. The benefit-risk calculation here is frequently misframed: untreated severe depression in pregnancy carries its own fetal and maternal risks. Sertraline and paroxetine have the most perinatal safety data; discuss PPHN risk (small but real for SSRIs late in pregnancy) and neonatal adaptation syndrome.¹⁸

Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency: rapid onset within days of delivery, confusion, lability, perceptual disturbance. It requires same-day secondary care referral. Maternal suicide is a real risk in the perinatal period and is underestimated by clinicians.

---

Youth & adolescent mental health

Youth mental health is a genuine crisis in NZ by any measure. Rates of anxiety, depression, self-harm, and eating disorders among young people have risen markedly over the past decade.¹⁹ Waitlists for secondary CAMHS are long. General practice is, by default, a frontline service.

Adolescent presentations are often indirect: school refusal, somatic complaints (headaches, abdominal pain), irritability rather than low mood, or a caregiver presenting on behalf of a young person who hasn't consented to be there. Seeing the young person alone — briefly, with clear confidentiality limits explained — is best practice and builds the therapeutic relationship that makes future help-seeking more likely.

Screening tools: The PHQ-A (adolescent version) and GAD-7 are appropriate from age 12+. For self-harm, ask directly — the evidence is clear that asking does not increase risk and often provides relief.²⁰

Medication in adolescents: fluoxetine is the only SSRI with robust RCT evidence in adolescent depression (NNT ~4, NNH for suicidality ~143 — benefit outweighs risk for moderate-to-severe illness).²¹ The FDA black-box warning on antidepressants and suicidality in under-25s reflects increased suicidal ideation (not completed suicide) in short-term trials; do not let it discourage appropriate prescribing in severe illness — but monitor closely in the first 4 weeks.

In NZ: Youthline, 1737, and the local PMHA youth stream are the primary referral pathways. For eating disorders, early referral to a specialist service is time-critical — medical instability and low BMI carry significant mortality risk and warrant prompt paediatric assessment alongside psychiatric care.²²

---

Across all presentations

Ask about sleep, exercise, and connection — not as lifestyle platitudes, but as data. Sleep disturbance is both a symptom and a perpetuating factor across virtually every mental health condition. Exercise has antidepressant effect sizes of 0.43–0.66 in meta-analyses, comparable to pharmacotherapy in mild-to-moderate illness.²³ Social isolation amplifies almost everything.

And ask about whānau. NZ general practice sits inside a bicultural context that mainstream clinical frameworks mostly ignore. Māori mental health is shaped by determinants — colonisation, land loss, institutional racism, socioeconomic disadvantage — that are not captured in a PHQ-9 score. Kaupapa Māori services (like Māori Mental Health teams and Whānau Ora) offer pathways that are more culturally safe for many patients. Knowing what's available locally matters.
