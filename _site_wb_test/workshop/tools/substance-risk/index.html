Evidence note. E-cigarettes have been in widespread use since ~2015. Long-term (20+ year) human outcome data do not yet exist. Most risk estimates are derived from short-term studies, animal models, in vitro data, and mechanistic inference from known carcinogens in aerosol. Risk comparisons with cigarettes are directionally valid but imprecise. Treat risk levels as indicative, not definitive.

New Zealand context — vaping
Legal status & regulationVaping regulated under Smokefree Environments and Regulated Products Act 2020. Nicotine vapes legal for adults. Sale to under-18s prohibited. Disposables restricted from Nov 2023.
Youth use2023 ASH Year 10 survey: ~24% of NZ Year 10 students reported vaping in the past month. Significant youth uptake driven by disposable devices. Māori and Pacific youth rates higher.
As a cessation toolCochrane 2023: e-cigarettes more effective than NRT for smoking cessation (RR ~1.7). PHARMAC does not fund e-cigarettes. Clinicians may support use as harm reduction for established smokers only — not as an initiation product.
Medsafe / clinical stanceRNZCGP position (2023): cautious support for vaping as cessation aid in adult smokers; strongly oppose youth vaping. Quitline 0800 778 778 supports transition away from both cigarettes and vapes.
Relative risks are indicative. Vaping evidence base is <10 years for most outcomes. Sources: US Surgeon General Vaping Report 2016, NASEM 2018, Cochrane reviews, IARC, BMJ/Lancet systematic reviews.

New Zealand context — alcohol
Low-risk guidelines (MoH NZ)Women: ≤2 SD/day, ≤10/week. Men: ≤3 SD/day, ≤15/week. At least 2 alcohol-free days/week. No amount is safe in pregnancy.
NZ burdenAlcohol causes ~800 deaths/year in NZ directly. Economic cost estimated at $7.8B/year. Māori have higher rates of hazardous drinking and alcohol-attributable harm.
Screening & brief interventionAUDIT-C recommended for all patients. Brief intervention (5 min) reduces consumption by ~20% in hazardous drinkers (Cochrane). HealthPathways for specialist referral to CADS.
PHARMAC-funded treatmentNaltrexone (oral) funded for alcohol dependence. Acamprosate funded. Disulfiram available. Refer to Addiction Medicine specialist for dependent patients. Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening — assess severity.
Sources: Wood AM et al. Lancet 2018; GBD 2016 Alcohol Collaborators Lancet 2018; Ronksley PE et al. BMJ 2011; IARC Monograph 100E; NZ Ministry of Health alcohol guidelines.

New Zealand context — cannabis
Legal statusCannabis remains illegal in NZ. 2020 referendum narrowly failed (53.4% No). Police have moved toward discretionary enforcement for personal use. Medicinal cannabis regulated separately.
PrevalenceNZ has one of the highest cannabis use rates in the world (~15% past-year use, adults). Youth use ~11% (ASH 2023). Māori use rates approximately 2× general population.
Clinical approachASSIST screening tool for cannabis. Brief motivational intervention effective. Refer to CADS for dependence. Cannabis withdrawal (days 1–7 post-cessation): irritability, insomnia, appetite loss, sweating — manage symptomatically.
Psychosis protocolFirst-episode psychosis associated with cannabis in 25–30% of NZ cases. Early Psychosis Intervention services available via DHB. Urgent referral if acute psychosis present. Cannabis cessation is part of treatment protocol.
Sources: Di Forti M et al. Lancet Psychiatry 2019; Meier MH et al. PNAS 2012 (Dunedin cohort); Hall W, Degenhardt L. Lancet 2009; US Surgeon General Cannabis Report 2019.

New Zealand context — nicotine products
NRT fundingNicotine patches, gum, and lozenges PHARMAC-funded for cessation. 14-week supply available via GP or Quitline. Most cost-effective intervention alongside brief advice.
Nicotine pouchesUnregulated as of 2024 in NZ — classified as neither tobacco nor medicine. Zyn, Velo widely available. No age restrictions enforced. Flavoured products driving uptake in youth.
Heated tobacco (IQOS)Regulated under Smokefree Environments Act. Sold legally in NZ. Marketed as "reduced harm" — some reduction in combustion toxicants but long-term safety data absent and still delivers nicotine dependence.
Clinical use of NRTCombination NRT (patch + short-acting form) more effective than monotherapy. NRT safe post-MI. Varenicline most effective single pharmacotherapy. Quitline 0800 778 778 provides phone-based NRT counselling.
Sources: Benowitz NL. NEJM 2010; Hartmann-Boyce J et al. Cochrane 2022; IARC Monograph 89; NZ Smokefree Environments Act; PHARMAC NRT schedule.

New Zealand context — caffeine
NZ safe limitsMoH: healthy adults ≤400mg/day. Pregnant women ≤200mg/day. Children and adolescents: lower limits — no formal NZ guideline but 2.5mg/kg/day often cited. Energy drinks: no sale to under-16 recommended.
Energy drink concernNZ consumption high among 15–24 year olds. Energy drinks combine caffeine (80–160mg/can) with sugar (~27g/can). Mixing with alcohol common and blunts perceived intoxication.
Clinical useCaffeine at 2.5mg/kg IV/oral is evidence-based for neonatal apnoea. Caffeine is an analgesic adjuvant. No funded restriction or treatment programme for caffeine dependence in NZ — manage as behavioural change.
BreastfeedingCaffeine passes into breast milk. MoH: ≤200mg/day while breastfeeding. Infant caffeine half-life much longer than adult (adults 5–7h; neonates up to 100h) — accumulation risk with high maternal intake.
Sources: Poole R et al. BMJ 2017 (umbrella review); Ding M et al. Diabetes Care 2014; Ross GW et al. JAMA 2000; Kennedy OJ et al. BMJ Open 2016; Drake C et al. J Clin Sleep Med 2013.
Free / added sugar intake
Free sugars = all added sugars + naturally occurring sugars in honey, syrups, fruit juices, and fruit concentrates. Does NOT include sugars in whole fruit, vegetables, or dairy. 1 teaspoon ≈ 4g. WHO recommends <6 tsp (25g)/day for additional health benefit; <10 tsp (50g) upper limit.

New Zealand context — sugar
NZ intakeNZ adults consume on average ~14% energy from free sugars — well above WHO 10% guideline. Children average ~15–16%. SSB consumption highest in Māori, Pacific, and lower-income populations. NZ has no sugar tax.
Dental health burdenTooth decay is the most common reason for child hospitalisation in NZ. Māori and Pacific children 2–3× higher rates. School dental service covers ages 0–17. Fluoridation covers ~56% NZ population.
Practical guidanceReplace SSBs with water (most impactful single change). Fruit whole > fruit juice. Read labels: "low fat" products often high sugar. Raisins and dried fruit: high free sugar — comparable dental risk to lollies.
Screening & resourcesNo specific NZ clinical guideline for dietary sugar assessment. Refer to Dietitian for high-risk patients (diabetes, NAFLD, obesity). Heart Foundation NZ and Diabetes NZ provide patient resources.
Sources: WHO Guideline Sugars Intake 2015; Moynihan & Kelly J Dent Res 2014; Malik VS et al. Circulation 2010; Imamura F et al. BMJ 2015; Yang Q et al. JAMA Intern Med 2014; Te Morenga L et al. BMJ 2013.
