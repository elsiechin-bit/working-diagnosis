Geriatrics

STOPP/START reference.

A structured tool for medication review in older adults — what to stop, and what might be missing from the list.

STOPP (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions) and START (Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment) are complementary criteria for identifying potentially inappropriate medications (PIMs) and potential prescribing omissions (PPOs) in adults aged 65 and over. Version 3 was published in 2023 and expanded the criteria based on updated evidence and European clinical expert consensus.1 STOPP/START has stronger evidence for reducing adverse drug events and hospital admissions than the US Beers Criteria, and is more applicable to New Zealand prescribing practice.

The key STOPP categories most relevant to NZ general practice are summarised below. This is not an exhaustive reproduction of the full criteria — the full tool is freely available and worth bookmarking (link below).

High-yield STOPP items (potentially inappropriate — consider stopping):

  • Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs — significant falls and fracture risk, cognitive impairment, dependence; avoid or deprescribe in all older adults where possible
  • Anticholinergic drugs — cumulative burden matters; includes tricyclic antidepressants, first-generation antihistamines, oxybutynin, tolterodine, and many others; cognitive load increases with each additional agent
  • NSAIDs — gastrointestinal bleeding risk, renal impairment, and fluid retention; avoid if eGFR <50 or in combination with anticoagulants without gastroprotection
  • Antipsychotics in dementia — increased stroke and mortality risk; use only when non-pharmacological management has been exhausted, at the lowest effective dose, with regular review
  • Antihypertensives in frail patients — orthostatic hypotension risk; reassess targets in the context of frailty and falls history; a resting BP of 120/70 may be harmful in an 85-year-old with a CFS of 6
  • Sulfonylureas with long duration of action — hypoglycaemia risk in older adults; glibenclamide in particular should be avoided
  • Proton pump inhibitors without ongoing indication — widely continued without documented indication; review at least annually
  • Opioids without laxative — constipation is not a side effect that can be managed reactively in older adults; prescribe a stimulant laxative concurrently

Key START items (potential omissions — consider adding):

  • Anticoagulation in AF with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 — direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) are generally preferred over warfarin in older adults; age alone is not a contraindication
  • Bisphosphonate after fragility fracture — the most common prescribing omission in NZ post-fracture care
  • ACE inhibitor or ARB in heart failure with reduced EF — evidence-based, often underprescribed in the very old
  • Statin in established atherosclerotic disease — age alone (up to at least 80) is not a reason to withhold statin therapy if there is established CVD and life expectancy is reasonable
  • Vitamin D in housebound or institutionalised patients — high risk of deficiency; supplementation is low-risk and inexpensive

The full STOPP/START v3 criteria are published open access in the European Geriatric Medicine journal (O'Mahony et al., 2023) and a freely available PDF can be found via the British Geriatrics Society. The NZF flags a number of STOPP items directly in drug monographs. For practical deprescribing support and patient-facing resources, the prescribe.org.nz and deprescribing.org websites are worth bookmarking.
