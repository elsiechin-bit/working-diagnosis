Osteoporosis & fracture prevention.

The most undertreated significant condition in New Zealand primary care following minimal trauma fracture.

Osteoporosis is defined by a T-score of −2.5 or below at the lumbar spine, femoral neck, or total hip on dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA). But the decision to treat should be based on fracture risk — not T-score alone. The WHO FRAX tool and the GARVAN fracture risk calculator are both appropriate for New Zealand practice; GARVAN has the advantage of incorporating falls history (a key independent risk factor) and was partly derived from an Australian cohort, making it more applicable locally. Most guidelines now use a 10-year hip or major osteoporotic fracture probability as the treatment threshold.1

DXA is indicated for: all women aged 65 and over; postmenopausal women under 65 with risk factors (family history of hip fracture, low body weight, smoking, high alcohol intake, corticosteroid use); all men aged 70 and over; and any person who has sustained a minimal trauma fracture. A minimal trauma fracture is one resulting from a fall from standing height or less — it is, by definition, a fragility fracture and should prompt assessment and treatment without waiting for DXA results.2

Post-fracture care is a significant quality gap in New Zealand. Internationally, fewer than 20% of patients who present with a fragility fracture receive appropriate assessment and treatment within the following 12 months — a phenomenon called the "treatment gap".3 Fracture liaison services (FLS) exist in some DHB areas and can help close this gap, but in their absence, the responsibility falls to the GP. If your patient has had a wrist, vertebral, or hip fracture and is not on treatment, it is worth reviewing.

First-line treatment remains an oral bisphosphonate — alendronate 70 mg weekly is fully subsidised in New Zealand and is appropriate for most patients. The main contraindication is significant renal impairment (eGFR <35 mL/min). Before starting, ensure calcium and vitamin D adequacy, exclude secondary causes of bone loss (thyroid disease, coeliac disease, hyperparathyroidism, hypogonadism, and chronic corticosteroid use), and address dental health — bisphosphonate-related osteonecrosis of the jaw is rare but worth a baseline dental review. For patients who cannot tolerate or absorb oral bisphosphonates, or in whom adherence is a concern, zoledronate 5 mg IV annually is an alternative — it is subsidised in New Zealand under Special Authority criteria.4

Calcium and vitamin D supplementation remains controversial in isolation — the evidence that it prevents fractures independently of bisphosphonate therapy is weak, and high-dose calcium supplementation may carry cardiovascular risk.5 In practice, ensuring adequate dietary calcium intake (approximately 1,000–1,200 mg/day from food) and correcting vitamin D deficiency (target 25-OH vitamin D ≥50 nmol/L) is reasonable adjunct therapy rather than primary treatment. Sunlight exposure, dietary sources, and supplementation all contribute.

Treatment duration deserves attention. After five years of oral bisphosphonate therapy, a "bisphosphonate holiday" of one to three years is appropriate for patients at moderate fracture risk (no hip or vertebral fracture, T-score above −2.5 at femoral neck). For high-risk patients — those with prior hip or vertebral fracture, or T-score below −2.5 after five years — continuation or switch to alternative agents (denosumab, teriparatide) should be considered in consultation with secondary care.
