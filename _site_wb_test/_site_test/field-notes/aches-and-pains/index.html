Aches and pains — the undifferentiated musculoskeletal presentation

What to look for, what not to miss, why statins and hypothyroidism are the most rewarding things to check, and why the management reflex that feels most thorough often does the most harm.

3 May 2026

"I just ache all over" is one of the most common opening lines in general practice, and one of the most information-poor. It tells you the patient is suffering. It tells you almost nothing else. Behind it sits a differential that runs from viral myalgia and deconditioning, through statin toxicity and vitamin D deficiency, past fibromyalgia and post-COVID syndrome, and into territory that warrants urgent action: polymyalgia rheumatica, inflammatory arthritis, occult malignancy, hypothyroidism, and — in the right clinical context — something serious enough to require the same-day phone call.

The clinical challenge with this presentation is not the diagnosis at either end of the severity spectrum, which is usually clear enough. It is the large middle territory: the patient who has widespread, chronic, non-inflammatory pain; who has had extensive investigation that shows nothing; who has been to multiple specialists who have confirmed nothing wrong; who is no better for any of it; and who is now sitting in front of you, having used up considerable healthcare resources, still in pain, and with a now-entrenched belief that there is something undetected that is being missed.

Understanding how that patient got there, and what to do differently at an earlier stage, is most of what this note is about.

Part I — The diagnostic structure

The first task with any "aches and pains" presentation is to categorise it correctly, because the management differs fundamentally depending on the category. There are three broad frameworks:

Inflammatory. Pain that is worse with rest and improves with movement. Morning stiffness lasting more than 45–60 minutes. Objective synovitis — joint swelling, warmth, loss of range of movement. Elevated CRP or ESR. Systemic features: fatigue, weight loss, fever. If this is the picture, the differential includes rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, axial spondyloarthropathy, polymyalgia rheumatica, crystal arthropathy (acute gout/pseudogout rather than widespread pain), systemic lupus, and infection. This group needs investigation and often rheumatology input.

Mechanical/structural. Pain that is worse with activity and improves with rest. Localised to specific joints or regions. Associated with physical use patterns — occupation, sport, body weight. Normal inflammatory markers. This is the most common category of musculoskeletal presentation in primary care and is managed with appropriate load management, physiotherapy, and analgesia.

Central/widespread. Pain that is diffuse, often poorly localised, associated with fatigue, sleep disruption, and cognitive symptoms. Normal or minimally elevated inflammatory markers. Normal imaging. A history of multiple negative investigations and multiple specialist visits that have found nothing. This is the category most likely to be mismanaged, and the one where the management makes the biggest difference — in both directions.

The critical point: these categories are not mutually exclusive. A patient can have central sensitisation overlaid on genuine osteoarthritis. A patient with a somatoform picture can also have a missed inflammatory diagnosis. The framework is for organising the clinical thinking, not for premature closure.

Part II — Don't miss these

Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is the first thing to think of in any patient over 50 with bilateral shoulder pain and stiffness, bilateral hip girdle pain and stiffness, or both. PMR is genuinely common — incidence of approximately 100 per 100,000 in those over 50 — and dramatically underdiagnosed, partly because it presents with "aches and pains" in an age group where musculoskeletal symptoms are assumed to be degenerative [1].

The 2012 EULAR/ACR classification criteria provide a useful clinical framework [2]:

  • Age ≥50
  • Bilateral shoulder aching
  • Abnormal CRP or ESR
  • Morning stiffness >45 minutes
  • New hip pain or limited range of movement
  • Absence of RF and anti-CCP
  • Absence of other joint involvement

The response to prednisolone is near-diagnostic: PMR typically produces dramatic improvement within 24–72 hours of starting prednisolone 15 mg daily. If there is no substantial improvement within two weeks, the diagnosis should be revisited. Failure to respond is a red flag for an alternative diagnosis — including malignancy, which can mimic PMR closely.

Always ask about giant cell arteritis (GCA) in patients with PMR. Approximately 15–20% of PMR patients have co-existing GCA; new-onset headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, or any visual symptoms in a patient with PMR is a clinical emergency requiring immediate high-dose steroids (prednisolone 40–60 mg, or IV methylprednisolone if visual symptoms are present) and urgent ophthalmology/rheumatology review [3].

Hypothyroidism causes proximal myopathy, diffuse musculoskeletal aching, fatigue, and cognitive slowing — a clinical picture that overlaps substantially with fibromyalgia. TSH is cheap and definitive. It should be ordered in virtually every undifferentiated widespread pain presentation. Hypothyroidism is common, undertreated thyroid disease causes significant symptom burden, and treatment is simple. The missed hypothyroid patient who has been labelled fibromyalgia and started on pregabalin is a preventable outcome.

Inflammatory arthritis — particularly early rheumatoid arthritis and psoriatic arthritis — can present diffusely before the characteristic joint distribution is apparent. Rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP antibodies, CRP, and ESR are appropriate screening tests. The hand and wrist joints, and the small joints of the feet, are the places to look; proximal interphalangeal and metacarpophalangeal involvement distinguishes RA from osteoarthritis (which favours distal interphalangeal joints and the base of the thumb).

Malignancy. Bone pain from metastatic disease or haematological malignancy (multiple myeloma, leukaemia, lymphoma) can present as non-specific widespread musculoskeletal pain. Myeloma in particular — most common in older adults, producing bone pain, fatigue, and sometimes a raised ESR — is worth keeping in the differential when the clinical picture is atypical, the ESR is strikingly elevated, the patient is over 60, or when a serum protein electrophoresis has not been done. Red flag features requiring urgent investigation: constitutional symptoms (unexplained weight loss, drenching night sweats, fever), pain that is constant and worse at night, palpable lymphadenopathy, anaemia.

Vitamin D deficiency and osteomalacia. Severe vitamin D deficiency causes bone pain, proximal muscle weakness, and diffuse musculoskeletal aching. Subclinical deficiency is extremely common in New Zealand — particularly in people with darker skin, those who cover their skin for cultural or occupational reasons, indoor workers, and the elderly [4]. 25-OH vitamin D should be checked in any undifferentiated pain presentation. An established insufficiency (<50 nmol/L) is worth treating regardless of whether it explains the symptoms, but in some patients — particularly those with florid musculoskeletal symptoms and a very low 25-OH D — treatment produces substantial improvement.

Hyperparathyroidism. Primary hyperparathyroidism causes bone pain, weakness, fatigue, constipation, nephrolithiasis, and mood disturbance ("bones, stones, groans, and psychic moans"). The presentation is often non-specific. Calcium should be checked in undifferentiated presentations, particularly in postmenopausal women.

Part III — Drugs as a cause

Medication-induced myalgia is underdiagnosed because it requires the clinician to actively think of it and because the patient does not connect the medication to the symptom.

Statins are the most important cause in primary care [5]. Statin-associated muscle symptoms (SAMS) affect approximately 5–10% of statin users and range from mild myalgia (pain without CK elevation) through myositis (pain with CK elevation) to rhabdomyolysis (severe CK elevation with myoglobinuria and potential AKI — rare but serious). The majority of statin muscle symptoms are myalgia without significant CK elevation.

Clinical approach to suspected statin myalgia:

  • Check CK. A normal CK in a symptomatic patient does not exclude statin myalgia; it does exclude significant muscle injury.
  • Check TSH and vitamin D — both worsen statin-associated muscle symptoms.
  • Check for relevant drug interactions: macrolide antibiotics (particularly clarithromycin), amiodarone, some calcium channel blockers, and antifungals inhibit CYP3A4 and raise statin plasma levels, increasing myopathy risk.
  • Trial off the statin for 4–6 weeks. Myalgia that resolves or substantially improves off the statin and returns on rechallenge confirms the diagnosis.
  • Rechallenge with a different statin: rosuvastatin and pravastatin are more hydrophilic (less muscle penetration) and have lower rates of SAMS; alternate-day dosing is sometimes tolerated when daily dosing is not.
  • CoQ10 supplementation is widely used by patients; the clinical evidence does not support a significant benefit, but it is safe, and there is no reason to discourage it if the patient finds it helpful.

Bisphosphonates can cause diffuse musculoskeletal pain — a product of the inflammatory response to their pharmacological action. The pain typically develops within days of the first dose and can be severe and unexpected; patients who do not know this may present to the GP with acute severe bone pain shortly after starting alendronate, which is alarming until context is established.

Aromatase inhibitors (letrozole, anastrozole, exemestane) cause arthralgias and myalgias in up to 50% of users, sometimes severely enough to affect adherence [6]. Women on aromatase inhibitors for breast cancer should be asked specifically about musculoskeletal symptoms; the symptom burden is substantial and often under-reported. Management options include: dose reduction, switching aromatase inhibitor, addition of duloxetine (some evidence), or physiotherapy; withholding or discontinuing needs oncology input.

Fluoroquinolone antibiotics (ciprofloxacin, norfloxacin) are associated with tendinopathy, particularly Achilles tendinopathy, with risk of tendon rupture. This risk is increased in older patients and those on corticosteroids. Advise patients on fluoroquinolones to report tendon pain promptly.

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (used increasingly in oncology — pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab) cause immune-mediated adverse effects including inflammatory arthritis, myositis, and polymyalgia-like presentations. Any patient on checkpoint inhibitor therapy who presents with new inflammatory joint or muscle symptoms should be flagged to their oncologist promptly.

Part IV — Fibromyalgia and central sensitisation

After the above causes have been considered and excluded or addressed, the patient with widespread, chronic, non-inflammatory pain who does not improve is most likely to have fibromyalgia or a closely related central sensitisation syndrome. This is not a diagnosis of exclusion in the sense of being a last resort; it is a diagnosis with specific clinical features and a specific neurobiological basis, and it is one of the most common chronic pain presentations in general practice.

The underlying mechanism is central sensitisation: an amplification of pain processing within the central nervous system, in which the gain on the pain signal is turned up, ordinary stimuli produce pain responses, and the volume of pain experience is disproportionate to any peripheral tissue damage [7]. This is not "psychosomatic" in the pejorative sense; it is a real neurobiological process with measurable correlates including altered brain connectivity patterns, reduced descending pain inhibition, and changes in neurotransmitter systems. Explaining this to patients — in language that validates their experience while also reframing the target of treatment — is one of the most useful clinical interventions available.

The 2016 ACR diagnostic criteria [8] replace the original tender-point examination and are more practical for general practice use. Diagnosis requires:

  1. Widespread pain index (WPI) ≥7 and symptom severity score (SSS) ≥5, OR WPI 4–6 and SSS ≥9
  2. Generalised pain, defined as pain in at least 4 of 5 regions, present for at least 3 months
  3. A fibromyalgia diagnosis is valid irrespective of other diagnoses — fibromyalgia can be present alongside inflammatory arthritis or other conditions

The WPI counts the number of regions with pain in the past week from a list of 19 body areas. The SSS captures fatigue, waking unrefreshed, cognitive symptoms, and somatic symptoms. The full criteria are available in the original publication [8].

Common comorbidities that are more prevalent in fibromyalgia and which, if present and unaddressed, perpetuate symptoms: irritable bowel syndrome, chronic fatigue syndrome/ME, primary headache disorders (especially migraine), interstitial cystitis, temporomandibular disorder, anxiety and depression, and sleep disorders (including restless legs syndrome and undiagnosed obstructive sleep apnoea).

Part V — Managing fibromyalgia and chronic widespread pain

The management evidence base is reasonably robust and the hierarchy is clear. What is less clear is how to deliver the evidence-based treatments within the time and resource constraints of general practice.

What works — in order of evidence:

Aerobic exercise has the strongest evidence base for fibromyalgia and for chronic widespread pain generally [9]. The mechanism involves multiple pathways: exercise induces endogenous opioid and endocannabinoid release, activates descending pain inhibitory systems, reduces central sensitisation, improves sleep, and addresses deconditioning. The dose does not need to be intense: low-impact aerobic exercise (walking, swimming, cycling) at moderate intensity, three to five times per week, has been shown to reduce pain intensity and improve function. The challenge is that patients in significant pain are often functionally impaired, avoidant of movement, and physiologically deconditioned — each of which makes starting exercise harder and more uncomfortable. A graded, graduated approach with realistic initial targets is more effective than ambitious prescriptions that the patient cannot achieve.

Pain education — specifically explaining the neuroscience of central sensitisation in accessible language — has been shown to reduce pain scores and catastrophising in clinical trials [10]. The concept of explaining why the pain is real but the target of treatment is the amplifier rather than the peripheral tissue is powerful and often produces a visible shift in the patient's understanding. This is not the same as telling the patient "it's all in your head." The explanation is: the signal from your body reaches the brain amplified; the brain processes this as significant pain even though there is no injury; the treatment targets the amplification.

Cognitive-behavioural therapy for pain addresses the maladaptive cognitive patterns — catastrophising ("this will never get better"), fear avoidance (reduced activity because of anticipated pain), and passive coping — that perpetuate chronic pain [11]. Referral to a pain psychologist, or to a structured CBT pain programme, is appropriate for patients with significant psychological contribution to their pain. In New Zealand, access varies substantially by location; the main centres have tertiary pain services with multidisciplinary input, but rural patients may have very limited access.

Sleep. Disrupted sleep amplifies pain and lowers pain thresholds; pain disrupts sleep. This bidirectional relationship creates a self-perpetuating cycle that is worth targeting explicitly. Addressing sleep — through sleep hygiene, treating comorbid sleep disorders (OSA, restless legs), and in some cases pharmacological sleep support — reduces pain independently of direct analgesic treatment.

Low-dose amitriptyline (10–25 mg nocte) has modest evidence for improving sleep and reducing pain in fibromyalgia and is commonly used as a first-line pharmacological adjunct [12]. It is not a primary antidepressant at these doses; the mechanism is primarily sedative and analgesic (through noradrenaline reuptake inhibition and sodium channel blockade). Side effects include morning sedation, dry mouth, constipation, and (with long-term use) cardiac conduction effects at higher doses. Check ECG if increasing beyond 75 mg or in patients with cardiac history.

Duloxetine (SNRI) has evidence in fibromyalgia for pain reduction independent of its antidepressant effects [12]. Useful particularly in patients with comorbid depression or anxiety, or those who do not tolerate amitriptyline. Starting dose 30 mg daily, titrate to 60 mg.

Pregabalin and gabapentin have licences for fibromyalgia in some jurisdictions (pregabalin has FDA approval for fibromyalgia). The evidence is modest, the side-effect burden (sedation, cognitive blunting, weight gain, dependence) is significant, and in practice these drugs are often continued indefinitely without reassessment of benefit. They are not contraindicated but should be a considered choice with regular review of whether ongoing use is justified. In CKD, dose reduction is required (see the renal field note).

What does not work — and commonly harms:

Opioids for chronic non-cancer musculoskeletal pain. This is one of the clearest messages in contemporary pain medicine, and one of the hardest to deliver to patients who have been managed with opioids for years [13]. Opioids do not improve function or quality of life in chronic non-cancer pain and cause significant harm: dependence, hyperalgesia (opioid-induced increase in pain sensitivity — the opposite of the intended effect), hormonal suppression, immunosuppression, cognitive impairment, constipation, and overdose risk. In fibromyalgia specifically, opioids are associated with worse outcomes than non-opioid management. The NZ clinical guidance on chronic non-cancer pain recommends against initiating long-term opioid therapy for fibromyalgia and most non-specific musculoskeletal conditions, and supports dose reduction and cessation in patients already established on opioids [14].

This is a difficult conversation and one that requires more than a single consultation. The patient who has been on tramadol for five years for fibromyalgia is not going to accept opioid reduction without a clear explanation, a concrete management plan for the transition, and trust in the GP. Unilateral forced reduction is harmful; collaborative, informed, graduated reduction with concurrent enhancement of non-opioid support is the appropriate approach.

Escalating investigations. The patient with established chronic widespread pain who has had extensive investigation does not benefit from further imaging, specialist referral, or diagnostic testing in the absence of new red flags or change in clinical pattern. Further investigation in this context reinforces the implicit message that there may be something serious that has not been found, increases anxiety, and generates incidental findings that require further management without improving outcomes. The most useful thing to do when the fibromyalgia diagnosis is established and the workup has been appropriate is to say clearly: "We have looked carefully and there is no serious disease. The problem is real and it is in how your nervous system is processing pain. That is actually the good news, because we know what helps."

Part VI — Post-viral and long COVID

Post-viral musculoskeletal pain has been recognised for decades — Ross River virus causes a prolonged arthralgia in New Zealand and Australia; Epstein-Barr virus post-infectious syndrome is a common cause of prolonged fatigue and pain in young adults; parvovirus B19 causes a polyarthropathy that can persist for months.

Long COVID (post-COVID condition) has added a large new population of patients with post-viral musculoskeletal symptoms, fatigue, and cognitive impairment [15]. The musculoskeletal features include diffuse myalgia, joint pain, and post-exertional malsymptom exacerbation (PEM) — worsening of symptoms following physical or cognitive effort that can be delayed 12–24 hours. PEM is an important distinguishing feature of long COVID and ME/CFS and distinguishes these conditions from fibromyalgia in management terms: the graded exercise approach that benefits fibromyalgia can be harmful in patients with significant PEM, where activity pacing (staying within the individual's energy envelope) is the recommended approach rather than graduated increase in exercise intensity [16].

Management of long COVID musculoskeletal symptoms: pacing, sleep support, symptom management, addressing comorbidities. Investigate for specific treatable complications (myocarditis, dysautonomia, small fibre neuropathy) if clinically indicated. NZ long COVID services have developed in the main centres; access to multidisciplinary support has been variable.

Part VII — The yellow flags

The yellow flags framework — originally developed in the back pain literature and now applied broadly to musculoskeletal presentations — identifies psychosocial risk factors for chronification of acute pain [17]. Identifying and addressing yellow flags at the acute stage is a more effective intervention than attempting to de-chronify established chronic pain.

Key yellow flags:

  • Catastrophising ("this pain means serious damage"; "I will never get better")
  • Fear-avoidance beliefs (avoiding activity because of anticipated harm)
  • Passive coping (waiting to be fixed rather than self-managing)
  • Work dissatisfaction or conflict
  • Compensation or litigation involvement (not because patients are malingering, but because the health and work system interactions around claims perpetuate illness behaviour)
  • Comorbid depression or anxiety
  • History of adverse childhood experiences or trauma (strong association with chronic pain vulnerability)
  • Poor social support

A patient with acute widespread pain presenting for the first time who has multiple yellow flags is a patient who benefits from brief psychosocial intervention — addressing the beliefs, providing good pain education, facilitating early return to activity — far more than from extended investigation and passive analgesia.

A working summary

The "aches and pains" presentation has a genuine differential and genuine don't-miss diagnoses. Polymyalgia rheumatica in the over-50s is the one most likely to be missed in early disease. Hypothyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, and statin myopathy are rewarding to check because they are common, treatable, and easy to overlook. Inflammatory arthritis and malignancy require red-flag vigilance.

After these are addressed, the most common explanation for chronic widespread pain is central sensitisation — fibromyalgia or its close relatives. The management that works is exercise, pain education, sleep, and cognitive strategies. The management that does not work, and causes harm, is escalating opioids, escalating investigations, and escalating specialist referrals that confirm nothing wrong while reinforcing the conviction that something serious is being missed.

The conversation about central sensitisation — explaining the neuroscience in a way that validates the patient's experience without pathologising or dismissing it — is one of the more clinically valuable things a GP can learn to do well.
