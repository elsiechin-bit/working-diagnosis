When the patient is joking

The disclosure that arrives dressed as a punchline, the clinical signal hidden in the laugh, and what the consultation room loses when humour is treated as decoration.

2 May 2026

A patient says, somewhere around minute four: "I'm probably wasting your time, ha ha — it's nothing." She has driven half an hour to get to the appointment. She has waited three weeks for it. She has rehearsed the sentence in the car park. She is not wasting anyone's time, and she knows she isn't, and what she is doing with the laugh is asking the GP to please not make her say the actual thing out loud unless the GP indicates it is safe to.

The bad version of the next thirty seconds is to laugh along and reassure her she's not wasting your time and move on. The good version is to register that the laugh has just done a substantial amount of clinical work, and to make space for whatever was underneath it without forcing her to admit she was scared.

This is most of what humour does in general practice. It is not decoration. It is not bedside manner. It is one of the main ways patients talk about the things they can't quite say. The GP who hears it as decoration is missing roughly a third of the consultation.

Part I — The joke as disclosure

The shame-and-fear topics in primary care are the predictable ones, and they almost all arrive at some point dressed as jokes.

The weight joke. "I know, I know, I've put on a few — Christmas was good to me." The patient who has gained 12 kilos, who knows their GP has noticed, who is preempting the conversation by performing the lecture themselves so it can't be performed at them. The clinical task is not to laugh and validate ("oh, Christmas, ha"), and it is not to immediately pivot into a BMI lecture. It is to notice that the joke is the chief complaint dressed as a deflection, and to ask, gently, whether they want to talk about it. The answer is sometimes no, in which case the right move is to leave the door open. The answer is often, eventually, yes.

The drinking joke. "Oh I might have one or two on the weekend — I won't tell you the actual number, ha ha." The patient is, in clinical fact, telling you the actual number — they are telling you that the actual number is high enough that they're frightened to say it out loud. The AUDIT-C is a useful instrument and one of its quieter virtues is that it gives the clinician a non-judgmental script for asking the question the patient just sidled up to. The wrong move, again, is to laugh and not return to it.

The "I'm getting old" joke. "I went into the kitchen the other day and forgot what I was looking for — I think I'm losing it." Said with a smile, frequently, in patients in their sixties and seventies. Sometimes it is benign and the right answer is to say so honestly. Sometimes it is the first time the patient has admitted, even to themselves, that they are frightened. The clinical task is to ask, with the same casualness the joke was offered: "How often is this happening?" The honest answer often comes back at a different volume than the joke.

The "probably overreacting" joke. "I'm sure it's nothing, but I thought I'd come in just to be told off for being a hypochondriac." Almost always: it is not nothing, and they know it isn't nothing, and they are managing the anxiety of not knowing by performing the role of the silly anxious patient before anyone else can cast them in it. The right response is roughly the opposite of agreeing with the self-deprecation. "It's not silly to come in. What made you decide to come this week?" — said matter-of-factly, treats the visit as legitimate and gives the patient permission to drop the protective framing.

The "I'm such an idiot" joke. Around medication non-adherence, missed appointments, not following advice, returning with the thing you told them to come back about three months ago. The shame is doing the same work the weight joke does — performing the lecture before anyone else can. The clinical move is not to absolve and not to lecture. It is to be a bit dull about it: "Right, so let's figure out what's actually going on with the medication." Treating the missed dose as a problem-solving question rather than a moral failure usually unsticks more than reassurance does.

The pattern across all of these: the joke is functioning as a permission slip. The patient is asking, with the laugh, whether the topic can be discussed without humiliation. The clinician's response — laugh-and-move-on, or receive-and-make-space — determines whether the consultation gets to the actual concern.

Part II — What the data actually show

The empirical literature on humour in clinical encounters is small but more interesting than it has any right to be.

The most-cited recent paper is Phillips and colleagues' 2018 analysis of 112 recorded primary and specialty care encounters [1]. They found humour in 59% of consultations — 131 distinct instances across 66 encounters — introduced almost equally by patients (63 instances) and clinicians (66) [1]. The most common topic of humour was the patient's medical condition itself (31% of instances), and humour clustered specifically in the counselling phase of the consultation rather than in history-taking or examination [1]. The authors' interpretation, framed cautiously: humour is doing work in clinical encounters that the formal model of consultation hasn't really accounted for, and a meaningful fraction of it is about easing the discussion of difficult topics.

Schöpf and colleagues' 2017 qualitative analysis of diabetes consultations went deeper into the function side [2]. They identified humour being deployed to soften disagreement, to manage the asymmetry of medical advice-giving, to introduce sensitive topics, and — interestingly — to manage the patient's own discomfort with not having met treatment goals. The clinician's task in their data was less about producing humour than about correctly receiving it.

The dark side of the literature is also worth knowing. Smith-Oka's 2022 ethnographic work in obstetric care documented humour as a vehicle for what the author called "obstetric violence" — humour used by clinicians at patients' expense, humour that intensified shame rather than dispelled it, humour that punched down [3]. The asymmetry of who is speaking matters. A patient making a joke about her body in clinic is doing something structurally different from a clinician making a joke about that same body. The first is often disclosure. The second is often harm.

The general principle this body of work points to: humour in clinical encounters is information, but the direction of the information depends on who is initiating it, what they are joking about, and whether the joke moves the consultation toward the patient's concerns or away from them.

Part III — When humour is defence, not disclosure

The disclosure framing is one of two things humour commonly does in the consult. The other is defence.

The defensive joke is structurally different. It isn't trying to crack the door — it's trying to keep it shut. The patient who jokes constantly to maintain rapport at the surface and prevent any actual content from being discussed. The patient who deflects every serious question with a one-liner. The patient who reassures the doctor that everything is fine, often with a laugh, in a pattern that is statistically more common in women than in men and that is connected, in the literature on health-care interactions, to a learned strategy of not being seen as a difficult patient [4].

Distinguishing the disclosure joke from the defence joke is mostly about what happens when you slow down. The disclosure joke is followed, if you make space, by the actual concern. The defence joke is followed, if you make space, by another joke. Both are clinically meaningful information. The defence joke usually means there is something the patient is not yet able to discuss, and the right clinical move is not to break through it but to register it, leave the door open, and come back another visit.

The hardest version of the defence pattern, and the one worth being explicit about, is deadpan suicidal humour. "Some days I think I'd be better off not being here, ha ha — sorry, dark joke." Said in the same tone as the weight joke. Sometimes it's a passing dark moment that the patient has metabolised and is sharing as evidence that they're handling things. Sometimes it's the only way the patient can introduce suicidality at all. The wrong response is to laugh along; the wrong response is also to immediately escalate into formal risk-assessment language that closes the conversation before it has opened. The right response is somewhere quieter: "I noticed that joke. Can you tell me a bit more about what you meant by it?" Said without alarm, without performance, without the patient feeling that they have triggered a protocol. What follows is sometimes a clarification that yes, it was just a dark joke. What follows is sometimes the first honest conversation about suicidality that this patient has ever had. The clinician's job is to make either outcome possible.

The general principle: when in doubt, the joke that touches on serious territory is information worth pausing on, regardless of how it was framed.

Part IV — When humour disappears

This is the section that is most specific to general practice, because it depends on knowing the patient over years.

Loss of humour is a clinical sign. The patient who used to be funny and isn't anymore is telling the longitudinal GP something the cross-sectional consultation cannot pick up.

In depression, the loss of humour appreciation is part of the broader anhedonic picture, and is usually one of the symptoms the patient mentions with the most precision. "I can't laugh at things any more. My partner showed me a video that would normally have had me on the floor and I just looked at it." The PHQ-9 doesn't ask this directly, but in clinical practice the question — "have you noticed that you're not finding things funny lately?" — picks up depressive cognition reliably and tends to land less defensively than the standard mood questions.

In frontotemporal dementia, particularly the behavioural variant, the literature documents a specific and clinically useful pattern: changes in humour appreciation precede other dementia symptoms by an average of nine years [5]. Patients shift away from satirical and absurdist comedy (which requires perspective-taking and theory of mind) toward more obvious slapstick. They develop a darker humour, sometimes inappropriate humour — laughing at funerals, at others' misfortune, at the wrong moments [5,6]. In the most florid form, "Witzelsucht" — compulsive joking, often punning, often with a flat or shallow quality — is a classic frontal-lobe sign [7]. The Clark et al 2015 study found altered humour responses in 100% of frontotemporal dementia patients and approximately 40% of Alzheimer's patients [5]. These are the kinds of changes that family members register before they have language for them — the partner who says "he's not really himself anymore, his sense of humour has changed" is reporting a clinical sign that the formal cognitive screens may not yet detect.

The longitudinal GP is the clinician most likely to register this. The hospital-based clinician sees the patient in a 20-minute encounter for the first time. The GP has known them for fifteen years. When the patient who used to make the same dry joke on every visit stops making it, that is information.

A related and less-studied phenomenon: the change in what a patient finds funny in the consultation itself. The longstanding patient who used to share a dry observation about something in the news, and now doesn't — or worse, who now finds funny things they previously wouldn't have — should prompt a quiet wondering about whether something has shifted. Sometimes the answer is "they've started antidepressants and the affect has flattened". Sometimes it's "they've had an unrecognised stroke". Sometimes it's "they've started drinking more". Sometimes it's the early phase of a dementing illness. The information is not specific to a diagnosis. It is specific to something has changed.

Part V — The clinician's own humour

What about the doctor's joke? The literature is more cautious here, and rightly so.

When clinician humour helps, it tends to be in a few specific registers. Self-deprecation — the doctor admitting they don't know something, or owning a mistake, with humour — humanises the asymmetry of the consultation and tends to make patients more willing to disclose. Warmth-coded humour — the small dry observation, the pun about the weather, the comment that signals "I am a person and so are you" — does real work in establishing the relationship that the rest of the consultation rests on. Tension-deflating humour — used carefully, to make space in a consultation that has become too tight — can rescue a stuck conversation, particularly when the patient initiates first and the clinician matches.

When it doesn't help, it usually fails in a few predictable ways. Humour that punches down — at the patient's body, history, intelligence, choices — does what punching down always does: it intensifies shame and closes disclosure [3]. Humour as deflection — the clinician who jokes to avoid sitting with a difficult emotion the patient has just expressed — leaves the patient stranded with whatever they were trying to share. Humour at the wrong moment — the breaking-bad-news consultation, the disclosure of abuse, the moment of acute fear — reads as the clinician's discomfort with the gravity of the situation, and the patient registers that they are alone with the weight of it.

The asymmetry matters. A patient making a self-deprecating joke about her body is doing one thing. A doctor making a self-deprecating joke about his own body is doing a second thing. A doctor making a joke about the patient's body is doing a third thing entirely, and that third thing is rarely a good idea regardless of the rapport. Who can make which joke is structurally constrained by power, and the constraint is appropriate.

The decline in clinician humour — which older GPs sometimes lament and younger GPs are sometimes accused of representing — is, in the honest version, partly a loss and partly a correction. The correction is that medicine used to permit a lot of humour at patients' expense that we are now appropriately less tolerant of. The loss is that some of the warmth and texture of the longstanding doctor-patient relationship was carried in casual humour that the more sanitised modern consultation has less room for. Both things can be true at once.

A working position

What I try to do, in summary, with humour in clinic:

Treat patient jokes as information, not decoration. Specifically, treat self-deprecating jokes about weight, drinking, sex, ageing, cognition, and "probably nothing" as the disclosure they usually are. Do not laugh and move on. Make space, casually, for the actual concern.

Distinguish disclosure jokes (which open if you slow down) from defence jokes (which deflect if you slow down). Both are information. Treat them differently.

Take deadpan suicidal jokes seriously without being alarming. The point is to make space, not to trigger a protocol. Ask gently. The patient's clarification is the clinical content.

Notice when the longstanding patient stops being funny, or starts being funny in a new way. The longitudinal advantage of general practice is the only place this gets seen.

Match patient humour rather than initiating it, most of the time. Use self-deprecation freely. Avoid jokes about the patient's body, history, or choices. Avoid humour at moments of gravity. Recognise that the asymmetry of the consultation makes some kinds of jokes safe for the patient that are not safe for the clinician.

And underneath all of it: register that humour is one of the main ways patients say the unsayable. The consultation that doesn't make room for that is, in roughly a third of cases, missing the point of the visit.
