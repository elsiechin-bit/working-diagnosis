Blood cancers in GP land

MGUS, CLL, MPNs, lymphoma survivors, and the patient on ibrutinib who turns up with a bruise. The conditions haematology now mostly manages from a distance, and what the long-term GP role actually involves.

2 May 2026

A patient turns up for her annual review. She has had MGUS for nine years. Her cousin had myeloma. Every year she gets a paraprotein measured, a full blood count, a U&E, a calcium. Every year she asks the same question ‚Äî does this mean I'm getting cancer? ‚Äî and every year the answer is the same: not today, probably not next year, but we keep watching.

The conversation is not glamorous. It is not what most GPs imagine when they think about cancer care. But it is, increasingly, what cancer care for the most common blood malignancies actually looks like ‚Äî long surveillance, brief treatment phases, longer surveillance again, decades of relationship with a haematology team that the patient might see every six to twelve months and a GP they see every couple of months in between.

Blood malignancies have, over the last fifteen years, become long-term conditions. Most patients with chronic lymphocytic leukaemia, chronic myeloproliferative neoplasms, monoclonal gammopathies, and treated indolent lymphomas spend most of their disease course not on intensive treatment, but on surveillance, oral targeted therapy, or long-tail post-treatment monitoring. The acute leukaemias remain a different story ‚Äî those patients live in haematology for the duration of their treatment and most of their post-transplant care. But the chronic and indolent malignancies ‚Äî which are most blood malignancies, especially in older patients ‚Äî have moved decisively into shared-care territory.

The GP role in that territory is not "diagnose and refer." It is the years of monitoring afterward, the first-contact assessment of every intercurrent symptom, the pharmacovigilance for a generation of new oral agents that GPs were not trained on, and the relationship that holds the patient through the years between haematology appointments. This piece is about what that work actually involves, condition by condition.

MGUS ‚Äî the most common premalignancy you'll never feel certain about

MGUS ‚Äî monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance ‚Äî is found in approximately 3% of people aged 50 and over and 5% of those aged 70 and over [1,2]. It is mostly an incidental finding: the patient has chronic back pain or anaemia or fatigue, the GP runs a wide screen, the protein electrophoresis comes back with a small monoclonal band, and a 25-year clinical relationship begins.

The headline number is that MGUS progresses to multiple myeloma, Waldenstr√∂m's macroglobulinaemia, AL amyloidosis, or related disorders at approximately 1% per year [2]. The cumulative risk over time is meaningful ‚Äî roughly 10% at 10 years, 18% at 20 years, 28% at 30 years [3] ‚Äî but the per-year risk is low enough that the right clinical posture is steady annual review, not anxiety.

Risk stratification matters and is mostly straightforward. The Mayo Clinic three-factor model uses three readily available variables: monoclonal protein <15 g/L vs ‚â•15 g/L; IgG isotype vs non-IgG (IgA or IgM); and normal vs abnormal serum free light chain ratio [2]. Low-risk MGUS (M-protein <15 g/L, IgG, normal FLC ratio) has approximately 5% lifetime risk of progression and can be followed every 2-3 years after initial confirmation [2]. Intermediate and high-risk MGUS need annual follow-up for life [2].

The practical primary care surveillance pattern, for the patient on long-term GP follow-up after haematology has signed off:

  • Annual full blood count, U&E, calcium, total protein, albumin, serum protein electrophoresis with paraprotein quantification, serum free light chains
  • Symptom review specifically targeted at CRAB features: hyperCalcaemia, Renal impairment, Anaemia, Bone pain
  • Re-referral to haematology if: new symptoms suggesting progression; paraprotein rises by ‚â•25% or ‚â•5 g/L; new anaemia, renal impairment, or hypercalcaemia; new bone pain or unexplained fractures

The piece most often missed is symptom review. The annual blood-screen-and-letter pattern can become so routine that the actual conversation about whether the patient is feeling well is skipped. The clinically useful prompt ‚Äî "have you had any new bone pain, any rib pain that's not gone away, any new fatigue or anaemia, any change in how often you're getting infections?" ‚Äî takes 30 seconds and is the part of the visit most likely to detect early progression. The blood tests show changes that a careful symptom history often picks up first.

The other piece worth being explicit about: MGUS patients have heard the word "cancer" attached to their condition. Many live with low-grade anxiety about it for years. A clear, repeated, calmly-stated framing ‚Äî "this is something we watch, the chance of it doing anything in any given year is small, we'd see the changes" ‚Äî is part of the clinical work, not separate from it.

CLL ‚Äî the chronic leukaemia that mostly waits

Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia is the most common adult leukaemia in Western countries. The clinical picture has shifted in the last decade in two directions simultaneously. The first: watch-and-wait remains standard for asymptomatic early-stage disease, often for years before any treatment is needed [4]. The second: when treatment is indicated, the era of FCR chemoimmunotherapy has largely ended ‚Äî first-line treatment in 2026 is much more often a BTK inhibitor (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib, or zanubrutinib) or a venetoclax-based regimen, often given as oral therapy at home [4,5].

The GP role in the watch-and-wait phase is significant. The haematology team typically reviews these patients every 3-12 months. Between visits, the patient lives in primary care. The clinical points worth being explicit about:

  • Lymphocytosis on routine FBC is the usual reason these patients are picked up. A persistent absolute lymphocyte count >5 √- 10‚Åπ/L in an older patient warrants haematology referral and flow cytometry, not annual repeat to see if it goes away.
  • Once diagnosed, GPs are often the first to spot early indications for treatment: progressive fatigue out of proportion to other causes, drenching night sweats, unintentional weight loss, increasing lymphadenopathy or splenomegaly noticed by the patient, recurrent infections, rapidly rising lymphocyte count (lymphocyte doubling time <6 months is a treatment indication), new cytopenias.
  • CLL patients have a real and durable immunodeficiency, both from the disease itself (hypogammaglobulinaemia is common) and increasingly from treatment. Vaccinate accordingly ‚Äî annual influenza, pneumococcal (PCV13 then PPSV23 per current schedule), shingles (Shingrix is preferred over Zostavax in any immunocompromised patient and is now the only Pharmac-funded shingles vaccine for the at-risk groups), COVID-19 boosters per the immunocompromised schedule.
  • Live vaccines are contraindicated in patients on or recently off treatment. Yellow fever, MMR (live attenuated), and Zostavax (the older live shingles vaccine, no longer funded) all need haematology input.
  • Routine skin checks matter ‚Äî non-melanoma skin cancer is more common in CLL and behaves more aggressively. Annual skin review is reasonable and often falls to the GP.

The surveillance bloods for a CLL patient on watch-and-wait are typically a 3-6 monthly FBC, with U&E, LFTs, LDH, and immunoglobulins less often. Beyond the numbers, the most clinically useful question is the same as in MGUS: how is the patient actually feeling? Symptom-led detection of progression is often earlier than the FBC alone would suggest.

Lymphoma survivors ‚Äî the cohort that has been growing for thirty years

The cohort of long-term lymphoma survivors in NZ general practice is large and growing. Five-year survival for diffuse large B-cell lymphoma now exceeds 60%; for Hodgkin lymphoma it exceeds 85%; for follicular lymphoma the median survival is well over 15 years. These patients are increasingly returning to general practice as their primary medical home for the rest of their lives, with haematology either discharging them or seeing them only annually after the five-year mark.

The clinical surveillance task has two parts: late effects of treatment, and detection of recurrence or second malignancies.

Late effects of treatment vary by what the patient received but the major categories are reasonably consistent:

  • Cardiovascular disease from anthracycline chemotherapy and from chest/mediastinal radiotherapy. Cardiomyopathy can present years to decades after exposure, and accelerated coronary disease is well-documented after mediastinal radiotherapy for Hodgkin lymphoma [6]. Aggressive cardiovascular risk factor management ‚Äî blood pressure, lipids, diabetes, smoking ‚Äî is appropriate, often with lower thresholds than in the general population.
  • Second malignancies, particularly breast cancer in women who received chest radiotherapy under age 30 (typically Hodgkin lymphoma survivors). NZ breast screening starts at 45, which may be too late for these women ‚Äî many international guidelines recommend starting MRI surveillance at 25-30 years old or 8-10 years post-radiotherapy, whichever is later. This is worth checking with haematology specifically for any female lymphoma survivor with chest radiotherapy in their history.
  • Thyroid dysfunction after neck or chest radiotherapy. Annual TSH is reasonable.
  • Infertility, early menopause, and bone density loss from chemotherapy.
  • Persistent fatigue and cognitive symptoms ("chemo-brain") which are real, often under-acknowledged, and can persist for years.

Surveillance for recurrence in long-term remission is increasingly low-tech ‚Äî symptom-directed history and examination at routine visits is the active ingredient. Routine surveillance imaging (CT scans) in asymptomatic patients in long-term remission has been moved away from in current guidelines because of the radiation exposure burden and the fact that most recurrences are detected clinically before they show up on imaging.

The lymphoma survivor's annual review, in primary care terms, is reasonably structured: cardiovascular risk factor review, symptom-directed history, examination including lymph node and abdominal exam, FBC and basic biochemistry, TSH if relevant radiotherapy history, plus age- and treatment-appropriate cancer screening. It's a fifteen-minute consult that does substantial clinical work.

MPNs ‚Äî polycythaemia vera and essential thrombocythaemia

The chronic myeloproliferative neoplasms ‚Äî polycythaemia vera (PV), essential thrombocythaemia (ET), and primary myelofibrosis ‚Äî are the older end of the haematology-meets-primary-care space. PV and ET are typically managed for years on oral therapy (low-dose aspirin and, in higher-risk patients, hydroxyurea) with periodic haematology review.

The clinical thesis for both PV and ET is roughly the same: the cause of premature death is thrombosis. Management is therefore primarily thrombosis prevention, not symptom management or cure [7,8].

For PV specifically, the targets are reasonably clean:

  • Haematocrit <0.45 is the central target, supported by the CYTO-PV trial which showed substantially reduced thrombotic events with this target compared with 0.45-0.50 [9]
  • Low-dose aspirin (75-100 mg daily) for most patients in the absence of contraindications
  • Hydroxyurea as first-line cytoreduction in high-risk patients (age >60 or prior thrombosis)
  • Regular phlebotomy to maintain the haematocrit target
  • Aggressive cardiovascular risk factor management ‚Äî these patients are at amplified thrombotic risk and conventional CV risk reduction (BP, lipids, smoking, diabetes) carries amplified benefit

The GP role between haematology visits is the practical surveillance: 3-monthly FBC if on hydroxyurea, BP review, annual U&E and LFTs, and being the first to spot symptoms of thrombosis or cytopenia. Hydroxyurea is generally well-tolerated but worth knowing the side-effect profile: macrocytosis (expected), oral and lower-leg ulcers (occasional but characteristic), squamous cell skin cancers (increased risk ‚Äî annual skin checks worth it), and rarely cytopenias requiring dose reduction.

For ET, the management algorithm is similar but more risk-stratified. Low-risk ET (age <60, no prior thrombosis, JAK2 wild-type) often needs only aspirin and surveillance; high-risk ET (age >60 or prior thrombosis or very high platelet counts) generally adds hydroxyurea. The driver mutation matters ‚Äî JAK2-mutated ET has higher thrombotic risk than CALR-mutated or MPL-mutated disease [7].

A practical primary care point for both: aspirin in PV and ET is for thrombosis prevention, not cardiovascular primary prevention in the conventional sense. The risk-benefit calculation is different from the general population's ‚Äî these patients should generally stay on aspirin even when conventional cardiovascular guidelines might pull non-MPN patients off it.

Smouldering myeloma and myeloma on treatment

A note on the evolving end of myeloma management. Smouldering multiple myeloma ‚Äî the entity between MGUS and active myeloma ‚Äî is now sometimes treated rather than observed in higher-risk forms, based on QuiRedex and E3A06 trial data showing improved progression-free and overall survival with early lenalidomide-based therapy in high-risk smouldering disease [10]. Most NZ practice still observes lower-risk smouldering myeloma, but this is one of the areas where the boundary between watching and treating is moving.

Myeloma itself is now a long-term condition in a way it wasn't twenty years ago. Median survival has roughly doubled with the introduction of proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib, carfilzomib), immunomodulatory drugs (lenalidomide, pomalidomide), monoclonal antibodies (daratumumab), and now BCMA-directed CAR-T and bispecific antibodies. Patients live for years on rotating regimens, mostly oral, with the GP managing intercurrent illness and side effects between haematology cycles.

What the GP should know about the new oral haematology agents

This is the part most GPs were not trained on, and where being aware of side-effect profiles makes a real clinical difference.

BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib, acalabrutinib, zanubrutinib) for CLL and some lymphomas. The major side effects worth recognising at the door:

  • Bleeding and bruising ‚Äî affects platelet function rather than count. The patient on ibrutinib who presents with new bruising is showing an expected on-target effect, not new disease [11]. Surgery and dental procedures need a hold of the drug, ideally with haematology guidance ‚Äî typically held 3-7 days pre and post procedure.
  • Atrial fibrillation ‚Äî substantially elevated incidence on ibrutinib (around 6-10% over the first year), lower with second-generation agents. New AF in a patient on ibrutinib is a haematology-flagged event, not a routine new AF. Anticoagulation choice is complicated by the bleeding risk.
  • Infections ‚Äî particularly respiratory and herpes zoster. Vaccinate, treat infections early and aggressively.
  • Diarrhoea ‚Äî common (10-20%), usually manageable.
  • Hypertension ‚Äî often emerges over months. BP monitoring matters.

Venetoclax (BCL-2 inhibitor) for CLL and AML ‚Äî tumour lysis syndrome is the major concern at initiation, managed in the inpatient or close-outpatient setting with hydration and monitoring. Once established, the drug is usually well-tolerated but neutropenia is common.

Lenalidomide and pomalidomide for myeloma ‚Äî fatigue, cytopenias (particularly neutropenia and thrombocytopenia), thromboembolism risk (these patients are usually on prophylactic anticoagulation or aspirin), and rash. Substantial risk of teratogenicity in any patient of reproductive age ‚Äî the prescribing programme in NZ is strictly controlled.

Hydroxyurea for MPNs ‚Äî covered above.

Daratumumab (anti-CD38 monoclonal antibody) for myeloma ‚Äî interferes with blood cross-matching for months after exposure, a practical issue if the patient needs urgent transfusion. The blood bank needs to know.

The general principle: any patient on a haematology oral agent who presents with a new symptom ‚Äî bleeding, bruising, palpitations, fever, new rash, persistent diarrhoea ‚Äî deserves a quick check of the agent's known side-effect profile and a low threshold for haematology contact.

A working position

The blood malignancy work in primary care is mostly long-tail: surveillance of premalignant states, watch-and-wait monitoring, lymphoma survivorship review, MPN dose-and-target management, and pharmacovigilance for a generation of oral targeted therapies. Most of it is reassurance-and-routine, occasionally interrupted by a finding that matters and a referral that needs to happen quickly.

The structural pattern worth noticing: haematology has progressively shifted these patients into shared care because they live longer than they used to. The shift is mostly good ‚Äî patients are seen by people who know them, in their own communities, between specialist visits. The risk is the shared care that becomes nobody's care: the patient whose annual MGUS review slides because nobody is sure whose job it is, the lymphoma survivor whose post-radiotherapy breast surveillance falls through the cracks because the standard breast screening programme wasn't designed for her exposure history, the CLL patient whose new AF gets started on apixaban without anyone checking that ibrutinib is on the medication list.

The GP work is not the dramatic part of cancer care. It is the steady part. It is not less important for being steady. It is the relationship that holds the patient through the decade after the diagnosis, and into the decade after that, and the work that determines whether those decades go well.
