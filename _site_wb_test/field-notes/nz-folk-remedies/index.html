Folk remedies, plant medicine, and the GP consultation

From horopito to hokey pokey, what New Zealanders are actually taking, what the evidence says, and why St John's Wort is the most dangerous thing in the health food aisle.

3 May 2026

Somewhere between the prescription pad and the waiting room, a significant proportion of New Zealanders are managing their health with remedies that did not come from a GP. They are rubbing kawakawa balm into arthritic knees, drinking noni juice on the advice of a cousin from Samoa, taking St John's Wort for depression because they read that it works, dosing themselves with colloidal silver, and applying mānuka honey to wounds with a confidence that ranges from culturally transmitted wisdom to something they found on a wellness Instagram account.

Most of them are not telling their GP. Studies across primary care settings consistently show that patients disclose complementary medicine use at rates well below actual use, partly from anticipating dismissal, partly because they don't think of supplements and herbal remedies as "medicines" in the category their GP needs to know about [1].

This is a problem that is sometimes theoretical — most folk remedies are ineffective but harmless — and occasionally very much not theoretical, in the case of the patient on warfarin who has started taking garlic supplements, or the patient on a combined oral contraceptive who has been managing her anxiety with St John's Wort for the past three months.

This note covers the major folk and plant medicine traditions in New Zealand's clinical landscape — rongoā Māori, Pacific plant medicine, and the European-settler tradition of honey, garlic, and whatever is prominently displayed at the pharmacy counter — with a focus on what the evidence actually shows, what the clinically important interactions are, and how to ask about it without conveying that you think the patient has been doing something embarrassing.

Part I — Rongoā Māori

Rongoā Māori is the traditional healing system of Māori, encompassing plant medicine (rongoā rākau), physical therapies (mirimiri and romiromi), and spiritual dimensions of healing that are not separable from the plant medicine in the traditional framework [2]. It is a living practice, not an archival curiosity; there are registered tohunga rongoā (traditional practitioners), and rongoā Māori services are funded in some contexts through the NZ health system.

A GP encountering rongoā in the consultation should approach it with the same genuine interest brought to any healing system that has been refined by generations of empirical use in a specific ecological context. Some of what is claimed for rongoā Māori is not supported by the clinical evidence base; some of it corresponds reasonably well to what we know pharmacologically; and the plants involved are often interesting.

Kawakawa (Piper excelsum) is the most culturally prominent of Māori medicinal plants, used in rongoā for a broad range of conditions — as a topical application for skin conditions, joint pain, and wounds, as a tea for digestive complaints and urinary problems, and in ceremonial contexts [3]. Pharmacological investigation has identified several active compounds in kawakawa including amides, flavonoids, and diayangambin (an alkaloidal compound with some mild pharmacological activity). The anti-inflammatory evidence is modest and largely preclinical; the topical use for skin irritation and joint pain is plausible given the compound profile [3]. Kawakawa is generally considered safe topically. Internal use in large quantities over extended periods has not been systematically studied for safety. Rare allergic reactions are reported. The clinical bottom line: kawakawa is unlikely to harm your patient, the topical use has some biological plausibility, and engaging with it as a legitimate part of their health practice is more useful than dismissing it.

Horopito (Pseudowintera colorata) is the most pharmacologically interesting of NZ's medicinal plants, and the one with the best evidence base for a specific clinical indication. The distinctive red-blotched leaves contain polygodial, a sesquiterpene dialdehyde with genuine, well-characterised antifungal activity [4]. Polygodial disrupts fungal cell membranes, and laboratory studies have demonstrated activity against Candida albicans comparable in some assays to conventional antifungals, with synergistic effects when combined with amphotericin B [4]. The commercial product Kolorex — derived from horopito extract — is used for vaginal and systemic candidiasis and has a small published clinical literature. This is not a folk remedy with merely theoretical plausibility; polygodial is a biologically active compound with a real mechanism. Whether the clinical outcomes in practice match the in-vitro and animal data requires more evidence than currently exists, but horopito is worth knowing about as something that may be taken alongside, or instead of, conventional antifungal treatment — and as a compound with genuine pharmacological activity that may in future have clinical applications.

Mānuka (Leptospermum scoparium) spans several categories. The use of mānuka bark, leaves, and steam in traditional rongoā predates the international mānuka honey industry by centuries. The medicinal use of mānuka honey deserves separate treatment below, but the traditional rongoā use — bark preparations for urinary complaints, leaves as an antiseptic application, steam inhalation — draws on genuine antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties of the plant [3]. The antibacterial activity of mānuka preparations is real; the specific clinical outcomes have been studied most thoroughly in the honey form.

Kūmarahou (Pomaderris kumerahou), known also as gumdigger's soap for its saponin-rich lather when wet, was used traditionally for chest conditions and respiratory complaints. There is a small body of pharmacological work on its compounds but limited clinical evidence. It remains in active rongoā use.

Koromiko (Veronica spp.) was traditionally used for diarrhoea and dysentery. The tannin content provides a plausible mechanism for the antidiarrhoeal effect — astringent action reducing GI secretion — and koromiko tea was widely used by both Māori and European settlers for GI complaints. This is the kind of folk remedy where the traditional use and the pharmacology align reasonably well, even in the absence of a randomised trial.

Part II — Pacific medicine in New Zealand

New Zealand's Pacific communities bring distinct healing traditions that are clinically relevant given the size and demographic characteristics of Pacific populations in NZ.

Noni (Morinda citrifolia, nonu in Samoan and Tongan) is one of the most widely used plant medicines in Pacific communities and one of the most studied Pacific traditional medicines, though the quality of the evidence varies considerably [5]. Noni fruit and juice are used for a range of indications including pain, infection, immune support, and general wellbeing. The pharmacological profile includes iridoid glycosides, polysaccharides, and various phenolic compounds with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties in vitro. The claims for clinical benefit substantially outrun the clinical evidence.

The safety concern that is worth knowing: noni juice has been implicated in cases of hepatotoxicity, including several published case reports of acute hepatitis in patients with no other identified cause, and one case of acute liver failure [6]. The causative mechanism is not established. The cases are rare relative to the volume of noni consumed worldwide, and most are associated with very high intake. Nonetheless, patients with pre-existing liver disease should be advised cautiously, and noni should be included in the differential when a Pacific patient presents with unexplained hepatitis.

Coconut oil occupies an interesting position: simultaneously promoted on wellness platforms as a panacea and periodically condemned in cardiovascular risk discussions for its saturated fat content. The direct clinical evidence for coconut oil's therapeutic uses — oral health (oil pulling), skin hydration, antimicrobial applications — is limited to small studies and mechanistic arguments. Topically, it functions as a reasonable emollient. The concern about saturated fat intake relates to large quantities consumed orally rather than typical topical use. Oil pulling (swishing coconut oil in the mouth for 15–20 minutes) is widely practised in some communities; it may have modest effects on oral bacterial load; it is not a substitute for brushing or flossing.

Herbal preparations from Fiji, Tonga, Samoa, and other Pacific nations are sometimes brought to New Zealand, obtained through community networks, or ordered internationally. These are less systematically studied than either rongoā Māori or the large European herbal pharmacopoeia. The practical clinical point is to ask, specifically and without judgement, whether patients are using any medicines or remedies brought from home or obtained through their community — both because of the potential for interactions and because it is important information for the clinical picture.

Part III — The European-NZ folk pharmacy

The Pākehā folk medicine tradition in New Zealand draws from English, Scottish, and Irish settler customs, modified by a century and a half of contact with both Māori and Pacific practices and, more recently, the globalised wellness industry. It is characterised less by a coherent traditional system than by a collection of kitchen-cupboard remedies of varying provenance.

Honey and lemon for colds and sore throats is the canonical example: universally practised, mildly plausible, difficult to test rigorously, and harmless. Honey has genuine demulcent properties and mild antimicrobial activity (hydrogen peroxide from glucose oxidase activity); the evidence for honey in upper respiratory symptoms, particularly in children, is actually reasonably positive [7]. Lemon contributes vitamin C at a dose likely below clinical significance for most well-nourished adults. The remedy as a whole probably works about as well as most OTC symptomatic treatments — which is to say modestly — and it is pleasant.

Mānuka honey deserves specific treatment because it occupies an unusual position: a folk remedy that has generated a substantial clinical evidence base, particularly for wound care [8]. The specific antibacterial properties of high-UMF mānuka honey derive from methylglyoxal (MGO), formed from dihydroxyacetone in mānuka nectar. Unlike the hydrogen peroxide activity of ordinary honey — which is inactivated by the enzyme catalase present in wound tissue — methylglyoxal is stable in the wound environment. High-grade mānuka honey has demonstrated efficacy in wound care for chronic wounds, burns, and MRSA-colonised wounds in clinical studies and is used in medical-grade honey dressings (including Medihoney, produced in Australia and New Zealand) [8,9]. This is genuine evidence for a genuine mechanism. The caveat: the UMF rating matters — a UMF 5+ product from the supermarket has different MGO concentrations from a UMF 20+ medical-grade product. The folk use of household mānuka honey on cuts and minor wounds is reasonable and probably beneficial; the evidence for treating complex or infected wounds is at UMF grades typically not found in kitchen cupboards.

Garlic (Allium sativum) has been proposed for everything from cardiovascular risk reduction to antimicrobial use. The evidence for modest blood pressure and lipid effects is present but not compelling at food intake quantities [10]. The clinically relevant issue is antiplatelet activity: garlic supplements, particularly in higher doses, inhibit platelet aggregation and may potentiate the effect of anticoagulants and antiplatelet agents. This does not mean patients on aspirin or warfarin need to stop cooking with garlic; it means that concentrated garlic supplements (in tablet or high-dose capsule form) are worth asking about in patients on anticoagulation, particularly perioperatively.

Ginger (Zingiber officinale) for nausea is one of the better-supported folk remedies, with a number of small randomised trials supporting its use in pregnancy-related nausea and motion sickness, though effect sizes are modest and the evidence for cancer chemotherapy-related nausea is mixed [11]. Ginger is safe in pregnancy at typical food and supplement doses. High-dose ginger supplements have antiplatelet properties — a pattern beginning to emerge as a theme.

Echinacea for colds is widely used and reasonably well studied. The Cochrane review concludes there is some evidence for modest reduction in cold duration and severity, though the evidence varies by product (species, plant part, preparation method) and the effect size is small [12]. It is generally safe for short-term use. It is theoretically immunostimulatory, which raises concerns in patients on immunosuppressants, though clinical evidence for this interaction is limited.

Colloidal silver is worth a specific mention because it is persistently popular in NZ wellness culture and is both ineffective and potentially harmful. Colloidal silver (silver nanoparticles or ionic silver in suspension) has no credible evidence base for any internal indication in a modern clinical context, and chronic or high-dose use causes argyria — a permanent blue-grey discolouration of the skin from silver deposition — and may cause renal and neurological harm [13]. This is distinct from silver-containing wound dressings (such as Aquacel Ag), which are topically effective antimicrobial products with well-characterised safety profiles. Internal use of colloidal silver is the category to advise against clearly.

Apple cider vinegar for metabolic and digestive indications has attracted substantial wellness industry promotion. The clinical evidence does not support the major claims. There are small studies suggesting modest effects on postprandial glycaemia when taken with meals, plausibly through delayed gastric emptying. The practical risks: dental enamel erosion from regular undiluted use; oesophageal irritation; and the opportunity cost of patients managing glycaemia with vinegar instead of evidence-based interventions.

Part IV — The interaction that matters most: St John's Wort

St John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) is not a uniquely New Zealand phenomenon, but it is sufficiently prevalent in NZ supplement use and sufficiently dangerous in specific combinations that it warrants its own section.

St John's Wort is one of the better-evidenced herbal treatments in the entire complementary medicine pharmacopoeia. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirm efficacy for mild to moderate depression comparable to standard antidepressants, with a generally more favourable side-effect profile than tricyclics and a somewhat more favourable profile than SSRIs for short-term use [14]. If the only clinical question were "does it work for depression?" the answer would be a qualified yes, for mild to moderate severity.

The problem is pharmacokinetic. St John's Wort is a potent inducer of cytochrome P450 3A4 and P-glycoprotein, two of the most important metabolic pathways in clinical pharmacology [15]. The list of drugs with clinically significant reduced efficacy when St John's Wort is co-administered includes:

  • Combined oral contraceptives and progestogen-only pills: reduced plasma concentrations leading to contraceptive failure. This is the interaction with the most significant documented consequences — unintended pregnancies have been attributed to it [15].
  • Warfarin: reduced anticoagulant effect, INR destabilisation. Both initiating and stopping St John's Wort in a patient on warfarin will cause INR shifts.
  • Antiretrovirals: particularly protease inhibitors and NNRTIs. Co-administration is contraindicated; the plasma level reductions are sufficient to risk virological failure and resistance development.
  • Ciclosporin: reduced plasma levels in transplant patients, with documented episodes of acute rejection attributed to St John's Wort use [16].
  • Some chemotherapy agents: including irinotecan; reduced exposure may compromise oncological outcomes.
  • Digoxin: reduced plasma concentrations.
  • Certain antiepileptics, SSRIs (serotonin syndrome risk), triptan medications.

The patient who is managing their low mood with St John's Wort, has not mentioned it to anyone, and is on a combined oral contraceptive is not a theoretical clinical scenario. It is a scenario that almost certainly occurs regularly in NZ general practice without being identified, because the product is bought over the counter, is not perceived as a medicine, and the patient is not asked.

The practical intervention is simple: ask. Specifically. "Do you take any vitamins, supplements, herbal remedies, or anything from a health food shop?" is a question worth adding to the regular medicines review, and worth asking when prescribing any of the drugs above.

Part V — How to ask, and what to do with the answer

The clinical evidence on disclosure rates is consistent: patients are more likely to disclose complementary medicine use when asked directly in a non-judgemental way, and less likely to disclose when they anticipate dismissal or criticism [1,17]. The GP who visibly sighs at the mention of kawakawa balm will be conducting incomplete medicines reviews for the rest of their career.

A useful frame: most folk remedies are being used because the patient wants to take care of their own health, which is a goal the GP shares. The clinical question is whether the remedy is safe, whether it interacts with anything, and whether it is being used instead of rather than alongside evidence-based treatment. The answer to most of these questions for most folk remedies is: broadly safe, probably no interactions, and used alongside rather than instead of. The conversation is then brief.

The exceptions — St John's Wort in a patient on the pill, colloidal silver in a patient who has been taking it for six months, high-dose garlic supplements in a patient on warfarin, noni juice in a patient with elevated LFTs — are worth knowing precisely so they can be identified when they arise.

There is also a category of folk remedy use where the relevant intervention is not to advise against the remedy but to ensure the underlying problem is being adequately managed. The patient who is managing chronic pain primarily with anti-inflammatory herbs and topical kawakawa is a patient whose pain management needs a proper clinical review. The patient who is treating what sounds like a depressive episode with St John's Wort has sought treatment for depression; the clinical response is to take the diagnosis seriously, assess severity, and have a conversation about the treatment evidence — including, if appropriate, the evidence for St John's Wort itself.

A working position

New Zealand has a rich and genuinely varied folk medicine landscape, shaped by one of the world's most distinctive indigenous botanical traditions, by Pacific healing practices, and by the usual mix of European folk remedies and contemporary wellness industry products. Some of this is effective. Some of it is plausible. Much of it is harmless. A small portion of it is dangerous in specific combinations.

The GP's role is not to adjudicate the worth of cultural healing practices or to act as a one-person evidence-based medicine watchdog for the health food industry. It is to know enough to ask the right questions, to identify the interactions that matter, and to engage with what patients are doing rather than what they disclose unprompted.

Horopito is interesting. Mānuka honey wound care is genuinely evidence-based at the right grade. Ginger for pregnancy nausea is reasonable. St John's Wort with the combined pill is a clinical problem.

And if someone is taking colloidal silver: have that conversation before the blue-grey tinge becomes permanent.
