Mandatory reporting in general practice

What NZ GPs are legally required to report, what they are professionally obliged to report, and why the gap between those two categories is where most of the clinical difficulty lives.

3 May 2026

The phrase "mandatory reporting" is used loosely in clinical conversation, and the looseness creates real problems. It conflates at least three distinct categories of obligation: things the law requires you to report on pain of criminal or civil liability; things your professional regulatory body requires you to report as a condition of professional registration; and things that the ethical weight of your position makes it effectively impossible not to report, even in the absence of a legal mandate. These three categories overlap but they are not the same, and understanding which applies in a given situation changes both the clinical calculus and the conversation with the patient.

What makes this territory particularly complex in New Zealand is that NZ sits in an unusual position internationally. For the reporting category that generates the most clinical and ethical difficulty — child abuse and neglect — NZ does not have mandatory reporting in the legal sense that Australia, the United States, and Canada do. Most NZ health practitioners believe they are mandatory reporters of child abuse. Most NZ health practitioners are, technically, wrong. The practical implications of that gap are significant, and worth understanding precisely.

This note covers the main reporting obligations for NZ GPs: what is legally required, what is professionally required, and where the ethical tensions are.

Part I — The NZ framework: mandatory, obligatory, and expected

A useful taxonomy.

Legally mandatory reporting means a statute creates a specific duty to report, with potential civil or criminal consequences for failure to comply. In NZ, the clearest examples are notifiable disease reporting, reporting of firearm wounds, and certain death notifications. These are not subject to clinical discretion: the obligation to report is triggered by the clinical finding.

Professionally obligatory reporting means the relevant regulatory body — the Medical Council of NZ, under the Health Practitioners Competence Assurance Act 2003 — has established that failure to report in defined circumstances constitutes professional misconduct or a departure from accepted standards, regardless of whether a specific statute requires it [1]. Reporting concerns about an impaired colleague, or acting on concerns about a child at risk, sits here for most practitioners. The legal risk is not a criminal charge for non-reporting; it is a professional conduct complaint.

Ethically expected reporting is the category most practitioners have in mind when they say "mandatory" in everyday conversation: the situation in which not reporting would be a moral failure even without a clear legal or regulatory mandate. Family violence disclosure, elder abuse, the patient who continues to drive with uncontrolled seizures and doesn't return calls — these situations generate obligations that are real and weighty even where the legal mandate is ambiguous or absent.

The mismatch between what practitioners believe the law requires and what the law actually requires is most significant in child protection, which is where it also matters most.

Part II — Child abuse and neglect: the permissive framework

Section 15 of the Oranga Tamariki Act 1989 provides that "any person who believes that any child or young person has been, or is likely to be, harmed (whether physically, emotionally, or sexually), ill-treated, abused, neglected, or deprived may report the matter to a social worker or a constable" [2].

The operative word is may. Not must. This is a permissive provision, not a mandatory one. It confers a right to report without liability; it does not create a criminal or civil obligation to report. New Zealand has deliberately not adopted the mandated reporter model used in Australia, the United States, and Canada, in which specified professional groups — including health practitioners — face criminal liability for failure to report reasonable suspicions of child abuse.

The implications are several.

First, NZ GPs are not, in the strict legal sense, mandatory reporters of child abuse. A GP who fails to report a genuine concern about child abuse does not commit an offence under the Oranga Tamariki Act. The concern can still be ventilated through a professional conduct complaint, and the Medical Council's position is that GPs have a professional obligation to take appropriate action when they have reasonable grounds to suspect a child is at risk [3]. But "appropriate action" encompasses a range of responses, and the legal exposure for non-reporting is professional rather than criminal.

Second, the "reasonable grounds" threshold matters. Mandatory reporting regimes in Australia and the US typically require reporting on a lower threshold — reasonable suspicion — than the clinical certainty many NZ practitioners have internalized as the threshold for action. Waiting for certainty before acting on child protection concerns is one of the more common and consequential errors in this area. The legal standard, even in a permissive framework, is not proof.

Third, the absence of mandatory reporting does not mean discretion is unlimited. The Medical Council's position, and the HDC's approach in relevant complaints, is that a health practitioner who observes significant indicators of abuse and takes no action may be in breach of professional standards, regardless of the absence of a criminal reporting obligation [3,4]. The practical gap between "may report" in the statute and "must act" in professional expectations is narrower than the statutory language suggests.

Why NZ has not adopted mandatory reporting is a policy question with a substantive answer. The concerns that have persistently shaped NZ policy include: evidence from Australian mandatory reporting regimes that volume of notifications overwhelms the child protection system without producing better outcomes for the children most at risk; evidence that over-reporting of low-risk situations consumes resources that should be directed at high-risk ones; and a specific NZ concern about the impact of mandatory reporting on Māori communities, given the disproportionate and historically fraught involvement of the state in Māori family life and the documented over-representation of Māori children in child protection proceedings [5]. These are not arguments that child abuse should go unreported. They are arguments about whether legal mandates produce the outcomes they intend. The evidence is genuinely mixed [6].

The Oranga Tamariki (Baby Uplift) controversy of 2019 — in which television footage of Oranga Tamariki removing a newborn from a Māori mother at Hawke's Bay Hospital generated a national crisis of confidence in the child protection system — is the background to any discussion of this topic in NZ in 2026 [7]. The subsequent inquiry found systemic issues in how Oranga Tamariki engaged with Māori families. The trust deficit between Māori communities and the state child protection system, and the impact of that deficit on willingness to engage with health services, is directly relevant to how GPs approach child protection conversations with Māori patients.

What to do in practice. The absence of mandatory reporting does not simplify the GP's clinical task. The decision of when to notify Oranga Tamariki and when to manage within the practice or through specialist referral remains a clinical judgement that should be:

  • Documented thoroughly, including the specific findings, the reasoning, and the action taken
  • Made in consultation with a colleague, practice nurse, or the SKIP line (Strengthening Families) or Child Protection Team where uncertainty exists
  • Informed by the indicators in the Practice Toolkit for Child Protection (NZMA and Paediatric Society of NZ) [8]
  • Not delayed by the pursuit of certainty: the threshold is reasonable concern, not proof

When in doubt, consult. The CYF Practice Toolkit advice — "when in doubt, ring" — applies regardless of the legal threshold.

Part III — The clear legal obligations

For these, clinical discretion is limited or absent. The obligation is triggered by the clinical finding.

Notifiable diseases. The Health (Notification) Regulations 2016, made under the Health Act 1956, require medical practitioners to notify the local Medical Officer of Health (MOH) or Regional Public Health unit on suspicion or diagnosis of a scheduled notifiable condition [9]. The list is long and updated periodically; it includes tuberculosis, measles, meningococcal disease, salmonella and other enteric infections, viral hepatitis, HIV (new diagnoses), sexually transmitted infections (under specific provisions), and a range of other conditions. Notification of suspicion is required for several high-priority conditions — you do not wait for confirmatory results before notifying for meningococcal disease or measles.

The reporting mechanism in most regions is an electronic notification through the regional public health unit's system, or by phone for urgent notifications. The obligation is immediate for conditions that require public health action (outbreak investigation, contact tracing), not retrospective. A GP who diagnoses tuberculosis has a legal obligation to notify, not a professional preference.

Gunshot wounds. Section 201 of the Crimes Act 1961 requires any medical practitioner who treats a person for a wound that the practitioner has reasonable cause to believe was caused by a firearm to report the matter to a constable [10]. This is not conditional on certainty about the mechanism, or on the patient's consent, or on whether the wound appears to be self-inflicted or inflicted by another. The obligation exists regardless of the patient's wishes. Failure to report is a criminal offence. The police notification can be made while the patient is being stabilised; it does not require pausing treatment.

Coroner notifications. The Coroners Act 2006 requires that deaths meeting specified criteria be reported to the coroner [11]. These include: deaths where the cause is unknown; deaths that were sudden and unexplained; deaths occurring in circumstances suggesting an element of violence, accident, or self-harm; deaths occurring during or following an anaesthetic or surgical procedure in defined circumstances; deaths that may be due to a medical mishap; and deaths in custody or otherwise involving state care. GPs encounter coroner notification most commonly in the context of unexpected community deaths, deaths in circumstances suggesting suicide, and deaths where the certification of cause is uncertain. The coroner, not the GP, determines whether a post-mortem is required and whether an inquest is held.

Driver fitness. This is the mandatory reporting obligation that generates the most clinical discomfort, because it most directly involves overriding patient confidentiality.

The Land Transport Act 1998 and Land Transport (Driver Licensing) Rule 1999 create an obligation on drivers to self-notify the New Zealand Transport Agency (NZTA) of medical conditions that affect their ability to drive safely [12]. The primary obligation is on the patient. However, where a patient has a condition that clearly affects driving safety, continues to drive, and either refuses to self-report or is not capable of understanding the obligation, the GP's professional obligation — and in some circumstances the legal position — is to notify NZTA.

The Medical Council's guidance is explicit: where a patient refuses to inform NZTA of a condition that creates a significant risk to themselves or others while driving, and where the GP has been unable to persuade them to do so, the GP should notify NZTA directly [3]. This overrides the standard confidentiality obligation because the risk is to third parties. The Privacy Act 2020 permits disclosure where it is necessary to prevent serious threat to safety [13].

The conditions that most commonly generate this obligation in general practice: uncontrolled epilepsy (the seizure-free period for driving is six months off therapy or twelve months on therapy for most licence categories [12]), moderate to severe dementia, uncontrolled type 1 diabetes with hypoglycaemia unawareness, acute stroke with residual deficits, and certain psychiatric conditions affecting insight and judgment. The NZTA medical standards documents (available from the NZTA website) set out the criteria by condition and licence class; these are the clinical reference documents, not the Drivers' Medical Summary, which is the GP reporting form.

The clinical approach: raise the issue directly with the patient first; document the conversation; give the patient the opportunity to self-report; set a clear time limit; if self-reporting has not occurred within that period, notify NZTA. The HDC has upheld complaints against GPs who failed to notify NZTA when it was clinically indicated [4].

Part IV — The obligated-but-not-mandated category

Elder abuse. New Zealand has no mandatory reporting obligation for elder abuse — neither the legal mandatory reporting of child abuse regimes, nor an equivalent professional mandate. The obligation on GPs is professional and ethical: to recognise, to document, to act appropriately, and to refer when the situation requires [3]. The Elder Abuse Response Service (EARS), operated by Age Concern, is the main referral pathway for community elder abuse concerns. The MATES programme and the Interrai Home Care assessment tool both incorporate elder abuse screening. The absence of a legal mandate does not diminish the obligation to act when the clinical picture is concerning.

Impaired practitioners. Section 45 of the Health Practitioners Competence Assurance Act 2003 provides that any health practitioner who has reasonable grounds to believe that a colleague poses a risk of harm to the public may notify the relevant authority (the Medical Council for doctors) [1]. "May" again, technically — but the Medical Council's good medical practice guidance makes clear that a practitioner who knows about a colleague's impairment and takes no action is not meeting professional standards. This is the category most practitioners find emotionally difficult and most underutilise. The Medical Council has a dedicated impaired practitioners process; the NZMA employee assistance programme is another avenue.

Family violence. The Family Violence Act 2018 does not create a mandatory reporting obligation for health professionals [14]. It does require certain agencies — primarily social services — to inquire about safety in specified circumstances. The NZ Health sector approach, reflected in the "Family Violence Assessment and Intervention Guideline" and supported by Ministry of Health guidance, is routine inquiry rather than mandatory reporting: all primary care patients, particularly those with relevant presentations, should be offered the opportunity to disclose family violence, and the pathway for safe referral should be available [15]. The clinical tools (the Composite Abuse Scale, the DV-e-screener) support structured inquiry. The documentation standards and safety planning obligations that follow a disclosure are clinical obligations even where the reporting obligation is not strict.

Part V — The ethical tensions

Confidentiality versus safety. The Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights establishes a strong patient right to confidentiality, and the Privacy Act 2020 creates a legal framework around health information. Both have limits. Disclosure of confidential information without consent is justified where it is necessary to prevent serious threat to the safety of the patient or others, and where the benefit of disclosure outweighs the privacy interest [13]. The examples above — driver notification, gunshot wounds, some child protection notifications — sit within this exception. The judgment in each case is whether the risk is serious enough, and whether disclosure is the least privacy-invasive response that adequately addresses it.

The therapeutic relationship. The concern that mandatory reporting obligations damage the therapeutic relationship is most acute in family violence and child abuse contexts. A patient who fears that disclosing domestic violence will trigger mandatory reporting may stop disclosing, which serves nobody's interests. The evidence on this from Australia's partial mandatory reporting regimes is mixed: some studies suggest mandatory reporting deters disclosure, others suggest that clear protocols reassure patients that help is available [16]. The NZ policy position — routine inquiry with appropriate referral, rather than mandatory reporting — is partly grounded in this concern.

The Oranga Tamariki question for Māori patients. The documented historical and contemporary over-representation of Māori children in care, the experiences of coercive state intervention, and the distrust generated by the 2019 newborn uplift controversy create a specific dynamic in child protection conversations with Māori patients [5,7]. Routine Oranga Tamariki notifications that might be appropriate in other contexts can, in some Māori family situations, trigger processes the family experiences as threatening rather than protective. This does not mean Māori children should receive less protection — manifestly the opposite is true. It means that the clinical and relational work of ensuring child safety in Māori families requires understanding of whānau context, engagement with Māori health services and kaupapa Māori providers where available, and awareness that notification to Oranga Tamariki is not the only or always the best tool for protecting a child's safety.

Clinical uncertainty and over-reporting. The inverse problem is real. A mandatory reporting framework that lowers the threshold to report produces more reporting, which can overwhelm the system and produce false positives with significant consequences for families. The GP who reports every bruise without clinical judgment about context has not met their child protection obligation; they have outsourced it to Oranga Tamariki. The clinical skill is in recognising the patterns that indicate genuine risk — the injury inconsistent with the explanation, the developmental regression, the child who flinches at specific adults, the parent who answers for the child in every interaction — and acting on them proportionately.

Part VI — International comparison

Australia operates a mandated reporter system for child abuse in all states and territories, with health practitioners designated as mandated reporters. The specifics vary by state — Western Australia includes emotional abuse; Queensland extends to certain domestic violence situations; reporting thresholds differ — but the general structure is consistent: reasonable suspicion triggers a mandatory legal obligation to report, and failure to do so is a criminal offence [17]. The Australian system generates substantially more notifications per capita than NZ's; evidence on whether outcomes for children are better as a result is contested.

United Kingdom does not have mandatory reporting of child abuse in statute — more similar to NZ — but Working Together to Safeguard Children 2023 creates very strong professional expectations for practitioners, with multi-agency safeguarding hubs (MASHs) providing coordination, and professional conduct consequences for failures to act comparable to those in NZ [18]. The UK has been debating mandatory reporting reform for over a decade; as of 2026, no legislation has passed.

United States has mandatory reporting in all 50 states, with health practitioners uniformly designated as mandatory reporters. The federal Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act (CAPTA) provides federal funding contingent on states having mandatory reporting laws. The US system generates very high notification volumes with significant false positive rates and substantial variation in investigation quality and child outcomes [19]. The literature on outcomes compared with permissive frameworks is complicated by the difficulty of controlling for the many other variables that affect child safety.

Canada has mandatory reporting in all provinces, similar to Australia.

The weight of international evidence does not clearly establish that mandatory reporting produces better child outcomes than well-resourced permissive systems with strong professional norms [6]. The strongest predictor of outcomes is not whether reporting is legally mandatory but whether the child protection system receiving notifications is adequately resourced to act on them.

A working position

A few things I think are defensible.

Know which of your obligations are legal and which are professional. The difference matters when you are explaining the situation to a patient. "I am required by law to report this" is accurate for notifiable diseases, gunshot wounds, and coroner notifications. It is not accurate for most child protection notifications and is legally imprecise for family violence disclosures. Using the language of legal mandate where no legal mandate exists is clinically dishonest and, if challenged, erodes trust.

The absence of mandatory reporting for child abuse does not lower the bar for clinical action. The threshold for acting — consulting, referring, notifying — should be reasonable concern about risk, not certainty about harm. Documentation of the clinical findings, the reasoning, and the action is as important in the permissive NZ framework as in a mandatory one.

Driver fitness notifications require a process, not just a clinical finding. The patient who continues to drive with uncontrolled epilepsy needs a clear conversation, documented, with a specific timeline for self-reporting and a clear statement of what you will do if they do not. The conversation is harder than the notification. Having it thoroughly and documenting it thoroughly is what makes the subsequent notification defensible.

For family violence and elder abuse, the question is not "do I have to report" but "what does this person need." The absence of mandatory reporting reflects a clinical philosophy: the GP's role is to make it safe to disclose, to provide access to support, and to respond to what the patient actually needs, not to process a notification. The clinical obligation to document, to ask safety questions, and to have a referral pathway is real regardless of the legal framing.

Engage with the cultural context. For Māori patients, the history and present of state child protection involvement shapes the clinical encounter in ways that the legal framework does not acknowledge. Understanding the local kaupapa Māori services, building relationships with Māori health providers, and being aware of the whānau context in child protection situations is part of the clinical skill, not an optional addition to it.

The GP who understands precisely what they are and are not required to do — and who can explain that clearly to a patient, without either overstating legal compulsion or understating professional obligation — is the GP who can have these conversations well. The conversations are hard. Having them well matters.
