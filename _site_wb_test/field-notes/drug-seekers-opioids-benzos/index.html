Drug seekers, opioids, and benzos

A working GP, in five parts — the framing, the opioid story, the benzo story, the ethics, and a working position.

2 May 2026

Part I — The framing problem

"Drug seeker" is a label every GP has used. It is also a label that has caused real harm. Both things are true. The honest version of this conversation begins with both.

Used carefully, the term names a real clinical phenomenon: a patient whose presentation is shaped, partly or mostly, by the desire to obtain a particular medication for reasons other than what the medication is being prescribed for. The patterns are familiar — the patient who arrives late on Friday with their regular doctor unavailable, who has lost their script, whose pain doesn't quite match the picture, who specifies a drug by brand name, who has visited several practices in the last six months. These patterns exist. Failing to recognise them costs the patient (in ongoing dependence), costs the system (in diversion and overdose), and costs the next patient who finds their legitimate request met with reflex suspicion.

Used carelessly, the term does damage. It pattern-matches on demographic features more than on behaviour. It sticks to a chart and biases every subsequent consult. It builds up, in primary care, a culture in which a patient whose pain is real, whose dependence is iatrogenic, whose distress at running out of medication is rational, gets treated as someone trying to get one over the doctor. It produces, at population level, exactly the under-treatment of pain that the opioid backlash was supposed to correct.

The framing problem is not solved by avoiding the term. It is solved by being clear, to ourselves, about what we are actually pattern-matching on. Behaviour, not demographics. Specific features in this consultation, not a label that has accumulated in this chart over years. And — crucially — a willingness to be wrong, in either direction, often enough that the suspicion never becomes reflex.

This piece is about what a working GP can sensibly do with that uncertainty in NZ in 2026, given an opioid environment that looks meaningfully different from the one our overseas colleagues describe, and a benzodiazepine environment that, in some ways, is worse and quieter.

Part II — The opioid story

The story everyone has heard is North American. In the United States and Canada, aggressive marketing of long-acting oxycodone from the late 1990s, combined with relaxed prescribing norms, produced sustained increases in opioid prescribing through the 2000s. The peak was followed by a pivot to illicit opioids — heroin, then fentanyl — as pharmaceutical supply was tightened. North American opioid-related mortality rose into the tens of thousands annually. This is the epidemic that the term "opioid epidemic" usually refers to [1].

NZ's story is different, in ways that are genuinely interesting.

NZ has not experienced an opioid epidemic on anything like the North American scale [2]. Population-level opioid use here has been comparatively restrained, with limited availability of high-potency long-acting formulations, fewer over-the-counter pathways, and an absence of the marketing environment that drove the US trajectory [2]. Annual opioid-related overdose deaths have been somewhere in the range of 35–47 per year in recent years, principally involving methadone, morphine, and codeine — the main opioids medically prescribed [2,3].

That is not the same as saying we have got it right. Between 2017 and 2021, almost one fifth of the NZ population received an opioid medicine each year [1]. Codeine and tramadol accounted for the bulk of this; strong opioids were prescribed less commonly. There was a slight overall decline 2017–2021, with a notable uptick in 2020–2021 [1]. Codeine was reclassified to prescription-only in NZ in 2020 [4]. Tramadol remains commonly prescribed despite known issues with variable metabolism and dependence potential [1].

The actual NZ drug crisis sits elsewhere. Past-year illicit opioid use in NZ in 2022/23 was approximately 0.4% [5]. Methamphetamine use is far more prevalent — somewhere around 1% past-year for the general population, considerably higher in specific districts and demographics [5]. Approximately 52–55% of NZ Needle Exchange Programme client visits in recent years have been to obtain equipment for methamphetamine injection [5]. The NZ drug-harms ranking study published in 2023 placed alcohol, methamphetamine, and synthetic cannabinoids as the three drugs most harmful to the population, with various opioid categories ranked fifth or lower [6].

The clinical implication is uncomfortable but worth holding clearly. The patient in front of you, presenting with a story that triggers concerns about prescription opioid misuse, is much less likely than a North American counterpart to be at the leading edge of a population-level opioid crisis. They are also, statistically, more likely to be living with iatrogenic dependence on legitimately-prescribed opioids than to be diverting to illicit supply — though neither is rare. The framing of "drug seeker" imported wholesale from the North American context can therefore be both empirically inaccurate and clinically misleading in NZ.

What we still need to do well: prescribe opioids only when indicated, at the lowest effective dose, for the shortest reasonable duration, with explicit conversations about expected duration of treatment at initiation [1,7]. Re-evaluate every long-term opioid prescription for a non-cancer indication on each renewal. Use the BPAC and ANZCA frameworks for chronic pain [1,7]. Recognise that opioid-induced hyperalgesia and withdrawal-driven pain are real, and that some patients on long-term opioids would be in less pain on lower doses or no opioids at all — but that getting them there is a months-to-years project, not a single consult.

It is also worth flagging what NZ doesn't have. Australia has SafeScript and similar real-time prescription monitoring systems that flag high-risk prescribing patterns at the point of dispense. NZ does not have a national equivalent. The clinical implication: the suspicion of duplicate prescribing across practices is harder to confirm or rule out, and the responsibility for identifying patterns rests on the individual prescriber's history-taking and the pharmacist's pattern recognition.

Part III — The benzo story

If the opioid story in NZ is the epidemic that mostly happened elsewhere, the benzo story is the epidemic that happened here, more quietly, and is largely still in progress.

In the year to September 2020, NZ pharmaceutical dispensing data showed that benzodiazepines and zopiclone continued to be prescribed in high volumes, despite recommendations for short-term use only [8]. Zopiclone is the only z-drug funded in NZ, and it is the most widely dispensed funded hypnotic [8]. Crucially, zopiclone is not a controlled drug in NZ, while benzodiazepines are Class C controlled drugs — which has produced a system in which the medicine with arguably the highest hypnotic prescribing volume sits outside controlled-drug oversight [8].

The age gradient is steep. The Health Quality and Safety Commission's Atlas of Healthcare Variation showed that 13.5% of 65–74 year olds and 25.8% of those aged 85 and over were dispensed a benzodiazepine or zopiclone in 2023 [9]. Approximately 10% of those aged 75+ were "regularly dispensed" these medicines, defined as a dispensing in at least three of four quarters [8]. At population level, this is one of the most prevalent psychoactive prescription patterns in NZ medicine.

The harms are well-characterised. Benzodiazepines and z-drugs are associated with increased risks of falls, fall-related fractures, motor vehicle crashes, cognitive impairment, dependence, and a probable association — debated, but not dismissable — with dementia incidence [8,10]. STOPPFall, the European screening tool for fall-risk-increasing drugs in older adults, lists both benzodiazepines and z-drugs in its 14 medication classes for review [10]. None of this is news. It has been known for at least two decades.

What's distinctive about the benzo problem, compared with the opioid problem, is that it doesn't fit the "drug seeker" narrative cleanly. The 82-year-old woman who has been on temazepam since her husband died in 2008 isn't seeking the drug in any pattern-matching sense. She is taking what she was prescribed, by people who meant well, in a system that did not have a deprescribing pathway built in. The harm to her is just as real as the harm of opioid dependence, but the moral framing is entirely different — there's no moral framing at all, just a quiet accumulation of risk.

The deprescribing literature is now reasonably mature. BPAC has published structured deprescribing guidance for benzodiazepines and zopiclone [11]. The basic moves are: identify candidates (especially older patients, high-dose, long-duration); have an explicit conversation about the goal; taper slowly (commonly by 10% of the dose every 1–2 weeks, sometimes substantially slower for very long-term users); use shorter-acting equivalents during the taper; and accept that not every patient will succeed, and that the failures are not failures of character [11]. CBT for insomnia has reasonable evidence as a non-pharmacological alternative for the underlying indication [12].

The ethical work in benzos is different from opioids. It is less about the drug-seeking consult and more about the patient who isn't seeking anything, who hasn't been offered anything else, who is on a medication they should never have remained on, with harms that are real and increasing.

Part IV — The ethics in the consult

A few principles I think actually hold.

Dependence and addiction are not the same thing. A patient on long-term opioids or benzos for a legitimate indication, who experiences withdrawal on cessation, is dependent. A patient whose use is causing harm, who continues despite that harm, who has lost control of their use, has a use disorder. Most patients are dependent without being addicted. Some are both. A few are neither. The clinical and ethical response is different in each case.

The patient who is dependent because of how we prescribed has a moral claim on us. A 60-year-old who has been on tramadol since a back injury in 2015, who has tried twice to come off it and failed, did not choose this state in any meaningful way. The ethical position is not "you need to come off this and I'm not going to keep prescribing it". It is "I'd like us to work toward you needing less of this; here's how, here's how long, here's what we'll do when it gets hard." That is a several-month project, sometimes longer, requiring continuity that primary care often struggles to provide.

Refusing to prescribe is also a clinical decision. Saying no to a request is not a failure of care. It is, sometimes, the most therapeutic thing in the room. The conditions for it being therapeutic rather than punitive are: a clear explanation of the reasons, an offer of an alternative pathway, no abandonment, and documentation that would survive review. The condition for it being punitive is the absence of those things.

Diversion happens but is much less common than the framing implies. Some prescription medication is diverted — by patients to others, or by patients who use a portion themselves and sell or share the rest. It does happen, and it is part of the picture. But treating every patient as a probable diverter is both empirically wrong and clinically corrosive.

The new patient who asks for a specific medication is not, on that basis alone, a drug seeker. Some patients are unusually well-informed about their own care. Some have travelled, have changed practices, have been on a regimen that worked. The right response is interest, not suspicion. The history sorts most of these consults within minutes; the suspicion sorts none of them.

Methamphetamine is a different conversation. Methamphetamine use disorder presents in primary care, and increasingly so [5]. The evidence base for pharmacological treatment is limited; psychosocial interventions remain primary. NZ's harm reduction infrastructure for stimulant use is thinner than for opioids, though the NZ Drug Foundation and the NZ Needle Exchange Programme remain the most important practical resources [13]. The opioid frame does not transfer to meth in any clinically useful way.

The relationship is the intervention. This may be the most important clinical fact in this whole space. People who use drugs problematically — whether iatrogenically dependent, addicted to prescribed opioids, regularly using meth — get better outcomes when they have a sustained relationship with a primary care clinician who is neither permissive nor punitive, who knows them, and who keeps showing up. There is no replacement for this. There is no protocol that works without it.

Part V — A working position

Pulling this together, the working position I land on is something like this.

The label "drug seeker" should be used with reluctance and precision. When you do find yourself reaching for it, ask whether you're naming a behaviour observed in this consultation, or pattern-matching on a chart entry from years ago. The second is almost always wrong; the first is sometimes right.

Opioid prescribing in NZ is, on the whole, more measured than the international story implies — which is good, but is not an excuse for casual long-term prescribing for chronic non-cancer pain, which remains very poorly supported by evidence and very strongly associated with harm.

Benzodiazepine and zopiclone prescribing in NZ is the larger quiet problem, with a quarter of the oldest age band on these drugs, almost none of whom should be. Deprescribing is genuinely hard, requires sustained engagement, and is one of the most consequential things general practice does for the elderly population.

The ethical centre of all of this is not a moral judgement on the patient. It is a clinician who knows the difference between dependence and addiction, who refuses to abandon iatrogenically-dependent patients, who is willing to say no when no is the right answer, and who maintains the relationship through the discomfort that requires.

The patient in the difficult consult is, much more often than the framing suggests, someone with whom the next ten years matter more than the next ten minutes. The ten minutes will determine whether the ten years go well.

That is the work.
