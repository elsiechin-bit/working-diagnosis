How general practice happened in New Zealand

A field guide to the institutional history of the specialty — from colonial bag-carriers to PHOs, via a series of reorganisations so numerous they now have their own historiography.

3 May 2026

There is a New Zealand tradition, observed so consistently across governments of every stripe that it has become structural, of reorganising the health system approximately once per decade. The reforms are always accompanied by a document with a name like Towards a Healthier New Zealand or Pae Ora or Health 2026, and they always promise to fix the postcode lottery, close the equity gap, and reduce administrative overhead. They never quite do any of these things, but they do produce a fresh set of acronyms, which are a comfort.

What gets lost in the reorganisation cycle is the history that produced the system in its current form. This is a brief attempt at that history — specifically the history of general practice in New Zealand, which turns out to be more interesting, more contested, and more politically instructive than the usual Powerpoint slide ("1938: Social Security Act; 2001: PHOs; 2022: Health NZ") suggests.

Part I — The colonial bag carrier (1840–1900)

General practice in colonial New Zealand began, as did most things in colonial New Zealand, in conditions that ranged from difficult to genuinely dangerous. The first formally registered medical practitioners arrived with the New Zealand Company and missionary groups in the 1840s, bringing whatever they had been trained in and a leather bag [1].

What they had been trained in was, by modern standards, not very much. Anaesthesia arrived in NZ in the late 1840s (ether was in use in Auckland by 1847 [1]), but germ theory was still some decades off, antibiotics were a century away, and the pharmacopoeia consisted largely of opium derivatives, mercury compounds, and optimism. The medical officer of a colonial settlement was less a clinician in the modern sense and more a general-purpose holder-of-bad-news-while-bodies-recovered-or-didn't.

The geographic problem was immediate. NZ is not large by area but is extremely awkward by terrain, and a district surgeon covering, say, the Maniototo or the West Coast was covering country with effectively no roads, no telephone, and at best a horse that didn't want to go in that direction. The result was that for much of rural NZ in the nineteenth century, the "general practitioner" was whoever had done some medical training and happened to live nearby, supplemented by rongoā Māori practitioners whose knowledge of local plants and conditions often exceeded that of the European-trained arrivals [2].

Formal registration began with the Medical Practitioners Act 1869, which established the right to practice medicine as requiring — for the first time — something resembling credential verification [1]. The 1914 Medical Act consolidated this into a more recognisable registration framework. Neither piece of legislation said much about what general practice was or how it should be organised; those questions were considered obvious enough not to require legislation, which in retrospect was optimistic.

Part II — The 1938 moment and its immediate complications

New Zealand's claim to have created the first comprehensive welfare state rests substantially on the Social Security Act 1938. The Act, passed by the first Labour government under Michael Savage, was ambitious in the manner of governments that have recently survived a depression: it promised universal provision for the sick, the old, the unemployed, and — pertinently for our purposes — universal free access to a general medical service [3].

The key word in that last sentence is "promised."

The New Zealand Medical Association, which represented the professional interests of the country's doctors, was not entirely enthusiastic about universal free healthcare. The concern was not, precisely, the principle — it was the implementation. "Free at the point of care" implied, as the NZMA read it, that doctors would become, in effect, public servants with government-determined income, which was not a career arrangement many had anticipated when they enrolled in medical school [3,4].

The negotiations that followed between the government and the medical profession were prolonged, and the result was a compromise that has shaped NZ primary care ever since. GPs would receive a government payment per service (a "benefit"), but they retained the right to charge patients a co-payment above that benefit. The free general medical service Savage had envisioned did not, in practice, materialise [3]. What materialised instead was a subsidy-plus-copayment model that looked like free healthcare if you were already healthy enough not to need much of it, and looked increasingly like a barrier to access the sicker and poorer you were.

This arrangement — capitation payment from the state, co-payment from the patient, GP as independent contractor rather than public employee — has remained the basic organisational fact of NZ general practice for eighty-five years. The equity problem identified in the Commonwealth Fund's 2024 comparative analysis [5] is, in part, the 1938 compromise still on the books.

Part III — Getting a college (1973, only twenty-one years after the British)

The Royal New Zealand College of General Practitioners was founded in 1973 [6].

The Royal College of General Practitioners in the UK was founded in 1952.

For those keeping score: NZ GPs practiced without a specialist college for twenty-one years after their British counterparts organised one. The implication — and it is not entirely unfair — is that for those twenty-one years, general practice in NZ was organisationally treated as the thing you did if you hadn't become a proper specialist. You finished your house jobs, possibly did a hospital year or two, and then acquired a building, a receptionist, and the collected paperwork of whoever's practice you were buying.

The RNZCGP changed this, slowly. Fellowship (FRNZCGP) was established as a credential; eventually, in the 1990s, vocational registration as a GP required it. The GPEP training programme was established to provide a structured postgraduate training pathway. The college produced Fellowship of the Royal New Zealand College of General Practitioners as a recognisable indicator of formal training, which remains the qualification for vocational registration today [6].

The college also, over time, became the institutional voice for the argument that general practice was not a residual category of medicine but a distinct specialty with its own intellectual tradition, its own consultation model, and its own evidence base. This argument has been more accepted in some quarters than others.

Part IV — The 1990s reform era, or: what happens when you run hospitals like Telecom

The 1993 health reforms under the National government occupy a special place in the history of NZ health administration, in the way that a particularly instructive failure occupies a special place in any discipline [4,7].

The theory was elegant, in the way that economic theories applied to healthcare tend to be elegant before they encounter healthcare. Area Health Boards were replaced by a purchaser-provider split: Regional Health Authorities (RHAs) would purchase health services, and Crown Health Enterprises (CHEs) would provide them. The CHEs were hospitals reconstituted as state-owned enterprises, expected to operate on commercial principles, compete with one another, and produce a surplus.

Hospital as competitive enterprise. This seemed to some observers — not all of them ideological — to contain a theoretical difficulty, in that hospitals providing emergency neurosurgery and obstetric services for geographically captive populations do not compete with anything in any meaningful sense. The CHE experiment produced, predictably, a series of hospitals trying to improve their financial position by reducing services to populations who could not go elsewhere to get them. The "efficiency gains" were visible on balance sheets; the equity losses were visible in emergency departments.

By 1996 the four Regional Health Authorities had been collapsed into one Health Funding Authority (HFA). By 2000, the incoming Labour government had abolished the CHEs, replaced them with twenty District Health Boards (DHBs) under the New Zealand Public Health and Disability Act 2000, and restored a broadly public model [4,7]. The DHB structure — twenty-one boards (one was later merged) covering defined geographic populations, with both funding and provider responsibilities — would survive until 2022.

For general practice specifically, the 1990s reforms were both destabilising and ultimately a catalyst. The Primary Health Care Strategy of 2001 — which replaced the old benefit-and-copayment model with capitation funding through Primary Health Organisations (PHOs) — was developed in direct response to the equity failures the reforms had exposed [7]. The PHO model was intended to align GP incentives with population health outcomes rather than service volume, and to allow practices serving high-deprivation populations to receive more appropriate funding.

Whether it worked is the subject of a literature that runs to several thousand pages. Short version: sort of, partially, unevenly, with significant remaining access barriers that are the subject of a different field note.

Part V — PHOs, capitation, and the long slow argument about co-payments

The Primary Health Care Strategy 2001 established PHOs as the organisational vehicle for primary care funding [7]. General practices enrolled their patient populations with PHOs, which received capitation funding from DHBs, which allocated it to practices weighted for age and gender. Under-6s were funded for free visits; VLCA (Very Low Cost Access) practices serving the highest-deprivation populations were funded at higher rates; Community Services Card holders received subsidised visits.

The co-payment problem did not disappear, because it couldn't disappear while the model retained a co-payment. Under-6s became free (eventually extended to under-14s in many practices, and under-13s universally from 2022). Working-age adults on lower incomes remained subject to co-payments that the Commonwealth Fund's 2024 data identify as material access barriers [5].

The capitation weighting problem — that funding per head was inadequate for the practices whose patients required the most time — was addressed, after twenty-three years, in 2025, when the capitation formula was reweighted to include deprivation, multimorbidity, and rurality for the first time since 2002 [8]. This is, by the standards of health policy, rapid. By the standards of people who couldn't afford to see a GP in the intervening years, it is a different kind of achievement.

Part VI — 2022, 2024, and wherever we are now

The Pae Ora (Healthy Futures) Act 2022 collapsed the twenty DHBs into a single national entity, Health New Zealand | Te Whatu Ora, and established Te Aka Whai Ora, a parallel Māori Health Authority with a specific commission to address the persistent and well-documented health inequities for Māori populations [9].

Te Aka Whai Ora was disestablished by the coalition government in 2024 [10].

This is, by international standards, a remarkably fast policy reversal — a major parallel commissioning structure created, staffed, and operating, abolished within two years of its establishment. The rationale given was administrative efficiency and the undesirability of a race-based health agency; critics observed that the equity data that motivated the Authority's creation had not changed [5,10].

Health New Zealand in 2025 is operating under a commissioner, appointed in response to a financial position described as unsustainable [11]. The capitation reweighting and full funding of GPEP training are, by recent standards, substantive interventions in the primary care funding picture. Whether they are sufficient to close the access gap that the 1938 compromise opened is a question that will take some years to answer.

A working summary

General practice in New Zealand has arrived at its current form through a process that can be charitably described as iterative and less charitably described as serially disorganised. The colonial bag-carrier became the suburban practice owner; the 1938 promise of free primary care became the co-payment model; the 1993 market experiment became twenty DHBs; the twenty DHBs became one Health New Zealand. The equity gap that the Social Security Act was meant to close remains, as of the Commonwealth Fund's most recent international comparison, among the worst in the developed world for a high-income country.

What has also survived, despite everything, is a general practice sector that the same international comparison ranks first in the world on Care Process — continuity, coordination, prevention, patient engagement, sensitivity to what the patient actually came for [5]. That combination (best on process, worst on equity) is, in a way, the summary of a health system that works extremely well for the people already inside it and extremely badly at bringing in the people who need it most.

The history explains the combination. The political question, which successive reorganisations have yet to adequately answer, is what to do about it.
