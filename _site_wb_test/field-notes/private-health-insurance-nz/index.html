Private health insurance in New Zealand

How the two-tier system works, who the players are, what gets covered (and what quietly doesn't), and why it matters every time you write a referral.

3 May 2026

New Zealand has a publicly funded health system — one of the foundational achievements of the 1938 Social Security Act — and also a flourishing private health insurance market. These two facts coexist without obvious contradiction, but understanding how they interact is one of the more practically useful pieces of contextual knowledge a GP can have. It shapes referral pathways, patient expectations, and the clinical dynamics of consultations that are nominally about something else entirely.

This note covers the structure of the private insurance market, how the main insurers work, what their policies actually cover, how ACC complicates the picture, and the specific ways in which private insurance intersects with general practice.

Part I — The structure of the market

New Zealand's health system is a hybrid. The public system — funded through general taxation and administered through Health New Zealand Te Whatu Ora — provides universal access to primary and secondary care services, but access to elective specialist care is rationed through clinical prioritisation frameworks. Wait times for non-urgent procedures are a persistent feature of the public system; they are also the primary market driver for private health insurance [1].

The private insurance market in New Zealand is relatively concentrated. As of the early 2020s, approximately 30 to 35 percent of the New Zealand population held some form of private health insurance, a figure that has remained broadly stable over the preceding two decades with modest fluctuation [2]. This is substantially lower than Australia (approximately 55 percent with hospital cover) but higher than many comparable countries with universal public systems [3].

The major players are:

Southern Cross Health Society is the dominant insurer and the one most GPs will encounter most often. It is a not-for-profit mutual society — owned by its members rather than shareholders — with approximately 900,000 members, representing roughly 60 to 65 percent of the private health insurance market [4]. This structure means Southern Cross does not return profits to external shareholders, but it does still need to maintain financial sustainability, which manifests in regular premium increases (often in the range of 5 to 10 percent annually in recent years) and periodic product restructuring. Southern Cross administers an affiliated provider network — a panel of specialists and facilities whose agreed pricing means members can access those providers with lower or nil gap payments.

nib NZ is the second-largest insurer, a subsidiary of the Australian-listed nib Holdings. nib entered the New Zealand market by acquiring Tower Health & Life in 2012 and has grown its market presence since [5]. As a listed company, nib operates on a profit-return model, which is a structural difference from Southern Cross that is occasionally visible in product design and premium trajectory.

Accuro Health Insurance is a smaller New Zealand-based mutual, historically with roots in the Wellington region. It occupies a niche in the market, and some patients — particularly those with longstanding policies from the period before nib's entry — will hold Accuro cover.

AIA NZ and Partners Life offer health insurance primarily as a component within life and income protection policy bundles, rather than as standalone health products. Their health benefits are usually structured differently to the major health insurers — often as defined-benefit or hybrid policies — and their role in the GP's daily referral calculus is smaller.

UniMed is a smaller mutual with historical origins in union and occupation-based coverage, still operating but with a limited market share.

Part II — What private health insurance actually covers

The architecture of a typical NZ private health insurance policy is built around a core gap: it is designed to cover what the public system either doesn't cover at all or doesn't cover in acceptable timeframes. Understanding this logic explains both what is included and what is conspicuously absent.

What is typically covered:

Specialist consultations. The majority of hospital and specialist policies include reimbursement for outpatient specialist consultations, subject to a GP referral. This is the most commonly triggered benefit for most insured patients and the benefit most directly relevant to general practice referral decisions.

Elective surgery. Inpatient and day-patient surgical procedures are the core benefit of most hospital policies. This is the primary reason most people buy private insurance — to avoid public waiting lists for procedures like joint replacements, cataract surgery, hernia repair, and laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

Diagnostic investigations. Imaging (MRI, CT, ultrasound) and certain specialist-requested investigations are covered under most policies, often subject to specialist referral. The coverage here matters because MRI and CT wait times in the public system can be substantial for non-urgent indications.

Cancer treatment. Most comprehensive policies include Pharmac-declined cancer drugs as a benefit — a provision that was politically salient when Herceptin (trastuzumab) funding was contested in the late 2000s and has remained a selling point since [6]. The interaction between Pharmac funding decisions and private insurance coverage is a recurring feature of NZ oncology practice.

Mental health. Coverage for mental health is uneven across products and has historically been a significant limitation. Southern Cross and nib both introduced improved mental health benefits in the mid-2010s in response to public pressure and awareness of mental health needs, but the detail of what is covered, for how long, and under what conditions varies substantially across policy types and vintages. Patients with older policies may have significantly worse mental health cover than those with recently issued products. This is worth checking rather than assuming.

What is typically not covered:

General practice consultations. Most standard hospital-and-specialist policies do not cover GP visits. There are policies — usually higher-premium "GP Plus" or "everyday cover" products — that include a GP benefit, but these are a minority of policies held. The practical implication is that private insurance generally does not reduce the financial barrier to primary care access; it reduces the barrier to secondary care access for those who already have a primary care relationship.

ACC-covered conditions. New Zealand's accident compensation scheme (ACC) provides cover for injury regardless of fault. Private health insurers do not cover treatment costs for conditions that ACC covers, and policies include ACC exclusion clauses as standard. The boundary between injury (ACC) and illness (insurance or public system) matters clinically: a patient with a torn meniscus from a weekend sport injury is an ACC claim, not an insurance claim. A patient with degenerative meniscal disease is an insurance claim. The distinction is sometimes genuinely uncertain and occasionally contested.

Pre-existing conditions. New policies include exclusions for pre-existing conditions, defined with varying degrees of specificity depending on the insurer. The standard approach is an exclusion for conditions for which the patient received treatment, advice, or medication in a specified look-back period (commonly three to five years) prior to the policy start date. Some insurers use underwriting at policy inception; others apply standard exclusion clauses without individual underwriting and then investigate at claim. The practical consequence is that patients who buy insurance after being diagnosed with a significant condition will often find that condition excluded.

Maternity. Maternity care is specifically excluded from most policies, on the basis that pregnancy is not an illness and that the public maternity system provides universal coverage.

Routine and preventive care. Immunisations, well-child checks, cervical screening, and similar preventive services are not covered by most policies, as they are funded through the public primary health care system.

Part III — ACC and the boundary problem

The interface between ACC and private insurance is a genuine source of clinical and administrative complexity, and it produces consultations that are less straightforward than they appear.

ACC covers personal injury caused by accident. This includes a broad range of presentations: fractures, soft tissue injuries, lacerations, burns, and treatment injury (injury caused by healthcare). It also covers occupational disease in defined categories. When ACC coverage applies, it funds treatment including surgery, physiotherapy, and specialist care — and private health insurance does not duplicate that funding.

The boundary problem arises because many clinical presentations are genuinely ambiguous. Degenerative spinal disease with an acute exacerbation after a fall: is this an injury (ACC) or an underlying disease process (insurance or public)? Rotator cuff pathology with a history of repetitive overhead work: occupational injury (ACC) or degenerative disease (public/insurance)? ACC's adjudication of these claims is not always aligned with the clinical history, and the outcome can determine whether a patient's surgery is funded through ACC, private insurance, or the public waiting list.

For GPs, the practical implications are: being precise in referral letters about whether there is an injury mechanism, being careful not to inadvertently frame a degenerative condition as injury-related in a way that creates a false ACC claim, and being prepared to help patients navigate a declined ACC claim if they believe it was wrongly decided.

Part IV — How private insurance intersects with general practice

The ways in which private insurance becomes concretely relevant in the consultation room are numerous and underappreciated.

Referral letters and pathway decisions. When a patient with private insurance needs specialist review, the referral goes to a private specialist rather than the public FSA pathway. The practical difference: the patient will likely be seen within days or weeks rather than months. The letter needs to support an insurance claim — it should be clinically complete, clearly state the indication for specialist review, and not inadvertently describe the presenting problem in terms that might invoke an exclusion clause. This is not about gaming the insurer; it is about accurate documentation. A GP who writes "this patient's chronic knee pain has been present for five years" is accurately describing the history but may be writing the basis for a pre-existing condition exclusion claim rejection.

Explaining what patients are actually covered for. Patients often have only a vague understanding of their own policy. A patient who has been paying Southern Cross premiums for fifteen years may believe they are covered for a service that their policy vintage excludes. They may not know that their mental health benefit has a session limit. They may not know that their hospital cover requires pre-authorisation for non-emergency admissions. GPs are not insurance advisors and should not hold themselves out as such, but being able to point a patient toward the right questions — "you'll want to call Southern Cross before booking the procedure and ask whether it's covered under your policy" — is useful clinical navigation.

Pre-authorisation. Most insurers require pre-authorisation for elective surgery and some specialist consultations. The insurer's approval is needed before the claim will be covered. Patients who proceed to private surgery without pre-authorisation and then discover the claim is declined are not uncommon. GPs cannot approve on behalf of insurers, but they can flag to patients that this step needs to happen before proceeding.

The uninsured patient in the private referral. Not every patient asking to be seen privately is insured. Some pay out of pocket, either because they are uninsured, their condition is excluded, or they want a specific specialist who is not on their insurer's affiliated network. The referral logic is the same; the financial implications for the patient are different. Specialist consultation fees in New Zealand range from approximately $200 to $400 or more for an initial consultation [7]. This is not trivial, and for patients who are not wealthy it is worth a brief conversation before writing a private referral as if it were the obvious path.

Mental health referrals and the coverage gap. The intersection of insurance and mental health referrals is particularly fraught. A patient with private cover asking for a private psychiatry or clinical psychology referral may find their policy covers a limited number of sessions, imposes a waiting period for mental health claims, or excludes specific diagnoses (pre-existing anxiety disorders are a common exclusion). The public pathway for mental health has its own access challenges. The practical result is that patients who need significant mental health input often fall between systems — too acute for unaided self-management, too complex for the sessions their insurance covers, unable to access the public system quickly enough. This is a known structural gap and acknowledging it honestly — rather than assuming private insurance solves the problem — is part of accurate clinical navigation.

The premium problem and the disappearing insured. Private health insurance premiums in New Zealand have risen substantially over the past decade, driven by claims inflation, the ageing insured population, and post-COVID surgical demand [8]. For many patients, the policy they took out in their thirties becomes unaffordable in their fifties and sixties — precisely when they are most likely to need it. The practical consequence is that GPs see patients who formerly had private cover and have lapsed, who now face public waiting lists for conditions they had expected to address privately. This is not a clinical problem for the GP to solve, but it is useful context for avoiding the assumption that a patient's previously expressed private insurance cover is still current.

Part V — Equity considerations

Private health insurance is not equitably distributed across the New Zealand population. Ownership of private cover correlates strongly with income, employment type, and ethnicity. Māori and Pacific people are substantially underrepresented among the insured relative to their population share, a disparity that reflects both affordability and historical access to employment-based insurance [9]. Older populations face sharply higher premiums; people with pre-existing conditions face exclusions or declined applications.

This creates a structural amplifier for existing health inequity. The private insurance market, to the extent it speeds access to specialist care, does so primarily for people who are already better resourced — while those most likely to have complex health needs are most likely to depend entirely on a public system under sustained resource pressure.

This is not an argument against private health insurance existing; it is an argument for holding its benefits and limitations clearly in mind, for not assuming that the presence of private cover means a patient's access problem is solved, and for being attentive to the ways in which navigating insurance complexity may itself present a barrier to some patients.

Part VI — A working summary for the consultation room

The core things worth knowing:

Most NZ private insurance covers specialist consultations, elective surgery, diagnostics, and cancer treatment — not GP care, not ACC-covered injuries, and not routine prevention.

Southern Cross is the dominant insurer and a mutual; nib is the main listed competitor. Policy vintage matters — older policies often have worse mental health and cancer drug cover.

Referral letters for private pathways should be clinically complete and accurate, and GPs are not responsible for pre-authorisation but should prompt patients to obtain it.

The ACC boundary is real and matters: injury is ACC, disease is public or insured, and ambiguous presentations need careful handling.

Pre-existing condition exclusions are standard. Mental health coverage is variable and often insufficient. Premiums are rising and some previously insured patients are no longer covered.

The equity gradient is significant. Private cover is concentrated in populations with fewer complex health needs; those with greatest need are most dependent on the public system.

None of this is a reason to approach privately insured patients differently in terms of clinical care. It is a reason to understand the system well enough to help patients navigate it accurately — which is, as in most aspects of general practice, most of the job.
