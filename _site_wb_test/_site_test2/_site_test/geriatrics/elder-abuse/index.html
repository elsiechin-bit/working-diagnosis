Identifying and responding to elder abuse in general practice — types, risk factors, the GP's obligations under NZ law, and the Age Concern EARS referral pathway.

Geriatrics

Elder abuse & neglect.

Estimated to affect 2–10% of older adults. Underidentified in primary care. Often perpetrated by someone the patient loves — which is exactly why it is so rarely raised.

Elder abuse encompasses physical abuse, psychological or emotional abuse, financial exploitation, sexual abuse, and neglect — including self-neglect. It is estimated to affect between 2% and 10% of community-dwelling older adults, with financial abuse probably the most prevalent and the most underreported.1 In the majority of identified cases, the perpetrator is a family member or trusted carer. This is the central clinical difficulty: the person the patient most depends on, and most protects, is often the person causing harm.

Risk factors on the patient side include: cognitive impairment (which increases both vulnerability and dependence on others), functional dependency, social isolation, depression, prior history of family violence, and significant disability. Risk factors on the carer side include: financial stress, substance use, mental health difficulties, carer burden, and a prior history of family violence. The presence of these factors should prompt active inquiry — not passive waiting for disclosure.

Identification requires attention to patterns across consultations. A single bruise in a plausible location is rarely diagnostic; a series of unexplained injuries, a pattern of missed appointments, a patient who defers entirely to a carer who answers all the questions, unexplained withdrawal of savings or changes in will — these are the signals that warrant a more explicit conversation. Schedule an appointment without the carer present where possible. Open-ended questions ("Are you safe at home? Is there anything at home that worries you?") are a starting point; some patients will not disclose on first asking, but the question signals that it is safe to do so.

New Zealand GPs have no mandatory reporting obligation for elder abuse, unlike the situation with child abuse. However, GPs have professional obligations under the Health Practitioners Competence Assurance Act 2003 to act in the patient's best interest — and a duty of care that encompasses their safety. The primary referral pathway is Age Concern's Elder Abuse Response Service (EARS), which provides free specialist assessment and advocacy nationally (0800 65 2105). In situations of immediate physical danger, police referral is appropriate.

Capacity is a central consideration. If the patient has capacity and declines intervention, the GP must ultimately respect that decision — even if it means the person remains in a harmful situation. This is clinically and ethically difficult, and it is worth acknowledging that directly with the patient: "I'm worried about you, and I want you to know this door is always open." Document the conversation, the patient's stated position, and your assessment of their capacity. If capacity is in doubt, or if the person is not safe in the immediate term, more assertive intervention (including a referral to a social worker or the Needs Assessment and Service Coordination service) may be warranted.

Financial abuse — the misuse of an older person's money, property, or legal rights, often by a family member with power of attorney or informal financial access — often presents subtly: a patient who cannot explain recent financial decisions, who has changed their will unexpectedly, or who cannot access their own funds. GPs are not financial investigators, but flagging concerns to EARS and encouraging the patient to speak with a lawyer or Citizens Advice Bureau is appropriate. Where the abuse involves misuse of an EPA, the PPPR Act provides mechanisms for review by the Family Court.
