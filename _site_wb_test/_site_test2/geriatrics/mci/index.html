Geriatrics

Mild cognitive impairment.

The territory between normal ageing and dementia — important to recognise, impossible to predict, and often the hardest conversation to have honestly.

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) describes a state of objective cognitive decline — measurable on standardised testing — that does not significantly impair daily functioning. It sits between age-related cognitive change and dementia, and it is clinically important because approximately 10–15% of people with MCI will convert to dementia per year, compared with 1–2% of cognitively normal older adults.1 It also matters because it is often the presentation that prompts families — not patients — to book an appointment.

The working definition requires: subjective cognitive complaint (from the patient or informant), objective cognitive impairment on testing, preserved general functional ability, and the absence of a dementia diagnosis.2 The MoCA is more sensitive than the MMSE for detecting MCI; a score of 18–25 typically falls in the MCI range, though interpretation requires age and education adjustment. Single-domain MCI (memory-only) and multi-domain MCI carry different prognostic implications — amnestic single-domain MCI has the highest rate of conversion to Alzheimer's disease.

Reversible contributors must be excluded before MCI is accepted as a diagnosis. The standard workup — thyroid function, B12, folate, FBC, eGFR, HbA1c, calcium, and LFTs — is appropriate. Depression is a particularly important differential: it impairs attention, processing speed, and memory in ways that can closely mimic MCI and should be treated before cognitive test results are interpreted at face value. Sleep disorders, medication effects (especially anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, and opioids), alcohol, and vascular risk factors should all be assessed.

There is currently no pharmacological treatment proven to slow conversion from MCI to dementia; cholinesterase inhibitors are not indicated at this stage.3 The most evidence-supported modifiable factors for slowing cognitive decline are: management of cardiovascular risk (hypertension in particular has the strongest evidence), physical exercise (structured aerobic exercise shows consistent benefit in RCTs), cognitive engagement, social activity, adequate sleep, and a Mediterranean-pattern diet. This is worth framing positively — it gives the patient something actionable rather than a diagnosis and a waiting room.

Monitoring is important. A six-monthly or annual cognitive review is appropriate — using the same tool each time to allow meaningful comparison. Functional change is the key clinical signal: when daily activities become impaired (managing finances, medications, or complex meals), MCI has likely converted to dementia. Document this explicitly, and use it as the trigger to revisit driving, EPA, and ACP conversations.

Communicating an MCI diagnosis requires care. Many patients have been worrying about dementia; "mild cognitive impairment" can be heard as confirmation of their worst fears, or — at the other extreme — as dismissal. Being explicit about what it means and does not mean ("there are real changes on the test, but your daily life isn't affected yet — and we don't know which way this will go") is more honest and more useful than either catastrophising or minimising. Provide resources, offer a follow-up, and check in on the carer — who is often doing most of the worrying.
