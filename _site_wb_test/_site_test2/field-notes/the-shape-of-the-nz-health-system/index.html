The shape of the New Zealand health system

What's structurally distinctive about NZ healthcare in international comparison — what we do uniquely well, what we don't, and what the comparative data actually show.

2 May 2026

There is a temptation, in writing about how NZ health care compares with other countries, to either oversell or undersell it. The oversell sounds like "NZ has one of the best health systems in the world," which is true on some metrics and badly misleading on others. The undersell sounds like "the system is broken," which is the experience of many patients on a Tuesday afternoon but doesn't survive contact with the international comparative data.

The honest version is more interesting than either. NZ has a system that is structurally unusual — two of its most important agencies, ACC and Pharmac, do things that no comparable country does in the same way. It performs unusually well on some standard measures of comparative health system performance, and unusually badly on others. It has just been through the largest structural reform in two decades, parts of which were reversed within twenty-four months. It funds primary care in a way that has been adequate for a long time and is becoming inadequate.

This piece is a guided tour. The aim is descriptive and comparative rather than polemical, though the equity finding at the end is hard to read calmly.

Part I — The structural shape

The NZ health system is, broadly, a tax-funded public system with a substantial private-delivery layer in primary care. About 80% of all health expenditure is publicly financed [1]. Public hospitals are free at the point of service for citizens, residents, and most work-visa holders. Most specialty care is delivered through public hospital systems, with a private hospital sector handling a meaningful share of elective work — particularly orthopaedics, gastroenterology, cardiology, and elective general surgery — for patients with private health insurance or willingness to pay out of pocket.

Primary care is structurally different from most comparable systems. NZ general practices are predominantly privately owned (by GPs, by corporate groups, or by Māori/Pacific health providers), funded through a mixture of capitation payments from Health New Zealand via Primary Health Organisations (PHOs), and patient co-payments at the point of consultation [2]. The capitation payment is calculated per enrolled patient per year, weighted (until recently) only for age and gender. Co-payments vary substantially by practice — capped at lower levels for Very Low Cost Access (VLCA) practices and Community Services Card holders, but free in some practices for under-14s and over-65s — and are a meaningful financial barrier for working-age patients on lower incomes.

This mixed model — public funding through capitation plus patient co-payments through private practice — sits between the British model (NHS general practice fully publicly funded, free at point of use, GPs as independent contractors) and the Australian model (Medicare bulk-billing or rebates per consultation, mostly private practice). It has the disadvantages of both — the queue management of a capitation system, plus the access barrier of a fee-for-service system — and is the single biggest structural weakness when NZ is compared with peer systems.

The hospital and specialty side is more conventional. Health New Zealand | Te Whatu Ora, established under the Pae Ora (Healthy Futures) Act 2022, replaced the previous structure of twenty regional District Health Boards with a single national entity. The reform consolidated planning, funding, and accountability at a national level, with the explicit goals of reducing postcode-lottery variation and producing a more equitable distribution of resources.

Te Aka Whai Ora (the Māori Health Authority), established alongside Te Whatu Ora in 2022 with a specific brief to commission services for Māori populations, was disestablished by the coalition government in 2024 [3]. Its functions were partially absorbed back into Te Whatu Ora and partially distributed elsewhere. The reform thus represented one of the more dramatic short-period structural changes in any developed health system in recent years: a major consolidation, paired with the creation of a parallel Māori commissioning structure, followed within twenty-four months by the disestablishment of that parallel structure.

The system in 2026 is therefore: a single national funder-provider for hospital and specialty care; a network of privately-owned, capitation-and-co-payment-funded primary care practices; two specialised agencies (ACC and Pharmac) that handle injury and pharmaceuticals respectively; and a layer of private hospitals and private health insurance that handles approximately a third of the population for selected elective work.

Part II — What's genuinely distinctive: ACC and Pharmac

If a New Zealander walks into a foreign health system and tries to use it, the two things they will miss are the things almost nobody else has.

ACC

The Accident Compensation Corporation, established under the Accident Compensation Act 1972 and operational from April 1974, is a no-fault personal injury insurance scheme that covers everyone in NZ — citizens, residents, work-visa holders, students, tourists — for personal injury caused by accident [4]. The scheme covers treatment costs, rehabilitation, and income compensation for inability to work, funded through compulsory levies on employers, earners, motor vehicle owners, and the Crown.

The defining trade-off, set in the original Woodhouse Report of 1967 and embedded in the 1972 Act, is the "social contract" of universal no-fault compensation in return for the relinquishment of the right to sue for personal injury [4,5]. New Zealanders cannot sue an at-fault party for personal injury damages, except for the rare case of exemplary damages. In return, anyone injured in an accident in NZ — at work, at home, on the road, on a hike, during a medical procedure — receives funded treatment, funded rehabilitation, and (for those who were earning) income compensation, regardless of how the accident occurred or whose fault it was.

The system is unique. No other country has the combination of universal coverage, no-fault basis, and the abolition of the right to sue for personal injury [4,5]. Some countries have no-fault elements (workplace injury schemes, motor vehicle injury schemes), but no other country has the unified, universal version that NZ adopted in the 1970s. The Australian states have considered comparable schemes intermittently but have not adopted them.

The administrative efficiency of ACC is substantial. Bismark and Paterson's 2006 Health Affairs analysis found administrative costs at approximately 10% of expenditure, compared with 50–60% under typical malpractice tort systems [5]. Compensation is delivered faster, to more patients, with substantially less litigation. Patient safety improvements have been more contested — research has not consistently demonstrated that no-fault systems improve safety more than tort systems do [5] — but the overall picture is of a scheme that has become a durable and politically untouchable feature of the NZ landscape.

The clinical implications for general practice are large. Most accidental injuries are managed within the ACC system rather than through standard health funding. The ACC45 form is a daily piece of NZ general practice. The categories of cover (treatment injury, gradual process injury, mental injury arising from physical injury, sexual abuse cover, recently the maternal birth injury cover added in 2022) shape clinical pathways that have no direct equivalent in the systems most international colleagues work in. Where the boundaries of ACC sit — what's an "accident" and what's not, what counts as a "treatment injury," when "gradual process" applies — is a recurring source of clinical and medicolegal complexity unique to NZ practice. (I've written about ACC's contested edges in more detail elsewhere.)

Pharmac

The Pharmaceutical Management Agency, established in 1993 and now governed under sections 67–74 of the Pae Ora Act 2022, is NZ's single national pharmaceutical purchaser [6]. It manages a capped national budget and decides which medicines and pharmaceutical products are subsidised on the Pharmaceutical Schedule [6]. Pharmac uses a combination of competitive tendering, sole supply contracts, reference pricing, bundling deals, risk-sharing agreements, and aggressive promotion of generics to extract favourable prices from international pharmaceutical suppliers [7]. As a result, NZ pays some of the lowest prices in the world for many medicines [7].

The trade-off is access. Operating within a fixed annual budget, Pharmac declines or delays funding for medicines whose cost-effectiveness is not favourable relative to existing alternatives. This includes a substantial proportion of newer cancer drugs, many drugs for rare diseases, and a number of medications widely funded in comparable systems. Coverage of cancer drugs in NZ has lagged Australia, the UK, and most European systems by a significant margin — older comparative analyses found NZ funding around a quarter of internationally-available cancer drugs versus 90%+ in comparable systems [8]. Some of this gap has narrowed in recent years through targeted funding initiatives (the 2024 Budget included specific allocations for new cancer medicines), but the structural gap persists.

This is the central Pharmac controversy. The agency is internationally admired for its negotiating model and the discipline of its capped budget; it is locally controversial because that discipline produces visible cases where individual patients cannot access medicines that their counterparts in Australia or the UK can. The Pharmac model has been studied by other countries — Ontario's drug program has explicitly drawn on Pharmac's negotiating strategies [8] — but no other country has adopted the full monopsony-with-capped-budget structure.

The clinical implication for general practice is that the working pharmacopoeia in NZ is narrower than in many comparable systems. Some prescribing decisions that Australian or UK colleagues would make routinely (a particular SGLT2 inhibitor, a particular GLP-1 agonist, certain newer biologics) require working within the Special Authority criteria attached to specific Pharmac-funded products, often with the patient paying out-of-pocket for unfunded alternatives or going without.

Part III — How NZ stacks up: the comparative data

The most-cited comparative analysis is the Commonwealth Fund's Mirror, Mirror series, which since 2004 has compared the performance of high-income health systems across access to care, care process, administrative efficiency, equity, and health outcomes. The 2024 edition compared ten countries: Australia, Canada, France, Germany, the Netherlands, NZ, Sweden, Switzerland, the UK, and the US [9].

The headline finding for NZ is more interesting than the overall ranking suggests.

NZ ranked 4th overall out of the ten countries [9]. The top three were Australia, the Netherlands, and the UK; the US was last by a substantial margin.

The domain-level findings are where the picture sharpens.

NZ ranked 1st on Care Process — the best in the world, among the ten compared, on a domain that includes prevention, safe care, coordination, patient engagement, and sensitivity to patient preferences [9]. This is a striking result. The category captures most of what defines the experience of being a patient in a primary-care-led system: continuity of care, coordination between providers, preventive activity built into the consultation, the responsiveness of the clinical encounter to what the patient is actually concerned about. NZ general practice, despite the consultation-length pressure, is rated as delivering this domain better than any of the comparison systems.

NZ tied with the US for worst on Equity [9,10]. This is the part that's hard to read. The Commonwealth Fund's equity domain captures differential access and experience by income — whether lower-income people report being unable to afford needed care, whether patients report unfair treatment or having concerns dismissed, whether physicians report patients being treated unfairly because of race or ethnicity. NZ's performance on this domain was consistently poor, with substantial gaps between higher- and lower-income access and experience, and significant levels of reported discrimination [10]. Among the ten countries compared, only the US scored worse — and on some specific physician-reported measures of patient discrimination, NZ scored worse than the US [10].

On Health Outcomes, NZ was identified by the report as among the countries "worthy of study" alongside Australia and Switzerland for outcomes of care [9], placing in the upper-middle of the comparison group on measures including life expectancy and avoidable mortality, though precise placement was complicated by data lag (NZ's 2021 mortality data were preliminary at the time of the report).

On Access to Care and Administrative Efficiency, NZ placed broadly mid-range across the ten-country comparison [9].

The combination — first on Care Process, tied last on Equity — reflects something true about NZ general practice that most local clinicians would recognise. The system is, on the whole, well-organised for the patients who are within it: continuity of care is good, coordination works, the relationship is the centre of gravity. But the system has substantial difficulty with the access end of the equation: the people who are not in the system, who can't afford the co-payment, who don't engage with primary care until the disease is advanced, who experience the encounter when it happens as not taking them seriously. The 2024 Commonwealth Fund finding is that this isn't a small problem. It is a problem on the scale of the worst-performing high-income comparator.

Part IV — Money, workforce, and the comparative context

Two further comparative facts shape the picture.

NZ spends approximately 9–10% of GDP on health, which places it broadly in the middle of the OECD distribution — below the US (around 17%), Germany, France, and Switzerland; above the UK and most Eastern European countries; comparable to Australia. Per capita spending is similarly mid-range. The implication, looking sideways at the Commonwealth Fund 2024 results, is that NZ achieves a 4th-place ranking with mid-range spending — a plausibly favourable cost-quality combination, by international standards.

The workforce picture is less favourable. NZ has approximately 3.6 GPs per 10,000 population, lower than Australia (around 4.0), the UK (around 4.6), and most Northern European systems (5–7) [11]. The RNZCGP has consistently documented that NZ trains and retains fewer GPs than peer countries, has an ageing GP workforce, and relies heavily on internationally-trained doctors. The 2025 announcement of full funding for GPEP years 2 and 3 (previously partly self-funded — anomalous internationally and a known disincentive for graduates choosing general practice) is the first significant workforce intervention in some years [12].

The equity dimension overlaps both money and workforce. Practices serving the highest-needs populations — those with the most multimorbidity, the most deprivation, the highest proportion of Māori and Pacific patients — tend to be the practices with the most workforce pressure, the most difficulty recruiting, and (until the 2025 capitation reweighting) the most underfunded relative to the time their patients require.

Part V — A working position

A few things that I think are defensible.

On the structural distinctiveness. ACC and Pharmac are the two genuinely unusual features of the NZ system, and on the whole both are durable successes — ACC for the universality and administrative efficiency it delivers; Pharmac for the pricing discipline it imposes. Both come with real and visible trade-offs (the narrowness of "personal injury" in the ACC framework; the access gap for newer drugs under Pharmac), but these are trade-offs of the kind built into the design, not failures of execution. The political consensus around both has been broadly stable across changes of government — neither agency is on any current political agenda for fundamental change.

On the 2022 reforms. The Pae Ora Act consolidation of twenty DHBs into Health NZ is structurally sensible and has reduced the worst of the postcode-lottery variation that characterised the DHB era. The disestablishment of Te Aka Whai Ora was a setback for the parallel Māori commissioning structure that had been emerging, and the equity findings in the Commonwealth Fund 2024 data suggest that whatever replaces it has substantial work to do. The system is still bedding in, and stable judgements about whether the reforms have worked are probably premature for several years yet.

On primary care funding. The capitation-plus-co-payment model is the structural weak point of the NZ system in international comparison. It is the part of the design that produces the equity findings. The 2025 capitation reweighting — including, for the first time since 2002, weighting for multimorbidity, rurality, and deprivation — is the most important intervention in this space in two decades. Whether it is sufficient to materially shift the equity picture remains to be seen.

On the comparative picture overall. NZ is, by international standards, a better-performing health system than most New Zealanders give it credit for, and a worse-performing one on equity than most New Zealanders are aware of. The first-place ranking on Care Process is real and worth defending; the tied-last ranking on Equity is real and worth taking very seriously. The system that produces both is the system we have.

The work of comparing health systems internationally is not, in the end, about ranking. It is about identifying what each country does that other countries can learn from. NZ's contributions to the global stock — the no-fault accident scheme of 1974, the monopsony pharmaceutical purchasing model of 1993, the integrated primary care framework that produces the Commonwealth Fund's top Care Process score — are real and underappreciated. NZ's deficits — the equity gap, the workforce shortfall, the structural co-payment barrier in primary care — are also real, and progress on them is what the next decade of system reform should be measured against.

That, at any rate, is the shape of the system, accurately described.
