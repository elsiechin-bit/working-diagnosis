❍ Field Note

Assisted dying — the ethics, the history, and the world map

A working GP's guide to the ethical architecture of assisted dying, the legislative history in NZ, Australia, the UK, North America, Europe, and Asia, and the landmark cases that changed the argument.

3 May 2026

Every GP, sooner or later, sits across from a patient for whom the question is no longer whether they are going to die from this illness, but what that death is going to look like. The question of whether medicine should be able to shorten the interval between now and then — on the patient's terms, under conditions the patient chooses — is one of the most consequential ethical questions in contemporary health policy, and the one that generates more heat relative to light than perhaps any other.

This piece is an attempt at the light. It does not take a position on whether assisted dying is the right policy. It does try to lay out, as clearly as possible, the ethical arguments on both sides, the legislative history in the jurisdictions that have moved (and those that haven't), the landmark cases that shifted the conversation, and the cultural and religious frameworks within which those arguments land differently in different parts of the world. A working GP who has not thought carefully about this territory is a working GP who is not fully prepared for the patients who will, eventually, raise it.

A note on terminology. Assisted dying, voluntary euthanasia, physician-assisted suicide, and medical assistance in dying (MAID) are used in different jurisdictions with overlapping and sometimes contradictory meanings. I will use them with the following distinctions throughout:

  • Voluntary euthanasia: the physician administers the lethal agent
  • Physician-assisted suicide (PAS): the physician provides the means; the patient self-administers
  • Assisted dying: umbrella term encompassing both, used where the distinction is not critical
  • MAID: the Canadian and increasingly international term for the regulated practice

Part I — The ethical architecture

The debate about assisted dying turns on a small number of arguments that have been examined thoroughly for two thousand years and that still do not resolve cleanly. Understanding the structure of the arguments matters because clinicians who encounter these questions in practice — in the consult room, in hospital ethics committees, in public submissions — need to be able to recognise what kind of argument they are hearing.

The autonomy argument. The strongest case for assisted dying begins with individual self-determination. In liberalism's foundational framing, individuals have the right to make decisions about their own bodies and their own lives, provided those decisions do not harm others. If a competent person with a terminal or irremediable illness decides that continued life involves suffering they do not consent to, and if that person persistently and voluntarily seeks assistance in dying, the argument is that the state's prohibition on assistance constitutes an intrusion on bodily autonomy that requires very strong justification. The same legal and ethical principle that gives a competent patient the right to refuse potentially life-saving treatment (a right established in common law and codified in NZ through the Code of Health and Disability Services Consumers' Rights) is, the argument runs, analytically continuous with the right to request assistance in dying [1].

The dignity argument. Closely related but not identical: the claim that there is something important about the manner in which a person dies, not only the fact of death. Prolonged dying involving uncontrolled pain, progressive loss of bodily function and personal dignity, or dependence on forms of care the patient finds intolerable is, on this account, a harm — not a neutral state that medicine and law should be indifferent about. The patient who wants to die while still cognitively intact, in their own home, in the presence of the people they choose, is expressing a preference about the quality of their remaining life that has moral weight [2].

The sanctity of life argument. The strongest case against is that human life has an intrinsic worth that cannot be waived by the individual, because the individual's life is not solely theirs to dispose of. This argument takes its most coherent form within religious traditions — most prominently Catholic, Islamic, and Orthodox Jewish — where life is held to be a gift from God, subject to divine rather than individual authority. But it also has secular versions: the claim that a society that permits and facilitates the ending of life has crossed a threshold that changes its relationship to its weakest and most vulnerable members, in ways that are not easily reversible [2,3].

The slippery slope argument. A different kind of argument: not that assisted dying is intrinsically wrong, but that permitting it under conditions of suffering and terminal illness tends, in practice, to expand. The Belgian and Dutch experience — in which legislation has been extended from terminal physical illness to non-terminal illness, to psychiatric suffering, and (in Belgium) to minors — provides some empirical support for this concern, though whether those extensions represent reasonable application of the same principles or problematic expansion is itself contested [4,5].

The palliative care alternative. The argument that excellent palliative care can adequately address the suffering that motivates requests for assisted dying, and that the appropriate societal investment is in palliative care rather than in legalising death. This is empirically partially true — good palliative care can manage a great deal of suffering — and partially contested by the experience of patients in jurisdictions with good palliative care who nonetheless seek assisted dying, suggesting that the desire to die at a time of one's choosing is not fully reducible to inadequately managed symptoms [6].

The disability rights critique. A specific and important challenge that comes from within progressive politics rather than conservative religion: the argument that legalising assisted dying, even with strict criteria, sends a message that some lives are less worth living than others, and that the subtle and not-so-subtle social pressure on disabled and elderly people to consider themselves a burden makes the concept of voluntary choice in this context more complicated than its proponents acknowledge [7].

The physician role. The tension between the Hippocratic tradition — "first, do no harm," which has historically been interpreted to preclude life-shortening — and the more recent patient-centred framing in which harm is defined by what the patient experiences as harmful, not what the physician judges to be objectively harmful. The British Medical Association moved from opposition to neutrality on assisted dying in 2021 after a membership survey; the Australian and New Zealand medical associations have similarly moved toward neutrality [8].

None of these arguments definitively resolves the question. What they do is structure it. A clinician who understands the argument from autonomy, the argument from sanctity, and the disability rights critique is a clinician who can follow the conversation wherever the patient takes it.

Part II — New Zealand: from Seales to the Act

The legal and public history of assisted dying in New Zealand is, more than most comparable histories, the history of one person.

Lecretia Seales was a Wellington barrister. In 2011, at the age of 38, she was diagnosed with a grade 4 glioblastoma — a brain tumour for which median survival after diagnosis is measured in months. She spent the following years practising law for as long as she was able, engaging with the public debate about end-of-life choice, and pursuing, in the final months of her life, a legal challenge to the existing prohibition on assisted dying.

In 2015, Seales applied to the High Court for declarations that her general practitioner would not be criminally liable under sections 160, 179, or 180 of the Crimes Act if he assisted her to end her life, or administered medication to that end [9]. The case was not a challenge to Parliament's authority to legislate; it was a narrow constitutional claim, framed in terms of the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990, that the Crimes Act provisions as they stood were inconsistent with her rights.

Justice Collins delivered his judgment on 4 June 2015. He found that there was no right to assisted dying in New Zealand law and that the question of whether such a right should exist was one for Parliament rather than the courts [9]. He was careful and non-dismissive in his analysis. Lecretia Seales died on 5 June 2015, the day after the judgment was delivered. She was 42.

The case galvanised public and parliamentary attention in a way that no previous attempt at assisted dying reform in NZ had managed. Her husband, Matt Vickers, spent the following years campaigning for legislative change. The RNZCGP and NZMA shifted positions. David Seymour, ACT Party MP for Epsom, introduced the End of Life Choice Bill in 2017 after extensive consultation. It passed its third reading in November 2019 after a conscience vote (69 to 51), and was subsequently put to a binding public referendum on 17 October 2020, coinciding with the general election. 65.1% of voters supported the Act [10].

The End of Life Choice Act 2019 came into force on 7 November 2021. The eligibility criteria are among the tightest of any jurisdiction that has legalised assisted dying: the person must have a terminal illness likely to end their life within six months (or twelve months for certain conditions), be experiencing significant and ongoing suffering that cannot be relieved in a manner acceptable to them, be aged 18 or over, be a New Zealand citizen or permanent resident (or ordinarily resident for five years), and have decision-making capacity [10]. The person cannot be eligible solely on the basis of mental illness, dementia, or disability. The attending and consulting medical practitioners must independently confirm eligibility. There is a mandatory minimum waiting period between first request and provision.

Section 8 of the Act — which prohibits health practitioners from initiating any discussion of assisted dying with a patient — has been among the more debated provisions in clinical practice. A clinician who believes assisted dying may be relevant to a patient's situation cannot raise it; the patient must raise it first. The tension with the clinical norm of offering patients full information about their options has been acknowledged without being resolved by the 2024 statutory review of the Act [11].

The data from the Act's first years have been watched carefully. In the twelve months to November 2022, 318 people were approved and 219 received assisted dying [12]. The numbers have grown incrementally since. There has been no evidence of the coercive dynamics or expansion of eligibility that opponents predicted; there has also been evidence, from qualitative research and family accounts, that the Act has worked as intended for the people who have used it.

Part III — Australia: the NT experiment and the state-by-state rollout

Australia has the unusual distinction of being the country that held the title of first jurisdiction in the world to legalise voluntary euthanasia — the Northern Territory's Rights of the Terminally Ill Act 1995 — and then almost immediately had that legislation overridden by the federal government [13].

Bob Dent, 66 years old, with end-stage prostate cancer, became the first person in the world to die under a legal assisted dying regime when, on 22 September 1996, he used a computer-controlled delivery system designed by Dr Philip Nitschke to administer a lethal dose of barbiturates [13]. Dent had written an open letter to the Australian Parliament before his death: "If I were to keep a dog in the same condition I am in, I would be prosecuted." Three further people died under the NT Act before it was overridden.

The Euthanasia Laws Act 1997, introduced by federal MP Kevin Andrews and passed with support across both major parties, used the federal government's constitutional power over the Territories to remove their right to legislate on voluntary euthanasia. The NT's law was thus extinguished not through its own democratic processes but through federal intervention — a fact that has remained a source of deep resentment in Territory politics for nearly three decades. Philip Nitschke, who had assisted all four of the NT deaths, went on to found Exit International, which has been operating in the grey spaces of assisted dying advocacy, technology, and practice (and occasional regulatory conflict) ever since.

The state-by-state legalisation that followed over the next two-and-a-half decades was slower, more cautious, and informed by the NT experience. Victoria's Voluntary Assisted Dying Act 2017 — the product of a parliamentary inquiry under former High Court Chief Justice Robert French — introduced the tightest criteria of any jurisdiction: terminal illness, expected death within twelve months (six months for most conditions), twice that timeframe for neurodegenerative disease, Australian resident, 18+, decision-making capacity, no coercion, multiple assessments, waiting periods [14]. It came into force in June 2019.

By 2023 every Australian state and the ACT had enacted voluntary assisted dying legislation. The Northern Territory — completing a historical circle with a particular resonance — also passed its own Act in 2023, now that the territory legislature had regained its ability to do so following federal legislative changes [15]. Australia thus moved from a patchwork with the NT as the isolated early mover to near-complete national coverage in under thirty years, with the middle three decades spent in legislative impasse.

Part IV — The English-speaking world: UK, Canada, United States

United Kingdom. Great Britain has not legalised any form of assisted dying for adults, and has been through more high-profile parliamentary debates and legal challenges without resolution than most comparable jurisdictions. The Suicide Act 1961 decriminalised the act of suicide itself but retained criminal liability for anyone who assisted another to commit suicide — a liability that has shaped all subsequent litigation.

The most important legal developments have been through the courts, not Parliament. In 2009, Debbie Purdy — a journalist with multiple sclerosis who planned to travel to Dignitas in Switzerland — brought a case seeking clarity from the Director of Public Prosecutions about whether her husband would be prosecuted for accompanying her. The House of Lords found in her favour, requiring the DPP to publish prosecution guidance [16]. The guidance, published in 2010, clarified that prosecution was unlikely in cases where assistance was given out of compassion by a close companion, with no financial motivation — establishing a de facto limited tolerance, without decriminalising.

Tony Nicklinson's case, which ran from 2012 to 2014, was more direct. Nicklinson had locked-in syndrome following a brainstem stroke in 2005 and communicated entirely by eye movement. He sought a declaration that it would be lawful for a physician to end his life at his request. The High Court, Court of Appeal, and Supreme Court all declined, finding that a change of this magnitude required parliamentary rather than judicial authority [17]. Nicklinson died of pneumonia eight days after the High Court ruling in August 2012, having refused food following the judgment.

Kim Leadbeater's Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill, introduced in July 2024, passed its second reading in the House of Commons in November 2024 by 330 votes to 275 — a margin that surprised most observers — and entered a protracted committee stage [18]. As of 2026 the legislation has not yet completed its parliamentary passage. Whether it represents the moment the UK finally reaches the destination that most comparable English-speaking jurisdictions have arrived at, or whether it represents another near-miss in a long series of them, remains to be seen.

Canada. Canada's trajectory has been among the most significant — and most debated — of any jurisdiction. Carter v Canada (Attorney General) [2015] SCC 5, a unanimous Supreme Court of Canada decision, struck down the Criminal Code provisions against assisted suicide as inconsistent with the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms in circumstances where an adult had a grievous and irremediable medical condition causing enduring and intolerable suffering [19]. Gloria Taylor, the primary plaintiff — a woman with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis — had died of a perforated colon before the ruling was handed down.

Bill C-14 (2016) introduced MAID in Canada, but with a requirement that the person's "natural death be reasonably foreseeable" — a limitation the courts subsequently found unjustifiably broad in Truchon v Attorney General of Canada (2019). Bill C-7 (2021) removed the "reasonably foreseeable" requirement and created two tracks: Track 1 for those whose natural death is foreseeable, with simplified process, and Track 2 for those with serious and irremediable conditions (including non-terminal) where death is not reasonably foreseeable, requiring a longer assessment period and specialist involvement [20].

Canada's MAID numbers have grown rapidly — from 1,018 in 2016 to 13,241 in 2022, representing 4.1% of all deaths in Canada that year [21]. The planned extension of MAID to persons whose sole underlying condition is a mental disorder has been repeatedly delayed by Parliament (from 2023 to 2024 and again to 2027) in the face of clinical, ethical, and political controversy about whether mental illness meets the criterion of "irremediability" in a clinically defensible way [21]. The Canadian experience has been watched closely by other jurisdictions as a data point on what expansion looks like in practice, and it has been cited by supporters and critics alike in ways that reflect their prior positions more than the data itself.

United States. The US has followed a different path, partly due to its federal structure. Oregon's Death with Dignity Act, passed by ballot initiative in 1994 and surviving a repeal attempt in 1997, was the first US assisted dying law. The key distinction from most other jurisdictions: Oregon's model (and all subsequent US state models) is physician-assisted suicide — the physician prescribes a lethal medication, but the patient self-administers. Active euthanasia where the physician administers remains illegal in all US states.

As of 2026, approximately ten US states have enacted Death with Dignity laws, with further states in legislative process. The cumulative Oregon experience, now nearly thirty years, has been extensively studied and shows a demographic profile (overwhelmingly white, educated, diagnosed with cancer, with existing hospice enrollment) and a reported motivation profile (fear of loss of autonomy and dignity more than pain management) that has informed policy debate globally [22].

The Supreme Court's treatment of the issue has been largely restrictive without being final. Washington v Glucksberg (1997) and Vacco v Quill (1997) declined to find a constitutional right to physician-assisted suicide under the due process and equal protection clauses respectively, finding that the issue was properly for states to decide [23]. The landmark Cruzan v Director, Missouri Department of Health (1990) had established the right of competent patients to refuse medical treatment, setting the foundation for subsequent right-to-die litigation while distinguishing it from the right to seek assistance.

Jack Kevorkian — "Dr Death" — occupies an unusual position in the cultural history of assisted dying in the US. An euthanasia advocate and practitioner who assisted an estimated 130 or more deaths between 1990 and 1998, operating outside any legal framework and in deliberate defiance of Michigan law, he was eventually convicted of second-degree murder in 1999 following the broadcast of a videotaped euthanasia on CBS's 60 Minutes [24]. His case illustrated both the genuine suffering of patients who sought him out and the risks of unregulated practice in the absence of legislative frameworks.

Part V — Europe: the Netherlands, Belgium, and Switzerland

The Netherlands and Belgium represent the most established and extensively studied assisted dying regimes in the world, and the most contentious, for those who are concerned about the direction of expansion.

The Netherlands. Dutch courts had been developing a de facto framework for euthanasia through case law since the 1970s. The Schoonheim case (1984) established that a physician who performed euthanasia in compliance with due care criteria could claim a defence of force majeure (conflict of duties); the Chabot case (1994) extended this to psychiatric suffering. The Termination of Life on Request and Assisted Suicide (Review Procedures) Act 2002 formalised these criteria into statute: the patient must experience unbearable suffering with no prospect of improvement, make a voluntary and persistent request while fully informed, and the physician must consult an independent second physician and comply with due care [4].

The Netherlands now records approximately 9,000 euthanasia deaths per year, representing around 5% of all deaths. The expansion of eligibility to patients with psychiatric illness, dementia, and (in 2023) to children aged 1–12 years with terminal conditions causing unbearable suffering has been gradual and remains deeply contested internationally [4]. Advocates point to the evidence base of careful regulation and low rates of abuse; critics point to the Groningen Protocol (a 2004 framework for euthanasia of severely ill neonates) as a marker of how far the practice has traveled from its origins.

Belgium. Belgium enacted its law on euthanasia in 2002, the same year as the Netherlands. It extended eligibility to minors (without any lower age limit, provided the condition is terminal and suffering unbearable) in 2014, making it the only country in the world to permit euthanasia of children without age restriction [5]. The psychiatric extension that has been controversial in the Netherlands has also operated in Belgium. The Tine Nys case — a young woman with a psychiatric diagnosis who received euthanasia in 2015 and whose family contested the legality of the process — generated years of legal proceedings before three physicians were acquitted in 2020.

Switzerland. Switzerland is an outlier: what is legal there is not euthanasia but assisted suicide, where the patient self-administers. The relevant provision of the Swiss Criminal Code, Article 115, prohibits assisting suicide only when it is done for "selfish motives," which has been interpreted to mean that compassionate assistance is not criminal. Organisations including Dignitas (founded in 1998 in Forch near Zurich) and Exit Deutsche Schweiz operate legally, providing assisted suicide to Swiss residents and — uniquely — to foreign nationals, a practice that has produced the phenomenon of "suicide tourism" to Switzerland from jurisdictions where assisted dying remains illegal [25]. Estimates suggest thousands of foreign nationals have travelled to Dignitas since its founding, including substantial numbers from the UK, Germany, and France.

Part VI — Asia: a different map

With the partial exceptions of India and South Korea, Asia has not moved toward legalising assisted dying in any form, and the explanations are largely cultural and religious rather than merely legislative.

Japan. Euthanasia remains illegal in Japan, and the legal boundaries have been tested through criminal cases rather than legislation. The Tokai University Hospital case (1995), in which a physician administered potassium chloride to a terminally ill patient at the request of family members, resulted in a murder conviction with conditions attached, and the court established criteria under which withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment (but not active euthanasia) might not constitute murder [26]. The case established passive euthanasia as a theoretical possibility while leaving active euthanasia firmly criminal.

Japanese cultural attitudes toward death are shaped by a particular intersection of Buddhist, Shinto, and Confucian influences. The Buddhist framework accepts death as a natural transition; Confucian emphasis on family obligation creates complex dynamics around end-of-life decision-making, including both the pressure to prolong life for family duty reasons and the concern about burdening others. Public polls have consistently shown majority support for some form of assisted dying, but that support has not translated into legislative movement, partly because of the medical profession's strong opposition and partly because of the association of death-hastening with the wartime context [27].

India. The Aruna Shanbaug case (2011) produced the Supreme Court's first serious engagement with assisted dying. Shanbaug had been in a persistent vegetative state since a brutal assault in 1973 — 37 years at the time of the ruling. The Court permitted passive euthanasia (withdrawal of treatment) in certain circumstances with court approval, while rejecting active euthanasia [28]. The Common Cause v Union of India [2018] five-judge bench went further, holding that the right to die with dignity was a fundamental right, that living wills (advance directives) were legally valid, and that passive euthanasia was constitutional [29]. Active euthanasia remains illegal. India thus has a formal legal framework for passive euthanasia and advance directives, sitting within a cultural context where death is shaped by Hindu concepts of karma and rebirth, and where the death-hastening question carries deep spiritual freight.

South Korea and Taiwan. South Korea enacted the Act on Decisions on Life-Sustaining Treatment for Patients in the Process of Dying in 2016, in force from 2018 — the first Asian country to provide a systematic institutional framework for withdrawal of futile life-sustaining treatment for terminally ill patients [30]. Taiwan's Patient Right to Autonomy Act 2019 extends this further, allowing advance directives to refuse life-sustaining treatment and artificial nutrition, enforceable when the patient lacks capacity [30]. Neither legislation permits assisted dying in the active sense; both represent meaningful movement toward patient autonomy at end of life within the predominantly Confucian-influenced contexts of East Asian medical culture.

China. There is no legislation on assisted dying in China, and public discourse on the subject is limited. Confucian values — filial piety, obligation to family, the stigma of death and dying — tend to militate against open discussion, and the cultural norm in Chinese medical contexts has historically been to protect family members from knowledge of terminal prognoses rather than to engage them in shared decision-making [31]. The palliative care movement is growing, but euthanasia debate has not reached legislative agenda in either the PRC or Taiwan's broader political sphere.

Part VII — The sociocultural variables

The distribution of assisted dying legislation across the world maps, incompletely but recognisably, onto a set of cultural and religious variables that are worth naming directly.

Catholic Christianity has been the single most consistent institutional opponent. The Vatican's position — that euthanasia and assisted suicide are "gravely contrary to the dignity of the human person" and violate the commandment against killing — is held as a matter of doctrinal certainty [32]. In predominantly Catholic countries (Ireland, Poland, the Philippines, most of Latin America), legalisation has not occurred. In mixed societies where the Catholic Church retains institutional influence, its opposition has been a significant factor in parliamentary debates — Australia and New Zealand were both examples, as was the UK.

Islam opposes assisted dying consistently across its major schools of jurisprudence. Life is amanah (a trust) given by Allah; suicide and the deliberate hastening of death are prohibited. In Muslim-majority countries, there has been no legislative movement toward legalisation [33]. In diaspora Muslim communities in Western countries, the concern about available assisted dying and its potential to create pressure on Muslim patients has informed community-level submissions to policy processes.

Judaism presents a more nuanced picture. Orthodox Judaism holds the preservation of life (pikuach nefesh) as an obligation that overrides almost all other considerations, and strongly opposes assisted dying. Reform and Progressive Judaism give substantially more weight to patient autonomy and the avoidance of suffering, and have been more supportive of legislation in jurisdictions where it exists [34].

Buddhism is often assumed to be compatible with assisted dying because of its acceptance of impermanence and its emphasis on compassion for suffering. The picture is more complicated. While some Buddhist traditions and teachers have expressed support for dying with dignity, others emphasise that consciousness at the moment of death is karmically significant and should not be artificially foreshortened. Thai Buddhism, for instance, has generally opposed euthanasia despite Thailand being a majority Buddhist country with a significant palliative care movement [27].

Secular liberal societies — particularly the Netherlands, Belgium, Canada, and the Scandinavian countries — have been the most willing to move, consistent with the political theory: individual autonomy as primary, state interference in individual life-and-death decisions requiring strong justification.

The disability rights critique deserves particular attention in a NZ context. Organisations including Not Dead Yet (US and internationally) and CCS Disability Action (NZ) have argued, as a matter of disability politics rather than religious ethics, that the conceptual foundation of assisted dying — that some conditions of existence are worse than death — is inherently devaluing of disabled lives, and that the "safeguards" of any legislation are inadequate against the subtle social and economic pressure on disabled people to consider themselves a burden [7]. This critique has been important in NZ's parliamentary debates and in the design of the End of Life Choice Act's eligibility criteria (which explicitly exclude disability as a standalone basis for eligibility).

Part VIII — Landmark cases

The legal history of assisted dying is substantially the history of individual people in extremity, litigating the question of their own deaths through the courts. The following cases have been the most consequential.

Karen Ann Quinlan, New Jersey, 1975–76. Quinlan fell into a persistent vegetative state following cardiac arrest in 1975, aged 21. Her father sought to have her ventilator removed; the hospital refused. The New Jersey Supreme Court's 1976 ruling that her father could authorise ventilator withdrawal — grounded in the constitutional right of privacy — established in American law the right to withdraw life-sustaining treatment [35]. Quinlan herself lived until 1985 on tube feeding following the ventilator's removal. The case was the foundational right-to-die decision in the common law world, and its progeny extended through every jurisdiction that followed.

Bob Dent, Northern Territory, 1996. As described above — the first legally assisted death in the world. The significance of Dent's death was not only the event itself but what followed: the immediacy of the federal override, the political polarisation that has defined Australian debate for the following three decades, and Philip Nitschke's trajectory from NT physician to international euthanasia activist and founder of Exit International.

Diane Pretty, United Kingdom, 2002. Diane Pretty had motor neurone disease and was paralysed below the neck. She could not commit suicide herself and sought immunity from prosecution for her husband if he assisted her. The UK courts and, ultimately, the European Court of Human Rights both refused, finding that Article 2 (right to life), Article 3 (prohibition of torture), Article 8 (private life), and Article 9 (freedom of conscience) of the ECHR did not encompass a right to die with another's assistance [36]. Pretty died in a hospice in May 2002. The case established the European human rights floor and has been cited in every subsequent UK and European jurisdiction that has considered the question.

Lecretia Seales, New Zealand, 2015. Discussed above. The case is distinctive in the personal history — the letter she wrote to parliament, the judgment delivered the day before she died, the subsequent referendum — and in the way it demonstrates how a single person, litigating their own death, can change the terms of a national conversation in ways that years of parliamentary debate had not.

Tony Nicklinson, United Kingdom, 2012–2014. Nicklinson's case pushed the UK courts as far as they could be pushed without legislative change, and the Supreme Court's divided judgment in 2014 is one of the most important pieces of common law writing on the question [17]. Several justices held that the Assisted Dying Act was a question for Parliament, while acknowledging the profound difficulty of Nicklinson's situation and leaving open the possibility that the courts might intervene if Parliament failed to act. The case has been the primary reference point for every subsequent UK parliamentary debate.

Carter v Canada (Attorney General), 2015. The unanimous Supreme Court of Canada decision that has been most influential internationally after Oregon's initial legislation. The court's framing of the rights question — a grievous and irremediable condition causing enduring intolerable suffering — has been adopted in modified forms by legislative drafters in multiple jurisdictions, including NZ [19]. The speed with which Canada subsequently expanded MAID beyond the Carter framework, through Truchon and Bill C-7, has made the Canadian trajectory both a reference point and a cautionary tale, depending on one's view of the expansion.

Terri Schiavo, Florida, 2003–2005. The Schiavo case was not a case about assisted dying in the usual sense — Schiavo was in a persistent vegetative state and not terminally ill in the conventional meaning — but it became the most politically charged right-to-die dispute in American history. Her husband, Michael Schiavo, sought removal of the feeding tube that had sustained her since her cardiac arrest in 1990; her parents opposed this vigorously, and the case produced Congressional legislation (the Palm Sunday Compromise, signed by President George W. Bush at midnight), repeated interventions by Florida's governor, and a political debate that illuminated, in compressed form, every fault line in the American culture wars around life, death, and the family [37]. The feeding tube was finally removed in March 2005; Schiavo died fifteen days later. The case produced a measurable increase in Americans completing advance directives.

A working position

Some things I think are defensible.

The ethical arguments do not resolve. The autonomy case and the sanctity case are both serious. A clinician who has dismissed either without engaging with it has not thought hard enough. The disability rights critique is not the same argument as the religious argument, and it deserves separate engagement.

The NZ Act is carefully drafted. The eligibility criteria are among the tightest in any jurisdiction, the section 8 prohibition on initiation is a meaningful constraint, and the experience of the first years provides no evidence of the expansionary dynamics opponents predicted. Whether the criteria are too tight — excluding, for example, patients with longer prognoses who are nonetheless suffering unrelievably — is a question the 2024 review raised without resolving.

Palliative care and assisted dying are not mutually exclusive. The international evidence from Oregon and the Netherlands suggests that patients who use assisted dying options do so with high rates of prior hospice and palliative care enrollment — the two are not alternatives. Investing in palliative care does not make the legislative question go away.

The cultural variation is not noise. The fact that the Netherlands and Japan have arrived at very different places is not a difference of irrationality versus rationality; it reflects genuine differences in the weight given to individual autonomy, family obligation, suffering, and death that are embedded in cultural and religious frameworks that deserve to be understood rather than merely noted.

Section 8 creates a clinical bind. The prohibition on health practitioners initiating discussion of assisted dying sits awkwardly in a clinical environment that is otherwise committed to offering patients full information about their options. A patient who might benefit from knowing that the End of Life Choice Act exists may not know to ask. This is worth continued policy attention.

The conversation is part of the work. Clinicians who are not able to engage with the question when patients raise it — who become evasive, who treat the question as abnormal or inappropriate — are not serving their patients well. Patients who ask about assisted dying, in a country where it is legal and they may be eligible, deserve a clear and clinically engaged response.

The question of when, under what circumstances, and by whose hand a human life ends has occupied philosophy, religion, law, and medicine for as long as any of them has existed. It will not be finally settled by a parliament's vote or a court's judgment. What those interventions do is set the legal and clinical framework within which the question gets answered, one patient at a time, in one consultation room at a time.

That, at any rate, is where it lands in general practice.
