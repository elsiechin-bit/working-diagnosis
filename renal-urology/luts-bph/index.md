## Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms and Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia

Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) in men include storage symptoms (urinary frequency, urgency, nocturia) and voiding symptoms (weak stream, straining, hesitancy, incomplete emptying). Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is histological enlargement of the prostate that often accompanies LUTS in older men. Prevalence increases with age: 50% of men >60 years have BPH; >80% by age 80. Most men with LUTS and BPH-compatible findings are treated medically with good outcomes. Early intervention improves quality of life and reduces risk of acute urinary retention and need for surgery.

## Definition and epidemiology

Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) are classified into storage symptoms (urinary frequency, urgency, nocturia) and voiding symptoms (weak urinary stream, straining, hesitancy, incomplete emptying, intermittency). Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) is histological enlargement of the prostate gland; affects microscopic structure and smooth muscle within the transitional zone. Epidemiology: LUTS affects 60–70% of men >70 years; BPH-compatible findings are present in 50% of men >60 years. Nocturia increases with age (average 1–2 episodes in men >50 years). Symptoms cause variable bother; 25–40% of men with moderate LUTS and BPH have significant impact on quality of life and seek treatment.

## Pathophysiology

BPH results from age-related prostate growth mediated by dihydrotestosterone (DHT), derived from testosterone via 5-alpha reductase. Stromal and epithelial cell proliferation increases prostate weight; prostate can grow 1–2% annually in some men. Enlarged prostate increases outflow resistance, requiring greater detrusor pressure for voiding. Secondary detrusor changes (increased muscle mass, trabeculation, loss of compliance) develop; detrusor overactivity causes storage symptoms (frequency, urgency, nocturia). Progressive outlet obstruction may lead to chronic incomplete emptying, elevated post-void residual, and risk of acute retention or upper tract dilatation. Voiding symptoms result from combination of anatomical obstruction (enlarged prostate) and detrusor dysfunction (impaired contractility, overactivity).

## Clinical presentation

**Storage-dominant LUTS**
Frequency (>8 voids daily), urgency (sudden need to void), nocturia (≥2 episodes nightly). Often accompanied by urge incontinence (sudden leakage). Associated with overactive detrusor; may occur independently of obstruction.

**Voiding-dominant LUTS**
Weak urinary stream (slow to start, reduced force), hesitancy (difficulty initiating void), straining (abdominal straining required for void), incomplete emptying (sensation of residual). Results from obstruction and/or detrusor weakness.

**Mixed LUTS**
Many men have both storage and voiding symptoms. Symptom severity varies; some men experience mild symptoms without bother; others have significant impact on quality of life and sleep.

**Acute urinary retention**
Sudden inability to void with suprapubic pain and palpable bladder. Usually precipitated by dehydration, alcohol, cold medications (anticholinergics), or diuretics in men with underlying obstruction.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Symptom onset and progression. Storage vs voiding dominant symptoms. Nocturia frequency (assess sleep disruption). Urinary stream character (weak, interrupted, slow). Hesitancy, straining, incomplete emptying. Prior episodes of retention. Medications (alpha-blockers, anticholinergics, diuretics, decongestants). Impact on quality of life.

International Prostate Symptom Score (IPSS): validated 7-question symptom score (0–35 scale, where <8 is mild, 8–19 moderate, >20 severe). Simple tool for baseline assessment and response monitoring.

**Examination**
Abdomen: suprapubic palpation for fullness (retention). Digital rectal examination (DRE): assess prostate size (normal ~20 g; enlarged is asymmetrical, smooth, boggy), consistency (firm vs nodular suspicious for cancer), tenderness (suggests prostatitis).

**Investigations**
Post-void residual (PVR) by transabdominal ultrasound: normal <50 mL, elevated >100 mL suggests obstruction risk. Elevated PVR indicates need for treatment or investigation of alternative diagnosis (neurogenic bladder, detrusor dysfunction).

Prostate-specific antigen (PSA): measure to assess prostate size (PSA correlates with prostate volume; lower PSA suggests smaller prostate, higher suggests larger) and exclude prostate cancer. Abnormal PSA (>4 ng/mL generally; age-adjusted in older men) warrants further investigation.

Uroflowmetry: measures peak flow rate (normal >15 mL/s; reduced flow suggests obstruction). Limited accuracy in primary care; reserved for specialist assessment.

Urinalysis: exclude haematuria (suggests alternative diagnosis, e.g. bladder malignancy) and infection.

Renal ultrasound: assess upper tract (exclude hydronephrosis from obstruction) if elevated PVR or recurrent retention.

Transrectal ultrasound or MRI: measures prostate size; reserved for specialist assessment in large prostate or when considering pharmacological intervention based on volume.

## Management

**Lifestyle modifications (first-line)**
Fluid management: limit evening fluid intake (avoid alcohol, caffeine, diuretics close to bedtime). Void at regular intervals (scheduled voiding). Double-void technique: void, wait a few minutes, void again to empty residual. Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises) strengthen sphincter, improve continence, may reduce urgency. Avoid anticholinergic medications (decongestants, antihistamines) that impair bladder emptying.

Most men with mild LUTS (IPSS <8) benefit from lifestyle measures alone and do not require medication.

**Pharmacological management**

Alpha-blockers: doxazosin 2–8 mg daily, terazosin 2–10 mg daily, tamsulosin 0.4–0.8 mg daily, alfuzosin 2.5–10 mg daily. Mechanism: relax smooth muscle in prostatic urethra and bladder neck, reducing outflow resistance. Onset ~2 weeks. Efficacy: improve symptom scores by 4–6 points on IPSS and increase peak urinary flow rate by 2–3 mL/s. Side effects: dizziness, syncope (first-dose effect with doxazosin/terazosin; gradual titration needed), retrograde ejaculation (tamsulosin 5–10%).

5-alpha reductase inhibitors: finasteride 5 mg daily or dutasteride 0.5 mg daily. Mechanism: inhibit conversion of testosterone to DHT, reducing prostate volume (~25% reduction at 1 year). Slower onset (6–12 months for full effect). Most beneficial in men with large prostate (>30–40 g on imaging or high PSA). Side effects: erectile dysfunction (~5%), reduced ejaculate volume, breast tenderness. Combination therapy (alpha-blocker + 5-ARi) superior to monotherapy in men with large prostate and moderate-severe symptoms.

Anticholinergics: oxybutynin, solifenacin. Mechanism: reduce detrusor overactivity; effective for storage symptoms (frequency, urgency, nocturia). Risk: increased PVR if obstructed (contraindicated if PVR >100 mL or straining on voiding). Side effects: dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, cognitive effects in elderly.

Beta-3 adrenergic agonist: mirabegron 25–50 mg daily. Relaxes detrusor during storage; improves frequency, urgency, incontinence. Avoids anticholinergic side effects. Monitor blood pressure (slight elevation possible). Recent addition to BPH arsenal.

**Surgical management**
Transurethral resection of prostate (TURP): endoscopic removal of obstructing prostate tissue. Considered for men with moderate-severe symptoms (IPSS >15) refractory to medical therapy after 3 months, recurrent retention, recurrent UTI, or large post-void residual (>100–150 mL). Success rate: symptom improvement in 80–90%; peak flow increase from ~10 mL/s to >15 mL/s.

Laser-assisted procedures (holmium laser enucleation of prostate, HOLEP) used for very large prostate (>45 g).

Prostatectomy rarely indicated unless very large prostate or concomitant pathology.

## Management of acute urinary retention

Acute retention: insert transurethral Foley catheter urgently (relieves pain and protects upper tract). Leave in situ for 3–5 days, then attempt trial without catheter (TWT). Success of TWT ~50% if first retention episode; lower if recurrent. Patients with recurrent retention often require permanent catheterization or surgical intervention.

Initiate or optimize medical therapy (alpha-blocker if not already on) during catheterization period. Measure PVR after TWT attempt. If PVR elevated (>100–150 mL), repeat void trial or plan surgical intervention. Refer to urology for assessment if TWT fails or recurrent retention.

## Complications

**Acute urinary retention**
Sudden inability to void; requires urgent catheterization. Risk increases with outlet obstruction, medications, urinary infection, or constipation.

**Recurrent urinary retention**
≥2 episodes requiring catheterization. Often leads to permanent catheterization or surgical intervention.

**Upper tract dilatation**
Chronic obstruction may lead to hydronephrosis and renal impairment if unrelieved.

**Urinary tract infection**
Elevated PVR and incomplete emptying increase UTI risk. Recurrent UTI in context of BPH may improve with treatment of obstruction.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**For uncomplicated BPH on medical therapy:**
Reassess at 4–6 weeks after initiating alpha-blocker to confirm symptom improvement and tolerance. Symptom score (IPSS) can document response. Annual PSA and DRE to monitor for prostate cancer. Measure PVR annually or if symptoms worsen.

**For men on combination therapy (alpha-blocker + 5-ARi):**
Initial response may take 6–12 months; reassess at 3–6 months to confirm efficacy. Monitor PSA trend (should decline on 5-ARi); PSA doubling time may indicate aggressive cancer.

**For men failing medical therapy:**
Urology referral for consideration of TURP or other intervention. Baseline PVR and uroflowmetry useful before surgical decision.

## Patient education

Explain that BPH is common, usually benign, and effectively managed with medication or lifestyle changes. Emphasize that early intervention improves symptoms and quality of life. Most men do not require surgery. Reinforce pelvic floor exercises and fluid management. Discuss realistic expectations (medication takes weeks to months for full effect). Address sexual side effects (retrograde ejaculation with alpha-blockers, erectile dysfunction potential with 5-ARi) to optimize adherence.

## Field notes

Lifestyle modifications (fluid restriction, scheduled voiding, pelvic floor exercises) are underutilized and should be first-line for mild LUTS. Alpha-blockers provide rapid symptomatic relief and are first-line pharmacotherapy; onset ~2 weeks. 5-alpha reductase inhibitors are slow-acting but significantly reduce prostate volume; most beneficial in men with large prostate (>40 g) and are often underutilized. Combination therapy (alpha-blocker + 5-ARi) is superior to monotherapy in men with large prostate and moderate-severe symptoms but costs higher. Anticholinergics are effective for storage symptoms (frequency, urgency) but contraindicated if PVR >100 mL (risk of retention). PSA elevation correlates with prostate size; do not overinterpret single PSA value without trend. Nocturia is often multifactorial (sleep apnea, diuretics, diabetes, heart failure); address underlying causes rather than treating with medication alone. TURP success rate is high (~80–90% symptom improvement) in carefully selected patients; consider if medical therapy fails after 3 months trial.
