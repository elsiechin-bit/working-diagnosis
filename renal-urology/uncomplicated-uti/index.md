## Uncomplicated Urinary Tract Infection

Uncomplicated urinary tract infection (UTI) is bacterial infection of the lower urinary tract in otherwise healthy, non-pregnant women without structural or functional urinary abnormality. Incidence is 5–15% annually; most are community-acquired. Diagnosis relies on clinical symptoms and urine culture or rapid diagnostic testing. First-line antibiotics are trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or nitrofurantoin. Early treatment prevents progression to pyelonephritis and reduces symptom duration.

## Definition and epidemiology

Uncomplicated UTI is bacterial infection of the bladder (cystitis) in non-pregnant women without indwelling catheter, neurogenic bladder, or structural urinary abnormality. Defined by characteristic symptoms (dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency) with positive urine culture (≥10² CFU/mL midstream clean-catch or ≥10⁵ CFU/mL for symptomatic patients). Epidemiology: women of childbearing age (15–50 years) have annual incidence of 5–15%; prevalence increases with age. Uropathogenic Escherichia coli (UPEC) accounts for 80–85% of cases; other common pathogens include Staphylococcus saprophyticus, Klebsiella pneumoniae, and Proteus mirabilis. Risk factors include female anatomy (short urethra, proximity to rectum), sexual activity, spermicide use, and previous UTI history.

## Pathophysiology

Most UTIs result from ascending infection via the urethra; UPEC expresses fimbriae (P fimbriae, type 1 fimbriae) that bind uroepithelial cells in the bladder trigone. Bacterial adherence initiates mucosal inflammation, triggering recruitment of neutrophils and innate immune response. Healthy urothelium is resistant to infection; protective factors include intact epithelial barrier, normal urine flow, Tamm-Horsfall protein binding bacteria, and acidic urine. Incomplete bladder emptying (residual urine >50–100 mL) predisposes to infection. Estrogen deficiency in postmenopausal women alters urogenital flora, increasing pathogenic bacteria.

## Clinical presentation

Dysuria (burning pain on urination) is cardinal symptom. Urinary frequency (normal is 4–6 times daily; increased to >8 times) and urgency (sudden need to void despite small volume) are typical. Suprapubic discomfort or pressure is common. Urinalysis dipstick often shows leukocytes, nitrites, or blood. Gross haematuria occurs in 5–10%. Systemic symptoms (fever, malaise) are absent in cystitis; presence suggests pyelonephritis. Examination reveals suprapubic tenderness but no loin pain or costovertebral angle tenderness.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Onset and duration of dysuria. Urinary frequency and urgency. Suprapubic discomfort. Absence of fever or loin pain (helps exclude pyelonephritis). Recent sexual activity. Previous UTI history and treatment. Current symptoms in diabetes, immunocompromise, or pregnancy (increases complication risk).

**Examination**
Vital signs assess for fever (suggests pyelonephritis). Suprapubic and costovertebral angle tenderness assessment. Absence of loin tenderness or fever supports uncomplicated cystitis diagnosis.

**Investigations**
Midstream clean-catch urine or catheterized specimen for culture and sensitivity. Urine dipstick: nitrites (positive in 40–50% of UTI; absence does not exclude) and leukocyte esterase (positive in 75–80%, highly sensitive). Presence of both nitrites and leukocytes increases likelihood. Microscopy: pyuria (>5 WBC per high-power field) supports diagnosis; casts suggest pyelonephritis.

Urine culture is gold standard; ≥10² CFU/mL in symptomatic women is diagnostic (lower threshold than historical ≥10⁵ CFU/mL). Culture identifies organism and antimicrobial sensitivities, guiding therapy if empirical antibiotic fails.

Blood cultures and FBC are not needed for uncomplicated cystitis in non-systemically unwell women.

## Diagnosis

Uncomplicated UTI is diagnosed clinically in symptomatic women: dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort. Urine dipstick or microscopy positive for leukocytes (and/or nitrites, blood). Urine culture positive for ≥10² CFU/mL typical urinary pathogen. Absence of fever and loin pain excludes pyelonephritis. Absence of symptoms of obstruction (straining, weak stream) excludes retention. Urine pregnancy test is important if childbearing age (to exclude pregnancy, which changes management).

## Management

**Antibiotic selection**
Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) double-strength 160/800 mg twice daily for 3 days is first-line in most regions; efficacy ~90% for uncomplicated cystitis. Alternatively, nitrofurantoin 100 mg twice daily for 5 days is suitable if TMP-SMX resistance suspected. Nitrofurantoin should not be used if eGFR <30 (accumulates in renal failure).

Fluoroquinolones (e.g. norfloxacin 400 mg BD for 3 days) are second-line due to resistance concerns and adverse effects; reserve for resistant organisms or intolerance to first-line agents.

Cephalexin 250 mg QID for 7 days is alternative if beta-lactam allergy (cephalosporin use acceptable if no severe penicillin reaction).

**Supportive care**
Increased fluid intake. Analgesia: paracetamol or ibuprofen for dysuria. Urinary tract analgesic (potassium citrate 3 g TDS, or phenazopyridine in some regions) may ease dysuria but is not essential.

**Monitoring**
Symptoms typically resolve within 24–48 hours of starting antibiotics. Persistent symptoms after 48 hours warrant repeat urine culture and reassessment (possible resistant organism, misdiagnosis, or pyelonephritis). Most women do not need repeat urine culture after treatment completion if symptoms resolve; follow-up culture is indicated only for treatment failure.

## Complications

**Pyelonephritis**
Untreated UTI can ascend to kidneys, causing pyelonephritis (fever, loin pain, nausea). Requires hospital admission and IV antibiotics. Risk increases with urinary obstruction, reflux, or immunocompromise.

**Recurrent UTI**
Some women experience recurrent episodes (>3 annually). Prophylaxis with continuous low-dose antibiotic (e.g. nitrofurantoin 50 mg nightly) or post-coital prophylaxis may be considered after urology evaluation excludes structural abnormality.

## Prevention and patient education

**Non-pharmacological measures**
Adequate hydration (2–3 L daily). Complete bladder emptying (avoid straining). Void after sexual intercourse. Avoid prolonged catheterization if possible. Avoid irritants (spermicide, douches).

**Prophylaxis in recurrent UTI**
Continuous low-dose antibiotic (nitrofurantoin 50 mg nightly or TMP-SMX 40/200 mg nightly) for women with >2 UTI annually. Duration typically 6–12 months. Post-coital prophylaxis (single-dose antibiotic within 2 hours of intercourse) is alternative if UTI correlates with sexual activity.

Cranberry products (juice, supplements) have weak evidence for reducing UTI recurrence; generally safe but expensive and not routinely recommended.

## Special populations

**Pregnant women**
Asymptomatic bacteriuria (positive urine culture without symptoms) occurs in 2–10% of pregnant women and must be treated to prevent pyelonephritis and adverse pregnancy outcomes. Treatment: TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin safe in second and third trimester (avoid nitrofurantoin near term due to risk of haemolytic anaemia in newborn). Fluoroquinolones avoided in pregnancy.

**Postmenopausal women**
UTI incidence increases due to estrogen deficiency and vaginal flora changes. Topical estrogen cream (conjugated estrogens 0.5–1 g intravaginally 2–3 times weekly) may reduce recurrent UTI incidence and is safe.

**Diabetes**
Increased UTI risk due to glycosuria and possible impaired immune function. Women with diabetes and UTI warrant closer follow-up (increased pyelonephritis risk). Glucose control reduces infection risk.

## Monitoring and follow-up

For uncomplicated UTI with typical presentation and response to antibiotics: reassure patient. No routine follow-up urine culture is needed after symptom resolution. Counsel on prevention measures (hydration, post-coital voiding).

For recurrent UTI (>3 annually in young women, >2 in postmenopausal women): urological assessment (renal ultrasound) to exclude anatomical abnormality. If imaging normal and no anatomical abnormality found, consider prophylactic strategies.

## Field notes

Uncomplicated cystitis is benign and self-limited in most women; three-day antibiotic course is effective and reduces adverse effects compared to longer courses. Avoid unnecessary imaging (renal ultrasound) unless recurrent UTI or atypical features. Rapid dipstick testing (leukocytes, nitrites) can guide empirical antibiotic initiation while culture results pending. Fluoroquinolones should be reserved for resistant organisms due to resistance concerns and adverse effects (tendinopathy, QT prolongation). Nitrofurantoin is excellent first-line for uncomplicated cystitis; contraindicated only if eGFR <30. Asymptomatic bacteriuria in non-pregnant, non-catheterized women does not require treatment (treatment does not reduce symptomatic UTI risk and contributes to resistance). Postmenopausal women benefit from vaginal estrogen if recurrent UTI; this is underutilized in practice.
