## Recurrent Urinary Tract Infection

Recurrent urinary tract infection (UTI) is defined as ≥3 episodes of cystitis or ≥2 episodes of pyelonephritis in 12 months. Affects 15–25% of women with prior UTI and causes significant morbidity and healthcare burden. Most recurrent UTI in otherwise healthy women is caused by reinfection with the same or different uropathogenic bacteria; relapse (same strain) is less common. Risk stratification and targeted investigations determine whether anatomical abnormality requires exclusion or whether non-pharmacological and prophylactic strategies suffice.


## Definition and epidemiology

Recurrent UTI is defined as ≥3 cystitis episodes or ≥2 pyelonephritis episodes within 12 months in non-pregnant women. May represent relapse (same organism within weeks, suggesting incomplete treatment) or reinfection (new organism or strain after symptom-free interval >2 weeks). Epidemiology: 15–25% of women experiencing first UTI develop recurrent episodes; risk increases with age, diabetes, incomplete bladder emptying, and sexual activity frequency. Most recurrent UTI in otherwise healthy women is uncomplicated and caused by reinfection; anatomical abnormality is uncommon (<5%) in non-pregnant women without pyelonephritis. Postmenopausal women have higher recurrence due to estrogen deficiency and altered flora.

## Pathophysiology

Most recurrent uncomplicated UTI results from reinfection with new bacterial strain; host and bacterial factors determine susceptibility. Bacterial factors: uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) with enhanced virulence factors (adhesins, toxins, polysaccharide capsule) are more likely to cause recurrence. Host factors: altered uroepithelial cell receptors (glycosphingolipid expression influenced by blood group antigen), incomplete bladder emptying (residual urine >100 mL favours bacterial growth), neurogenic bladder, estrogen deficiency increasing pathogenic flora, genetic predisposition (impaired innate immune response to UPEC). Anatomical abnormality (reflux, obstruction, diverticulum) predisposes to recurrent pyelonephritis or severe infection.

## Clinical presentation

Symptoms identical to uncomplicated UTI: dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort. May occur at variable intervals (monthly, sporadically). Fever and loin pain indicate pyelonephritis, warranting urgent assessment. Some women report symptom-free interval between episodes (suggesting reinfection); others note clusters of infection during stress, hormonal changes (menstrual cycle, menopause), or increased sexual activity (suggesting cofactor role).

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Number and timing of UTI episodes in past 12 months. Characteristics: cystitis only or pyelonephritis. Prior imaging or investigations. Identified risk factors: incomplete emptying (hesitancy, weak stream), diabetes control, spermicide use, sexual activity frequency, postmenopausal status, prior urological abnormality or surgery. Obstetric history (reflux in childhood, prior catheterization).

**Examination**
General assessment for signs of pyelonephritis (fever, loin tenderness). Urinary retention assessment: suprapubic palpation for fullness (suggests retention). Post-void residual volume can be estimated by ultrasound (normally <50 mL; >100 mL suggests incomplete emptying).

**Investigations**
Renal ultrasound is first-line imaging for recurrent UTI in non-pregnant women. Assesses kidney size (small suggests chronic disease or scarring), hydronephrosis (suggests obstruction), parenchymal echogenicity (hyperechoic suggests scarring from chronic pyelonephritis), polycystic changes, and focal lesions. >95% sensitive for hydronephrosis.

Urine culture from acute episode confirms organism and sensitivities; helps guide prophylactic choice if resistance identified.

DMSA nuclear medicine scan assesses renal scarring in recurrent pyelonephritis; reserved for cases with fever and prior pyelonephritis (not routine for uncomplicated cystitis recurrence).

Cystography or urodynamics are not indicated for uncomplicated recurrent cystitis in women without neurological symptoms or history of reflux.

## Differential diagnosis

**Asymptomatic bacteriuria**
Positive urine culture without symptoms. Should not be treated in non-pregnant, non-catheterized women (treatment does not reduce symptomatic UTI risk; increases resistance). Reassure patient and advise no treatment needed.

**Urethritis or vulvovaginitis**
Dysuria with negative urine culture. May present as recurrent "UTI-like" symptoms. Requires different management (topical agents, antifungals, or antivirals depending on cause).

**Interstitial cystitis**
Chronic suprapubic pain with urinary frequency and urgency but negative urine cultures. Warrants cystoscopy for diagnosis; specialist assessment needed.

## Management

**Non-pharmacological strategies (first-line)**
Increased fluid intake: 2–3 L daily promotes urine dilution and frequent voiding. Adequate bladder emptying: ensure complete void (double-void technique if incomplete emptying suspected). Post-coital voiding within 2 hours of intercourse reduces infection risk significantly if UTI correlates with sexual activity. Avoid spermicide (alters vaginal flora, increases pathogenic bacteria). Cranberry products (juice, supplements) have weak evidence but are safe; cost limits routine recommendation.

Behavioral modification: avoidance of urinary irritants (douches, feminine hygiene sprays), loose-fitting clothing, adequate hydration during menstrual cycle.

**Prophylactic antibiotic therapy**
Indicated if non-pharmacological measures fail or frequent recurrence severely impacts quality of life. Two strategies:

Continuous prophylaxis: low-dose antibiotic nightly for 6–12 months. Options include nitrofurantoin 50 mg daily (excellent urinary concentration, low systemic absorption, well-tolerated), trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 40/200 mg daily, or cephalexin 125 mg daily. Efficacy is 80–90%; recurrence common upon discontinuation. Allows symptom-free periods during prophylaxis.

Post-coital prophylaxis: single-dose antibiotic within 2 hours of intercourse if UTI clearly correlates with sexual activity. Options: single-dose TMP-SMX DS or nitrofurantoin 100 mg. Efficacy is 90–95% in women with intercourse-related UTI.

**Estrogen replacement in postmenopausal women**
Topical vaginal estrogen (conjugated estrogens 0.5–1 g intravaginally 2–3 times weekly, or vaginal estradiol tablets) restores normal lactobacilli flora and reduces recurrent UTI incidence significantly. No systemic absorption at recommended doses. Safe for long-term use. Often underutilized despite evidence.

**Manage modifiable risk factors**
Glycaemic control in diabetes (reduces glycosuria, improves immune function). Hormone replacement therapy: estrogen-containing therapy may increase UTI risk; consider alternative or topical estrogen in postmenopausal women with recurrence.

## Complications

**Pyelonephritis**
Ascending infection to kidneys. Requires hospital admission and IV antibiotics. Repeated episodes may cause renal scarring and chronic kidney disease.

**Renal scarring**
Develops in 10–15% of women with recurrent pyelonephritis. DMSA scan detects scarring; progressive scarring is rare in adults but may compromise renal function if bilateral.

**Chronic kidney disease**
Repeated pyelonephritis episodes may contribute to progressive renal function loss, particularly if bilateral scarring develops.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**For uncomplicated recurrent cystitis:**
After imaging excludes anatomical abnormality, reassure patient. Trial non-pharmacological strategies for 3–6 months. If recurrence continues and impacts quality of life, initiate prophylactic antibiotic with 6–12 month duration. Re-assess after discontinuation; some women achieve symptom-free periods despite prior pattern.

**For recurrent pyelonephritis:**
DMSA scan to assess scarring. Specialist urology referral for imaging review and consideration of more intensive investigation (e.g., cystoscopy, urodynamics) if structural abnormality suspected.

**Follow-up urine culture:**
Not needed for uncomplicated recurrent cystitis if symptoms resolve with treatment. Culture indicated for recurrent pyelonephritis to guide antibiotic choice.

## Prevention

Non-pharmacological measures (hydration, post-coital voiding, spermicide avoidance) are first-line and should be emphasized regardless of imaging findings. Prophylactic antibiotic is reserved for women with frequent recurrence despite non-pharmacological measures or severe impact on quality of life. Vaginal estrogen in postmenopausal women is underutilized and should be offered.

## Patient education

Explain that recurrent UTI does not indicate serious disease in most healthy women. Imaging will exclude structural abnormality. Hydration and post-coital voiding are simple, effective prevention. Prophylactic antibiotic is safe long-term option if non-pharmacological measures insufficient. Asymptomatic bacteria does not require treatment. Symptom recurrence should prompt repeat culture to confirm diagnosis (avoid misdiagnosis of other urinary symptoms as UTI).

## Field notes

Uncomplicated recurrent cystitis in young healthy women has low anatomical abnormality rate (<5%); imaging is more reassurance than diagnostic necessity. Non-pharmacological strategies are underutilized and should be emphasized strongly before prophylaxis. Nitrofurantoin 50 mg nightly is excellent choice for continuous prophylaxis (concentrated in urine, minimal systemic absorption, well-tolerated). Post-coital prophylaxis is highly effective if intercourse clearly correlates with infection; this strategy is underutilized. Topical vaginal estrogen in postmenopausal women is highly effective and often overlooked; should be offered before or alongside antibiotic prophylaxis. Recurrent pyelonephritis (not just cystitis) warrants more aggressive investigation (DMSA scan, specialist input) due to scarring risk. Most women with uncomplicated recurrent cystitis do not benefit from specialist referral; reserve urology input for pyelonephritis, atypical features, or imaging abnormality.
