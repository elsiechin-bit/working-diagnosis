## Chronic Kidney Disease – Diagnosis

Chronic kidney disease affects 10–15% of adults and is often undiagnosed until advanced. Definition relies on reduced glomerular filtration rate (eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m²) and/or persistent albuminuria. Most cases are mild and progress slowly; however, early detection and risk stratification enable intervention to slow progression and reduce cardiovascular complications.


## Definition and epidemiology

Chronic kidney disease is defined as persistent (≥3 months) reduction in glomerular filtration rate (eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73m²) and/or markers of kidney damage (albuminuria, haematuria, structural imaging abnormalities). CKD is staged by eGFR: G1 (≥90), G2 (60–89), G3a (45–59), G3b (30–44), G4 (15–29), G5 (<15). Albuminuria categories: A1 (<30 mg/mmol), A2 (30–300), A3 (>300). Epidemiology: CKD stage G3-G5 affects 8–10% of adults in developed countries; prevalence increases with age, diabetes, and hypertension. Māori and Pacific Islander populations have higher incidence due to high prevalence of diabetes and hypertension. Most individuals with CKD G1-G3a do not progress to kidney failure; however, CKD with albuminuria and reduced eGFR carry higher progression risk.

## Pathophysiology

Kidney disease reduces the number of functioning nephrons, increasing filtration burden on remaining glomeruli. Hyperfiltration leads to glomerular hypertension, proteinuria, and progressive sclerosis. Common mechanisms include glomerulonephritis (immune-mediated), diabetic nephropathy (hyperglycaemia-driven glomerular basement membrane thickening and mesangial expansion), hypertensive nephrosclerosis (chronic hypertension), and tubulointerstitial disease (drugs, infection, obstruction). Reduced eGFR impairs clearance of small solutes (creatinine, urea), leading to uraemia in advanced disease. Loss of renal endocrine function (erythropoietin, calcitriol production) drives anaemia and mineral bone disease.

## Clinical presentation

**Early CKD (G1-G3)**
Most are asymptomatic. Often discovered incidentally on routine screening (elevated serum creatinine or urine protein) or when investigating other conditions.

**Advanced CKD (G4-G5)**
Fatigue, nausea, poor appetite, pruritus, muscle cramps. Peripheral oedema or pulmonary oedema from sodium and water retention. Hypertension is present in >80%. Signs of uraemia: confusion, hiccup, pericardial friction rub (rare, suggests pericarditis). Anaemia symptoms: dyspnoea, fatigue, pallor.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Duration of kidney disease. Prior episodes of haematuria, proteinuria, or hypertension. Family history of CKD, polycystic kidney disease, or early kidney failure. Drug history: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, lithium, contrast agents. Diabetes, hypertension, autoimmune disease. Urinary symptoms: dysuria, frequency, haematuria.

**Examination**
Blood pressure (CKD is both cause and consequence of hypertension). Signs of volume overload: oedema, hypertension, pulmonary crackles, jugular venous pressure elevation. Signs of uraemia: poor oral hygiene, pruritus marks, muscle wasting. Abdominal palpation: enlarged or palpable kidneys (polycystic disease).

**Investigations**

Serum creatinine and eGFR: use KDIGO formula (CKD-EPI) which incorporates age, sex, and race to estimate GFR. eGFR <60 defines CKD. Serial eGFR assesses progression rate. Creatinine is filtered but also secreted; eGFR slightly overestimates true GFR in advanced CKD.

Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) from early morning spot urine is preferred to dipstick. <30 mg/mmol normal, 30–300 microalbuminuria, >300 macroalbuminuria. Proteinuria is significant marker of progression and cardiovascular risk.

Electrolytes: potassium, sodium, bicarbonate. Hyperkalaemia develops in advanced CKD. Metabolic acidosis from reduced renal excretion of hydrogen ions.

Full blood count: anaemia is common; check iron studies.

Calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase: assess mineral bone disease. Hypocalcaemia and hyperphosphataemia develop in advanced CKD. PTH level confirms secondary hyperparathyroidism.

Uric acid: elevated; assess for gout risk.

Lipids: dyslipidaemia is common and increases cardiovascular risk in CKD.

Liver function tests: establish baseline.

Fasting glucose or HbA1c: screen for diabetes.

Urinalysis: blood, protein, casts, white cells. Casts or dysmorphic RBCs suggest glomerulonephritis. Sterile pyuria may suggest tubulointerstitial disease.

Renal ultrasound: assess kidney size (small kidneys suggest chronic disease; normal size in early disease). Exclude hydronephrosis (obstruction), polycystic changes, or renal artery stenosis. Ultrasound is first-line for structural assessment.

Renal artery Doppler or CT angiography if suspected renal artery stenosis (resistant hypertension, abrupt worsening of renal function after ACE/ARB initiation).

## Severity assessment and CKD staging

**KDIGO classification**
Staging by eGFR and albuminuria categories determines risk of progression and cardiovascular events.

G1–G2 with A1: low risk (eGFR ≥60, no albuminuria).
G3–G5 with A1 or G1–G2 with A2–A3: moderate to high risk (reduced eGFR or albuminuria present).
G4–G5: very high risk (eGFR <30 indicates imminent or current kidney failure).

**Proteinuria assessment**
UACR <30 mg/mmol: normal.
UACR 30–300 mg/mmol: microalbuminuria; indicates early glomerular damage and cardiovascular risk.
UACR >300 mg/mmol (≈1 g/day): macroalbuminuria; indicates significant proteinuria and high progression risk.

**Progression risk**
Rapid eGFR decline (>5 mL/min/1.73m² annually) suggests aggressive disease.
Presence of albuminuria increases progression risk 2–4 fold.
Diabetes, hypertension, and reduced eGFR are independent progression risk factors.

## Management

**Blood pressure control**
Target BP <120/90 mmHg in CKD (KDIGO 2021). ACE inhibitor or ARB are first-line for CKD G3-G5 with albuminuria or diabetes, as they reduce glomerular hypertension and proteinuria. Caution: initial eGFR drop of 5–10% expected; monitor 1–2 weeks after initiation. Hyperkalaemia risk increases with reduced eGFR; monitor potassium.

**Management of albuminuria**
ACE inhibitor or ARB reduces albuminuria and slows progression. Add second antihypertensive (diuretic, calcium channel blocker, beta-blocker) if BP control inadequate.

**Glycaemic control**
Target HbA1c 7% (53 mmol/mol) in most patients; relax to 8% (64 mmol/mol) if advanced CKD to avoid hypoglycaemia. Use GLP-1 receptor agonist or SGLT2 inhibitor (SGLT2i reduce proteinuria and slow progression independent of glucose control).

**Lipid management**
Statin for all CKD patients age >50 or those with diabetes. Target LDL <1.8 mmol/L if known CVD.

**Anaemia management**
If Hb <100 g/L and eGFR <60, investigate: iron studies (target ferritin 100–500 µg/L, TSAT 20–50%), vitamin B12, folate. Iron supplementation if iron-deficient. Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) if persistent anaemia despite iron repletion and eGFR <30.

**Mineral bone disease management**
Monitor calcium, phosphate, PTH. Avoid hypercalcaemia. Limit phosphate-rich foods. Phosphate binder (calcium carbonate or non-calcium binder) if phosphate >1.5 mmol/L. Calcitriol or calcimimetic if PTH elevated despite phosphate control.

**Avoid nephrotoxins**
NSAIDs are contraindicated in CKD G4-G5 or those with reduced eGFR + proteinuria; increase hyperkalemia and progression risk. Contrast agents require hydration and reduced-osmolar agents in advanced CKD. Careful dose adjustment of drugs cleared by kidney.

**Cardiovascular risk reduction**
Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin) for secondary prevention in CKD + established CVD; consider for primary prevention if high 10-year CVD risk. Manage other CVD risk factors.

## Monitoring and follow-up

**Frequency**
CKD G1-G2: annual eGFR and UACR if risk factors present.
CKD G3a: annual eGFR and UACR.
CKD G3b: 6-monthly eGFR and UACR.
CKD G4: 3-monthly eGFR and UACR; consider nephrology co-management.
CKD G5: monthly monitoring; establish renal replacement therapy plan.

**Trajectory assessment**
Plot eGFR over time to assess slope of decline. Rapid decline (>5 mL/min annually) suggests progressive disease and warrants investigation for reversible causes or primary glomerulonephritis.

**Nephrology referral**
Refer at eGFR <30 (G4), earlier if rapidly progressive, or if complex secondary causes suspected.

## Complications

**Progressive kidney failure**
Leads to end-stage renal disease (ESRD, eGFR <15). Requires dialysis or transplantation.

**Mineral bone disease**
Secondary hyperparathyroidism, vascular calcification, osteoporosis. Increases fracture risk and cardiovascular mortality.

**Anaemia**
From reduced erythropoietin production and shortened RBC lifespan. Treatable with iron supplementation and ESAs.

**Hypertension**
Both cause and consequence of CKD. Difficult to control; often requires >3 agents.

**Cardiovascular disease**
CKD itself is independent CVD risk factor; proteinuria increases risk further.

**Acute kidney injury**
Patients with CKD are at higher risk of AKI with superimposed insults (sepsis, dehydration, nephrotoxic drug).

## Prevention and risk factor modification

**Modifiable risk factors**
Tight blood pressure control (target <120/90). Glycaemic control in diabetes. Lipid management. Smoking cessation. Healthy diet (low sodium, moderate protein). Weight management.

**Non-modifiable factors**
Age, sex, ethnicity, genetic predisposition. Māori and Pacific Islander populations have higher CKD prevalence.

## Field notes

Early CKD G1-G3 often remains asymptomatic; screening is important in high-risk populations (diabetes, hypertension, age >60). eGFR-based staging is simple and universally applicable; combine with albuminuria assessment for risk stratification. ACE/ARB are standard first-line, reducing proteinuria and slowing progression independent of BP reduction; initial mild eGFR drop is expected and does not indicate harm. SGLT2 inhibitors are increasingly used for additional slowing of progression and cardiovascular protection. Rapid eGFR decline warrants investigation for secondary causes (obstruction, vasculitis, medication-induced). Nephrology referral is appropriate once eGFR reaches G4 (<30) to plan long-term renal replacement therapy and manage mineral bone disease. Most people with CKD G3 remain stable for years; reassure and focus on cardiovascular risk reduction and progression prevention.
