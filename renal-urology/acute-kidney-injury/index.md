## Acute Kidney Injury

Acute kidney injury is rapid deterioration in renal function (increase in serum creatinine or decrease in urine output) over hours to days. Approximately 10–20% of hospitalised patients develop AKI; community-acquired AKI is less frequent but serious. Early recognition and intervention—fluid resuscitation for prerenal causes, drug cessation for medication-induced AKI, or urinary obstruction relief for postrenal AKI—can prevent progression to acute kidney failure requiring dialysis.


## Definition and classification

Acute kidney injury is rapid (hours to days) loss of renal function defined by creatinine rise and/or oliguria. KDIGO staging: Stage 1 (creatinine 1.5–1.9× baseline or urine output 0.5–0.9 mL/kg/hour), Stage 2 (creatinine 2–2.9× baseline or urine output 0.5 mL/kg/hour for 6–12 hours), Stage 3 (creatinine ≥3× baseline or urine output <0.3 mL/kg/hour for ≥24 hours, or anuria for ≥12 hours). AKI is categorised by pathophysiology: prerenal (inadequate renal perfusion, ~60% of AKI), intrinsic renal (parenchymal damage, ~35%), postrenal (obstruction, ~5%).

## Pathophysiology

**Prerenal AKI**
Occurs when glomerular filtration pressure falls due to systemic hypotension or decreased renal perfusion (haemorrhage, sepsis, dehydration, cardiogenic shock). Tubular function remains intact; AKI is rapidly reversible if perfusion restored. Most common in community.

**Intrinsic renal AKI**
Parenchymal damage: acute tubular necrosis (ischaemia from hypotension, toxic drug—gentamicin, amphotericin, cisplatin), glomerulonephritis, acute interstitial nephritis (medication, infection, autoimmune), rhabdomyolysis (myoglobinuria damages tubules), pigment nephropathy (haemoglobin in massive haemolysis).

**Postrenal AKI**
Obstruction of urine flow: urinary retention (post-prostatectomy, neurogenic bladder), bladder obstruction (tumour, clot), ureteric obstruction (stone, tumour, fibrosis), or urethral obstruction.

## Clinical presentation

**Prerenal**
Signs of volume depletion: tachycardia, hypotension, reduced skin turgor, dry mucous membranes. History of recent bleeding, diarrhoea, vomiting, reduced oral intake, or sepsis. Urine output low but initially present; dark urine (concentrated) common.

**Intrinsic renal**
Oliguria or anuria. Oedema, hypertension, pulmonary rales (volume overload from reduced GFR). Rash or joint pain (vasculitis). Haematuria or haemoptysis (glomerulonephritis). Myalgia and dark urine (rhabdomyolysis). Fever and rash (interstitial nephritis).

**Postrenal**
Anuria or significant oliguria. Suprapubic fullness (urinary retention). Flank pain (stone, obstruction). Absence of urine output despite adequate intake.

## Assessment and investigations

**History**
Timeline: when did symptoms start? Recent illness (sepsis, diarrhoea), medication change (NSAIDs, ACE/ARB, aminoglycosides started recently), trauma, muscle injury, surgery. Baseline renal function if known. Urine output: is patient passing urine normally?

**Examination**
Vital signs: hypotension or hypertension. Volume status: skin turgor, mucous membranes, JVP, oedema, lung crackles. Muscle tenderness (rhabdomyolysis). Rash, arthritis, or systemic signs (vasculitis).

**Investigations**

Serum creatinine and urea: assess severity and trend. Compare to baseline if available. Electrolytes (potassium, sodium, bicarbonate): hyperkalaemia is dangerous. Calcium, phosphate: hypocalcaemia and hyperphosphataemia develop if prolonged.

Urinalysis: dark urine (myoglobin, haemoglobin, bilirubin). Casts and dysmorphic RBCs suggest glomerulonephritis. Eosinophiluria suggests acute interstitial nephritis. Dipstick positive for blood but RBC casts absent suggests myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis).

Urine sodium and osmolality: help distinguish prerenal from intrinsic AKI. Prerenal: low urine sodium (<20 mmol/L) and high osmolality (>500 mOsmol/kg) reflect intact tubular reabsorption. Intrinsic AKI: high urine sodium and low osmolality reflect tubular dysfunction.

Fractional excretion of sodium (FENa): FENa = (urine Na × plasma creatinine) / (plasma Na × urine creatinine) × 100. FENa <1% suggests prerenal; >2% suggests intrinsic AKI. Limited use if patient on diuretics.

Full blood count: anaemia if bleeding. Elevated WBC or left shift (infection).

Urine creatinine kinase (CK) and myoglobin: if rhabdomyolysis suspected.

Coagulation (INR, APTT): assess for DIC if severe.

Renal ultrasound: assess kidney size (small suggests chronic disease, not AKI), exclude hydronephrosis (postrenal obstruction). Increased echogenicity may suggest acute interstitial nephritis.

Renal artery Doppler if renal artery stenosis suspected (rapid eGFR decline after ACE/ARB initiation).

## KDIGO staging

Stage 1: creatinine 1.5–1.9× baseline, urine output 0.5–0.9 mL/kg/hour for 6–12 hours.
Stage 2: creatinine 2–2.9× baseline, urine output 0.5 mL/kg/hour for 12+ hours.
Stage 3: creatinine ≥3× baseline, urine output <0.3 mL/kg/hour for 24+ hours, or anuria for 12+ hours.

## Management by AKI type

**Prerenal AKI**
IV fluid resuscitation: isotonic crystalloid (normal saline or balanced crystalloid). Initial bolus 500 mL–1 L over 20 minutes, assess response. Target urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hour, heart rate <100 bpm, blood pressure normalisation. Reassess volume status regularly.

Hold NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, and ARBs temporarily (often withheld until creatinine stabilises).

Treat underlying cause: sepsis (antibiotics), bleeding (transfusion), cardiac failure (inotropes).

Most prerenal AKI reverses within 24–72 hours of fluid repletion.

**Intrinsic renal AKI**
Identify and treat underlying cause: glomerulonephritis (refer for biopsy, immunosuppression if rapidly progressive), acute interstitial nephritis (stop offending drug), rhabdomyolysis (aggressive IV hydration, urine alkalinisation, monitor for hyperkalemia).

Careful fluid management: avoid volume overload which exacerbates pulmonary oedema in AKI. Central line useful for careful volume titration if haemodynamically unstable.

Treat hyperkalaemia: restrict potassium, diuretics (furosemide 40–80 mg IV), shift potassium intracellularly (insulin 10 units IV + dextrose 25 g IV, salbutamol nebulised, calcium resonium 15 g TDS), or emergent dialysis if life-threatening (peaked T waves, ventricular arrhythmia).

Monitor and manage mineral bone disease: phosphate binders if phosphate elevated, dietary restriction.

Anaemia: if Hb <70 g/L or symptomatic, consider transfusion.

**Postrenal AKI**
Urgent imaging: renal ultrasound or CT to confirm obstruction. Urology referral for obstruction management (catheterisation for retention, stent or percutaneous nephrostomy for upper tract obstruction, lithotripsy for stones).

IV hydration before relief to prevent post-obstructive diuresis (can lead to severe volume depletion and acute hypotension). After relief, monitor urine output carefully; limit IV fluids if polyuric.

## Monitoring and supportive care

**Daily monitoring**
Vital signs, urine output, weight. Serum creatinine and electrolytes daily (at least) during acute phase. ECG if potassium >6.5 mmol/L or symptomatic (peaked T waves).

**Nutrition**
Reduced protein intake (0.6 g/kg/day) to minimise uraemia. Adequate calories (carbohydrate) to prevent catabolism. Fluid restriction if oliguric and fluid overload.

**Dialysis indications**
Refractory hyperkalaemia (>7 mmol/L despite conservative management), metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1), severe uraemia (urea >60 mmol/L), or pulmonary oedema despite diuretics. AKI requiring dialysis usually lasts days to weeks; most patients recover renal function.

## Complications

**Hyperkalaemia**
Life-threatening; can cause fatal arrhythmia. Manage urgently with calcium, insulin, beta-2 agonist, or dialysis.

**Fluid overload**
Pulmonary oedema, hypertension. Manage with diuretics and fluid restriction; dialysis if refractory.

**Metabolic acidosis**
Accumulation of organic acids from reduced excretion and catabolism. Usually mild; manage with bicarbonate if severe (pH <7.1).

**Infection**
AKI patients are at high risk of nosocomial infection. Monitor for fever, sepsis.

## Recovery and follow-up

Most AKI reverses within days to weeks if underlying cause treated. Monitor creatinine after discharge; some patients have persistent CKD (never fully recover renal function). Educate on medication avoidance (NSAIDs, nephrotoxic drugs) and risk factor management to prevent recurrent AKI.

## Patient education and prevention

Explain importance of staying hydrated, especially in hot weather or with diarrhoea. Avoid NSAIDs without medical supervision. If diabetic or hypertensive, continue BP and glucose control. Report new urinary symptoms or oliguria immediately.

## Field notes

Prerenal AKI is most common and usually rapidly reversible with IV hydration—do not delay fluids if volume depleted. Intrinsic AKI from acute tubular necrosis is serious but often recovers if underlying insult removed and supportive care provided. Postrenal AKI is preventable with prompt recognition and relief of obstruction. Hyperkalaemia in AKI is life-threatening; do not delay treatment. Most AKI resolves within 1–4 weeks; dialysis is temporary bridge, not permanent. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs during AKI recovery. Screen for CKD after AKI recovery; some patients progress to chronic disease.
